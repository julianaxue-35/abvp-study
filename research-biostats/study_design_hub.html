Study Designs

Every design answers a different question and yields a different measure. The fastest exam hook is direction of reasoning: cohort reasons forward from exposure, case-control reasons backward from outcome, cross-sectional captures both at one instant.

The organising framework ref

  • Descriptive studies describe who, what, where and when. No comparison group, so they generate hypotheses rather than test them.
  • Analytic observational studies add a comparison group and measure an association, but the investigator only observes; the exposure is not assigned.
  • Experimental studies have the investigator assign the intervention, ideally at random. This is the only category that establishes causation directly.

Exam trap

The ecological fallacy: an association seen between group-level rates does not prove the same association holds at the individual level. A case report or series can never prove a risk factor, because it has no comparison group.

Cohort starts from exposure and follows forward; case-control starts from the outcome and looks back. Original construction.

Cohort: yields incidence and relative risk; establishes temporality; inefficient for rare outcomes. Case-control: yields the odds ratio; efficient for rare or slow diseases; prone to recall and selection bias; the odds ratio approximates relative risk only when the disease is rare. Analytic cross-sectional: yields prevalence; cannot establish temporality, so reverse causation is possible.

Experimental studies ref

The investigator assigns the intervention. A randomised controlled trial allocates subjects at random to intervention or control. Randomisation balances known and unknown confounders, which is the source of the RCT's causal strength.

  • Control group: comparison without the intervention (placebo or standard of care).
  • Blinding: single or double; reduces information, observer and placebo effects.
  • Intention-to-treat: analyse subjects in their originally assigned group regardless of protocol completion, preserving the benefits of randomisation.

Statistical Inference

Hypothesis testing, confidence intervals, and the two ways a test can be wrong. The traps here are conceptual, not arithmetic.

Hypothesis testing ref

  • Null hypothesis (H0): no difference or association (for example, relative risk = 1).
  • Alternative hypothesis (H1): a difference or association exists.
  • p-value: the probability of the observed data, or something more extreme, if the null hypothesis were true, compared to a preset alpha (commonly 0.05).
High-yield trap

A p-value is not the probability that the null hypothesis is true, and not the probability the result occurred by chance. It only says the data are unlikely under the null.

Statistical vs clinical significance

With a large enough sample, a trivially small, clinically meaningless difference can reach statistical significance. Always judge effect size, not just whether p is below 0.05.

Confidence intervals ref

A 95% confidence interval is the range that would contain the true value 95% of the time on repeated sampling. It conveys both direction and magnitude, which a bare p-value does not.

  • For a ratio measure (relative risk, odds ratio) the null value is 1. An interval that includes 1 is not significant.
  • For a difference measure (mean or risk difference) the null value is 0. An interval that includes 0 is not significant.
  • Interval width is precision; larger samples and lower variability narrow it.

Error types and power ref

The two ways an inference can be wrong. Original construction.
  • Type I (alpha): a false positive; concluding an effect exists when it does not.
  • Type II (beta): a false negative; missing a real effect.
  • Power = 1 minus beta; conventionally 0.80 or higher. It increases with larger sample size, larger true effect, higher alpha, and lower variability. Sample size is the main lever.

Mnemonic: Type I is the false alarm (cried wolf); Type II is the miss (missed the wolf).

Bias and Validity

Bias is systematic error built into the study; it does not shrink with a larger sample. Random error does. Keeping that line clear answers a surprising share of items.

MisclassificationA form of information bias. Non-differential (errors equal across groups) usually biases toward the null; differential (errors differ by group) can bias either way

Exam trap

Random error reflects chance, shrinks with larger samples, and shows up as wider confidence intervals. Bias is systematic and is not fixed by a larger sample.

Confounding and effect modification ref

A confounder must meet all three criteria:

  • associated with the exposure,
  • an independent risk factor for the outcome, and
  • not on the causal pathway between exposure and outcome.

Control by design: randomisation (balances unknown confounders too), restriction, matching. Control by analysis: stratification (Mantel-Haenszel) and multivariable regression.

Clarification

Effect modification is different from confounding. Here the true effect genuinely differs across levels of a third variable (for example, a handling intervention helps adults but not puppies). It is a real finding to report by stratum, not a nuisance to adjust away.

Validity, reliability and sampling ref

  • Internal validity: the study correctly measures what it set out to, free of bias and confounding.
  • External validity (generalisability): results apply beyond the sample. A tightly controlled study can have high internal but low external validity.
  • Reliability (precision) is consistency on repetition; validity (accuracy) is closeness to truth. A measure can be reliable but not valid. Observer agreement is quantified with the kappa statistic, which corrects for chance agreement.

Probability sampling (supports inference): simple random, systematic, stratified, cluster. Non-probability sampling (weaker): convenience, purposive. Convenience samples are a common source of selection bias and a frequent distractor for "what weakens generalisability".

Tests and Measures

Diagnostic test characteristics with worked numbers, the measure each design produces, choosing a statistical test, and the line between association and causation.

Diagnostic test evaluation ref

A diagnostic accuracy study compares a test against a reference standard in a 2x2 table.

The diagnostic 2x2. Sensitivity and specificity read down the columns; predictive values read across the rows. Original construction.
  • Sensitivity = TP / (TP + FN): of truly diseased animals, the proportion the test flags. A property of the test, prevalence-independent.
  • Specificity = TN / (TN + FP): of truly healthy animals, the proportion cleared. Also prevalence-independent.
  • PPV = TP / (TP + FP) and NPV = TN / (TN + FN): clinically useful, but they depend on prevalence.

Worked example

100 truly infected cats: the test flags 90. 100 truly uninfected cats: the test flags 5. Then Sensitivity = 90/100 = 90%, Specificity = 95/100 = 95%. If only 20 of 1000 screened cats are truly infected (2% prevalence): true positives = 18, false positives = 49, so PPV = 18/(18+49) = 27%, despite a sensitive and specific test.

High-yield trap

As prevalence falls, PPV falls (more positives are false positives) even though sensitivity and specificity are unchanged. This is why low-prevalence intake screening (for example, FeLV) needs confirmatory testing. Mnemonics: SnNout (sensitive, Negative rules out), SpPin (specific, Positive rules in).

Measures of association by design ref note

Full calculations live in your Data and Analysis material; here is only the design-to-measure mapping.

  • Relative risk: ratio of incidence in exposed to unexposed; from cohort and experimental designs. Null = 1.
  • Odds ratio: from case-control designs; approximates relative risk when disease is rare. Null = 1.
  • Prevalence ratio: from cross-sectional designs.
  • Attributable risk (risk difference): absolute excess risk in the exposed; from cohort and experimental designs.

Association vs causation ref

The Bradford Hill considerations help judge causality: temporality, strength, dose-response, consistency, plausibility, coherence, experimental evidence, specificity, analogy.

Exam trap

Temporality (cause precedes effect) is the one essential criterion. The rest are supporting considerations, not a checklist that all must be met. Association is never proof of causation.
