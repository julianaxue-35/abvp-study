The 54% half of the domain, built as methodology: how to measure disease frequency, quantify associations, track mortality, and run population surveillance. Operational shelter metrics live in Data and Analysis; diagnostic test characteristics live in the Study Design hub.

Measures of Disease Frequency

Two families of measure: incidence counts new disease over time (risk), prevalence counts existing disease at a moment (burden). Mixing them up is the most common error in this subdomain.

Incidence ref

  • Cumulative incidence (incidence risk): the proportion of an at-risk group that develops new disease over a defined period. A dimensionless proportion, so the time period must always be stated. CI = new cases / population at risk.
  • Incidence rate (incidence density): new cases divided by total animal-time at risk, giving cases per unit time (for example, cases per 100 dog-days). It accommodates animals entering and leaving at different times.
  • Attack rate: cumulative incidence measured over the limited period of an outbreak.
Shelter note

Shelter populations are unstable, with widely varying lengths of stay, so the animal-time denominator of the incidence rate is the more rigorous choice. Cumulative incidence still tracks trends closely and is simpler for routine monitoring.

The at-risk denominator

Only animals capable of developing new disease belong in the denominator. Exclude animals already infected at the start, animals arriving already infected, animals that recovered before the interval, and animals removed so quickly they had no exposure opportunity.

Clarification

Excluding the recovered cats assumes they are immune. For feline URI (herpesvirus and calicivirus) recovered cats can be reinfected or relapse, so they are arguably still at risk. The exclusion is a valid simplification only if recurrent cases are tracked as a separate metric.

Prevalence ref

  • Point prevalence: the proportion with disease at a single instant (for example, FeLV positivity on intake day).
  • Period prevalence: the proportion with disease at any time during an interval.

Prevalence reflects existing burden, which is what drives confirmatory-test interpretation, pharmacy budgeting, and housing planning.

Linking incidence and prevalence ref

Incidence fills the pool, recovery and death drain it. Original construction.

When prevalence is low and stable, Prevalence ≈ Incidence × average Duration. A chronic, long-duration disease therefore shows a high prevalence relative to its incidence; a rapidly fatal or quickly resolving disease shows a low one.

Measures of Association and Impact

Association measures ask how strongly an exposure and an outcome travel together. The design dictates which measure you can compute, and ratio measures share a null value of 1.

The measures ref

Relative risk (risk ratio)Incidence in exposed / incidence in unexposedCohort, experimental1

Above 1 means increased risk, below 1 means protection, for ratio measures. The attributable risk is an absolute measure of the excess risk carried by the exposure, useful for judging public-health impact rather than just strength of association.

Exam trap

The odds ratio approximates the relative risk only when the disease is rare. With a common outcome the odds ratio overstates the relative risk. Case-control studies cannot yield a true relative risk because they sample on outcome, not on the population at risk.

Mortality and Morbidity

Three death metrics that look similar and are routinely confused. The entire distinction is the denominator.

Death metrics ref

MetricNumeratorDenominatorTells you
Crude (all-cause) mortality rateDeaths from any causeTotal population at risk of dyingOverall death burden
Cause-specific mortality rateDeaths from one diseaseTotal population at risk of dyingPopulation threat of one disease
Case fatality rateDeaths from one diseaseConfirmed cases of that diseaseVirulence or severity
High-yield trap

Cause-specific mortality and case fatality rate share a numerator but differ in denominator. Cause-specific mortality uses the whole population; case fatality rate uses only confirmed cases. A disease can have a high case fatality rate (very lethal once caught) yet a low cause-specific mortality (few animals catch it).

Worked example

Ten dogs are confirmed with parvovirus in an outbreak and four die. Case fatality rate = 4 / 10 = 40%. If those 10 cases occurred among 200 dogs at risk, the cause-specific mortality = 4 / 200 = 2%.

Surveillance

Surveillance is the ongoing systematic collection and interpretation of health data so problems are caught early and interventions can be judged. It is the population equivalent of an examination.

Types of surveillance ref

  • Passive: the system waits for cases or reports to come to it. Cheap but under-detects.
  • Active: the team deliberately seeks out cases (for example, routine intake testing or daily walk-throughs). More complete, more resource-intensive.
  • Syndromic: tracks clinical syndromes (for example, coughing dogs) rather than confirmed diagnoses, for earlier warning.
  • Sentinel: monitors a defined subset as an early indicator for the wider population.

The sensitivity of a surveillance system is the proportion of true cases it actually detects. A passive system usually has lower sensitivity than an active one.

Endemic, epidemic, and the epidemic curve ref

Endemic is the usual, expected baseline level of a disease in a population. An epidemic (outbreak) is occurrence clearly in excess of that expected level. Plotting cases by time of onset produces an epidemic curve, whose shape suggests the mode of spread.

Curve shape points to mechanism: one peak for a shared source, repeating waves for animal-to-animal spread. Original construction.

During an investigation a clear case definition lets cases be counted consistently by time, place and animal, and attack rates compared across subgroups point to the highest-risk group and the likely source.
