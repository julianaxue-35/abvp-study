Welfare Frameworks

Animal welfare science has moved from a reactive "freedom from harm" model to a proactive positive welfare framework. Understanding these frameworks — and how they apply to shelter settings — is a foundation for policy and ethical reasoning on the exam.

Five Freedoms Brambell Commission 1965 / FAWC

Developed by the UK Brambell Committee (1965) and formalized by the Farm Animal Welfare Council (FAWC). Originally designed for farm animals; now universally applied. Framed negatively — as freedoms from adverse states.

#FreedomPractical provision
1Freedom from Hunger and ThirstReady access to fresh water and a diet to maintain full health and vigour
2Freedom from DiscomfortAppropriate environment including shelter and comfortable resting area
3Freedom from Pain, Injury or DiseasePrevention or rapid diagnosis and treatment
4Freedom to Express Normal BehaviourSufficient space, proper facilities, and company of the animal's own kind
5Freedom from Fear and DistressConditions and treatment that avoid mental suffering
Exam trap — Five Freedoms are aspirational, not absoluteThe Five Freedoms represent an aspirational standard. In shelter settings with resource constraints, all five cannot always be fully met simultaneously. The framework's value is as a structured lens for identifying welfare deficits — not as a pass/fail checklist. Attempts to achieve one freedom may compromise another (e.g., group housing for socialization may increase disease risk).

Five Domains Model Mellor 1994 / updated 2017

Developed by David Mellor (New Zealand). Expands the Five Freedoms into a more nuanced, integrative model that explicitly includes positive welfare states (not just freedom from negatives). Organized into four physical/functional domains feeding into a fifth mental domain.

DomainFocusPositive welfare goal
1 — NutritionAdequacy of feed and waterAnimal is well-nourished and hydrated
2 — Physical environmentThermal comfort, substrate, spaceAnimal is comfortable and mobile
3 — HealthDisease, injury, physiological functionAnimal is in good health and free from pain
4 — Behavioural interactionsIntra/inter-species, human–animal, environmentAnimal can express motivated behaviours without frustration
5 — Mental stateAffective experiences emerging from domains 1–4Animal experiences predominantly positive affective states (curiosity, pleasure, contentment, vitality)
High-yield — Domain 5 is the key advance over Five FreedomsThe Five Domains model explicitly evaluates the animal's subjective experience (mental state), not just its physical status. A shelter animal may meet all Five Freedoms criteria (no hunger, no disease) but still suffer psychologically due to chronic stress, understimulation, or social deprivation. Domain 5 captures this gap.

Welfare Assessment: Inputs vs Outcomes ASV / Fraser 2008

Welfare can be assessed by measuring either what is provided to the animal (inputs) or what the animal actually experiences (outcomes). Both are complementary and are used in shelter auditing and accreditation.

TypeWhat is measuredExamples
Input-based (resource-based)Resources and husbandry providedSpace per animal (m²), diet composition, number of staff, enrichment items provided, veterinary visit frequency
Outcome-basedAnimal's actual welfare stateBody condition score, disease prevalence, stereotypy rate, positive affective indicators (play, exploration), stress hormone levels, Fear-Free assessment scores
Why both matterInput measures are easy to audit and regulate but do not guarantee good welfare (an animal may have adequate space but be chronically stressed). Outcome measures directly reflect the animal's experience but are harder to standardize. A complete welfare audit uses both.

Key outcome indicators used in shelters

  • Behavioural indicators of positive welfare: exploration, play, relaxed body posture, voluntary social approach to humans, grooming behaviour
  • Behavioural indicators of negative welfare: stereotypies (repetitive pacing, bar-chewing), hiding, hypervigilance, freezing, aggression, reduced food intake
  • Physiological indicators: cortisol/corticosterone levels, heart rate variability, immune suppression markers
  • Clinical indicators: upper respiratory infection rate, diarrhoea prevalence, wound healing, body condition score progression

Resource Allocation in Shelter Ethics

Shelters operate under resource constraints that require difficult triage and allocation decisions. Three ethical pillars, developed in the shelter medicine literature, provide a principled framework for these decisions. These are heavily tested on the ABVP exam.

The Three Ethical Pillars Newbury et al. / ASV Guidelines

#PillarCore principle
1No animal made worseShelter admission or management decisions must not result in a worse welfare outcome for any individual animal than if the animal had not entered the shelter system. Overcrowding, housing animals beyond capacity, or failing to meet basic care needs violates this pillar.
2No life worse than deathEuthanasia is preferable to maintaining an animal in a state of suffering that cannot be relieved. When an animal's quality of life falls below a reasonable threshold and cannot be improved with available resources, humane euthanasia is the ethical choice. Conversely, euthanasia of an animal with good prospects — including feral cats with the ability to live independently — is not justified under this pillar.
3Equitable resource distributionResources (staff time, medical care, kennel space, enrichment) must be distributed equitably across all animals in the shelter's care. Animals whose needs exceed available resources must not languish — either additional resources are secured or the population is managed through transfers, returns-to-field, or euthanasia decisions that restore equitable care for remaining animals.
Exam trap — overcrowding dilemmaA shelter stops euthanasia to reduce killing, but responds to the resulting overcrowding by housing 3 dogs in a run designed for 1. This directly violates Pillar 1: the decision to stop euthanasia has made the animals in care worse off. Stopping euthanasia is not an ethical choice if it results in overcrowding that compromises basic welfare. The ethical path requires either securing additional resources, increasing transfers, or making euthanasia decisions that allow adequate care for remaining animals.
Exam trap — euthanasia of feral/independent catsEuthanasia of a feral or outdoor cat that has a good chance of living independently outside the shelter is NOT justified under Pillar 2 ("no life worse than death"). An outdoor cat that can survive and thrive independently is not experiencing a life worse than death; therefore, euthanasia is not ethically compelled. Return-to-field or TNR are appropriate alternatives. This is a classic exam scenario.

Short-Term Welfare Compromises ASV / Shelter Medicine textbook

  • Short-term welfare compromises are acceptable only in the context of temporary holding or rehabilitation, where a defined endpoint exists and the compromise is necessary to achieve a better long-term outcome (e.g., brief quarantine isolation).
  • They are not ethically justifiable as an ongoing steady state. A shelter that routinely houses animals in conditions below the threshold of acceptable welfare, with no plan to restore adequate care, is operating unethically regardless of intent.
  • The time-limited exception: a brief period of suboptimal housing (e.g., 48 hours in a small crate during a disease outbreak while the main ward is disinfected) is acceptable IF it is genuinely time-limited, actively managed, and the animal's welfare is monitored throughout.
High-yield — "temporary" must be definedThe acceptability of a short-term compromise depends on having a clear timeline and a plan for restoration. "We'll fix it eventually" is not sufficient. The exam tests whether students understand that indefinite suboptimal conditions are NOT justified by resource constraints alone — they require an active management response.

Euthanasia Decision-Making Framework ASV Guidelines / Reeve et al.

  • Quality of life assessment: the primary basis for euthanasia decisions is the animal's current and projected welfare, assessed using validated tools (e.g., QoL scales, behavioural assessments). Physical illness alone is not sufficient — treatability and resource availability must also be considered.
  • Treatable vs manageable vs untreatable: most shelter protocols classify animals as: (T) treatable within available resources; (M) manageable with ongoing care; or (U) untreatable / quality of life unacceptable. Only U-category animals have unambiguous ethical justification for euthanasia on welfare grounds alone.
  • Behavioural euthanasia: may be indicated when an animal poses an unacceptable public safety risk that cannot be mitigated, or when severe behavioural pathology precludes an acceptable quality of life and is not amenable to modification with available resources. This is a legitimate but heavily scrutinized decision requiring documented behavioural assessment by a qualified professional.
  • Population-level vs individual decisions: population-level euthanasia (reducing intake to match capacity) is ethically distinct from individual welfare euthanasia. Population-level decisions must be transparent, consistently applied, and based on written criteria.

Ethical Decision Making

Shelter veterinarians and managers regularly make decisions with significant ethical weight under conditions of uncertainty, time pressure, and resource constraints. Understanding the psychological and structural factors that influence these decisions is both an exam topic and a practical leadership skill.

Moral Distress Jameton 1984 / veterinary adaptation

  • Definition: moral distress occurs when a person knows the ethically correct course of action but is constrained from acting on that knowledge by institutional, resource, or authority barriers. First described by Jameton (1984) in nursing; well-documented in veterinary contexts.
  • Distinction from moral uncertainty: moral uncertainty is not knowing what the right thing to do is; moral distress is knowing but being unable to act. Both are common in shelter work but require different responses.
  • Sources in shelter medicine: being required to euthanize treatable animals due to space constraints; being unable to provide adequate pain management due to budget limitations; witnessing ongoing substandard care without authority to change it; pressure from management or the board to compromise animal welfare for financial or public relations reasons.
  • Consequences: untreated moral distress leads to compassion fatigue, burnout, staff turnover, and ultimately compromised animal care. It is a significant driver of the high turnover in animal shelter work.
  • Mitigation strategies: transparent and consistent decision-making criteria; peer support and ethics consultation mechanisms; leadership that acknowledges and legitimizes staff concerns; structured debriefs after difficult cases.
High-yield — moral distress ≠ burnoutMoral distress is a specific construct: the knowledge-action gap caused by constraints. Burnout is a broader syndrome of exhaustion and cynicism. Moral distress causes burnout but is a distinct phenomenon. The exam may ask candidates to distinguish between them or to identify appropriate institutional responses.

Pragmatic Shelter Decision-Making: 6 Factors ASV / Miller & Zawistowski

Shelter decisions about individual animals and population management are influenced by six pragmatic factors that must be considered alongside ethical principles:

#FactorExample
1Animal's welfare and quality of lifeCurrent pain level, behavioural indicators, prognosis for recovery
2Available resourcesBudget, staff capacity, kennel space, veterinary skill, foster network
3Community contextLocal adoption demand, rescue network capacity, public perception, community demographics
4Legal obligationsStray hold requirements, mandatory holding periods, evidence animal rules, euthanasia statutes
5Mission and organizational valuesOpen-admission vs limited-admission policy; no-kill commitment; humane euthanasia policy
6Stakeholder interestsPrevious owner, adopter, rescue partner, community donor expectations, staff morale
Exam trap — resource constraints do not override welfareFactor 2 (available resources) cannot ethically override Factor 1 (animal welfare) to the point of causing ongoing suffering. Resource constraints justify triage and prioritization, but they do not justify maintaining animals in conditions that are affirmatively harmful. If resources are insufficient to provide minimum acceptable care, population reduction through euthanasia is more ethical than ongoing overcrowding.

Heuristics and Cognitive Bias in Clinical Settings Kahneman / Croskerry / Vet adaptation

Clinical and ethical decisions in shelter medicine are subject to the same cognitive shortcuts and biases that affect all medical decision-making. Recognizing these helps clinicians improve the quality of their reasoning.

Bias / heuristicDescriptionShelter example
Availability heuristicOverestimating the probability of events that come easily to mind (often because they are recent or dramatic)Overweighting the risk of aggression in pit bull–type dogs after a recent incident, leading to unfair euthanasia decisions
AnchoringOver-relying on the first piece of information encountered when making decisionsA dog labelled "aggressive" on intake from animal control; all subsequent behavioural assessment is skewed by that label
Affective biasDecisions driven by emotional response to the animal's appearance or species rather than objective welfare assessmentAllocating disproportionate resources to neonatal kittens (emotionally compelling) relative to adult cats with similar medical need
Confirmation biasSeeking information that confirms a prior belief while discounting disconfirming evidenceA clinician who believes a dog is dangerous interprets neutral behaviours as confirmatory threats
Status quo biasPreference for the existing state of affairs; change is perceived as lossReluctance to implement new euthanasia criteria even when current criteria are inconsistent or harmful
High-yield — mitigation strategiesDe-biasing strategies include: structured decision tools (standardized behavioural assessment forms); checklists; requiring explicit documentation of the reasoning behind unusual decisions; seeking a second opinion for high-stakes decisions; and cultural norms that reward deliberate reasoning over rapid intuitive judgments.

Shared vs Informed Decision Making AVMA / veterinary ethics literature

ModelCore principleContext
Informed decision makingThe veterinarian provides complete and accurate information; the client (or in shelter contexts, the organization) makes the final decision based on that informationTraditional model; emphasizes autonomy of the decision-maker; appropriate when the client has the capacity and context to make an informed choice
Shared decision makingThe veterinarian and client (or stakeholder) deliberate together, integrating clinical expertise with the client's values and circumstances, to reach a mutually acceptable decisionMore appropriate for complex, value-laden decisions with multiple acceptable options; increasingly favoured in human medicine and recommended in veterinary practice guidelines
  • In shelter contexts, shared decision making involves the shelter vet, shelter director, and sometimes community stakeholders (e.g., rescue partners, board members) in decisions with significant welfare, legal, or reputational implications.
  • The veterinarian's role in shared decision making is not to advocate for a predetermined outcome but to ensure that the decision is grounded in accurate welfare assessment and realistic outcome data.
  • Key AVMA principle: the veterinarian has an ethical obligation to provide a recommendation and to advocate for the animal's welfare, even in a shared model. Neutrality is not an ethical option when the animal's interests are at stake.

Q1frameworks

Which of the following best describes the key advance of the Five Domains Model over the original Five Freedoms?

B is correct. The Five Domains Model (Mellor) introduced Domain 5 — the animal's mental state — as an integrative outcome emerging from the four physical/functional domains. This allows assessment of positive welfare states (curiosity, play, contentment) not merely the absence of suffering. The Five Freedoms, particularly Freedom 5 (freedom from fear and distress), are reactive and deficit-focused. The Five Domains model is bidirectional: it seeks to maximise positive experiences, not just eliminate negative ones.

A shelter cat lives in a clean, adequately sized cage with food, water, and daily health checks. She does not display any disease signs and meets all Five Freedoms criteria. However, she spends most of the day hiding, rarely explores, and has not played since admission 3 weeks ago. Which assessment framework is MOST useful for identifying this welfare deficit?

C is correct. Although this cat meets the Five Freedoms (no disease, adequate space, food, water, no obvious fear trigger), she is experiencing a significant welfare deficit characterised by persistent negative affective states (hiding, inactivity, loss of play behaviour). The Five Domains Model, particularly Domain 5 (mental state), is specifically designed to capture this kind of subjective experiential welfare deficit and to target it for intervention through enrichment, social housing, or other proactive positive welfare strategies.

Q4resource-allocation

A shelter has reached capacity. The director proposes stopping all euthanasia and instead placing 3 adult dogs per double-size kennel run (designed for 1–2 dogs) to accommodate the overflow. Which ethical pillar does this proposal most clearly violate?

A is correct. Pillar 1 ("no animal made worse") prohibits management decisions that result in a worse welfare outcome for animals in care. Tripling the occupancy of kennels beyond their designed capacity predictably increases disease transmission, social conflict, stress, and injury risk — making the animals' welfare actively worse. The intent to save lives does not ethically justify causing harm to the animals who remain in care. A healthy, well-socialised feral cat with a good chance of surviving independently outdoors does NOT meet this threshold — its life is not worse than death. Euthanising it solely because it cannot be adopted as an indoor pet violates Pillar 2. The ethically appropriate path is return-to-field or referral to a TNR programme. Q7decision-making

Which of the following BEST defines moral distress as distinguished from moral uncertainty?

C is correct. Jameton (1984) defined moral distress as the specific experience of knowing the right thing to do but being prevented from doing it by external constraints. This is distinct from moral uncertainty (not knowing what is right) and from burnout (a broader syndrome of exhaustion and disengagement). In shelters, moral distress is commonly triggered by being required to euthanize treatable animals due to space limitations, or witnessing substandard care without authority to change it.
Q8decision-making

A shelter veterinarian notices that the staff allocates disproportionately high resources to neonatal kittens while adult cats with comparable medical need receive delayed care. Which cognitive bias BEST describes this pattern?

C is correct. Affective bias (sometimes called the "affect heuristic") refers to decisions driven by emotional response to an animal's appearance, age, or species rather than objective welfare assessment. Neonatal kittens are emotionally compelling — small, vulnerable, visually appealing — and frequently attract resource allocation that is disproportionate to clinical need relative to adult cats. Anchoring would describe over-relying on initial information; availability heuristic would describe overestimating risk based on memorable events; confirmation bias would describe selectively interpreting evidence to confirm a prior belief.
Q9decision-making

A dog is brought to a shelter with an intake note from animal control stating "aggressive — bit officer." All subsequent behavioural assessments by shelter staff find the dog calm and friendly. Staff continue to describe the dog as "dangerous" and recommend euthanasia. Which cognitive bias is MOST prominently demonstrated?

B is correct. Anchoring bias is demonstrated when individuals over-rely on the first piece of information encountered — in this case, the intake note labelling the dog "aggressive." Subsequent assessments that disconfirm this label are discounted rather than integrated. A related phenomenon is labelling bias: once a label is applied, it shapes all subsequent perception of the animal. Structured behavioural assessment protocols with documented criteria help counteract anchoring by requiring evidence-based evaluation independent of intake labels.

Q13frameworks

The Five Freedoms were originally developed by which body, and for which animal population?

C is correct. The Five Freedoms originated with the UK Brambell Committee report (1965) and were formalised by the Farm Animal Welfare Council (FAWC) for intensively farmed animals. Q14frameworks

Which Five Freedom maps MOST directly to providing hiding boxes for cats in shelter housing?

D is correct. Freedom 5 (Freedom from Fear and Distress) requires conditions and treatment that avoid mental suffering. Providing hiding boxes directly addresses the cat's ability to escape perceived threats and control its exposure to stressors — a core driver of fear and distress in a shelter environment. While hiding could also be linked to Freedom 4 (expressing normal behaviour, since hiding is a normal feline behaviour), the primary welfare rationale for hiding boxes is reduction of fear and distress, making D the best answer. Q15frameworks

The Five Opportunities framework (Yeates 2013) is described as the "positive counterpart" to the Five Freedoms. Which pairing CORRECTLY matches a Five Freedom to its corresponding Five Opportunities equivalent?

C is correct. Each Five Freedom maps to a positive opportunity: Freedom 5 (from fear/distress) → Opportunity for interest and confidence (mental enjoyment). Freedom from Hunger maps to Opportunity for selection of dietary inputs; Freedom from Discomfort maps to Opportunity for control of the environment; Freedom to Express Normal Behaviour maps to Opportunity to express normal behaviour (particularly group housing for social species); and Freedom from Pain/Injury/Disease maps to Opportunity for pleasure, development and vitality. Q16frameworks

When assessing welfare using the Five Domains Model, a shelter auditor evaluates how often an animal experiences a negative affective state, how intense the state is, and how long it persists. These three dimensions belong to which aspect of welfare scoring?

B is correct. Welfare scoring under the Five Domains Model uses multiple dimensions to characterize the welfare significance of any affective state: intensity (how severe the experience is for each animal), duration (how long the mental state lasts), frequency or probability (how often it occurs), and scope (how many animals are affected). Together these dimensions allow a nuanced welfare impact assessment — an intense but brief experience (e.g., a 30-second blood draw) may have less welfare impact than a low-intensity but chronic state (e.g., persistent mild social isolation). Options A, C, and D describe different assessment tools or domains but not this specific scoring framework.
Q17frameworks

A shelter installs exercise wheels for cats and observes a subsequent decrease in repetitive pacing behaviour. In the welfare assessment input-outcome framework, which pairing does this scenario BEST illustrate?

C is correct. The welfare assessment framework pairs specific inputs with their expected outcome indicators. Toy and exercise enrichment is the relevant input; the target outcome is the presence or absence of stereotypic and repetitive behaviours (e.g., pacing, bar-chewing, over-grooming). A reduction in stereotypies following enrichment provision is a validated positive welfare outcome. Diet → BCS (A) is a separate pairing; human company → caregiver approach/avoidance (B) is another; and routine vet care → infectious disease signs (D) is a third. Understanding these input-outcome pairs is critical for welfare auditing and enrichment programme evaluation.

A shelter implements a new socialisation programme: volunteers spend 20 minutes per day with each cat. Which of the following outcomes would be the MOST valid welfare indicator to track for this input?

C is correct. In the input-outcome welfare assessment framework, human company (the input in this scenario) maps to outcomes reflecting the animal's behavioural response to humans: voluntary approach, avoidance of caregivers, and aggression. Approach toward caregivers and reduced hiding indicate improved human-animal relationship and reduced fear — the expected positive outcome of socialisation. Q24resource-allocation

A shelter director argues: "We cannot harm one animal in order to benefit another." This statement reflects which ethical pillar?

A is correct. Pillar 1 contains an important sub-principle: the prohibition extends not only to animals being made worse by the charity's management, but also to harming one animal in order to benefit another. For example, denying an animal pain medication so that those resources can be redirected to a "more adoptable" animal violates Pillar 1. This is a subtle but high-yield distinction — Pillar 1 is not merely about overcrowding or neglect, but about any management decision that actively worsens an individual animal's welfare, including cross-animal resource substitution that causes harm.
Q25decision-making

Which of the following BEST distinguishes shared decision making from informed decision making in a veterinary shelter context?

B is correct. These are distinct models: informed decision making (sometimes called the "informed consent model") places the decision-making authority with the client or organisation — the veterinarian's role is to provide complete, accurate information, after which the decision-maker acts autonomously. Shared decision making involves both parties actively deliberating, exchanging information, and reaching a mutually agreed outcome. In practice, shelter medicine increasingly favours shared decision making for complex, value-laden decisions (euthanasia, population management) while informed decision making is appropriate for lower-stakes clinical choices. Option A has the definitions reversed — a common exam error.

Unlike human medical ethics, veterinary ethical decisions are characterised by the absence of a solid conceptual consensus. Which of the following strategies is MOST directly recommended to address this challenge in shelter organisations?

C is correct. Because veterinary ethics lacks the conceptual consensus that grounds much of human medical ethics, the recommended approach is structural and cultural rather than doctrinal: (1) cultivate a culture of respect for diverse ethical positions, (2) create dedicated time for ethical discussion (not just ad hoc), and (3) publish transparent organisational statements outlining the principles used in decision-making. Option A (enforcing a single ethical theory) creates rigidity and suppresses legitimate dissent. Option B (deferring to one person) removes the deliberative process that improves decision quality. Q27decision-making

After a highly publicised bite incident involving a pit bull–type dog at a neighbouring shelter, a shelter veterinarian begins recommending euthanasia for all pit bull–type dogs with any bite history, even when behavioural assessments indicate low risk. Which cognitive bias is MOST likely driving this change?

D is correct. The availability heuristic causes people to overestimate the probability of events that are cognitively salient — typically because they are recent, dramatic, or emotionally charged. A high-profile bite incident at a nearby facility makes the risk of aggression highly available in memory, leading the veterinarian to overweight this risk when evaluating subsequent dogs. This is distinct from anchoring (over-relying on first information about a specific dog), affective bias (emotional response to appearance), and confirmation bias (selectively interpreting evidence). Structured, validated behavioural assessment tools applied consistently are the recommended countermeasure against availability-driven decisions.
Q28decision-making

A shelter has used the same intake behavioural assessment tool for 12 years. Literature now shows the tool over-predicts aggression in fearful dogs, leading to unnecessary euthanasia. Staff resist switching to a validated alternative, citing familiarity and past success. Which cognitive bias MOST characterises this resistance?

C is correct. Status quo bias is the preference for the existing state of affairs, where change is perceived as a loss even when evidence supports it. In this scenario, staff prefer a familiar tool despite evidence of harm (over-prediction of aggression → unnecessary euthanasia) because switching requires effort, creates uncertainty, and disrupts established workflows. Status quo bias is particularly relevant in organisations where current practices have historical momentum. Availability heuristic (A) would involve overestimating risk from memorable events. Affective bias (B) involves emotional responses to animal appearance. Anchoring (D) involves over-relying on initial information about a specific animal, not resistance to process change.
Q29decision-making

Moral distress (Jameton 1984) is characterised by repeated exposure to situations where a clinician knows the right action but is prevented from acting. Which of the following describes the MOST likely organisational consequence of chronic, unaddressed moral distress among shelter staff?

C is correct. Repeated, unaddressed moral distress does not produce adaptation or resilience — it produces erosion of emotional well-being. This leads sequentially to compassion fatigue (the cost of sustained empathic engagement), burnout (exhaustion and disengagement), and staff turnover. High turnover in shelter medicine is well-documented and is substantially driven by moral distress. The downstream consequence is compromised animal care: experienced staff leave, institutional knowledge is lost, and the animals lose consistent, skilled caregivers. Option A and D incorrectly imply that exposure to distress is beneficial. Option B is not supported — moral distress does not systematically reduce euthanasia rates.
Q30decision-making

A shelter veterinarian believes a particular cat is feral based on the intake note, and interprets all of the cat's behaviours during assessment — freezing, dilated pupils, rapid breathing — as confirming this. The cat actually responds warmly to slow, quiet approach but this is not tested because the veterinarian assumes the result. Which bias is MOST prominent?

D is correct. Confirmation bias involves seeking or interpreting evidence in a way that confirms a pre-existing belief, while discounting or failing to seek disconfirming evidence. In this scenario, the veterinarian interprets stress responses (freezing, dilated pupils, rapid breathing — all of which can be caused by fear in a socialised cat, not only in a feral one) as confirming ferality, and does not test the alternative hypothesis (slow, quiet approach). This is distinct from anchoring bias, which would be over-relying on the initial "feral" label but still remaining open to updating — confirmation bias goes further by actively shaping information-gathering to avoid disconfirmation. Standardised behavioural assessment protocols that require testing specific hypotheses are the recommended structural countermeasure.
