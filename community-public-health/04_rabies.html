Overview & Virology

Rabies is a fatal zoonotic encephalitis caused by neurotropic lyssaviruses. It is the single highest-priority rabies topic on the ABVP Community & Public Health domain because of its public health, legal, and shelter-operational implications.

Blueprint placement

  • Community & Public Health = 11% of the exam (~38–39 of 350 questions). Five subdomains: zoonoses (general), rabies, vector-borne disease, shelter medicine public health, and community cats & TNR.
  • Rabies is consistently identified in ABVP prep materials as among the highest-yield topics within this domain due to its legal, clinical, and One Health dimensions.
  • Expect scenario-based questions on bite-quarantine durations, PEP decision-making, euthanasia-for-testing indications, and staff exposure management.

Taxonomy & classification CDC / ICTV

  • Family Rhabdoviridae, genus Lyssavirus. The rabies virus (RABV) is genotype 1, the predominant species worldwide and the only lyssavirus circulating in the Americas.
  • Bullet-shaped, enveloped, single-stranded, negative-sense RNA virus (~180 × 75 nm). The lipid envelope makes it easily inactivated by soap and water, household bleach (1:32 dilution), alcohols, and most standard disinfectants — a critical contrast with non-enveloped shelter pathogens such as CPV and FPV.
  • Five viral proteins: nucleoprotein (N), phosphoprotein (P), matrix protein (M), glycoprotein (G), and large protein (L/RNA-dependent RNA polymerase). The G protein mediates cell entry and is the primary target for virus-neutralizing antibodies (VNAs); it is the antigen in all licensed vaccines.
  • RABV is phylogenetically divided into variants associated with specific reservoir host species (e.g., raccoon variant, skunk variant, bat variants). Variant identity matters for epidemiology but not for vaccination or PEP choice, because cross-protection is complete among RABV variants.
Exam trapBecause rabies virus is enveloped it is readily destroyed by soap and water — yet students sometimes assume all neurotropic viruses are environmentally hardy. The environmental hardiness of CPV (non-enveloped) does not apply to rabies.

Epidemiology in the United States CDC

  • Domestic reservoirs have been eliminated in dogs in the US. The major wildlife reservoirs are raccoons (eastern US), skunks (central and California), foxes (Texas/Alaska), and bats (all 49 continental states plus Hawaii — bat RABV variants account for the majority of human rabies deaths in the US in the modern era).
  • Cats are now the most commonly reported rabid domestic animal in the US (surpassing dogs) — largely because cat vaccination and stray-cat management lag behind dog programs. This is a recurrent exam fact.
  • Approximately 1–3 human rabies deaths per year in the US; globally ~59,000 deaths/year, >95% from dog bites in Africa and Asia.
  • Post-exposure prophylaxis (PEP) is administered to ~55,000 Americans annually; cost ~$3,000–$7,000 per course.
  • Bat bites may be unrecognized (small punctures, nocturnal exposure, persons who cannot provide history — sleeping individuals, young children, intoxicated persons). A bat found in a room with a sleeping person or a child is a PEP indication in most jurisdictions.
Exam trap — cats, not dogs, are #1 domestic rabid animal in the USMany students assume dogs are the most commonly reported rabid domestic animal. In the US, cats hold that distinction. Dogs remain #1 globally.

Environmental stability CDC / NASPHV

  • RABV survives in saliva and neural tissue for a short time under cool, moist conditions but is rapidly destroyed by drying, UV light, heat, and common disinfectants.
  • The virus does not survive on environmental surfaces long enough to pose an indirect contact (fomite) transmission risk under normal conditions. Transmission requires fresh salivary inoculation into a wound or mucous membrane.
  • This means fomite disinfection protocols for rabies focus on preventing contact with fresh saliva (wound care, PPE) rather than extended environmental decontamination as required for CPV.

Pathophysiology & Transmission

Understanding the centripetal-then-centrifugal spread of RABV in neural tissue explains both the variable incubation period and why the salivary glands test positive before death.

Transmission routes CDC / NASPHV

  • Bite wounds: the overwhelmingly dominant route. Virus in saliva is inoculated directly into muscle or subcutaneous tissue.
  • Saliva into mucous membranes or open wounds: scratches that break skin, saliva contact with eyes/mouth. Less efficient but epidemiologically relevant in shelter staff injuries.
  • Aerosol: documented only in bat-cave spelunkers (extremely high viral load) and laboratory accidents. Not a shelter risk under normal circumstances.
  • Transplant: organ/corneal transplant from undiagnosed rabid donors — rare, documented cases.
  • Intact skin, handling an animal, or contact with blood, urine, or feces does not constitute an exposure requiring PEP. This distinction is tested repeatedly.
Exam trap — what is NOT an exposureContact with blood, urine, feces, or intact skin from a rabid or suspected-rabid animal is not a rabies exposure and does not require PEP. Only saliva (or neural tissue/CSF/eye fluid) into a wound or mucous membrane constitutes an exposure.

Pathogenesis notes / textbook

  • Step 1 — Local replication: after inoculation, RABV replicates in local myocytes and/or enters peripheral nerve endings directly. Initial replication in muscle is low-level and may last days to weeks.
  • Step 2 — Centripetal spread: virus travels retrograde via axoplasmic transport along peripheral nerves toward the spinal cord and brainstem. Speed ~50–100 mm/day. This is why bites to the face, neck, or highly innervated areas have shorter incubation periods than bites to distal limbs.
  • Step 3 — CNS amplification: once in the spinal cord, rapid ascent to the brain with infection of limbic system, brainstem, cerebellum, and cortex. Neuronal dysfunction produces the clinical signs. Gross and histologic lesions are often surprisingly mild relative to the severity of signs (diffuse encephalitis without massive necrosis).
  • Step 4 — Centrifugal spread: virus spreads outward from the CNS along efferent nerves to highly innervated peripheral organs, including the salivary glands, cornea, skin hair follicles, and adrenal glands. Salivary gland infection produces infectious saliva. This centrifugal phase explains why the DFA test can be positive on skin biopsies (nuchal hair follicles) ante-mortem.
Key exam point — incubation periodThe incubation period is typically 3–8 weeks in dogs and cats but ranges from 2 weeks to 6 months (rarely up to 1 year). The 10-day quarantine period for dogs, cats, and ferrets is based on the well-established fact that these species shed virus in saliva no more than a few days before the onset of clinical signs and die within days of sign onset — so a healthy animal at 10 days could not have had infectious saliva at the time of the bite.

Neuropathology textbook

  • Negri bodies: eosinophilic intracytoplasmic inclusion bodies found in infected neurons, most reliably in the hippocampus (Ammon's horn), cerebellum (Purkinje cells), and brainstem. Pathognomonic but present in only ~70–80% of confirmed cases; absence does not rule out rabies.
  • The preferred ante-mortem and post-mortem confirmatory test is the direct fluorescent antibody (DFA) test on fresh brain tissue (brainstem, cerebellum, hippocampus). DFA is the gold standard — sensitivity and specificity approach 100% when performed on adequate, unfixed tissue.
  • Histopathology (H&E for Negri bodies) and virus isolation are supplementary; RT-PCR on saliva, CSF, or skin biopsy can provide ante-mortem confirmation in humans.

Clinical Signs & Diagnosis

Rabies produces a progressive, invariably fatal encephalitis. Recognizing the clinical stages and species-specific presentations is essential for triggering appropriate shelter and public health responses.

Clinical stages in dogs and cats textbookNASPHV

Prodromal1–3 daysPersonality change (shy animals become friendly, friendly become aggressive or shy), fever, anorexia, licking/chewing at bite site, mydriasis. Often the only stage visible in shelter animals.
Excitative ("furious")1–7 daysAggression, restlessness, pica, photophobia, hypersalivation, vocalization changes, seizures. Animals may bite without provocation and wander.
Paralytic ("dumb")2–4 daysAscending LMN paralysis, jaw drop, inability to swallow (appears to choke on food or water — the source of "hydrophobia" as a misnomer in veterinary patients), dysphagia, voice change, progressive posterior paresis, recumbency, coma, death.
Exam trap — the "dumb" formAnimals (especially cattle) may present with the paralytic form only, with no aggressive phase. A cow or dog with sudden jaw drop, drooling, inability to eat, and unexplained pharyngeal paralysis should prompt rabies as a differential before the mouth is examined without PPE.
High-yieldNot all rabid animals progress through all three stages or in the listed order. Both forms are fatal; average time from first clinical sign to death is 2–10 days in dogs and cats. An animal that has shown clinical signs for more than 10 days and is still alive is unlikely to be rabid, but the diagnosis cannot be excluded without DFA testing.

Species-specific notes CDC / NASPHV

  • Dogs and cats: subject to the 10-day observation rule (see Shelter Exposure Protocol tab). Both forms occur; cats more often show the furious form.
  • Wildlife (raccoons, skunks, foxes): diurnal activity in a nocturnal species, uncoordinated gait ("drunk" raccoon), loss of fear of humans, self-mutilation. However, a single behavioral aberration is not diagnostic; DFA is required.
  • Bats: a bat found on the ground, unable to fly, or in an unusual location (indoors) should be treated as potentially rabid. Bat rabies strains (e.g., silver-haired bat RABV, big brown bat RABV) account for the majority of US human cases. Bat contact PEP thresholds are lower than for terrestrial mammals.
  • Cattle and large animals: often present with paralytic form, pharyngeal paralysis, bloat, straining — easily mistaken for hardware disease, choke, or dystocia. Rabies is a differential in any large animal with unexplained CNS signs.
  • Small rodents and lagomorphs (mice, rats, squirrels, rabbits): rarely involved in rabies transmission in the US; bites from these animals almost never require PEP without epidemiological evidence of exposure, per CDC. However, state health department consultation is always advised.

Diagnosis CDC

Post-mortem (definitive)

  • Direct fluorescent antibody (DFA): gold standard. Requires fresh, unfixed brain tissue (brainstem, cerebellum, hippocampus). Sensitivity ~98–99% for adequate specimens. Formalin-fixed or frozen/thawed tissue reduces accuracy.
  • Specimens must be refrigerated (not frozen) and submitted to a state public health lab within 24–72 hours. Labs use the DFA and may confirm with virus isolation or RT-PCR.
  • Histopathology for Negri bodies is supplementary — not used for definitive modern diagnosis.

Ante-mortem (humans and occasionally animals)

  • RT-PCR on saliva, nuchal skin biopsy (hair follicles), CSF, or corneal impression smear. Multiple samples over time increase sensitivity.
  • Serology (VNA titres) is unreliable ante-mortem in unvaccinated individuals because antibody develops late and may be absent until the terminal stage.
Exam trap — do not refrigerate the head in dry ice or freeze itFreezing then thawing brain tissue degrades fluorescent antibody staining quality and can give a false-negative DFA. Heads submitted for rabies testing should be refrigerated (not frozen) and shipped on wet ice or refrigerant packs, packed to prevent leakage. Freezing is acceptable only if transit time exceeds 72 hours and the lab is consulted first.

Shelter Exposure Protocol

Shelters must have written rabies exposure response protocols covering animal quarantine, staff exposure management, and public health reporting. The NASPHV Compendium of Animal Rabies Prevention and Control (updated periodically; most recent version should be consulted) is the primary reference document.

The 10-day observation rule NASPHV Compendium

  • Applies to dogs, cats, and ferrets only — the three domestic species for which the pre-clinical shedding window is well established.
  • If a healthy dog, cat, or ferret bites a person, it may be placed under 10-day observation (by a licensed veterinarian or under veterinary supervision) in lieu of immediate euthanasia and testing, provided it remains healthy throughout.
  • Rationale: these species do not shed rabies virus in saliva more than a few days before clinical signs appear, and they die within days of sign onset. A healthy animal at day 10 post-bite had non-infectious saliva at the time of the bite.
  • If the animal becomes ill or dies during the 10-day period, it should be euthanized and submitted for DFA testing immediately. PEP should be initiated for the exposed person while awaiting results if clinical signs are consistent with rabies.
  • Observation vs. quarantine: "observation" means the animal is monitored for clinical signs; "quarantine" implies physical restriction. Most jurisdictions use both terms interchangeably for bite animals but have specific statutory requirements. Shelter staff should be familiar with local ordinances.
Exam trap — 10-day rule does NOT apply to wildlife or most livestockThe 10-day rule is species-specific. A raccoon, bat, skunk, fox, or coyote that bites a person cannot be observed; it should be euthanized and submitted for testing, or PEP initiated. Similarly, the 10-day observation rule is not validated for horses or cattle.

Unvaccinated shelter animal bitten by suspect-rabid wildlife NASPHV Compendium

  • Currently vaccinated animal (vaccine up to date): revaccinate immediately; place under 45-day observation. The 45-day period is not the same as the 10-day bite-quarantine — it is the post-exposure observation for the animal itself.
  • Unvaccinated animal with owner: owner may elect immediate euthanasia (with brain submitted for testing) OR strict 6-month isolation quarantine with vaccination at entry and at 1 month before release. Release requires a negative ante-mortem test is not feasible for animals, so the quarantine period is used to allow the incubation period to pass.
  • Unvaccinated stray / owned animal for which owner refuses quarantine: humane euthanasia and DFA testing per local ordinance.
  • Overdue-for-vaccination animal: managed on a case-by-case basis; treat as unvaccinated in most protocols unless a veterinarian determines the animal is likely protected.
High-yield numbers10 days (bite quarantine for healthy dog/cat/ferret) · 45 days (observation period for currently-vaccinated animal exposed to potentially rabid wildlife) · 6 months (isolation quarantine for unvaccinated exposed animal whose owner elects against euthanasia). These numbers appear on the exam.

Staff exposure management NASPHV / OSHA

  • Any shelter employee bitten or scratched by an animal that cannot be immediately ruled out as rabid should wash the wound thoroughly with soap and water for at least 15 minutes — this step alone reduces transmission risk significantly.
  • Report immediately to a supervisor and seek medical evaluation; the local or state health department must be notified per mandatory reporting laws in all US states.
  • PEP decision is made by the attending physician in consultation with the local health department, based on animal species, vaccination status, availability for testing, and epidemiological risk in the region.
  • Pre-exposure prophylaxis (PrEP): shelter veterinarians, veterinary technicians, wildlife rehabilitators, and animal control officers are classified as Category II (frequent risk) by the CDC and should receive the 3-dose pre-exposure vaccine series (days 0, 7, 21–28) and have titres checked every 2 years (Category II) or more frequently (Category I, e.g., rabies laboratory workers).
Shelter protocol requirementShelters should maintain records of pre-exposure vaccination status for all at-risk staff. If a pre-exposure-vaccinated staff member is bitten, they require only 2 doses of PEP vaccine (days 0 and 3), without rabies immune globulin (RIG). Unvaccinated individuals require the full PEP series with RIG.

Post-Exposure Prophylaxis (PEP) & Vaccines

PEP is essentially 100% effective when administered correctly and promptly after exposure. Delay, incomplete courses, or omission of RIG are the main causes of failure in developed countries.

Human PEP protocol CDC ACIP recommendations

Previously unvaccinated individuals

  • Wound care: immediate, thorough washing with soap and water for 15 minutes (povidone-iodine if available). Do not suture immediately if possible; this reduces virus inoculation depth.
  • Rabies immune globulin (RIG): 20 IU/kg total dose on day 0. As much of the dose as anatomically feasible is infiltrated directly into and around the wound; the remainder is given IM at a distant site. RIG provides immediate passive immunity during the 7–10 days before active vaccine-induced immunity develops. RIG must be given on day 0 only — if not given initially, it can be given up to day 7 but not after day 7 (it blunts the active immune response).
  • Vaccine: 4 doses of cell-culture or purified chick embryo cell vaccine on days 0, 3, 7, and 14. (Immunocompromised individuals receive a 5th dose on day 28.) Route: IM into the deltoid in adults; anterolateral thigh in children. Never inject into the gluteus — poor immune response.

Previously vaccinated individuals (complete pre-exposure or prior PEP)

  • Wound care as above.
  • No RIG.
  • Vaccine: 2 doses on days 0 and 3 only.
Exam trap — RIG injection site and timingRIG must be infiltrated into/around the wound — do NOT give the entire dose IM at a distant site. Giving RIG after day 7 may actually impair vaccine response. RIG and vaccine should not be injected in the same syringe or at the same anatomical site.

Veterinary vaccines NASPHV Compendium / AAHA / AAFP guidelines

  • All licensed veterinary rabies vaccines in the US are killed (inactivated) or recombinant (canarypox-vectored for cats). There are no licensed modified-live rabies vaccines for dogs or cats in the US.
  • Initial vaccination: 1 dose at ≥12 weeks of age (product-label dependent; some labels allow 12 weeks, others 16 weeks). A booster is given 1 year later, then every 1 or 3 years depending on the product label and local ordinance.
  • The 1-year and 3-year products are immunologically equivalent; the difference is the label duration of immunity and the regulatory approval period. Using a 1-year product does not provide less protection per dose — it is a regulatory distinction, not a biological one.
  • Rabies vaccination is legally required for dogs in all US states. Requirements for cats vary by state and jurisdiction; many require cat vaccination but not all.
  • Oral rabies vaccination (ORV): used for wildlife population management (raccoons, coyotes, foxes) via baits containing modified-live or vaccinia-recombinant vaccine dropped from aircraft or distributed by hand. ORV programs have dramatically reduced fox and raccoon rabies in targeted regions and are a key One Health intervention.
Exam trap — 1-year vs. 3-year productA dog vaccinated with a 1-year product is not considered "overdue" if only 11 months have elapsed. But a dog vaccinated with a 1-year product 13 months ago is overdue, and most protocols treat it as unvaccinated for exposure risk purposes. This has significant implications for post-exposure quarantine duration decisions.

Vaccine-associated adverse events AVMA / USDA

  • Injection site sarcomas in cats (feline injection-site sarcomas, FISS): rabies and FeLV vaccines historically associated; current AAFP guidelines recommend rabies vaccination in the right distal hind limb (for limb amputation if sarcoma develops). Adjuvanted vaccines have a higher but still rare association; recombinant (non-adjuvanted) rabies vaccine is preferred for cats partly for this reason.
  • Vaccinial rabies: impossible with killed or recombinant vaccines in dogs and cats. A dog cannot develop rabies from a licensed rabies vaccine in the US.
  • Rare hypersensitivity reactions (anaphylaxis) occur at a low rate; observation for 20–30 minutes post-vaccination in high-throughput clinic settings is reasonable practice.

Public Health & Law

Rabies is a compulsorily notifiable disease in all US states. Shelter personnel operate at the intersection of veterinary medicine, public health, and animal control law — understanding the legal obligations is exam-relevant.

Mandatory reporting CDC / state statutes

  • Suspected or confirmed animal rabies must be reported to the local or state public health department in all jurisdictions. The reporting party is typically the examining or attending veterinarian, the shelter director, or animal control.
  • Human bites or potential human exposures must be reported; the health department then manages PEP coordination and epidemiological investigation.
  • Confirmed rabies cases are reported nationally through the CDC via state health departments and published in the Journal of the American Veterinary Medical Association (JAVMA) annual rabies surveillance report.

Legal framework for shelters NASPHV / state/local ordinance

  • Vaccination at intake: many jurisdictions legally require rabies vaccination of all dogs (and some cats) upon impoundment. Shelters should know the local requirements and have protocols for cases where animals cannot be immediately vaccinated (age, illness).
  • Bite quarantine authority: animal control agencies have statutory authority to impound and quarantine biting animals; shelters operating as contracted animal control facilities must have written protocols consistent with local ordinance.
  • Euthanasia for testing: staff must understand that a head submitted for DFA is euthanized by intracardiac (IC) injection only if the animal is under deep general anesthesia, or by other AVMA-approved methods that avoid brain damage. Gunshot to the head destroys the brainstem and is not acceptable for animals intended for DFA testing unless no alternative exists and the cerebellum and hippocampus are preserved.
  • Liability and documentation: thorough documentation of bite incidents, quarantine decisions, vaccination history, staff exposure events, and all communications with the health department reduces institutional liability and supports epidemiological investigation.
Euthanasia method for brain submissionIV pentobarbital overdose (or another AVMA-approved method that preserves brain architecture) is required when an animal must be euthanized for rabies DFA testing. Warn referring staff: gunshot to the head destroys the brainstem — the region required for the DFA test — and renders the specimen non-diagnostic.

Bite animal quarantine: regulatory framework NASPHV Compendium / state statutes

Bite animal quarantine is a statutory mechanism — not a discretionary clinical recommendation. The regulatory authority to order and enforce quarantine is vested in the state or local public health department and/or animal control agency, not in the individual shelter veterinarian.

Their rabies transmission potential during a non-clinical phase cannot be reliably excluded by observation. If exposure occurred, the correct action is immediate euthanasia and DFA submission — not a 10-day hold.

The 10-day window: why it works for dogs and cats

Dogs and cats can only transmit rabies virus in their saliva for a limited window before clinical signs appear. Research has established that if a dog or cat is alive and clinically normal at 10 days post-bite, it was not shedding virus at the time of the bite. The 10-day quarantine therefore definitively rules out rabies transmission from that animal without requiring euthanasia or DFA testing.

This 10-day window applies specifically to dogs and cats — it is not valid for ferrets (different incubation/shedding kinetics) or for wildlife.

Who has authority to order quarantine

  • The state or local public health department is the primary authority for ordering bite animal quarantine when human exposure is involved. The shelter veterinarian does not independently have statutory authority to order, modify, or lift a health-department-issued quarantine.
  • For animals that bite within the shelter (staff or visitor exposure), the shelter must notify the health department immediately. The health department then determines the quarantine protocol — the shelter cannot substitute its own clinical judgment for the regulatory order.
  • Shelters with animal control contracts must have written protocols consistent with local ordinance and health department expectations. These must be reviewed when state or local regulations change.
  • A shelter veterinarian may provide clinical information to support the health department's risk assessment, but the quarantine decision rests with the regulatory authority, not with the shelter.

Permissible treatments during quarantine

  • Animals under quarantine may receive supportive and palliative care, food, and water. Emergency medical intervention is permitted.
  • Rabies vaccination is NOT administered during quarantine — it would confound the observation period and the dog/cat is legally considered a potential rabies suspect until the 10-day window closes.
  • The quarantine record must document daily observation status: "alive and clinically normal" or "onset of clinical signs" for each day of the 10-day period.
Exam Trap: Vaccinating during quarantine invalidates the observation Administering rabies vaccine to a biting animal while it is under the statutory 10-day quarantine is incorrect. The vaccine does not retroactively eliminate the pre-bite transmission risk, and it complicates the regulatory record. Vaccination occurs after the quarantine period concludes, or at the next required interval, per the Compendium schedule.

NASPHV Compendium of Animal Rabies Prevention and Control NASPHV

  • The Compendium is published by the National Association of State Public Health Veterinarians and updated periodically. It is the authoritative reference for vaccine schedules, quarantine periods, and exposure management used by veterinarians, public health officials, and animal control agencies across the US.
  • Exam questions about standard quarantine durations, observation periods, and PEP triggers are grounded in the Compendium. Students should be familiar with the most current edition.
  • The Compendium lists approved rabies vaccines and their licensed durations. A vaccine used outside its label duration (e.g., giving a 1-year product and not boostering for 2 years) means the animal is considered inadequately vaccinated for legal and public health purposes.

One Health Framework for Rabies

Rabies is a model One Health disease — its control requires integrated action at the human-animal-environment interface. Understanding this framework at the level required for the ABVP exam means being able to articulate the interdependence of dog/cat vaccination, wildlife management, and human public health.

Pillars of rabies elimination WHO / CDC / GARC

  • Dog vaccination: achieving and sustaining ≥70% vaccination coverage in the domestic dog population breaks the transmission cycle. Dogs are the reservoir responsible for >99% of human rabies deaths globally. WHO, OIE (now WOAH), and FAO support the goal of zero human deaths from dog-mediated rabies by 2030 (Zero by 30).
  • Responsible dog ownership & population management: reducing free-roaming dog populations through spay/neuter, registration, and community education reduces the pool of susceptible individuals and bite incidence.
  • Human PEP access: ensuring affordable, accessible PEP in low-income countries is the immediate life-saving intervention while vaccination programs scale up.
  • Wildlife management (ORV): oral rabies vaccination programs for raccoons and coyotes in the eastern US and for foxes in Europe have demonstrated that wildlife RABV variants can be geographically contained or eliminated. Continued ORV programs prevent westward spread of raccoon rabies.
  • Surveillance and reporting: a functional, integrated surveillance system that captures animal and human cases in real time allows rapid response to outbreaks and tracks progress toward elimination.

Shelter medicine's role in the One Health framework ASV / HSUS

  • Shelters are a critical vaccination point for free-roaming and owner-surrendered animals that otherwise have no veterinary contact. High-volume, low-cost (HVLC) vaccination clinics conducted by or in partnership with shelters directly increase herd immunity in the community dog and cat populations.
  • TNR (trap-neuter-return) programs for community cats, when conducted with rabies vaccination of all cats handled, contribute to population-level immunity in a difficult-to-reach population.
  • Shelters serve as surveillance nodes: animals that arrive dead or moribund, or that exhibit neurological signs, may be the first indication of a local rabies case. Protocols for safe handling, testing, and reporting are therefore a public health function, not only an animal welfare function.
  • Community engagement and education about rabies prevention — avoiding wildlife contact, reporting bats found indoors, vaccinating pets — are core shelter outreach activities that directly reduce human exposure risk.
High-yield One Health pointThe shelter veterinarian is simultaneously a veterinary clinician, a public health practitioner, and a community educator with respect to rabies. The ABVP exam tests whether candidates understand this integrated role — not just the clinical facts about RABV.

Global burden and equity considerations WHO / Global Alliance for Rabies Control

  • ~59,000 human deaths/year globally, predominantly children in Africa and Asia from dog bites.
  • The economic burden includes PEP costs (~$8.6 billion/year globally in direct and indirect costs), productivity losses, and the cost of livestock deaths (rabies also kills cattle and other livestock).
  • Rabies disproportionately affects people in rural, low-income areas without access to PEP. Investment in dog vaccination is more cost-effective than investment in PEP availability alone.
  • The US spends an estimated $300 million/year on rabies prevention (vaccination, surveillance, PEP, ORV programs), yet virtually eliminates human deaths from the disease — a demonstration of the return on One Health investment.

Q1virology

Rabies virus belongs to which family and genus?

B is correct. Rabies virus is the type species of genus Lyssavirus, family Rhabdoviridae. Flaviviridae contains West Nile and dengue; Paramyxoviridae contains distemper and canine parainfluenza; Reoviridae contains bluetongue.
Q2virology

Which structural protein of rabies virus is the primary target for virus-neutralizing antibodies and is the antigen used in all licensed vaccines?

C is correct. The G protein is the surface spike glycoprotein that mediates cell entry via acetylcholine receptor and other receptors, and is the dominant neutralizing antigen. All licensed vaccines stimulate anti-G antibody.
Q3virology

Why is rabies virus more easily inactivated by common disinfectants than canine parvovirus (CPV)?

A is correct. The lipid envelope is the key. CPV is non-enveloped and persists in the environment for months; RABV is enveloped and is destroyed rapidly by drying, UV, soap, bleach, and alcohols. This distinction is a classic exam trap.
Q4pathophysiology

A dog is bitten on the left hind foot by a raccoon. Which of the following best explains why this dog would be expected to have a longer incubation period than a dog bitten on the face?

B is correct. RABV travels centripetally via retrograde axoplasmic transport at ~50–100 mm/day. A bite on the foot requires transport across the entire hind limb and spinal cord before reaching the brain, whereas a facial bite is centimeters from the brainstem.
Q5pathophysiology

What is the significance of the centrifugal phase of rabies virus spread in the context of ante-mortem diagnosis?

C is correct. Once RABV reaches the brain, it spreads centrifugally to peripheral organs including salivary glands and skin hair follicles. This makes ante-mortem RT-PCR on saliva or nuchal skin biopsy possible in humans suspected of having rabies.
Q6clinical / dx

However, they are absent in ~20–30% of confirmed cases, so the DFA test is the gold standard, not histopathology.
Q7clinical / dx

A shelter receives a stray dog showing sudden jaw drop, drooling, and an inability to eat or drink. A technician opens the dog's mouth to examine for a foreign body. What is the most important concern?

C is correct. Jaw drop with hypersalivation and dysphagia in a stray animal is a classic presentation of the paralytic ("dumb") form of rabies. Examining the mouth without PPE constitutes a potential mucous membrane or wound exposure to saliva. Rabies must be considered before any unprotected examination.
Q8clinical / dx

A rabies-suspect animal must be euthanized and its brain submitted for DFA testing. Which euthanasia method is CONTRAINDICATED?

C is correct. Gunshot to the head destroys the brainstem, cerebellum, and hippocampus — the tissue required for DFA testing. This renders the specimen non-diagnostic. Any method that preserves brain architecture (IV barbiturate overdose, deep anesthesia followed by IC injection) is appropriate.
Q9shelter protocol

The 10-day observation rule applies to which three domestic species after a bite incident?

B is correct. Dogs, cats, and ferrets are the only domestic species for which the pre-clinical shedding window is well established and the 10-day observation rule applies. Rabbits, guinea pigs, and horses are not covered by the 10-day rule.
Q10shelter protocol

A currently vaccinated dog is bitten by a stray cat that tests positive for rabies. Per NASPHV Compendium recommendations, what is the appropriate management of the dog?

C is correct. A currently vaccinated animal exposed to a confirmed or probable rabid animal should be revaccinated immediately and observed for 45 days. The 6-month isolation quarantine is for unvaccinated animals whose owners choose not to elect euthanasia. The 10-day rule applies to biting incidents involving domestic animals and humans, not to animal-animal exposures.
Q11shelter protocol

A bat is found in a room where a child was sleeping alone. The child has no visible bite marks. What is the recommended course of action?

C is correct. Bat bites are often imperceptible. A bat in the room with a sleeping person, an unattended child, or an intoxicated person is a PEP indication in most jurisdictions because the person cannot reliably confirm or deny a bite. PEP should be initiated unless the bat can be tested and tests negative.
Q12shelter protocol

Which of the following contacts with a rabies-suspect animal does NOT constitute a rabies exposure requiring PEP evaluation?

C is correct. Intact skin contact does not constitute a rabies exposure. Exposures require virus contact with broken skin or mucous membranes. Bites through thin gloves, saliva on open wounds, and saliva on mucous membranes are all exposures requiring evaluation.
Q13pep & vaccines

An unvaccinated person is bitten by a bat. What is the correct administration of rabies immune globulin (RIG)?

B is correct. RIG must be infiltrated into and around the wound to neutralize virus at the inoculation site. Any remainder is given IM at a site distant from vaccine administration. It is given only on day 0; giving it after day 7 can blunt the vaccine response. It is not given IV and not repeated.
Q14pep & vaccines

A shelter veterinarian who completed the 3-dose pre-exposure prophylaxis series 3 years ago and has confirmed adequate antibody titres is bitten by a suspected-rabid raccoon. What is the correct PEP protocol?

B is correct. Previously vaccinated individuals (prior complete pre-exposure series or prior PEP with demonstrated antibody response) receive only 2 booster doses of vaccine (days 0 and 3) and no RIG. RIG can actually suppress the anamnestic immune response in pre-vaccinated individuals and is contraindicated.
Q15pep & vaccines

Which of the following is true regarding 1-year vs. 3-year veterinary rabies vaccines?

B is correct. The duration designation is a regulatory distinction based on challenge studies conducted to obtain the specific label claim. The vaccines are immunologically equivalent; using a 1-year product does not provide inferior protection per dose. However, for legal and public health purposes an animal vaccinated with a 1-year product must be revaccinated annually to remain "currently vaccinated."
Q16pep & vaccines

Which of the following best describes the reason a recombinant (canarypox-vectored) rabies vaccine is preferred over an adjuvanted killed vaccine in cats?

C is correct. Feline injection-site sarcoma (FISS) is associated with adjuvanted vaccines (and other injected products). Recombinant rabies vaccines (e.g., canarypox-vectored) are non-adjuvanted and are therefore preferred by current AAFP vaccine guidelines to reduce FISS risk. Duration of immunity under the label is 1–3 years depending on product.
Q17public health / law

Which domestic animal species is currently the most frequently reported rabid domestic animal in the United States?

B is correct. Cats surpassed dogs as the most commonly reported rabid domestic animal in the US, largely because vaccination rates in cats (especially free-roaming cats) lag behind those in dogs, and some jurisdictions do not legally mandate cat vaccination. This is a high-frequency exam fact.
Q18public health / law

A shelter staff member is bitten by an impounded stray dog of unknown vaccination status. The dog is available for observation. What is the immediate first step?

C is correct. Immediate thorough wound washing is the single most important first step — it significantly reduces viral inoculation. Then report and seek medical/public health evaluation. The decision about PEP is made in consultation with the health department. Euthanasia is not required if the animal is available for 10-day observation.
Q19public health / law

The NASPHV Compendium of Animal Rabies Prevention and Control is best described as:

C is correct. The NASPHV Compendium is an evidence-based, nationally recognized reference document but is not a federal regulation. States and localities may adopt its recommendations into law or exceed its standards. Veterinarians and shelter staff should follow the Compendium's guidance and local law, whichever is more stringent.
Q20public health / law

From a One Health perspective, which single intervention has been shown to be most cost-effective for preventing human rabies deaths globally?

B is correct. Dogs are the reservoir responsible for >99% of human rabies deaths globally. Mathematical modeling and field evidence consistently show that achieving ≥70% vaccination coverage in domestic dogs breaks transmission chains and is more cost-effective than PEP availability alone. This is the foundation of the WHO/FAO/WOAH Zero by 30 strategy.
