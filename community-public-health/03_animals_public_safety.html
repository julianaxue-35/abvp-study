Community Cat Definitions

Understanding the vocabulary is essential — "free-roaming," "stray," and "feral" are not interchangeable, and the distinction between stray and feral is based on socialization level, not origin.

The umbrella: free-roaming cats ONENOTE

  • Free-roaming cats is the broadest term — encompassing owned cats allowed outdoor access, stray cats (escaped, lost, or abandoned), and feral cats.
  • Stray cats were once socialized to humans but became lost, abandoned, or escaped.
  • Feral cats are untamed and not socialized to humans. They may live in varying degrees of dependence on humans (e.g., colony feeders) but are defined by their socialization level, not by whether they were born outdoors.
  • This stray vs. feral distinction is not consistently used in published literature.
  • ~15% of cats presented for ovariohysterectomy in several studies were feral.

Habitat and ranging behaviour ONENOTE

  • Female cats have smaller home ranges than males.
  • Cats fed by humans show smaller home ranges — closer to those of owned pet cats.
  • Food and resource availability affects cat density and dispersal patterns within an area.
  • Human behaviour (especially abandonment) is the primary driver of free-roaming population growth.
Exam Trap: Feral ≠ born outdoors Feral cats are defined by lack of socialization to humans, not by birth location. A kitten born outdoors can become socialized with early handling; an adult owned cat abandoned outdoors retains socialization. The distinction directly affects outcome decisions (TNR vs. socialization attempts for adoption).

Population Dynamics

Feline reproductive potential is high. The ≥70% sterilization threshold is one of the most frequently tested board facts in this subdomain.

Reproductive statistics ONENOTE

  • Cats are seasonally polyestrous.
  • 1.4 litters per year; 4–5 fetuses per pregnancy; approximately 3 live births per litter.
  • An unspayed female can produce 50–100 kittens in a lifetime.
  • Population growth rates as high as 150% reported despite kitten mortality of 50–75% for kittens under 6 months.
  • Increasing food availability → more frequent births, larger litters, higher kitten survival rates.
Board-tested threshold — population medicine
TNR requires ≥70% sterilization of intact females
Must be sustained within a geographically locked cohort to outpace feline fecundity (1.4 litters/year, ~3 live births/litter). Applies specifically to the intact female population, not total colony size. Below this threshold, the population continues to grow despite the TNR program.
Correction: ≥70% threshold not in your ONENOTE notes Your notes describe reproductive rates and management options but do not explicitly state the ≥70% sterilization threshold for population stabilization. This is a high-yield board fact from population medicine modeling that is directly tested. Anything below 70% sterilization of intact females fails to outpace feline reproductive capacity.

Pre-trapping acclimatization Position Statement

  • Place food inside unsecured (unset) traps for 2–7 days before formal trapping begins.
  • Simultaneously withhold ALL external food sources in the colony area.
  • Goal: cats become comfortable entering traps; trapping efficiency improves; trap-shyness (especially in females) is reduced.
Exam Trap: Acclimatization ≠ starvation The protocol places food inside unset traps while withholding food from other sources — directing the cats' food motivation toward the trap. Withholding all food and water for 14 days is inhumane and not recommended.

Post-return outcomes Literature

  • Confirmed death rate post-return to capture site: approximately 6% in two separate studies.
  • Cats that survive to adulthood are expected to do well for several years or longer.
  • Human feeding is associated with improved survival and longer lifespans.
  • Sterilization improves health: reduces pyometra, mammary tumours, and fighting-related disease (FIV).

Management Strategies

A spectrum of approaches exists for managing free-roaming cat populations. The ABVP exam tests knowledge of evidence, limitations, and appropriate contexts for each.

Exam Trap: The Vacuum Effect Removing cats from an area without preventing immigration and reproduction causes rapid repopulation by surrounding cats moving into the vacated territory. TRE programs that are not sustained intensively fail due to this effect. TNR reduces immigration by maintaining sterilized residents that defend territories.

TNR vs TVHR

TVHR is frequently tested as a foil to TNR. The key distinction: TVHR prevents reproduction while preserving gonadal hormone production, with significant real-world disadvantages.

What TVHR involves ONENOTE

  • Males: vasectomy — reproductive ducts severed, testes retained, testosterone produced normally.
  • Females: hysterectomy — uterus removed, ovaries retained, oestrogen/progesterone produced normally.
  • Result: infertility without gonadal hormone suppression.

Conclusion: TVHR is not recommended over TNR Based on current data, TVHR is unlikely to be more effective than traditional TNR when considered holistically. It preserves nuisance behaviours, ongoing disease risk, and hormone-driven pathologies, while being harder to perform and more expensive. Only one modelling study has suggested superiority; this has not been reproduced in field studies.
Exam Trap: "TVHR is more humane than TNR" False. TVHR preserves yowling, fighting, and urine-spraying, along with ongoing FIV/FeLV and mammary tumour risk. Standard gonadectomy (TNR) reduces all of these. The only thing TVHR and TNR share is infertility.

Welfare, Legal & Public Perception

Welfare is assessed using a validated visual scale. Legal frameworks vary widely. Public opinion strongly favours TNR over euthanasia across all published surveys.

Welfare assessment ONENOTE

Visual welfare scale — four parameters

  • Body condition score (BCS)
  • Coat condition (matting, alopecia, soiling)
  • Nasal and/or ocular discharge; ear crusting
  • Injuries (wounds, lameness, missing eye/tail/limb)

Key finding: fewer clean feeding stations → more thin/apathetic cats. Sterilized adults are more likely to be obese and less likely to have skin lesions than intact adults, but more likely to carry a permanent disability — reflecting survival bias in colonies with sterilization programs.

Disease prevalence — zoonotic risk Literature

  • Infectious disease and parasitic infection rates in free-roaming cats are similar to owned cats — they do not represent greater public health reservoirs.
  • Toxoplasma: cats shed oocysts briefly post-initial infection. Risk in free-roaming cats is similar to owned cats. Human infection mostly from unwashed produce or undercooked meat.
  • Rabies: cats are the most frequently reported domestic animals to test positive in the US. NASPHV guideline: vaccinate all TNR cats with 3-year rabies vaccine; re-trap at 1 year then every 3 years. Even a single immunization provides significant public health benefit — feral cats mount adequate antibody after one injection.

Legal considerations ONENOTE

  • Laws vary significantly between states, counties, and municipalities — no uniform framework.
  • Some jurisdictions protect free-roaming cats under anti-cruelty laws; others do not.
  • Key local law questions: feeding prohibitions in public parks; "caretaker" definitions and associated liability; available grants; permitted management methods in the jurisdiction.
  • Veterinarians and caretakers participating in TNR may face liability under wildlife protection laws in some jurisdictions.
  • All involved in TNR should be familiar with state and local laws before beginning a program.

Public perception Literature

  • Studies consistently show high attachment between colony caretakers and cats they care for, even without physical contact.
  • Majority of surveyed public favour TNR programs.
  • Euthanasia is consistently identified as the LEAST acceptable option for community cat management.
  • Overwhelming majority prefer leaving free-roaming cats outdoors over capture and euthanasia — even knowing a cat could die of traumatic injury within 2 years.
  • Significant polarisation between bird conservation professionals and cat colony caretakers.
  • No management strategy — lethal or non-lethal — succeeds at large scale without public cooperation and stakeholder inclusion.
Predation and ecology Cats are opportunistic hunters. Managed colonies whose members are well-fed spend less than 1% of their time hunting wildlife. Documented cases exist where island cat removal actually worsened native species outcomes by eliminating apex predators and allowing mesopredator (rat) populations to increase, causing higher net predation on vulnerable bird species.

Dog Bites & Public Safety

Dog bites are a significant public health concern and carry specific shelter documentation and disclosure obligations. The ABVP exam tests epidemiology, risk factors, shelter protocols, BSL, and the 10-day rabies rule.

Epidemiology CDC / Literature

  • Approximately 4.5 million dog bites per year in the United States.
  • Approximately 800,000 require medical attention; about half of these are children.
  • 30–50 fatalities per year attributable to dog bites; children and the elderly are most often affected.
  • Children under 5 and the elderly are at highest risk of serious injury or death.
  • Most bites occur with familiar dogs known to the victim — not random stranger attacks.

Risk factors ASV Guidelines

Animal risk factors

  • Intact males — significantly higher bite rate than neutered males
  • Resource guarding behaviour
  • Fear-based aggression
  • Pain-induced aggression
  • Predatory behaviour (especially toward small children)
  • Prior bite history

Human and situational risk factors

  • Children under 5 (lack of ability to read warning signals; face-level proximity)
  • Elderly (slower reflexes; reduced physical resilience)
  • Unfamiliar dogs encountered in unfamiliar contexts
  • Approaching a dog that is eating, sleeping, or caring for puppies
  • Running or making sudden movements around dogs

Shelter obligations — documentation & disclosure ASV Guidelines

  • All known bite incidents must be documented in hardcopy or electronic form and maintained in the animal's record.
  • This documentation must be provided to adopters, foster carers, and transfer partners — this is a non-negotiable disclosure obligation.
  • The public must be prevented from having contact with animals that pose a high bite risk — clearly mark and restrict access to housing areas for these animals.
  • Before any live outcome for an animal with a history of mild to moderate aggressive behaviour, a careful, in-depth risk assessment must be conducted.
  • Training in animal body language and safe handling reduces — but does not eliminate — bite risk for staff.
Exam Trap: Disclosure is mandatory An animal with a documented bite history cannot be placed without disclosing that history to adopters, fosters, and transfer partners. Failure to disclose creates significant legal liability for the shelter. Documentation must be in writing — verbal disclosure alone is insufficient.

Bite wound management ONENOTE

  • Immediately wash the wound with soap, preferably an antiseptic with virucidal activity (e.g., povidone-iodine) and water.
  • Irrigate under pressure: 20 mL syringe + 18G needle with saline.
  • Cover the wound and seek medical attention promptly.
  • Elevate the hand if it is injured.
  • All bites that break the skin carry infection risk — immediate washing reduces this risk.
  • Dog bites: more common; common pathogens = Staphylococcus aureus, Streptococcus, Pasteurella.
  • Cat bites: less common but more likely to become infected — deep puncture wounds + Pasteurella = high risk.

The 10-day rabies observation rule ASV / NASPHV

  • Any dog, cat, or ferret that bites a human must be observed for 10 days, regardless of vaccination status.
  • At intake, shelter staff must ask whether the animal has bitten anyone within the past 10 days.
  • Aggression may be a symptom of rabies — biting animals must be managed per state/local regulations.
  • If the animal dies for any reason within the 10-day window → rabies testing is mandated; head must be cooled, not frozen, and shipped to an approved laboratory.
  • Personnel who routinely handle animals should receive pre-exposure rabies vaccination (PrEP).
  • Local public health authorities can be contacted for guidance on management of biting animals of other species.

Dangerous dog law & Breed-Specific Legislation (BSL) AVMA Position

  • Dangerous dog laws: based on individual animal behaviour history — ban or restrict dogs based on documented dangerous behaviour regardless of breed. Generally considered more effective and equitable than BSL.
  • Breed-Specific Legislation (BSL): prohibits or restricts specific breeds (often pit bull-type dogs, Rottweilers, German Shepherds) regardless of individual temperament or behaviour.
  • AVMA position: BSL is NOT effective at reducing bite incidence. Does not account for individual dog behaviour, owner responsibility, or victim circumstances.
  • BSL creates significant operational challenges for shelters: animals may be legally euthanized solely based on breed/appearance, not temperament.
  • Evidence-based alternatives to BSL: owner education, bite prevention programs, enforcement of existing dangerous dog laws, responsible ownership ordinances.
AVMA: BSL is ineffective and not supported The American Veterinary Medical Association, along with the ASV and most major veterinary organisations, opposes BSL as it is not evidence-based for reducing dog bite incidence. The ABVP exam tests the AVMA position — know that BSL is opposed by professional organisations and that individual behaviour-based assessment is the preferred approach.
