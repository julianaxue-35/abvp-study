Sexual Abuse & Forensic Protocol

ASPCA 9-step forensic examination protocol — alternative light source, DNA swab collection, cavity flush, clinical findings (restraint vs penetration injuries), imaging, and follow-up exam. Based on ASPCA Sexual Abuse Forensic Exam guidelines.

ASPCA 9-Step Forensic Examination Protocol

Sexual abuse of animals is increasingly recognised as a form of animal cruelty with distinct psychological and legal dimensions. The forensic examination must follow a specific sequence to preserve evidence — deviation from the sequence can destroy critical samples.

Core principle — absence of injury does NOT exclude sexual abuse"No injuries does NOT exclude" is the single most important point in sexual abuse cases. Many animals show no gross injuries. A complete forensic examination must be performed regardless of the absence of visible external findings, because microscopic, cytological, and DNA evidence may be present even when the physical exam appears normal.

The 9-Step Protocol ASPCA Sexual Abuse Forensic Exam Guidelines

1
Consult law enforcement before beginning
Contact the investigating officer and notify them before starting the examination. Confirm chain-of-custody procedures, who will receive the evidence samples, and whether additional law enforcement documentation is required at the scene. Document the officer's name, badge number, and the time of contact in the record.
2
Alternative light source (ALS) examination
Use a Wood's lamp (long-wave UV, ~365 nm) or a forensic light source with a 415 nm excitation filter combined with orange-tinted goggles (orange goggles filter the background light and allow fluorescent material to be visualised). Scan the entire body surface systematically, including all orifices and nails. Also scan bedding, cage surfaces, and any objects the animal contacted. Document and photograph all areas of fluorescence before touching them. Biological fluids (semen, saliva, urine) may fluoresce under ALS, though not all fluids fluoresce reliably.
3
DNA swab collection
Collect a minimum of 2 swabs per area from: body orifices (oral, vaginal, rectal), external genitalia, external rectum and rectal mucosa, and all areas of ALS fluorescence identified in Step 2. Use sterile cotton or nylon swabs. Allow all swabs to air-dry completely before packaging — do not seal wet swabs in plastic. Package in paper envelopes (not plastic), label with case number, anatomical location, date, time, and collector's name.
4
Flush vaginal and rectal cavities separately
Using a red rubber catheter and sterile saline: gently instil saline into the vaginal cavity, aspirate and reinfuse 3 times (flush), and collect the final aspirate in a sterile red-top tube. Repeat this process separately for the rectal cavity using a fresh catheter. Label tubes with cavity location, animal ID, case number, date. Store refrigerated (4°C) — NOT frozen; freezing can destroy cellular and DNA evidence. This technique recovers spermatozoa, DNA, and epithelial cells from within the cavity.
5
Cytology samples
Prepare cytology smears from the vaginal and rectal cavity flush material. Air-dry slides and label. These may demonstrate spermatozoa, atypical epithelial cells, or inflammatory infiltrates. Sperm can persist in the vaginal vault for several days in dogs and are identifiable on cytology.
6
Full physical examination — specific focus areas
Perform a complete head-to-tail physical exam with specific attention to:
  • Contusions and abrasions of: genitalia, rectum, neck, flank, ears, abdomen, hind legs, ventral tail base, perineal region
  • Broken or avulsed nails: clip broken nails into a labelled paper evidence envelope (may contain perpetrator DNA)
  • Speculum or otoscope examination of the vaginal vault and rectal canal: visualise lacerations, bruising, or foreign material
  • Ligature marks around muzzle, genitalia, or legs
  • Bodily fluids on hair coat, skin, in the mouth, genitalia, or rectum
7
Take rectal temperature LAST
The rectal thermometer is inserted into the rectum and could destroy or contaminate rectal evidence. Always take temperature at the END of the examination, after all rectal swabs and flush samples have been collected. Document this sequence in the record — "temperature taken at end of examination to preserve rectal evidence."
8
Screening diagnostics
CBC and serum biochemistry panel, urinalysis (UA — see UA for sperm note below), fecal flotation, full-body radiographs (skeletal injuries, foreign bodies in genital/rectal tract), ± heartworm test (dogs), ± FeLV/FIV test (cats). Urinalysis of a female dog may reveal spermatozoa on sediment examination — this is a specific and diagnostically significant finding.
9
Follow-up examination at 24–48 hours
Injuries from sexual abuse may not be apparent at initial presentation, particularly bruising and mucosal tears, which can evolve over the following 24–48 hours. A scheduled follow-up examination is a required component of the ASPCA protocol — not optional. Document new or evolving findings at follow-up and photograph.
Exam trap — temperature last, DNA swabs before bathingTwo common protocol errors: (1) taking rectal temperature before rectal swabs destroys rectal evidence — temperature is always LAST; (2) bathing the animal before ALS examination and swab collection destroys all surface biological evidence. The sequence matters.

Clinical Findings in Sexual Abuse Cases

Clinical findings are categorised as restraint injuries (caused by holding the animal during the assault), penetration injuries (caused by the sexual act itself), and other findings. Remember: normal examination does NOT exclude sexual abuse.

Restraint injuries ASPCA Sexual Abuse Guidelines

LocationFinding
Tail base and dorsumBruising and abrasions from gripping, pressing down, or holding the animal in position
Flanks and lateral thoraxGrip-mark bruising bilaterally from hands or thighs holding the animal
Ears and neckAbrasions, alopecia, and bruising from holding the head in position or restraining vocalisation
MuzzleLigature marks or abrasions from muzzling the animal to prevent biting
Genitalia and legsLigature marks from tying the animal in position — rope fibres or indentation marks

Penetration injuries ASPCA Sexual Abuse Guidelines

FindingDetails
Bruising and tearing of genital-rectal-anal tissueLacerations of the vulvar lips, vestibule, vaginal wall, or anal ring. May be subtle — requires speculum examination.
Vaginal, colonic, or anal dilation with weak sphincter toneAbnormal laxity of the vaginal introitus or anal sphincter without other explanation (e.g., not parturition-related in a recently whelped dog).
Bleeding, prolapse, or perforationRectal or vaginal prolapse, frank haemorrhage, or evidence of perforation (peritonitis, gas in the abdominal cavity on radiographs).
Foreign objectsForeign objects retained in the rectum, vagina, cervix, or uterus — identified on radiography or by manual/speculum examination.

Other significant findings ASPCA Sexual Abuse Guidelines

  • Bodily fluids on hair coat, skin, or in body orifices: ALS-positive fluorescent areas on the fur or skin, or semen confirmed on cytology or PCR from swab samples.
  • Spermatozoa on urinalysis: a urinalysis sediment from a female dog showing spermatozoa is a forensically significant finding that strongly supports sexual assault.
  • Animal is relaxed or highly reactive during invasive examination: both can be relevant — either may reflect prior experience with invasive handling. Neither confirms nor excludes; both should be documented without interpretation in the record.
  • Imaging findings: full-body radiographs may reveal foreign objects in the genital or rectal tract, gas pockets in the uterus or vagina (pneumovagina from penetration), evidence of peritonitis (loss of abdominal detail), or skeletal restraint injuries.
Urinalysis for sperm — a specific findingThe detection of spermatozoa on urine sediment examination of a female dog is a high-yield, specific finding for sexual abuse. Sperm can enter the bladder via the short female urethra following vaginal assault. This finding is testable on the ABVP exam.

Legal and reporting considerations

  • Sexual abuse of animals is reportable under animal cruelty statutes in most US states. File a report with animal control or law enforcement.
  • Human DNA may be recovered from swabs, the cavity flush, or broken nail material — this evidence is analysed by the forensic laboratory for human identification, not veterinary STR profiling.
  • The PACT Act (2019) explicitly includes sexual exploitation of animals as a federal felony.
  • Research on the Link indicates that individuals who perpetrate sexual abuse on animals are at elevated risk for other sexual offences involving humans — law enforcement should be aware.
  • Maintain chain of custody for all evidence samples. DNA evidence collected with improper chain of custody is inadmissible.

Q1Sexual Abuse

A dog presented under suspicion of sexual abuse has a normal physical examination with no external injuries. Which statement BEST reflects how to proceed?

B is correct. "No injuries does NOT exclude" is the core principle of sexual abuse forensic examination. Many animals show no gross injuries. Microscopic findings (spermatozoa on cytology or UA), DNA evidence on swabs, and ALS-detectable biological fluids may be present even when the physical exam is entirely normal. Always proceed with the full protocol. Do not bathe before forensic evidence collection, and do not wait for confirmation from law enforcement — the examination IS the investigative step.
Q2Sexual Abuse

During the ASPCA sexual abuse forensic protocol, rectal temperature should be taken at which step?

D is correct. Rectal temperature is always taken LAST in the sexual abuse examination protocol. The rectal thermometer inserted before rectal swabs and flush could destroy or contaminate the rectal evidence — displacing semen, breaking spermatozoa, and introducing lubricant contamination. Document in the record that temperature was obtained last in order to preserve rectal evidence integrity.
Q3Sexual Abuse

When collecting DNA swabs from the vaginal orifice of a dog suspected of sexual abuse, the ASPCA protocol specifies:

B is correct. The ASPCA protocol specifies a minimum of two swabs per area, to allow duplicate analysis (one for immediate testing, one held in reserve). Swabs must be air-dried before packaging — wet swabs in sealed containers degrade DNA rapidly. Store in paper (never plastic). Label with case number, exact anatomical location, date, time, and collector's name. Formalin destroys DNA; frozen plastic containers also degrade samples through condensation.
Q4Sexual Abuse

Samples collected by flushing the vaginal cavity should be stored in which container and at which temperature?

C is correct. The cavity flush (vaginal or rectal) is collected in a sterile red-top tube (no additives, no anticoagulant) and stored refrigerated at 4°C — not frozen. Freezing disrupts cellular morphology needed for cytology (sperm identification) and can degrade some DNA. EDTA and heparin anticoagulants can interfere with downstream PCR-based DNA analysis. Formalin destroys both DNA and cellular morphology.
Q5Sexual Abuse

Which of the following is a PENETRATION injury finding in a sexual abuse case, as opposed to a restraint injury?

C is correct. Penetration injuries include: bruising and tearing of genital/rectal tissue, vaginal or anal dilation with weak sphincter tone, bleeding, prolapse, perforation, and retained foreign objects. Ligature marks around the muzzle, bilateral flank bruising, and dorsal neck abrasions are all restraint injuries — caused by holding or immobilising the animal during the assault, not by the sexual act itself. Distinguishing these two categories helps reconstruct the event.
Q6Sexual Abuse

Spermatozoa identified on urinalysis sediment examination of a female dog is MOST significant because:

B is correct. Spermatozoa in the urine of a female dog is abnormal and forensically significant. The female dog's short, straight urethra allows semen deposited in the vagina to migrate into the bladder. This finding on routine UA sediment is a high-yield exam point — it is specifically mentioned in the ASPCA protocol as a reason to perform urinalysis in all suspected sexual abuse cases. Spermatozoa are not a normal UA finding.
Q7Sexual Abuse

The ASPCA sexual abuse protocol requires a follow-up examination at 24–48 hours primarily because:

B is correct. Soft tissue injuries — particularly bruising and mucosal tears from penetration — may not be visible at initial examination and become apparent over the following 24–48 hours as haemorrhage and inflammatory changes develop. The follow-up exam is a required component of the ASPCA protocol and should be planned at the initial visit. Document all new or evolving findings with photographs at the follow-up.
Q8Sexual Abuse

Which forensic light source parameter is specified in the ASPCA sexual abuse protocol for surface examination?

B is correct. The ASPCA protocol specifies a 415 nm excitation filter (forensic light source) with orange goggles, OR a standard Wood's lamp (long-wave UV ~365 nm). The orange goggles are essential — they filter the background blue excitation light and allow biological fluorescence to be visualised. Without the goggles, fluorescent areas are obscured by the excitation light. Areas of fluorescence are documented by photograph before any swabbing.
