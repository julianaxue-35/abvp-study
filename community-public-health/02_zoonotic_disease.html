Overview & Classification Framework

Zoonoses are diseases transmitted between vertebrate animals and humans. Understanding the classification, epidemiological terminology, and the shelter's unique occupational and community risk profile is the foundation for all zoonosis questions on the ABVP exam.

Blueprint placement

  • Community & Public Health = 11% of the exam (~38–39 of 350 questions). This subdomain (Zoonotic Disease) carries 21% of the domain weight — making it the joint highest-weight subdomain alongside Animal Cruelty.
  • Expect scenario-based questions: a shelter staff member with a cat scratch and a rash, a bite wound management decision, a ringworm outbreak protocol, or a question about which immunocompromised persons are at highest risk.
  • Cross-domain connections: rabies (Subdomain 4, built separately); vector-borne disease (Subdomain 3); reportable/emerging disease (Subdomain 5). Ringworm also appears in Physical Health (Infectious Disease subdomain).

Definitions and transmission categories WHO / CDC

  • Zoonosis: any disease or infection naturally transmissible between vertebrate animals and humans. WHO estimates >60% of known human infectious diseases are zoonotic in origin; ~75% of emerging human pathogens are animal-origin.
  • Anthroponosis: disease that humans can transmit to animals (reverse zoonosis / zooanthroponosis). Example: humans can transmit SARS-CoV-2 to cats, mink, and other species — an emerging shelter concern.
  • Amplifying host: a host in which the pathogen replicates to high levels, increasing transmission potential (e.g., deer mice for hantavirus; domestic dogs for Leishmania in endemic areas).
  • Dead-end host: infected but does not contribute to further transmission (e.g., humans for most arboviruses — West Nile, EEE).
  • Reservoir host: the species (or environmental source) in which a pathogen maintains itself long-term. The reservoir is not always the source of human exposure.
Key frameworkFor the ABVP exam, organizing zoonoses by transmission route (direct contact, aerosol, fecal-oral, vector-borne, bite/scratch) is more clinically useful than by agent type, because the route determines the PPE and protocol needed in the shelter setting.

High-risk populations in the shelter context CDC / ASV

Immunocompromised individuals are at disproportionate risk from zoonoses that are mild or asymptomatic in healthy adults. The ABVP exam tests this concept repeatedly.

  • HIV/AIDS and other T-cell immunodeficiencies: increased susceptibility to Cryptosporidiosis, Toxoplasmosis, Bartonellosis (bacillary angiomatosis), Salmonellosis, and disseminated Rhodococcus equi.
  • Solid organ transplant recipients on immunosuppression: Cryptosporidiosis, Toxoplasmosis, Salmonella, ringworm (dermatophytosis).
  • Chemotherapy patients / hematologic malignancies: broad susceptibility due to neutropenia; Capnocytophaga canimorsus sepsis from dog bites (asplenic individuals also at extreme risk).
  • Pregnancy: Toxoplasmosis (fetal loss, congenital infection), Listeriosis, Q fever (abortion in livestock workers).
  • Children under 5 years: Toxocariasis (visceral/ocular larva migrans), Salmonella (severe disease), ringworm (tinea capitis).
  • Elderly (>65 years): increased severity of most enteric zoonoses; Capnocytophaga sepsis.
Exam trap — Toxoplasma and pregnancyToxoplasmosis risk in pregnant women comes from primary infection during pregnancy, not from reactivation of prior immunity. A woman who is seropositive before pregnancy is generally NOT at risk of transmitting the parasite to her fetus. The risk is from NEW exposure during pregnancy. Owning a cat is not a high-risk activity if the woman avoids changing litter (or wears gloves) and the cat is an indoor-only hunter-free cat.

Bacterial Zoonoses in the Shelter

Bacterial zoonoses are the most frequently tested category on the ABVP exam within this subdomain. Key pathogens are Salmonella, Campylobacter, Bartonella (cat scratch disease), Leptospira, Pasteurella, Capnocytophaga, and Q fever (Coxiella). Each is organized by agent, transmission, shelter risk, and clinical significance.

Salmonellosis CDC

  • Agent: Salmonella enterica serovars — over 2,500 serovars; non-typhoidal Salmonella is the shelter-relevant form. Typhoid fever (S. Typhi) is a human-to-human disease and is not a shelter zoonosis.
  • Reservoir and source in shelters: dogs and cats can carry Salmonella subclinically, especially animals fed raw meat diets. Reptiles (turtles, iguanas, snakes) are high-prevalence carriers — a significant concern in shelters accepting exotic animals. Young, stressed, or ill animals have higher shedding rates.
  • Transmission: fecal-oral. Contaminated food (especially raw meat), contaminated surfaces, direct handling of infected animals without hand hygiene.
  • Human disease: gastroenteritis (diarrhea, cramping, fever, vomiting) 12–72 hours after exposure; usually self-limiting in healthy adults (3–7 days). Invasive disease (bacteremia, focal infection) occurs in immunocompromised individuals, the elderly, and infants.
  • Shelter implications: raw meat diets (including raw commercial pet foods) are a meaningful risk factor for Salmonella shedding; the ASV does not recommend raw meat diets in shelter settings. Routine hand hygiene after handling animals, animal waste, and food is the primary prevention.
Exam trap — reptiles and childrenCDC estimates reptiles account for ~74,000 Salmonella infections per year in the US. Children under 5, the elderly, and immunocompromised individuals should avoid contact with reptiles, chicks, ducklings, and backyard poultry. This applies to shelter education and adoption counseling.

Campylobacteriosis CDC

  • Agent: Campylobacter jejuni (most common), C. coli. Gram-negative, microaerophilic curved rods.
  • Reservoir: dogs and cats, especially puppies and kittens (younger animals have higher prevalence). Also poultry, cattle, sheep.
  • Transmission: fecal-oral; contact with diarrhetic animals is highest risk. Undercooked poultry and contaminated water are the primary human exposure routes, but shelter animals are a recognized source.
  • Human disease: diarrhea (often bloody), abdominal cramps, fever — onset 1–10 days post-exposure, self-limiting in 1 week in healthy adults. Post-infectious Guillain-Barré syndrome is a rare sequela (~1 in 1,000 cases of C. jejuni infection).
  • Shelter implications: puppies and kittens with diarrhea should prompt PPE and hand hygiene protocols. Co-infection with Salmonella or Giardia is common in shelter animals with GI signs.

Cat Scratch Disease — Bartonellosis CDC

  • Agent: Bartonella henselae (predominantly), B. quintana (more associated with lice/poverty). Small, gram-negative, intracellular.
  • Vector and reservoir: domestic cats are the primary reservoir. The cat flea (Ctenocephalides felis) is the vector between cats; flea feces (flea dirt) containing Bartonella are inoculated into humans via scratch wounds or into cats grooming flea bites. Direct cat-to-human transmission occurs via scratches or bites contaminated with flea feces, not via direct salivary transmission.
  • Feline bacteremia: cats are generally asymptomatic; bacteremia is more prevalent in kittens, stray cats, cats with flea infestations, and shelter cats. Seroprevalence can be very high in shelter cat populations.
  • Human disease (immunocompetent): cat scratch disease — primary inoculation papule/pustule at scratch site, followed by regional lymphadenopathy (enlarged, tender, sometimes suppurating lymph nodes) 1–4 weeks later. Fever, malaise. Usually self-limiting over weeks to months.
  • Human disease (immunocompromised): disseminated, life-threatening disease — bacillary angiomatosis (vascular proliferative skin lesions; may mimic Kaposi sarcoma), bacillary peliosis hepatis, bacteremia, neuroretinitis. HIV/AIDS patients are at highest risk.
High-yield — prevention is flea controlBecause the cat flea is the essential vector for cat-to-cat transmission and the source of flea feces that drive human infections, flea control is the single most important preventive measure for Bartonellosis in the shelter. Declawing cats is not recommended as a preventive strategy. Hand hygiene and wound care after scratches remain important.
Exam trap — route of transmission to humansCats do not transmit Bartonella henselae via their saliva or directly by biting; transmission requires contaminated flea feces inoculated via the scratch or bite wound. An indoor, flea-free cat is low-risk regardless of its serostatus.

Leptospirosis CDC / AVMA

  • Agent: Leptospira interrogans — multiple pathogenic serovars. Maintenance hosts vary by serovar (e.g., L. icterohaemorrhagiae: rats; L. canicola: dogs; L. pomona: swine, cattle).
  • Transmission: primarily through contact with urine or urine-contaminated water/soil from infected animals. Organism enters via mucous membranes, conjunctiva, or breaks in skin. Person-to-person transmission is rare.
  • Reservoir: rodents (especially Norway rats) are the classic maintenance host and shed in urine without clinical disease. Dogs (especially unvaccinated outdoor dogs) can be infected and shed in urine. Cats are relatively resistant.
  • Human disease (Weil's disease): biphasic illness — initial leptospiremic phase (fever, chills, headache, myalgia, conjunctival suffusion) followed by immune phase in severe cases (icteric leptospirosis / Weil's disease): jaundice, renal failure (acute tubular necrosis), hemorrhage, pulmonary hemorrhage, myocarditis. Case fatality can reach 5–15% in severe cases.
  • Shelter implications: dogs admitted from flood events, outdoor environments, or animal hoarding situations are at higher risk. Shelters in rodent-infested buildings are an occupational exposure concern. PPE (gloves, eye protection) when handling potentially infected dog urine. Dog vaccination (4-serovar leptospiral bacterin) is available and recommended in endemic areas.
Exam trap — cats and leptospirosisCats are frequently cited as resistant to clinical leptospirosis and are not significant sources of human infection compared with dogs and rodents. The ABVP exam may contrast cat vs. dog risk for leptospirosis — cats are not a meaningful occupational risk for this pathogen in the shelter.

Pasteurellosis and Capnocytophaga CDC

Pasteurella multocida

  • The most common bacterial pathogen isolated from dog and cat bite wounds. Part of normal oral flora of dogs (~50–75%) and cats (~75%).
  • Human disease: rapidly progressive cellulitis at bite/scratch wound, often within hours; may progress to tenosynovitis, septic arthritis, osteomyelitis, or bacteremia. Cats inflict deep puncture wounds that inoculate Pasteurella into deeper tissues.
  • Treatment: amoxicillin-clavulanate is first-line for bite wound infections; penicillin allergy — doxycycline or fluoroquinolone. Do not use first-generation cephalosporins — poor activity against Pasteurella.
Exam trap — do not use first-gen cephalosporins for bite woundsCephalexin and cefazolin have poor activity against Pasteurella multocida. Bite wound infections require amoxicillin-clavulanate (or equivalent broad-spectrum cover for both Pasteurella and anaerobes).

Capnocytophaga canimorsus

  • Gram-negative, slow-growing, fusiform rod; normal oral flora of dogs (and cats). Transmitted by bite, scratch, or lick on broken skin.
  • In healthy individuals: usually causes mild or no disease.
  • In high-risk individuals: life-threatening sepsis, DIC, meningitis, endocarditis, gangrene. Highest risk: asplenic individuals (functional or surgical asplenia), alcoholics, those on immunosuppressive therapy. Case fatality ~30% in septic asplenic patients.
  • Shelter implication: all dog bite victims should be counseled about Capnocytophaga. Asplenic staff or volunteers must be identified in shelter health and safety programs; even a minor lick on an open sore is a potential life-threatening exposure for an asplenic person.
High-yield — asplenic individuals and dog exposureAn asplenic person who is licked by a dog on a minor abrasion and develops fever within 24–72 hours should seek emergency care immediately. Capnocytophaga canimorsus sepsis can progress to multiorgan failure and death within 24–48 hours in asplenic individuals.

Q Fever — Coxiella burnetii CDC / NASPHV

  • Agent: Coxiella burnetii — obligate intracellular bacterium (now classified as gamma-Proteobacteria, not Rickettsia). Extremely environmentally stable; resistant to heat, drying, and many disinfectants. Forms a spore-like small cell variant that can survive in dust for months to years.
  • Reservoir and source: primarily ruminants (cattle, sheep, goats). Cats and dogs can also be infected. Parturient animals shed massive quantities of organisms in placental fluids, amniotic fluid, fetal membranes, and birth products. Also shed in milk, urine, and feces.
  • Transmission to humans: primarily aerosolization of contaminated dust or birthing materials — not bite or direct contact. Ingestion of unpasteurized dairy products is a secondary route.
  • Shelter relevance: pregnant cats (queens) undergoing parturition in a shelter are a Q fever risk. Shelters with farm-animal contacts or wildlife rehabilitators handling ruminants face higher risk.
  • Human disease: most infections (~60%) are asymptomatic or mild. Symptomatic acute Q fever: abrupt onset fever, severe headache, myalgia, pneumonia, hepatitis. Chronic Q fever (endocarditis, chronic fatigue): more common in those with prior valvular disease or immunocompromised status.
Exam trap — Q fever is aerosol-transmittedQ fever is not transmitted by contact alone. A shelter worker who assists a parturient cat without respiratory protection and becomes ill with fever, headache, and pneumonia-like symptoms should prompt consideration of Q fever. No bite, scratch, or broken skin is required.

Psittacosis — Chlamydophila psittaci CDC

  • Agent: Chlamydophila psittaci (formerly Chlamydia psittaci). Obligate intracellular.
  • Reservoir: birds — particularly psittacines (parrots, macaws, cockatiels, parakeets), but also pigeons, raptors, and other avian species. Infected birds may appear healthy.
  • Transmission: inhalation of dried feces, respiratory secretions, or feather dust from infected birds. Also through bites.
  • Human disease: pneumonia with atypical features — non-productive cough, fever, headache, myalgia. Presentation similar to other atypical pneumonias (Mycoplasma, Legionella). Severe cases: multi-organ involvement, endocarditis.
  • Shelter relevance: shelters that accept birds — particularly parrots and pigeons — have an occupational psittacosis risk. All birds should be handled with respiratory protection and bird rooms adequately ventilated. Psittacosis is a nationally notifiable disease.
  • Treatment: doxycycline is first-line for both avian and human disease.

Parasitic Zoonoses in the Shelter

Parasitic zoonoses are high-yield because they connect the Physical Health domain (parasite control) with the Community and Public Health domain. Key pathogens include Toxoplasma, Toxocara, Cryptosporidium, Giardia, and hookworms.

Toxoplasmosis CDC

  • Agent: Toxoplasma gondii — obligate intracellular coccidian parasite. Cats are the only definitive host and the only species that can shed environmentally resistant oocysts in feces.
  • Transmission to humans: (1) ingestion of tissue cysts in undercooked meat (most common route); (2) ingestion of oocysts from environment contaminated by cat feces; (3) vertical transmission from primary maternal infection during pregnancy; (4) organ transplant/blood transfusion (rare).
  • Oocyst shedding window: a cat typically sheds oocysts for only 1–3 weeks following primary infection, then develops immunity and stops shedding. Oocysts require 1–5 days of sporulation in the environment before becoming infectious — meaning fresh litter changed daily poses minimal risk even from a shedding cat.
  • Congenital toxoplasmosis: fetal infection with chorioretinitis, hydrocephalus, intracranial calcifications, and developmental delay occurs only with primary maternal infection during pregnancy. Reactivation of latent infection in immunocompetent pregnant women does not typically transmit to the fetus.
  • Immunocompromised disease: reactivation of latent bradyzoites causes toxoplasmic encephalitis — the most common cause of brain abscess in HIV/AIDS patients with CD4 <100 cells/µL.
High-yield — practical risk counselingThe shelter practitioner's public health message: pregnant staff should wear gloves when handling litter and wash hands thoroughly. They should avoid cleaning litter boxes if possible. Having a cat does not mean she must give it up — the risk from her indoor, non-hunting, routinely tested cat is extremely low. The greater risk of toxoplasmosis for most Americans is eating undercooked meat.
Exam trap — definitive host specificityCats are the ONLY definitive host for Toxoplasma gondii. Dogs, humans, and all other warm-blooded animals are intermediate hosts that harbor tissue cysts but do NOT shed oocysts in their feces. This means dogs are NOT a source of Toxoplasma oocysts.

Toxocariasis — Visceral and Ocular Larva Migrans CDC

  • Agent: Toxocara canis (dog roundworm) and T. cati (cat roundworm). Larval stages (L2) cause human disease; adult worms do not develop in humans (dead-end hosts).
  • Transmission: ingestion of embryonated eggs from contaminated soil, sandboxes, or directly from surfaces soiled with infected dog/cat feces. Geophagia in young children is a major risk factor. Paratenic hosts (raw liver from infected animals) are an additional route.
  • Prepatent period and environmental persistence: eggs are not immediately infectious — they require 2–4 weeks to embryonate in the environment. Eggs are hardy and can survive in soil for years. Cat and dog defecation in public playgrounds and sandboxes is a significant public health concern.
  • Human disease:
    • Visceral larva migrans (VLM): larvae migrate through liver, lung, brain, heart. Presents as fever, eosinophilia, hepatomegaly, cough. More common in young children with pica.
    • Ocular larva migrans (OLM): larva migrates to the eye causing granuloma, uveitis, and potentially permanent visual impairment or blindness. Often in older children (5–10 years); may present without systemic eosinophilia.
    • Covert toxocariasis: common subclinical infection — seroprevalence in the US ranges from ~14% in the general population to >30% in economically disadvantaged communities.
  • Prevention: regular deworming of dogs and cats (especially puppies and kittens, which have highest egg burdens), covering sandboxes, preventing animal defecation in playgrounds, hand hygiene for children.

Hookworm — Cutaneous Larva Migrans CDC

  • Agent: Ancylostoma braziliense (most common cause of cutaneous larva migrans), A. caninum, Uncinaria stenocephala. These are dog and cat hookworms that cannot complete their life cycle in humans.
  • Transmission: direct skin penetration by infective L3 larvae from contaminated soil. Typical exposure: walking barefoot on sandy soil or beaches contaminated with dog/cat feces.
  • Human disease: pruritic, serpiginous, advancing skin track (creeping eruption) as larvae migrate through skin at ~1–2 cm/day. Does not progress to systemic disease in most cases; larvae die within weeks to months. Eosinophilic enteritis from A. caninum is a rare complication.
  • Prevention: deworming shelter dogs and cats, footwear in potentially contaminated areas, not allowing dogs to defecate on beaches or playgrounds.

Cryptosporidiosis CDC

  • Agent: Cryptosporidium parvum (cattle, humans), C. felis (cats), C. canis (dogs), among others. Protozoan coccidian.
  • Transmission: fecal-oral; oocysts are immediately infectious when passed. Extremely resistant to chlorine disinfection at standard water treatment concentrations.
  • Human disease: watery, profuse, non-bloody diarrhea with cramping — self-limiting in immunocompetent individuals (1–2 weeks). In immunocompromised individuals (especially HIV/AIDS with CD4 <100): severe, life-threatening, chronic diarrhea; biliary and pulmonary involvement possible. There is no reliably curative treatment for immunocompromised patients.
  • Shelter implications: young, stressed shelter animals (especially kittens and puppies) have higher Cryptosporidium prevalence. Handling diarrheic animals without gloves and hand washing is an occupational risk, especially for immunocompromised staff.
Exam trap — chlorine resistanceCryptosporidium oocysts are highly resistant to chlorine bleach at concentrations used for routine shelter disinfection. Effective interventions include hydrogen peroxide (accelerated hydrogen peroxide products), steam, and ammonia — though ammonia compatibility with other cleaning products must be considered. Hand hygiene with soap and water (not hand sanitizer alone) is essential because alcohol-based sanitizers have limited activity against oocysts.

Giardiasis CDC

  • Agent: Giardia duodenalis (syn. G. intestinalis, G. lamblia). Assemblage A and B infect humans; assemblage C and D are predominantly dog-specific; assemblage F is cat-specific.
  • Zoonotic potential: the zoonotic risk from dogs and cats is lower than commonly perceived because most animal-specific assemblages (C, D, F) do not readily infect humans. Assemblage A (zoonotic) does occur in dogs and cats and can infect humans, but the primary human risk is via contaminated water and human-to-human transmission.
  • Human disease: diarrhea (often steatorrhea, malabsorption), bloating, flatulence, nausea — typically lasting 1–3 weeks; may become chronic.
  • Shelter implications: Giardia is extremely common in shelter animals and a significant cause of diarrhea. Standard shelter disinfectants (quaternary ammonium compounds) have limited activity against Giardia cysts; accelerated hydrogen peroxide and quaternary ammonium with adequate contact time, plus mechanical cleaning, are recommended.

Fungal & Other Zoonoses

Dermatophytosis (ringworm) is the most important fungal zoonosis in shelter practice. Sporothrix and select others are lower-yield but testable. Viral zoonoses other than rabies (covered in Subdomain 4) are addressed briefly here.

Dermatophytosis (Ringworm) ASV / CDC

  • Agents: Microsporum canis (most common in cats; zoonotic), Trichophyton mentagrophytes (rodents, some dogs), Microsporum gypseum (soil; geophilic). M. canis is the predominant zoonotic species in shelters.
  • Transmission: direct contact with infected animal or person; indirect contact with spores on fomites (bedding, grooming equipment, exam tables). Spores are environmentally persistent for 12–18 months under dry conditions.
  • Animal presentation: circular areas of alopecia, scaling, and broken hairs, most commonly on the face, pinnae, and forelimbs. However, up to 20–30% of infected cats may be asymptomatic carriers — a clinically normal coat does not rule out infection.
  • Diagnosis: Wood's lamp fluorescence (apple-green) for M. canis — but only ~50% of M. canis strains fluoresce; a negative Wood's lamp does not rule out infection. Fungal culture (DTM / dermatophyte test medium) with PCR confirmation is the gold standard.
  • Human disease: tinea corporis (body), tinea capitis (scalp, predominantly in children — can cause permanent alopecia if untreated), tinea faciei, tinea manuum. Lesions are circular, itchy, scaling, erythematous rings.
  • Treatment (animals): lime sulfur dips (effective but malodorous), enilconazole rinses, topical miconazole/chlorhexidine shampoos, systemic itraconazole or terbinafine. Combination of systemic and topical therapy is more effective than either alone.
  • Shelter outbreak control: isolate infected animals; decontaminate environment (dilute bleach 1:10 on non-porous surfaces; vacuuming + HEPA filtration; laundering with heat); staff PPE (gloves, gown, dedicated footwear); systematic testing of all exposed animals; foster care for isolated animals accelerates shelter capacity recovery.
Exam trap — Wood's lamp sensitivityWood's lamp detects only approximately 50% of M. canis infections. A negative Wood's lamp does NOT rule out ringworm. DTM culture is required for definitive diagnosis. This distinction is heavily tested.
High-yield — ringworm is the #1 zoonosis by volume in shelter practiceRingworm is the most frequently transmitted zoonotic disease in shelter settings — not Salmonella, not Toxoplasma. It is highly contagious, environmentally persistent, and produces outbreaks that strain shelter capacity. Understanding the shelter management protocol for a ringworm outbreak is essential exam preparation.

Sporotrichosis CDC

  • Agent: Sporothrix schenckii complex — dimorphic fungus. Mold phase in environment; yeast phase in host tissue.
  • Traditional route: traumatic inoculation from environmental source (thorns, plant material — "rose thorn disease").
  • Zoonotic transmission: in Brazil, an epidemic linked to cats — transmission via scratch or bite from infected cats. Cat-associated sporotrichosis is an emerging concern in shelter cats from endemic regions. Infected cats typically have ulcerative nodular skin lesions and nasal involvement.
  • Human disease: lymphocutaneous form (most common) — nodular lesion at inoculation site with chain of lymphangitic nodules progressing proximally. Disseminated disease in immunocompromised individuals.
  • Treatment: itraconazole is first-line for both feline and human lymphocutaneous sporotrichosis.

Viral zoonoses (non-rabies) CDC

Influenza A — interspecies transmission

  • Canine influenza virus (CIV H3N8, H3N2) does not currently infect humans. However, shelter staff contact with dogs during CIV outbreaks is an OHS point of awareness for novel reassortments, given influenza A's pandemic potential.
  • Cat-to-human transmission of influenza A (H7N2) was documented in a 2016–2017 outbreak in a New York City animal shelter — a shelter worker developed mild influenza-like illness. This was a novel event; routine CIV is not a human health risk, but influenza A in cats should prompt reporting to public health authorities.

SARS-CoV-2 (reverse zoonosis / anthroponosis)

  • Humans can transmit SARS-CoV-2 to cats, dogs, and other species (mink, big cats). Cats appear more susceptible than dogs. Evidence of cat-to-human transmission exists but is considered rare under normal shelter conditions.
  • Shelter implication: staff with COVID-19 should avoid contact with animals if possible; routine screening of shelter animals for SARS-CoV-2 is not warranted but may be triggered by cluster events.

Monkeypox (Mpox)

  • Prairie dogs and small exotic rodents have been implicated in US outbreaks (2003: pet store-to-human transmission via prairie dogs exposed to infected Gambian giant pouched rats). Shelters accepting exotic rodents should be aware of this risk.

Risk Reduction & Occupational Health

Shelters are occupational environments with zoonotic disease risk for staff and volunteers. The ASV Guidelines for Standards of Care, CDC, and OSHA provide the framework for written zoonosis risk reduction programs. This is directly exam-tested.

Hierarchy of controls in the shelter CDC / OSHA

The occupational health framework applies engineering controls, administrative controls, and PPE in order of effectiveness:

Control levelExamples in zoonosis context
Elimination / substitutionNot accepting high-risk species (e.g., wild-caught reptiles) when alternatives exist; eliminating raw meat diets from shelter feeding protocols
Engineering controlsIsolation wards with dedicated ventilation; HEPA filtration for ringworm isolation rooms; handwashing sinks at room exits; negative-pressure isolation for respiratory pathogens
Administrative controlsWritten SOPs for zoonosis risk; staff health screening and pre-employment risk disclosure; no eating/drinking in animal areas; mandatory hand hygiene protocols; mandatory reporting of bites and potential exposures; training on zoonosis recognition

High-yield — hand hygiene is the single most impactful interventionAcross all fecal-oral zoonoses (Salmonella, Campylobacter, Cryptosporidium, Giardia, Toxocara), thorough hand washing with soap and water before eating, after handling animals, and after cleaning is the single most impactful preventive measure. Hand sanitizer has limited activity against non-enveloped pathogens and oocysts.

Written zoonosis risk program requirements ASV Standards of Care

  • ASV Guidelines recommend all shelters have a written zoonosis risk reduction program that includes: (1) identification of zoonotic risks present in the shelter; (2) protocols for PPE use; (3) bite and exposure reporting procedures; (4) education for staff and volunteers; (5) identification and accommodation of at-risk employees.
  • At-risk staff categories: pregnant women, immunocompromised individuals, children (if applicable to shelter context), elderly. These individuals should be counseled about specific risks (Toxoplasmosis for pregnancy, Cryptosporidiosis for immunocompromised) and may need role modifications.
  • All bite wounds and potential zoonotic exposures must be reported and documented. Workers' compensation implications and mandatory reporting requirements (e.g., rabies exposure) make documentation a legal obligation, not just a best practice.

Adoption counseling and pet-owner education CDC / ASV

  • Shelter veterinarians and adoption counselors have a public health obligation to discuss zoonotic risks with adopters, particularly for households with immunocompromised members, pregnant women, young children, or elderly individuals.
  • Key adoption counseling points for immunocompromised adopters: regular veterinary care, internal parasite testing and control, ectoparasite control (flea prevention for Bartonella), avoidance of raw meat diets, and immediate veterinary attention for animals with diarrhea or skin lesions.
  • Pregnant adopters: counsel on Toxoplasma (indoor cat, not hunting, regular litter changes with gloves), cat scratch disease prevention (flea control), and avoidance of handling parturient animals.
  • Families with young children: counsel on Toxocara (regular deworming, hand hygiene for children, supervised sandboxes), Campylobacter/Salmonella (hand washing after animal contact), and ringworm recognition.
Shelter policy point — Healthy Pets Healthy PeopleThe CDC "Healthy Pets Healthy People" framework emphasizes that the benefits of pet ownership generally outweigh zoonotic risks for most households when proper hygiene and veterinary care protocols are followed. The shelter's role is to educate, not to discourage adoption. Denying adoption to immunocompromised applicants is neither evidence-based nor ethically appropriate in most circumstances — it requires individualized counseling.

One Health Framework for Zoonotic Disease

The One Health approach recognizes that human health, animal health, and environmental health are inextricably linked. Zoonotic disease is the paradigmatic One Health problem — it cannot be effectively controlled from a single-species perspective. The ABVP exam tests whether candidates can articulate this integrated framework.

Definition and pillars of One Health WHO / FAO / WOAH / CDC

  • WHO, FAO (Food and Agriculture Organization), and WOAH (World Organisation for Animal Health, formerly OIE) jointly define One Health as "a collaborative, multisectoral, and transdisciplinary approach — working at the local, regional, national, and global levels — with the goal of achieving optimal health outcomes recognizing the interconnection between people, animals, plants, and their shared environment."
  • The triad: human health + animal health + ecosystem/environmental health. No pillar can be effectively addressed in isolation.
  • Key One Health statistics: ~60% of known human infectious diseases are zoonotic; ~75% of emerging human pathogens are animal-origin; most pandemic-potential pathogens (influenza, SARS, Ebola, Nipah, HIV) have animal origins.

Shelter medicine's role in One Health ASV / CDC

  • Surveillance function: shelters intake animals from diverse community sources and are ideally positioned to detect unusual disease patterns (novel zoonoses, outbreak clusters) early. A shelter with multiple animals showing neurological signs or unusual pneumonia should trigger public health notification.
  • Vaccination as herd immunity: shelters vaccinating all impounded animals against rabies and other diseases contribute to population-level immunity in the community animal population — directly reducing human exposure risk. High-volume low-cost (HVLC) clinics extend this function into the community.
  • Parasite control: systematic deworming and ectoparasite treatment of shelter animals at intake directly reduces the burden of Toxocara eggs, hookworm larvae, and flea-transmitted Bartonella in the community.
  • Community education: adoption counseling, public outreach, and veterinary guidance at the point of adoption is a scalable public health intervention that reaches populations who may not otherwise access veterinary or public health services.
  • TNR and community cat management: vaccinating free-roaming cats during TNR programs reduces the population of unvaccinated, potentially parasitized animals that interact with the community and wildlife.
High-yield One Health exam pointThe shelter veterinarian simultaneously functions as a veterinary clinician, a public health practitioner, a community educator, and a disease surveillance node. The ABVP exam tests this integrated role — knowing the clinical features of zoonoses is necessary but not sufficient; understanding the public health implications and shelter operational responses is required for full-mark answers.

Emerging and re-emerging zoonoses: the shelter as a sentinel CDC / WHO

  • Factors driving emergence: land use change (wildlife-human-livestock interface); climate change (expanding vector ranges for Lyme, West Nile, plague); global trade in live animals; antimicrobial resistance in zoonotic pathogens; increased density of immunocompromised humans.
  • Role of shelters in early detection: clusters of unusual disease in multiple species, rapid neurological deterioration, or unusual distribution of respiratory signs should prompt consultation with state veterinarian and public health authorities. Shelters should have contact information for local/state animal disease reporting on file.
  • Antimicrobial-resistant zoonoses: MRSA can be transmitted between humans and companion animals (bidirectional). Shelter animals have been identified as both recipients of MRSA from colonized humans and occasional sources of MRSA for staff. Standard infection control (hand hygiene, PPE) prevents transmission. Routine MRSA screening of shelter animals is not recommended but may be warranted during outbreak investigation.

Q1overview

Which of the following best defines a zoonosis?

B is correct. The WHO definition of a zoonosis is a disease or infection naturally transmissible between vertebrate animals and humans under natural conditions. Option D is partly true historically but does not define a zoonosis — a disease must be capable of ongoing natural transmission between species to qualify.
Q2overview

A pregnant shelter employee is concerned about exposure to cats. Which of the following statements about Toxoplasma gondii and pregnancy is most accurate?

B is correct. Congenital toxoplasmosis requires primary maternal infection during pregnancy. Women with prior immunity are protected. Cats typically shed oocysts for only 1–3 weeks after primary infection. The oocyst shedding window, sporulation requirement (1–5 days), and the high proportion of human Toxoplasma infections from undercooked meat all reduce the practical risk from indoor cats. Dogs are intermediate hosts and do NOT shed oocysts.
Q3overview

An asplenic staff member is licked on a small abrasion on their hand by a dog in the shelter. Three days later they present to the ER with high fever, hypotension, and purpuric rash. Which pathogen is most likely responsible?

C is correct. Capnocytophaga canimorsus is the classic cause of rapidly progressive fulminant sepsis in asplenic individuals following dog (or cat) contact — including licking on broken skin. The purpuric rash and rapid deterioration are hallmarks. Asplenic persons have dramatically impaired opsonization and are unable to clear this encapsulated gram-negative organism. Mortality approaches 30% in asplenic patients. This scenario is a recurring ABVP exam vignette.
Q4bacterial

A shelter receives a confiscated collection of reptiles including juvenile turtles. Which zoonosis is most directly associated with handling these animals, and which population is at highest risk?

B is correct. Reptiles — including turtles, lizards, and snakes — are high-prevalence carriers of non-typhoidal Salmonella. CDC estimates reptiles account for ~74,000 Salmonella infections annually in the US. Children under 5, the elderly, and immunocompromised individuals are at highest risk of invasive (bacteremic) disease. The FDA banned the sale of small turtles (<4 inches) in 1975 specifically because of this risk.
Q5bacterial

The primary mechanism of Bartonella henselae transmission from cats to humans is:

B is correct. The cat flea (Ctenocephalides felis) is the vector between cats; flea feces (containing Bartonella) deposited on the cat's skin are inoculated into humans when scratch wounds occur. The cat's saliva is not the direct source — saliva becomes contaminated with flea feces when the cat grooms. An indoor, flea-free cat is low-risk regardless of its Bartonella serostatus. Flea control is therefore the most important preventive measure.
Q6bacterial

A shelter employee develops fever, severe headache, myalgia, and cough 10 days after assisting a stray queen with a complicated parturition without wearing respiratory PPE. The most likely diagnosis is:

C is correct. Coxiella burnetii (Q fever) is transmitted by inhalation of aerosolized birthing fluids, placental material, and fetal membranes from infected animals — including cats. The incubation period is typically 2–3 weeks (range 2–6 weeks). No bite, scratch, or skin break is required; the aerosol route is the primary mode of transmission. N95 respirator, gloves, gown, and eye protection are required for shelter staff assisting parturient animals in areas with Q fever risk.
Q7bacterial

A shelter dog with a history of outdoor exposure and known rodent activity in the shelter is found to have acute renal failure, icterus, and conjunctival suffusion. Which zoonosis should be considered, and what is the appropriate PPE for staff handling this animal?

B is correct. The clinical picture — renal failure, jaundice, and conjunctival suffusion (bleeding into the conjunctival membranes) — in a dog with rodent exposure is classic for leptospirosis (Weil's disease). Leptospira is shed in urine and enters humans via mucous membranes or skin breaks. Staff handling this dog's urine or body fluids should wear gloves and eye protection (splash risk). Cats are relatively resistant to leptospirosis and are not a significant occupational risk for this pathogen.
Q8bacterial

A shelter staff member is bitten on the hand by a cat. Within 4 hours the wound is red, warm, and rapidly spreading. The most appropriate first-line antibiotic for empirical treatment is:

B is correct. Pasteurella multocida, the predominant pathogen in cat bites, has intrinsic resistance to first-generation cephalosporins (cephalexin, cefazolin). Amoxicillin-clavulanate provides coverage for Pasteurella, anaerobes, and staphylococci — the empirical standard for bite wound infections. Clindamycin and metronidazole also have poor activity against Pasteurella when used alone. This is a classic antibiotic-selection exam trap.
Q9parasitic

Which of the following is the ONLY definitive host for Toxoplasma gondii?

C is correct. Cats (domestic and wild felids) are the only definitive hosts for Toxoplasma gondii — they are the only species in which sexual replication of the parasite occurs, leading to oocyst formation and fecal shedding. All other warm-blooded species, including dogs and humans, are intermediate hosts that harbor tissue cysts (bradyzoites) but never shed environmentally resistant oocysts.
Q10parasitic

A 7-year-old child presents with a unilateral visual disturbance and decreased visual acuity. Ophthalmologic examination reveals a retinal granuloma. Serology shows eosinophilia. No systemic signs are present. The most likely diagnosis is:

B is correct. Ocular larva migrans (OLM) from Toxocara canis presents in school-age children (typically 5–10 years) as a retinal granuloma with visual impairment, often without the systemic eosinophilia and hepatomegaly seen in visceral larva migrans (VLM). The two forms tend to present in different age groups: VLM in toddlers with pica, OLM in older children. A. braziliense causes cutaneous larva migrans (skin tracking), not ocular disease.
Q11parasitic

An immunocompromised cat adopter with HIV/AIDS and CD4 count of 80 cells/µL reports profuse watery diarrhea that has persisted for 3 weeks after adopting two kittens with diarrhea from a shelter. Which pathogen is most likely responsible and what is the key disinfection challenge?

B is correct. Cryptosporidium causes chronic, life-threatening diarrhea in immunocompromised patients (particularly HIV/AIDS with CD4 <100). There is no reliably effective treatment in immunocompromised hosts. The key disinfection challenge is that Cryptosporidium oocysts are highly resistant to chlorine bleach at standard concentrations used in most shelters. Accelerated hydrogen peroxide, steam, and mechanical cleaning are more effective. Hand washing with soap and water is essential; alcohol hand gels are inadequate against oocysts.
Q12parasitic

A child develops a serpiginous, intensely pruritic, advancing skin track on the sole of the foot after playing on a beach where stray dogs were present. The most likely diagnosis and causative organism are:

B is correct. Cutaneous larva migrans (CLM), or "creeping eruption," is caused by the skin penetration of Ancylostoma braziliense (hookworm) larvae from contaminated soil or sand. The larvae migrate through human skin at 1–2 cm/day creating a characteristic serpiginous track but cannot complete their life cycle in humans. Walking barefoot on contaminated sandy beaches is the classic exposure scenario. Treatment: topical or oral ivermectin or albendazole.
Q13fungal

A Wood's lamp examination of a kitten with circular areas of alopecia on the face is negative. Which of the following is the most appropriate next step?

B is correct. Wood's lamp (UV at 365 nm) produces apple-green fluorescence in M. canis infections, but only approximately 50% of strains fluoresce. A negative Wood's lamp does NOT rule out ringworm. Fungal culture (DTM — dermatophyte test medium, or Sabouraud's) with color change and microscopic confirmation is the diagnostic standard. PCR on hair samples is increasingly available and provides faster results. Histopathology is not routinely used for ringworm diagnosis.
Q14fungal

A ringworm outbreak occurs in a shelter's kitten room. Which of the following environmental decontamination approaches is most appropriate for hard, non-porous surfaces?

B is correct. Dilute bleach (1:10 or 1:32 sodium hypochlorite) is effective against dermatophyte spores on non-porous surfaces when properly applied with adequate contact time and after mechanical cleaning. For soft materials (bedding, towels), heat laundering (>60°C) and HEPA vacuum for spore removal are required. Quaternary ammonium compounds have limited sporicidal activity against dermatophytes. Accelerated hydrogen peroxide is also effective on hard surfaces but the claim that bleach is ineffective against dermatophytes is incorrect.
Q15fungal

In an immunocompromised HIV-positive cat adopter, which of the following Bartonella manifestations is most likely to occur if the patient is scratched by a flea-infested cat?

C is correct. In immunocompromised individuals — particularly those with HIV/AIDS — Bartonella henselae infection can disseminate rather than remaining localized. Bacillary angiomatosis (vascular proliferative skin and systemic lesions resembling Kaposi sarcoma) and bacillary peliosis hepatis (blood-filled hepatic cysts) are the characteristic severe presentations. Immunocompetent individuals typically develop self-limiting regional lymphadenopathy (cat scratch disease) only. Tinea corporis and OLM are unrelated to Bartonella.
Q16risk / occ health

According to the hierarchy of controls, which of the following is the MOST effective strategy for preventing zoonotic disease in a shelter?

B is correct. The hierarchy of controls ranks elimination and substitution as the most effective strategies because they prevent the hazard from existing. PPE (option A) is the LEAST effective level in the hierarchy, as it relies on correct and consistent use. Engineering controls and administrative controls are intermediate. In practice, shelters use all levels simultaneously, but the question tests the hierarchy concept.
Q17risk / occ health

A shelter volunteer discloses that she is 14 weeks pregnant. What is the MOST appropriate action?

B is correct. Complete exclusion (option A) is neither evidence-based nor legally appropriate in most contexts. Doing nothing (option C) fails the duty of care. The correct approach is individualized risk counseling, identification of highest-risk tasks (assisting parturient animals, handling litter without gloves, working with diarrheic animals), role modifications where reasonable, and ensuring proper PPE. This reflects both the ASV occupational health framework and OSHA general duty of care principles.
Q18risk / occ health

Which single hand hygiene measure is most effective against the broadest range of zoonotic pathogens in a shelter setting, including non-enveloped viruses and protozoan oocysts?

B is correct. Soap and water hand washing is effective against the broadest spectrum including oocysts (Cryptosporidium, Toxoplasma, Toxocara eggs) and non-enveloped viruses (parvovirus, calicivirus) for which alcohol-based sanitizers have limited or no activity. Alcohol-based sanitizers are adequate for enveloped pathogens (Salmonella, most bacteria) but should not be used as a substitute for soap and water when oocyst or non-enveloped pathogen exposure is possible. Chlorhexidine gluconate is effective against bacteria but has limited activity against oocysts and non-enveloped viruses.
Q19one health

Approximately what percentage of known human infectious diseases are zoonotic in origin, according to WHO estimates?

C is correct. WHO estimates that over 60% of known human infectious diseases are zoonotic, and approximately 75% of emerging human pathogens have animal origins. This statistic is the core epidemiological justification for the One Health approach — human and animal health cannot be effectively managed in isolation. It is a foundational ABVP exam fact for this domain.
Q20one health

From a One Health perspective, which of the following shelter activities MOST directly contributes to reducing zoonotic disease risk at the community population level?

B is correct. Systematic deworming reduces the burden of Toxocara, hookworm, and other parasites that can be shed into the environment long after adoption. Flea prevention at adoption reduces Bartonella transmission in the community. These interventions have population-level impact beyond the individual animal. Options A, C, and D are important for individual occupational protection but do not have the same community-scale One Health impact. The ABVP exam tests this distinction between individual-animal and population-level thinking.
