Asilomar Accords

A landmark 2004 agreement among approximately 20 shelter leaders establishing a shared categorization language to eliminate euthanasia of healthy and treatable animals and allow cross-shelter data comparison.

Population Management

Speed

Historical context Maddie's Fund 2004

  • Developed in 2004 at a summit in Pacific Grove, California by approximately 20 shelter leaders.
  • Goal: build alignment across disparate welfare philosophies and create a shared language for comparing outcomes.
  • Success is tracked by monitoring the percentages of live release versus euthanasia within each of the four categories over time.
  • Not a legal mandate; a voluntary framework adopted by participating organizations to enable meaningful national data comparison.

The four categories Asilomar / Maddie's Fund

CategoryDefinitionKey nuance for the exam
Healthy Vaccinated against core diseases, no sign of illness or injury that compromises quality of life, behaving appropriately for age and species. Requires age at or above 8 weeks. An animal under 8 weeks cannot be classified as Healthy regardless of appearance.
Treatable: Rehabilitable Condition is treatable with medical or behavioral intervention; good prognosis if treatment is provided within a reasonable timeframe. Classification is based on community standard, NOT shelter resources. A shelter being unable to treat the condition does not make it Untreatable.
Treatable: Manageable Requires ongoing management (chronic condition or behavioral issue) but quality of life is acceptable; a committed owner could manage it long-term. Same community-standard basis. Shelter budget and staffing are irrelevant to this classification.
Unhealthy and Untreatable Suffering that cannot be alleviated within community-standard resources, or behavioral risk to public safety that cannot be sufficiently mitigated. Does NOT mean the animal is unsavable by any organization. It means care is beyond what a community-standard caring owner can realistically provide.
Correction: Age cutoff for "Healthy" The Asilomar framework explicitly requires age at or above 8 weeks for the Healthy category. Your ONENOTE notes do not specify this cutoff. Animals under 8 weeks must be classified separately even if they appear clinically healthy. Source: Maddie's Fund Guide to the Asilomar Accords Definitions.
Correction: "Treatable" categories are resource-independent Your notes suggest the Treatable designation depends on the individual shelter's resources. This is incorrect. Treatability is determined by what a reasonable, caring owner within that community can provide, not by what the specific shelter can offer. A shelter's limited budget cannot reclassify a treatable animal as untreatable. Source: Maddie's Fund Guide to the Asilomar Accords Definitions.
Correction: "Unhealthy and Untreatable" does not mean unsavable This category does not mean the animal cannot be saved by any organization. It means the level of care required exceeds what a community-standard caring owner can reasonably provide. A specialty rescue with greater resources could still save such an animal. Source: Maddie's Fund Guide to the Asilomar Accords Definitions.
Exam Trap: Community-referenced, not shelter-referenced The Asilomar matrix is pegged to the community, not to any individual shelter. The same clinical condition may yield different categories in different communities depending on what caring owners there can realistically provide. Two shelters in the same city should produce the same classification for the same animal.

How shelters use Asilomar data ASV 2022

  • Track the percentage of live outcomes and euthanasia within each category separately over time.
  • Healthy and Treatable animals that are euthanized represent unmet goals; Unhealthy and Untreatable euthanasia is an expected and appropriate outcome.
  • Shelters increasingly aim for zero euthanasia in the Healthy and Treatable categories, with euthanasia limited to Unhealthy and Untreatable cases plus public safety.
  • Data is reported to Shelter Animals Count (SAC) using standardized Asilomar-aligned intake and outcome categories.

Capacity for Care (C4C)

C4C defines the maximum number and specific types of animals an organization can care for humanely at any given time. Operating within it is a legal and ethical obligation, not a target.

Definition and components ASV 2022

  • C4C is dynamically bounded by five factors: financial resources, physical facility assets, personnel hours and skills, housing space, and availability of live outcomes.
  • Operating beyond C4C is explicitly defined as an unacceptable practice. It is not an emergency option or a temporary measure.
  • Operating within C4C is scientifically proven to decrease LOS, reduce disease rates, lower euthanasia, and maximize live releases.

Humane Housing Units (HHUs) ASV 2022

  • HHUs are the foundational determinant of C4C. They include in-shelter cages, foster placements, and off-site housing with rescue partners.
  • Enclosures that are too small, structurally inappropriate, or that cannot meet basic welfare standards cannot be counted toward C4C, regardless of how many physical cages exist.
  • A shelter with 200 physical cages may have a true C4C of 120 if 80 cages are undersized or non-compliant.
Exam Trap: Raw cage count is not C4C Always distinguish between the number of physical cages and the number of Humane Housing Units. Only HHUs that meet welfare standards count. Non-compliant units must be excluded even if they are currently housing animals.

Staffing as a C4C determinant ONENOTE notes

  • NACA minimum: 15 minutes per animal per day for basic care (feeding and cleaning only). This is a floor, not a welfare target.
  • If total required care minutes exceed available staff minutes, the shelter has exceeded its staffing C4C regardless of physical space.
  • Animals with medical or behavioral needs require more than 15 minutes; staffing capacity must account for these additions.
Exam Trap: C4C has multiple ceilings A shelter can exceed C4C in staffing (not enough people) while still having empty cages, or in housing (no available HHUs) while having enough staff. All five components must be assessed simultaneously.

Strategic response to chronic C4C strain ASV 2022

  • Organizations that frequently operate near or above C4C must initiate a structured strategic planning process to align capacity with community needs long-term.
  • Short-term responses include transport, emergency foster appeals, intake holds, and rescue partnerships.
  • Compromising animal care standards is never an acceptable short-term response.

Track System and Flow-Through

Managing animals as two distinct populations with separate operational metrics, housing standards, and outcome timelines is essential for maintaining both welfare and throughput.

Exam Trap: Slow track full = no new slow-track assignments When slow-track units are 100% full, no additional animals may enter slow track until a slot opens via adoption, transfer, or another outcome. The correct response is to work on accelerating slow-track exits, not to double-house or compromise standards.

Open Selection ONENOTE notes

  • Allows prospective adopters or rescue partners to view, select, and begin paperwork for an animal during its mandatory stray-hold period.
  • Prospective adopters are explicitly informed that an owner reclaim could still occur before the hold expires.
  • Benefit: converts mandatory waiting time into productive live-release preparation. Minimizes LOS and population size simultaneously without bypassing legal protections for the owner.

Ways to reduce Length of Stay (LOS) ASV 2022

Approved strategies

  • Eliminate unproductive holds: e.g., no hold for puppies or kittens awaiting a second vaccine if the first was given at intake.
  • Open selection during mandatory stray-hold periods.
  • Field reunification: scan for microchip in the field and return lost pets directly to owners without shelter admission.
  • Foster care: removes animals from the in-shelter population while they await adoption or recover from illness/surgery.
  • Return-to-Field (RTF): for healthy unowned community cats. Minimizes space-related euthanasia and preserves intake capacity for animals that truly need sheltering.
  • Transport and transfer partnerships with regional rescue organizations.
  • Off-site adoption events, expanded adoption hours, and foster-to-adopt programs.
  • Remove discriminatory barriers: strict income verification, mandatory home visits, and breed bans prolong LOS without improving outcomes. Exclusionary policies are discriminatory and counterproductive.

Community cats: RTF vs. TNR ONENOTE notes

  • Return-to-Field (RTF): healthy unowned community cats are admitted to the shelter briefly for sterilization and vaccination, then returned to their outdoor location. RTF cats ARE counted in the SAC BasicDataMatrix (they enter the shelter's system).
  • Trap-Neuter-Return (TNR): cats are sterilized and vaccinated by community organizations without entering the shelter. TNR cats are NOT counted in the shelter's BasicDataMatrix intake (only logged in the Community Services Matrix).
Correction: RTF cats are admitted; TNR cats are not Your notes conflate RTF and TNR. RTF cats are admitted and counted in the shelter's intake statistics. TNR cats are not admitted; they are only tracked in the Community Services Matrix. This distinction matters for live release rate (LRR) calculations. Source: SAC BasicDataMatrix 2021.

Population Formulas

Five calculation frameworks are essential for the ABVP boards. Know each formula by name, variables, and a worked example. All formulas derive from the core relationship: population = admissions x ALOS.

1 of 5 : Average Daily Population
Avg Daily Population = Avg Daily Admissions x ALOS
Core insight: this is the master relationship. Reducing ALOS or reducing admissions (or both) is the only way to sustainably reduce daily population size. Example: 10 admissions/day x 12-day ALOS = 120 animals in the building at any given time.
2 of 5 : Required Holding Capacity (RHC)
RHC = MDA Intake x Required Holding Period
MDA = Monthly Daily Average. Example: MDA intake of 15 dogs/day x 4-day legal hold = RHC of 60 stray-hold units. Must account for mandatory legal holds, rabies quarantine periods, and age-based restrictions.
3 of 5 : Adoption-Driven Capacity (ADC)
ADC = Target ALOS x MDA Adoptions
Tells you how many adoption-floor spaces you need to sustain your target adoption rate. Example: target ALOS of 5 days x MDA adoptions of 4/day = ADC of 20 adoption-floor units needed.
4 of 5 : Staffing Hours Required for Basic Daily Care
Staff Hours = (Minutes Per Animal x Avg Daily Population) / 60
Baseline: 15 min/animal/day for feeding and cleaning. Example: 80 dogs x 15 min / 60 = 20 staff hours required per day for basic care alone. Animals with medical/behavioral needs require more.
5 of 5 : Staff Capacity for Daily Care (SCDC)
SCDC = Total Daily Staff Care Minutes / Minutes Required Per Animal
The staffing ceiling. Example: 2,400 staff minutes available / 15 min per animal = SCDC of 160 animals. If your census exceeds 160, welfare is compromised regardless of physical cage availability.

Formula relationships and exam applications ASV 2022

Question typeFormula to useWhat it tells you

"How many adoption-floor spaces do we need?"ADC = Target ALOS x MDA AdoptionsAdoption floor sizing for a given throughput target

Exam Trap: 15 minutes is a floor, not a target The 15 min/animal/day NACA standard covers feeding and cleaning only. It is the minimum to determine staffing capacity, not a recommendation for adequate welfare. Animals with medical or behavioral needs require substantially more daily staff time. Citing 15 minutes as "adequate care" would be incorrect on the exam.

Worked calculation examples ONENOTE notes

Example 1: RHC calculation

A municipal shelter has a 4-day legal stray hold. Their MDA stray intake is 15 dogs/day. How many stray-hold housing units are required?

RHC = 15 x 4 = 60 housing units

Example 2: Staffing capacity

A shelter has 2,400 minutes of total staff time available for basic animal care each day. The NACA standard is 15 min/animal/day. What is the SCDC?

SCDC = 2,400 / 15 = 160 animals

Example 3: Impact of reducing ALOS

A shelter admits 10 dogs/day and has a current ALOS of 12 days (daily population = 120). If ALOS is reduced to 6 days, what happens to daily population?

New Pop = 10 x 6 = 60 animals (50% reduction)

Rounds and Welfare

Population rounds, medical rounds, and protocols for vulnerable populations form the daily operational backbone of welfare-compliant population management.

Population Rounds vs. Medical Rounds ONENOTE notesASV 2022

TypePurposeTeamFrequency
Population Rounds Systematic review of every animal to keep the broad management plan on track; identifies when and what care is needed Cross-functional: intake, medical, behavior, husbandry, outcomes. ALL departments. Daily
Medical Rounds Physically deliver the care identified in population rounds; individual clinical follow-up and treatment Veterinary team and vet techs As clinically indicated

Three questions answered for every animal in population rounds

  • How are you doing? Current behavioral and physical status; welfare assessment; eating, drinking, stress indicators.
  • What is your pathway, and are there any updates or concerns? Fast vs. slow track assignment; medical flags; behavioral progress notes; foster or transport candidacy.
  • What are your next steps? Upcoming procedures, adoption events, transport dates, foster placements, or escalation plans.

Any welfare-compromising or stay-extending need identified during rounds must be addressed promptly. Deferral to the next day is not acceptable.

Exam Trap: SAC pediatric age cutoff is under 5 months Your ONENOTE notes use "4 to 5 months" as the pediatric cutoff. The SAC BasicDataMatrix defines the cutoff as strictly under 5 months. Use "under 5 months" when answering data and statistics questions about animal age categories.

Isolation vs. Quarantine ONENOTE notes

AreaWho goes hereCurrent health statusKey requirements
Isolation Animals with confirmed or suspected active infection; animals showing clinical signs of disease Currently SICK Separate air handling; dedicated PPE; staff visit last; segregated by species and pathogen where possible

Exam Trap: Quarantine is NOT for sick animals Quarantine is for apparently healthy animals with exposure risk. A dog exposed to a confirmed parvo case goes to quarantine. A dog showing vomiting and hemorrhagic diarrhea goes to isolation. A newly admitted stray with no known exposure goes to standard intake holding.

Problem-oriented approach to intake ONENOTE notes

Correct sequence

  1. Data collection: history (owner, records), physical examination, behavioral observation
  2. Problem identification: list active and potential problems from the data
  3. Plan formulation: diagnostics, initial therapy, pathway assignment
  4. Assessment and follow-up: monitor, adjust plan, update rounds team

Geriatric minimum database at intake PE

  • PCV/TP: packed cell volume and total protein to screen for anemia and protein abnormalities
  • Urine specific gravity (USG): evaluates renal concentrating ability
  • Urine dipstick: screens for glucosuria, proteinuria, and other abnormalities

Outcomes and Planning

Population management begins before an animal enters the building and continues through every stage of its stay. Both admission and outcome sides of the equation must be actively managed.

Admission planning ONENOTE notes

Two intake pathway types

  • Controlled/predictable pathway: owner surrenders by appointment, scheduled transports, pre-arranged transfers. Can be managed proactively by adjusting appointment availability to match C4C.
  • Uncontrolled but predictable pathway: strays, animal control intakes, cruelty seizures. Cannot be refused but can be anticipated statistically. Requires ongoing evaluation to monitor impact on total capacity.

Population management begins before admission: an animal should only be admitted if the shelter has the capacity and resources to provide the specific care that animal requires.

Live release rate (LRR) calculation methods ASV 2022

Three distinct methods exist. Knowing which method a shelter uses is critical for comparing data between organizations.

MethodFormulaNote
1. StandardLive outcomes / Total live intakesMost common; excludes animals that died or were owner-intended euthanasia at intake
2. Outcomes-basedLive outcomes / (Total outcomes excluding current residents)Denominator excludes animals still in care; measures what happens to animals that leave
3. Net intake(Intakes minus euthanasia) / Total intakesMeasures what proportion of intakes result in a live outcome from the intake side
Correction: Three distinct LRR calculation methods Your ONENOTE notes present LRR as a single calculation. The ASV Shelter Terminology (2017) documents three distinct methods. Because shelters can use any of the three, reported LRR values are not directly comparable across organizations unless you know which method was used. Source: ASV Shelter Terminology 2017.

Cross-reference: The three-method LRR table also appears in the Data Analysis hub (Subdomain 5 of 7) from a data validation and cross-shelter comparison angle.

SAC intake categories SAC 2021

  • Stray/at large: animals found or brought in as strays from the community
  • Owner surrender: animals whose owners voluntarily relinquish them
  • Owner-Intended Euthanasia (OIE): owners bring animals specifically to be euthanized. This is a distinct intake category, not bundled under owner surrender.
  • Transfer in: animals received from another organization
  • Return: previously adopted animals returned by the adopter
  • Born in care: animals born to animals already in the shelter's care
Correction: OIE is a distinct intake type Owner-Intended Euthanasia (OIE) is a separate intake category in the SAC BasicDataMatrix, not a subset of owner surrender. Conflating OIE with owner surrender inflates the owner surrender count and distorts intake and outcome statistics. Source: SAC BasicDataMatrix 2021.

Euthanasia within a wellness framework ASV 2022

  • Euthanasia must be considered when animal welfare cannot be managed due to physical or behavioral disease, or due to overcrowding, and when no other remedies exist to relieve suffering.
  • Euthanasia is never an acceptable substitute for providing basic daily care or for managing overcrowding that could be addressed by reducing intake or increasing throughput.
  • Euthanasia for space constraints alone is appropriate only when C4C is genuinely exceeded and all other options (transport, foster, intake holds) have been exhausted.

Strategic planning and the long view ASV 2022

  • Organizations that frequently operate near or above C4C must initiate a structured strategic planning process to align capacity with community needs.
  • Strategic plans should be reviewed quarterly to monitor progress and maintain relevance.
  • Short-term responses to acute C4C strain: emergency transport, foster appeals, intake appointments, rescue partnerships. Never reduce care standards.

Foster Care

Foster care extends sheltering capacity into community homes, improves welfare outcomes, and is essential for neonatal animals. Key exam areas: planning formulas, pathway types, disease control, caregiver requirements.

Benefits, Goals and Costs ONENOTE

Core benefits: Safe environment during active immunity development (pups/kittens), socialization and enrichment opportunities, rehabilitation potential, reduces physical shelter housing demand, saves daily care costs per animal.

Common program goals: increase life-saving capacity, improve individual welfare, increase C4C, reduce shelter LOS, increase adoption potential, maximize existing resources.

Program costs and risks to acknowledge: Legal agreement overhead, carer communication and training burden, risk of caregiver welfare failures, pressure toward positive outcome regardless of animal's interest, zoonotic and infectious disease risk to household members and resident pets.

Foster Home Calculation Formulas ONENOTE

Monthly foster homes needed
Winter: monthly kitten intake divided by 2
Peak season: monthly kitten intake divided by 3
If average LOS in foster is long, you need more homes than the formula suggests. Always recruit more than expected need.
Census audit formula
Feb 1 count = Jan 1 count + animals sent to foster in January minus animals returned from foster in January
Exam Trap: Peak season uses the smaller divisor (3), not the larger one. More kittens coming in means each home can absorb a smaller litter (divided into smaller groups), so more homes are needed per intake unit. Always recruit above calculated need to buffer for attrition.

Disease Control and Infectious Risk ONENOTE

Accumulated infectious risk

Risk = n (number of animals ever in the home, not current census). Risk level is retained after animals leave; it does not reset when animals are adopted or returned. Periodically emptying homes completely breaks the accumulation cycle.

Example: 1 resident cat + 4 kittens = risk 5. Four kittens adopted, 1 remains = still risk 5. One new kitten added = risk 6.
Exam Trap: Accumulated infectious risk does NOT reset when animals leave. The number only increases with each new animal placed, regardless of how many have already been adopted out.

Family unit concept

Animals with similar prior exposures and infectious potential who can safely share space. Family units are unlikely to transmit new diseases among themselves unless a new pathogen is introduced. Units should consist of animals who can comfortably share space without behavioral conflict. Housing multiple unrelated family units together substantially increases disease transmission risk.

Pre-placement screening

  • Physical examination, vaccination, behavioral assessment.
  • Infectious disease screening: CPV, FPV, coccidia, ringworm (prior to placement).
  • Zoonotic disease assessment: scabies, ringworm, rabies status confirmed.
  • Fecal exam; broad-spectrum dewormer at 2 to 3 weeks of age, then repeat every 2 weeks until 2 months old, then monthly.
  • Coccidia: ponazuril at 2 to 3 weeks, retreat 7 to 14 days later.
  • FeLV/FIV: inform foster carer of the possibility of exposure. Resident cats must be FeLV/FIV negative.
  • Heartworm testing in cats: limitations apply. A negative result does NOT rule out infection.

Routine care schedule

  • Puppies and kittens: exam, weigh, deworm, and revaccinate (DHPP/FVRCP) every 2 weeks until 18 to 20 weeks of age.
  • Rabies vaccination at 3 months (12 weeks).
  • Recheck appointments assigned to staff or volunteers not in frequent contact with the main shelter population.

Foster home environment requirements

  • Young animals placed in easily disinfectable areas for the first 2 weeks (calibrated to incubation periods of core pathogens).
  • Households with personal puppies or kittens under 20 weeks should NOT foster, due to incomplete antibody protection.
  • Resident cats must be FeLV/FIV negative before fostering cats.
  • Resident pets must be current on core vaccines: FVRCP or DHPP, rabies; parasite prevention maintained.
Exam Trap: The 2-week quarantine period in a disinfectable area is not arbitrary. It maps to the incubation window of core shelter pathogens. Households with unvaccinated or incompletely vaccinated pets under 20 weeks are ineligible as foster homes.

Socialization ONENOTE

  • Puppies and kittens under 4 months require daily handling and positive exposure to novel stimuli during the socialization window.
  • Without adequate socialization, animals may develop chronic fear, anxiety, and inability to adjust to normal stimuli.
  • Singletons benefit from pairing with another well-screened singleton; the behavioral benefit generally balances the infectious risk.
  • Adding a singleton to an already healthy litter generally does NOT balance well (infectious risk outweighs behavioral gain for the singleton).

Caregiver Requirements and Program Management ONENOTE

Legal and labor requirements

  • Minimum age 18 years for a legally binding foster agreement. Younger individuals may participate with an adult as primary carer.
  • Shelter staff fostering their employer's animals at home creates state labor law (overtime) risks. This arrangement has resulted in regulatory fines; it should be avoided or carefully reviewed with legal counsel.
  • Police background checks are a common but not universal regulatory screening requirement.
Exam Trap: Staff fostering is legally distinct from volunteer fostering. State labor laws around hours of care, compensation, and overtime have been triggered by staff fostering arrangements. This is a recognized compliance risk.

Foster coordinator competencies

  • Knowledgeable about shelter operations and policies.
  • Training and experience to guide caregivers through medical and behavioral cases.
  • Working knowledge of animal health and behavior.
  • Meticulous record keeping and strong communication skills.

Caregiver training and level systems

  • Training content: basic animal care and behavior, zoonotic disease risks, disease transmission and reduction strategies, home preparation, record keeping, sanitation protocols, problem recognition.
  • Resources: foster manual, in-person orientation sessions, monitoring sheets for appetite, elimination, growth, and behavior.
  • Level systems: new carers start with straightforward animals (e.g., 6 to 8 week healthy kittens) and progress to medical, post-surgical, behavioral, elderly, or neonatal cases.
  • Mentorship programs pair experienced and new foster carers.
  • Best retention: match skills to case complexity, provide rest breaks, and create growth opportunities.

Monitoring and data collection

  • Monitor: weight, appetite, elimination, signs of illness, behavior changes.
  • Online monitoring systems recommended to track status in real time.
  • Minimum mandatory data points: reason for foster, age, date placed, date returned, and outcome.
  • Track: average LOS waiting for foster pick-up, average LOS in foster, current census, number of active carers.
Exam Trap: Home visits after placement can deter applicants and are labor intensive. The environment often changes after the visit, limiting its predictive value. Risk-stratified or targeted visits are preferred over mandatory universal visits.
