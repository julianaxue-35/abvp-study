Disease Surveillance

Routine disease surveillance transforms clinical observations into quantitative data that drives decisions, detects outbreaks early, and justifies resource allocation.

Formal Definition ONENOTE

Disease surveillance is defined as two inseparable components:

  • The ongoing, systematic collection, orderly consolidation, analysis, and interpretation of health-related data in a population.
  • The prompt dissemination of this information to the people who are in a position to act on it.
Exam Trap Both components are required. A system that collects and analyzes data but does not promptly communicate findings to decision-makers does not fully satisfy the definition of disease surveillance.

Why Disease Surveillance Matters ONENOTE

  • Assess population health in quantitative terms.
  • Set objective and measurable disease-related priorities.
  • Identify outbreaks before they escalate.
  • Plan and monitor the effectiveness of preventive and control measures.
  • Rapidly identify new diseases or conditions entering the population.
  • Justify grant proposals and funding applications with data.
  • Clearly communicate population health status to constituencies (board, donors, regulators).

Even when disease incidence is stable, quantifying endemic disease levels facilitates discussions about which diseases deserve priority attention and resource allocation.

Surveillance Implementation: Steps 1 to 8 ONENOTE

StepActionKey Detail
1 Create a disease monitoring list Short and manageable. Focus on common diseases, particularly problematic ones, and those tied to shelter medical goals. Prioritize data likely to influence thinking and action.
2 Calculate baseline (endemic) frequencies Establish the usual incidence of each monitored disease to provide a reference level for detecting deviations.
3 Use an annual medical profile Standard tool to summarize yearly occurrence of all monitored diseases and track longitudinal trends.
4 Monitor high-incidence diseases more frequently High-incidence diseases (e.g., feline URI) require at least monthly monitoring. Active outbreaks trigger even more frequent review.
5 Write disease descriptions and train staff Clear, written descriptions of each monitored disease. Staff must be trained to recognize signs and report promptly.
6 Count only first episode per timeframe For diseases that can recur in the same animal, only the first episode during a specified monitoring period counts as a new (incident) case.
7 Collect denominators and numerators separately Intake data provides denominators (animals at risk). Medical data provides numerators (cases). Movement, outcomes, and daily observations supplement for additional analyses.
8 Regular review of collected data Monthly reviews maintain staff engagement and data quality. They allow timely notification of health changes to shelter administration.

Requirements for an Effective Surveillance Program ONENOTE

  • Good individual animal identification system.
  • A functioning medical records system.
  • Staff understanding of why surveillance matters.
  • Agreement on which diseases to include.
  • Clear, standardized definitions of each monitored disease.
  • Prompt disease reporting and incentives to encourage reporting.
  • A management plan for affected animals when disease is identified.

Population Health Snapshot: What to Report ONENOTE

Shelters should regularly provide a snapshot of population disease status including:

  • Incidence and prevalence of major infectious diseases in the facility.
  • Mortality due to natural causes.
  • Number and percentage of animals euthanized for medical reasons.

Basic Metrics

Four core epidemiological metrics underpin shelter disease monitoring. Confusing incidence with prevalence is one of the most common exam errors in this domain.

Core Definitions ONENOTE

MetricDefinitionExam Pearl
Morbidity Frequency of illness within the population (e.g., incidence rate of disease). Morbidity = illness frequency. Distinct from mortality (death).
Mortality Frequency of death due to natural causes. Natural deaths only. Euthanasia is a separate, managed outcome category. Both tracked in the population health snapshot.
Incidence rate Number of newly diagnosed cases of a disease divided by the number of animals that could develop that disease during a specified period. New cases only. Denominator = susceptible population at risk during the period. A RATE over TIME.
Prevalence Percentage of cases of a disease present in a population at a single given point in time. A snapshot. Includes both new and existing active cases. A PROPORTION at a POINT IN TIME.
Exam Trap A shelter census on October 1st finds 15 of 100 cats with URI signs today: this is 15% PREVALENCE (point-in-time snapshot), not incidence. Incidence requires tracking new cases over a defined time period in the susceptible population.

Recurring Disease: Counting Rule ONENOTE

For diseases that can occur more than once in the same animal, only the first episode during a particular timeframe is counted as a new or incident case. Subsequent episodes in the same animal within the same monitoring period are excluded from the incidence numerator.

Exam Trap A dog recovers from URI in week 1 then develops URI again in week 3 of the same monitoring period. The second episode does NOT count toward incidence. Counting it would inflate the rate and misrepresent true new disease burden in the population.

Three-Axis Analysis of Disease Frequency ONENOTE

A sudden increase in disease frequency almost always signals a breakdown in health-management protocols or signals the need for new ones. Analyze disease frequency across three axes to generate actionable hypotheses:

AxisExamplesGenerates Hypotheses About
Host Kittens vs. adults; vaccinated vs. unvaccinated; intake source Which subpopulations are most susceptible and why
Location Holding ward vs. adoption floor; isolation vs. general population Spatial transmission routes, environmental contamination, housing design issues
Time Spring vs. fall; kitten season; post-adoption event; after staffing change Seasonal patterns, event-linked causes, procedural changes that preceded the increase

Analyzing all three axes together helps explain the cause of increased frequency, the precipitating event, and factors sustaining transmission in the shelter.

Data Integrity Rules ONENOTE

  • Denominators come from shelter intake data (animals at risk of the disease).
  • Numerators come from medical data (animals diagnosed with the disease).
  • If both clinical signs AND definitive diagnoses are collected, they must be kept in separate data fields to prevent double-counting a single disease episode.
  • The primary cause of surveillance data failure is failure to reach a definitive diagnosis: undiagnosed cases cannot be classified or counted in the system.
Exam Trap Cross-shelter comparisons of crude, unadjusted incidence rates can be unfair and epidemiologically misleading. Baseline circumstances that cannot be changed between shelters (intake profile, geography, facility design, species mix) may be entirely different, confounding any raw comparison.

Length of Stay and ALOS

LOS is simultaneously a welfare indicator and a capacity management tool. ALOS drives the housing capacity formulas and reveals operational bottlenecks when stratified by subgroup.

Consequences of Increasing LOS ONENOTE

  • Increases disease risk through prolonged pathogen exposure.
  • Amplifies animal stress (boredom, frustration, behavioral deterioration).
  • Leads directly to overcrowding as incoming animals fill housing occupied by long-stay residents.

Reducing ALOS is one of the highest-leverage interventions available: it simultaneously improves welfare, decreases disease risk, and increases intake capacity without adding a single housing unit.

ALOS Definition and Formula ONENOTE

Average Length of Stay (ALOS) measures the average speed with which animals move through the shelter.

ALOS Formula
ALOS = sum of LOS of all animals of interest / total number of animals contributing to LOS in the period
Specify the period: month, season, or year. Calculate separately for dogs and cats, and for relevant subgroups to isolate bottlenecks.

Uses of ALOS Data ONENOTE

  • Identify patterns in animal transit speed through the facility.
  • Set LOS reduction goals and gauge progress toward them.
  • Reveal associations between elevated ALOS and disease risk in specific areas or subgroups.
  • Identify which subgroups, procedures, or shelter areas disproportionately slow animal transit.
  • Focus operational efforts on the specific impediments causing delays.

Factors to Stratify ALOS By

FactorOperational Insight
Source of animal (seized vs. stray vs. owner surrender)Court-hold seizures often have very long ALOS driven by legal hold periods, which should be tracked separately from voluntary intakes
General health status (healthy vs. sick)Sick animals with long LOS identify where medical throughput is a bottleneck
Breed typeCertain breeds (large dogs, brachycephalics) may have consistently longer LOS requiring targeted marketing strategies
Seasonal intakePeak-season intakes may have different flow dynamics than off-season animals
Volunteer availabilityPeriods with reduced volunteer presence may correlate with longer LOS due to fewer offsite events, reduced socialization, and less exercise
Exam Trap When a specific subgroup (e.g., court-ordered custody animals) has ALOS of 45 days vs. 5 days for owner surrenders, the correct response is to identify and address the specific procedural or legal impediments causing that delay, not to stop accepting animals from that category.

Capacity for Care Formulas

Two housing capacity formulas quantify the relationship between housing units, ALOS, and sustainable intake. The NACA 15-minute standard is the parallel measure for staffing capacity.

C4C Definition ONENOTE

Capacity for Care (C4C) is the ability of a shelter to comprehensively meet the physical, medical, and behavioral needs of its resident animals. Adverse conditions result when:

  • A shelter houses more animals than it can humanely accommodate (housing C4C exceeded).
  • A shelter has too few staff to provide adequate basic daily care (staffing C4C exceeded).

Housing Capacity Formulas ONENOTE

Intake Capacity (total new animals that can be housed in a defined period)
Intake = (number of humane housing spaces x number of days) / ALOS
Use when you know your housing count and ALOS and want to calculate total intake capacity over a period (e.g., a 30-day month).
Daily Admissions Target
Daily admissions = number of humane housing units / ALOS (days)
Use to set a daily admission cap that avoids exceeding housing C4C. This is the sustainable daily intake rate.
Three Uses of These Formulas
  1. What ALOS is required to avoid overcrowding, given a fixed intake volume and housing count?
  2. How many animals can be admitted in a specific period, given available housing units and known ALOS?
  3. What is the predicted effect of reducing ALOS on intake capacity? (Halving ALOS doubles capacity with the same housing.)
Worked Example A shelter has 60 dog housing units and ALOS of 6 days. Daily admissions target = 60 / 6 = 10 dogs per day. Monthly intake capacity = (60 x 30) / 6 = 300 dogs. If ALOS is halved to 3 days: daily target doubles to 20 dogs, and monthly capacity doubles to 600, without adding a single cage.

Staffing Capacity: NACA 15-Minute Standard ONENOTE

Inadequate staffing leads directly to biosecurity breaches, increased infectious agent exposure, and rising disease frequency.

NACA Minimum Standard
15 minutes per animal per day for basic care
Basic care = feeding AND cleaning ONLY. This is a minimum floor, not a target. Shelters should strive to increase staff time per animal beyond this minimum.

Staffing capacity audit: compare these three steps:

StepAction
1Estimate total time staff SHOULD spend in basic animal care daily (current census x minimum 15 minutes per animal).
2Calculate actual hours staff ARE engaged in basic care daily.
3Compare the two. The gap reveals the staffing shortfall.
Exam Trap The 15-minute NACA standard covers only feeding and cleaning. It is explicitly a MINIMUM, not a sufficient level of care. A shelter that meets this standard is meeting only the bare survival floor. Medical care, enrichment, behavioral assessment, training, and socialization all require additional staff time above this minimum.

Data Collection, Analysis and Communication

Data quality depends on written protocols, staff accountability, and regular structured review. Poor data is not neutral: it wastes resources and produces misleading analyses that can harm population health outcomes.

Written Protocols Requirement ONENOTE

An effective surveillance program must be valued, planned, and well-executed. Written protocols must specify:

  • What data is collected (which metrics, which diseases, which fields).
  • Where each data element is recorded.
  • When collection and review events occur.
  • By whom each task is performed.

Everyone involved in data collection must be trained and held accountable for providing accurate, complete information.

Stratified Incidence Rates ONENOTE

Separate incidence rates must be calculated for different subgroups: by age, source, and time period at minimum. A single facility-wide incidence rate obscures subgroup variation and prevents targeted intervention.

Exam Trap A low overall incidence rate can coexist with a very high rate in a specific high-risk subgroup (e.g., neonatal kittens in one ward). Reporting only the aggregate figure hides the signal. Stratify to find actionable patterns.

Review Cadence and Monitoring Frequency ONENOTE

  • Monthly data reviews maintain staff interest in surveillance and attention to data quality.
  • Monthly reviews allow timely notification of health changes to shelter administration and other staff.
  • Monitoring frequency is related to disease incidence, severity, and whether the shelter has specific goals tied to that disease.
  • High-incidence diseases such as feline URI (FURI) require at minimum monthly monitoring.
  • Active disease outbreaks trigger more frequent monitoring than the baseline schedule.
  • Some analyses can be prespecified (quarterly reports, annual medical profile); others arise as the data generates new questions.

Data Quality and Staff Understanding ONENOTE

When staff understand the rationale for and importance of complete, accurate data collection, data quality rises. Resources are wasted collecting poor-quality data, and analyses of that data can be actively misleading.

Staff who work directly with animals often have valuable insight into why disease is manifesting as it is (e.g., a new cleaning protocol, a housing configuration change, a period of low volunteer attendance). Interpretation requires their input alongside the numbers.

Live Release Rate: Three Calculation Methods ASV Terminology 2017

Key Fact: No Single LRR Formula The ASV Shelter Terminology 2017 document describes three distinct LRR calculation methods. Each produces a different numeric result from the same dataset. Knowing which method a shelter uses is essential when comparing rates across organizations.
MethodFormulaNotes
1 Live outcomes / total live intakes Intake-based rate. Can be affected by how OIE is categorized in the denominator.
2 Live outcomes / total outcomes (excluding current residents) Disposition-based rate. Excludes animals still in care from denominator.
3 (Total intakes minus euthanasia) / total intakes Includes all euthanasia in the numerator adjustment. Often used to track shelter reform progress.
Exam Trap Comparing LRR figures between shelters or across time periods is only valid if the same calculation method was used. A shelter reporting 90% LRR by method 2 and another reporting 85% by method 1 cannot be directly compared without knowing their intake profiles and calculation methods.
