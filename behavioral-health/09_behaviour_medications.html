Principles and When to Treat

Before reaching for a drug, the shelter veterinarian makes a behavioural diagnosis and plans the whole package: medication is never a substitute for behaviour modification, enrichment, and good housing.

Pre-drug checklist

  • Make a behavioural diagnosis; know the indications, doses, side effects, and contraindications of the likely drugs. your notes
  • Prescribe a concurrent behaviour modification (BMOD) program and train staff to administer therapy. your notes
  • Plan to monitor for effect and side effects (inappetence, vomiting, diarrhoea, ataxia, sedation, increased aggression, serotonin syndrome). your notes
  • Account for expense, and plan adoption and follow-up. your notes
ASV: medication is never used in isolation Psychotropic medication must always be paired with a structured BMOD program, enrichment, and environmental modification. Prescribing drugs as a standalone fix for poor design or missing enrichment does not meet welfare standards. ASV

When drug therapy should be considered

  • When wellbeing is compromised by shelter life and enrichment (exercise, companionship, training) has not improved the animal's state. your notes
  • Anxious or fearful animals still hiding or trembling after 2 to 3 days and not eating, eliminating, or grooming, or that are overactive or compulsive. your notes
  • Dogs with separation-related problems (SRP) do not show signs in the shelter but still benefit from a behaviour program plus drug therapy. Urine marking is also reduced. your notes

The three P's and the working diagnosis

Behavioural pharmacology aims to address neurotransmitter alterations, decrease anxiety, fear, and arousal, and make behavioural and environmental modification easier. The trigger is assessed by Provocation (what is the trigger), Proportion (intensity of the reaction), and Persistence (ability to recover). your notes

Exam trap: recovery time and what we are changing A behaviourally normal, resilient dog should recover from a mild to moderately stressful event within a couple of minutes at most; longer recovery suggests an abnormal pattern or compromised welfare. Medication alters the negative emotional state underlying the behaviour, not the behaviour itself. your notes

Working diagnosis and arousal

  • Identify the behaviour, trigger, target, and motivation; recognising motivation informs the choice of fast versus short-acting and long versus short-term medication. your notes
  • High physiologic arousal (hackles up, dilated pupils, hard panting, heavy salivation, poor recovery) may respond better to clonidine than to trazodone. your notes

Medication choice and monitoring

  • Weigh administration, cost, availability, abuse potential, and legal requirements. A short-term agent is ideal, but an underlying disorder, a long stay, or anticipated future problems may favour a long-term baseline drug. your notes
  • Assign someone to monitor frequency, duration, and intensity of the behaviours of interest, and plan for the animal going home. your notes
  • A written management and education plan accompanies verbal instructions: treatment goals, how to give the drug, side effects to watch for, and who to follow up with. your notes

Disorders and First-Line Treatment

Match the drug to the disorder, always alongside behaviour modification and enrichment.

DisorderFirst-line approach
Anxiety (cat)Enrichment, alternative housing, or foster first; if a fearful cat keeps hiding (even in the litter box) despite hide and perch boxes, a benzodiazepine, with alprazolam preferred at 0.125 mg per cat once or twice daily
Anxiety (dog)Fluoxetine or paroxetine once daily
Compulsive disorderClomipramine or fluoxetine, plus BMOD stressing consistency and activity
SRPShort-departure program plus fluoxetine or clomipramine
Urine markingFluoxetine (dogs and cats); buspirone gives about a 50% reduction in cats

Compulsive disorder detail

  • Repetitive, sustained, out-of-context behaviour: tail chasing, pacing, weaving, self-licking, leg or foot chewing, self-directed aggression, cage licking, persistent barking; in cats, tail chasing or chewing and overgrooming. your notes
  • Genetic predisposition exists (German Shepherd and Staffordshire to tail chase, Doberman to flank suck), and signs appear with prolonged confinement and during transitions. your notes
Exam trap: duration and weaning for compulsive disorder Continue drug therapy for at least 3 weeks after a satisfactory reduction is seen, then wean gradually over 3 to 4 weeks while continuing behaviour modification. your notes

Pheromone therapy (Feliway, Adaptil) is a useful adjunct; details are in the Adjuncts tab. your notes

Neurotransmitters

Most behaviour drugs act on one of a handful of neurotransmitter systems. Knowing the system explains the indication, the side effects, and the dangerous combinations.

The systems

  • Glutamate: the primary excitatory transmitter; NMDA is one receptor type. NMDA blockers (ketamine, memantine) give analgesia and address pain-associated anxiety; high doses cause a dissociative or cataleptic state. your notes
  • GABA: synthesised from glutamate; the major inhibitory transmitter; increasing GABA is sedating and controls vigilance, anxiety, muscle tension, seizures, and memory. Benzodiazepines act here; flumazenil reverses them. your notes
  • Dopamine: reward, motivation, bonding, attention, voluntary movement. Excess gives stereotypy and compulsion; deficiency gives reduced alertness, anxiety, depression, and parkinsonian signs. your notes
  • Noradrenaline (NE): from dopamine; mood and arousal. Alpha-2 agonists (clonidine, dexmedetomidine, xylazine) inhibit NE release for sedation, analgesia, and (at low doses) anxiolysis. your notes
  • Adrenaline (epinephrine): the acute stress response. Beta-blockers (propranolol) block the physical signs of fear; alpha-2 agonists reduce fear by blocking NE. your notes
  • Serotonin (5-HT): from tryptophan; controls mood, impulsivity, anxiety, panic, satiety, nausea, cognition, aggression, sleep, pain, and thermoregulation. Low serotonin gives depression, anxiety, and aggression. your notes
  • Acetylcholine: the only transmitter not made from an amino acid (choline plus acetyl-CoA); supports memory, arousal, and attention. TCA anticholinergic effects give dry mouth, dry eyes, urine retention, constipation, mydriasis, and increased heart rate. your notes

Core Drug Classes

The five classes you will be tested on most: baseline SSRIs and TCAs, and the situational benzodiazepines, alpha-2 agonists, and trazodone.

SSRIs

  • Fluoxetine, paroxetine, sertraline, citalopram, escitalopram. They block serotonin reuptake; full effect takes 4 to 8 weeks. Fewer cardiac effects and may be safer in seizure-prone pets, but can reduce thyroid hormone. your notes
  • Indications: sertraline (panic, generalised anxiety, compulsive); fluvoxamine and fluoxetine (hyperactivity, aggression, compulsive); paroxetine (impulsivity, aggression, ritualistic). your notes
  • Fluoxetine: separation anxiety, aggression, feline urine marking, compulsive disorder; dogs 1 to 2 mg/kg SID, cats 0.5 to 1 mg/kg SID; can alter blood glucose (caution in diabetics); wean if used over 4 weeks. your notes
Exam trap: the fluoxetine washout Fluoxetine has a very long half-life in dogs (at least 5 days), so switching from fluoxetine to an MAOI (selegiline) needs a 5-week washout to avoid serotonin syndrome. your notes

TCAs

  • Clomipramine, amitriptyline, doxepin. They inhibit NE and serotonin reuptake and carry anticholinergic, antihistaminic, and alpha-1 effects, so side effects are broader; they also lower the seizure threshold and are contraindicated in seizure disorders. your notes
  • Clomipramine (Clomicalm): the strongest serotonergic TCA; licensed for canine separation anxiety with a behaviour plan; 2 to 4 mg/kg q24h (q12h may work better); a 2-week washout when switching to an MAOI; ineffective for owner-related aggression. your notes
  • Amitriptyline: traditionally used for cats urinating outside the box (cost and familiarity) and may help feline interstitial cystitis via NE-mediated pain reduction. Doxepin: high antihistaminic effect, used mainly for pruritus. your notes

Benzodiazepines

  • Act on GABA-A; onset 1 to 2 hours, lasting a few hours up to 8 to 10. Mild sedative at low doses, anxiolytic at moderate, hypnotic at high. Indicated for intense anxiety or panic (storms, noise phobia, separation, car rides, feline spraying). About 67% effective for anxiety cases. your notes
  • Specific agents: alprazolam for acute fear and panic (and with clomipramine for noise phobia); clonazepam and clorazepate for chronic generalised anxiety; oxazepam as a feline appetite stimulant; chlordiazepoxide for feline urine spraying. your notes
  • Drug-specific doses from your formulary: alprazolam 0.02 to 0.1 mg/kg PO PRN or q8 to 12h (cat 0.125 to 0.25 mg per cat PO q12h); lorazepam 0.02 to 0.1 mg/kg PO PRN or q8 to 24h (cat 0.25 mg per cat PO PRN). Oral diazepam is generally avoided in cats because of the rare risk of fatal acute hepatic necrosis. your notes
  • Disinhibition can increase exploration and appetite and reduce avoidance, but can also increase aggression, so use caution and basket-muzzle or crate training in animals with an aggression history. Paradoxical excitation is best discovered with owners present. your notes
  • Rare acute hepatic necrosis can occur in cats after oral diazepam, so screen liver function at baseline and recheck 3 to 5 days after starting. Always wean (10 to 25% per week) to avoid anxiety, anorexia, tremors, or seizures. your notes

Alpha-2 agonists

  • Clonidine, dexmedetomidine, detomidine. They inhibit NE release in the locus ceruleus; anxiolysis can occur at doses below those needed for sedation. Clonidine is given 1.5 to 2 hours before an event, up to twice daily; reversible with atipamezole. your notes
  • Dexmedetomidine oromucosal gel (Sileo) is labelled for canine noise aversion and still helps once fear has begun (onset at least 30 minutes, more like 1 to 2 hours); useful for poor intake coping and noisy transport. your notes
  • It is not suitable for severe fear with handling because it must be given oromucosally; the site goes pale from vasoconstriction, and if swallowed, high first-pass metabolism makes the effect low and the overdose risk low. Contraindicated with cardiac disease or NE-raising drugs. your notes

Trazodone (SARI)

  • A serotonin 2A antagonist and reuptake inhibitor that also blocks histamine and alpha-1; a fast-acting situational drug and a bridge while a baseline takes effect. Used for generalised and separation anxiety, storm phobia, and calming hospitalised pets. your notes
  • Avoid before a neurologic examination, and use caution with impaired primary haemostasis (it decreases platelet aggregation). Dog 2 to 5 mg/kg PRN or q12h (3 to 4 mg/kg q12h for kennel stress, generally under 10 mg/kg daily); cats 12.5 to 50 mg per cat (onset about 3 hours in cats versus 1 to 2 in dogs, so trial the dose first). your notes
  • Low-dose intake trazodone in the first 48 hours has been associated with lower respiratory illness, shorter stays, and higher adoption. std ref
Exam trap: trazodone is dose-dependent Trazodone gives targeted anxiety reduction at the lower end of its range. At higher doses the effect at serotonin receptors becomes less specific, so sedation, hypnotic effect, and side effects increase. Do not just raise the dose to chase the hypnotic effect. your notes

Other Agents and Serotonin Syndrome

The secondary agents, the hormonal and lithium options, and the one toxicity you must recognise instantly.

Buspirone (azapirone)

  • A partial serotonin 5-HT1A agonist; a mild anxiolytic for taking the edge off and building the confidence of an overly fearful pet, useful alongside an SSRI or clomipramine. your notes
  • It does NOT impair motor function or cognition (a good first choice for mild chronic fear), gives about a 50% reduction in feline urine marking, but can disinhibit and increase aggression. Dose about 0.5 to 1 mg/kg BID (2 mg/kg for pretravel); weeks to effect. your notes
Exam trap: where buspirone fails Buspirone is NOT indicated for panic disorders and is not proven for intense fear, phobias, compulsive disorders, or canine aggression. your notes

Gabapentin

  • An alpha-2-delta-1 ligand that reduces release of glutamate, NE, and substance P. It is structurally similar to GABA but does NOT act at the GABA receptor. Used for neuropathic pain and seizures, with anxiolytic properties; dog 10 to 30 mg/kg PRN or TID; sedation usually resolves. your notes
  • Effective for feline fear and anxiety before transport or vet visits. your notes
Exam trap: xylitol in human gabapentin Human oral liquid gabapentin commonly contains xylitol, which is toxic to dogs. Always use a xylitol-free suspension for canine patients. your notes

MAOIs and selegiline

  • MAOIs prevent breakdown of NE, dopamine, and serotonin. Selegiline selectively inhibits MAO-B in the dog, used for canine cognitive dysfunction, some pituitary-dependent hyperadrenocorticism, and fearful dogs with strong behavioural inhibition; dog 0.5 to 1 mg/kg SID in the morning. your notes
  • Mandatory washout before an MAOI: 5 weeks from fluoxetine, 2 weeks from clomipramine, and avoid all other serotonergic agents. your notes

Mirtazapine

  • A post-synaptic tetracyclic that enhances NE and 5-HT1A serotonergic transmission via alpha-2 antagonism; a serotonin receptor antagonist, so less likely to cause serotonin syndrome. Antiemetic and appetite-stimulating. your notes
  • Cats 1.88 to 3.75 mg per cat q72h; dogs about 0.6 to 1.3 mg/kg q24h, equivalent to 3.75 mg (under 10 kg) up to 15 mg (over 35 kg), to a maximum of 30 mg total daily. your notes
Corrected per your approval: mirtazapine maximum Your notes read "0.6 mg/kg up to a max of 30 mg/kg." The maximum is 30 mg TOTAL daily, not per kilogram; the per-kg figure was a transcription error (verified against veterinary dosing references, where the canine dose is about 0.6 to 1.3 mg/kg q24h to a maximum of 30 mg daily). The dog tablet range of 3.75 to 15 mg is consistent with this cap. corrected

Antihistamines, hormonal therapy, lithium

  • Antihistamines (hydroxyzine, diphenhydramine, others) for mild anxiety, overactivity, or sleep; anticholinergic, so dry mouth and constipation, contraindicated with glaucoma, urine retention, or hyperthyroidism. Cyproheptadine is an antihistamine with antiserotonergic effects and is an appetite stimulant. your notes
  • Hormonal therapy (progestins) for feline inappropriate urination and some anxiety or aggression, but serious side effects (diabetes mellitus, adrenocortical and bone-marrow suppression, mammary disease). your notes
  • Lithium for some forms of severe unpredictable aggression; highly toxic with a narrow window (therapeutic 0.8 to 1.2 mEq/L). your notes

Serotonin syndrome

Exam trap: serotonin syndrome triggers and antidote It occurs when serotonergic drugs stack (SSRI plus TCA plus MAOI, or with tramadol, amitraz, St John's wort, buspirone, and others). Signs resolve within 48 hours if the dose is halved or stopped; the specific antagonist is cyproheptadine (1.1 mg/kg every 1 to 4 hours), with diazepam for seizures and IV fluids for cardiovascular support. your notes

Adjuncts (Administered Non-Pharmaceutical Agents)

Lower-risk agents that are given to the animal to support, and sometimes substitute for, drug therapy.

InterventionKey points
PheromonesAct via the vomeronasal organ; cat F3 (cheek) and F4 (appeasing) and dog-appeasing pheromone, with weak to moderate evidence. Spray lasts about 6 hours, a diffuser covers 50 to 70 square metres for 30 days; good for small shelter rooms with negative-pressure airflow
Milk protein (alpha-casozepine; Zylkene)Similar structure to GABA; 15 to 30 mg/kg daily, started 1 to 2 days before a stressor; the dietary form is helpful mainly in cats, since large dogs would need too much
L-theanineGreen tea derivative; reduces glutamate and raises GABA and dopamine; used for chronic anxiety; mild GI upset or drowsiness
ProbioticsGut-brain axis; a novel strain improved anxiety behaviours, heart-rate variability, and cortisol; costly

Environmental enrichment (olfactory scent programs, music, and audio-volume guidance) overlaps the welfare and enrichment material and is kept in that subdomain rather than here. scope note
