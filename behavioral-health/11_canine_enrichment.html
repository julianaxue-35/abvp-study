Human Social Interaction

Short human contact gives a real but transient cortisol benefit. The behavioural benefits are bigger and last longer than the physiological ones, and foster is the strongest single intervention.

Short petting and play sessions

  • Cortisol falls during 20 to 30 minutes of petting or play, then does not persist an hour later; stacking multiple sessions in the same day adds no further benefit. your notes
  • Sex of the human can matter: women tend to produce greater cortisol reduction than men in some studies. Dogs alone in a novel enclosure vocalise much more than when petted, played with, or just sharing space with a passive stranger. your notes
  • 15 minutes with a person lowers heart rate, raises heart-rate variability, and reduces standing during the interaction. your notes
Exam trap: cortisol drop only in strays A 15 minute petting session lowered cortisol ONLY in dogs admitted as STRAYS, not in owner-relinquished dogs. This subpopulation split is high-yield. your notes

Reading versus exercise (both 15 minutes daily)

  • Both events produced more back-and-forth motion afterward. Exercise gave a greater reduction in door jumping. Reading gave a larger overall reduction in undesired behaviours, both jumping AND barking. your notes

Sustained programs

ProgramOutcome
3 times a week for 8 weeks
(walks, play with a person or toy, grooming, training, food, praise, petting)		Did NOT reduce baseline cortisol. DID increase sociability: dogs were more likely to approach the experimenter and initiate interaction in the kennel before a leashed walk.
Every other day for 2 months
(play, training, petting)		Improved obedience, docility, and sociability versus unenriched controls.

Foster effects

  • Dogs returning from foster were more obedient, sought social interaction with handlers, responded more readily to cues, jumped on strangers and barked less, and yawned more. Cortisol was relatively unaffected. The case for foster rests on the behavioural changes, not the cortisol. your notes
  • A 1 to 2 night foster stay produced cortisol BELOW the standard shelter baseline. Cortisol rose on return to the shelter, but did NOT exceed the pre-foster shelter baseline. your notes

Limiting visitors

  • During high-visitor times, dogs spend more time at the front of pen (FOP), standing and barking. A 2014 study that blocked adopter access in the afternoons documented noise levels more than 12 dB lower than pre-intervention. Sedentary behaviour rose in the morning before opening and in the afternoon during the intervention, locomotor activity fell, and the frequency and duration of repetitive behaviours fell in the afternoon. your notes
  • Trade-off: visitor restrictions reduce kennel stress but can lengthen stay and reduce adoptions, so dose them carefully. your notes

Training and counter-conditioning

  • In-kennel training: more sitting, lying down, and quiet behaviour, and less jumping in the kennel. your notes
  • Out-of-kennel training: reduced aggression. your notes
  • Consistency is the rule: once training ceased for 1 week, aggression toward other dogs returned to pre-intervention levels. your notes
  • Counter-conditioning (CC) takes about 20 seconds per dog; operant conditioning takes about 2 minutes per dog. Daily pairings of a door chime with food (3 to 5 times a week for 3 weeks) reduced kennel noise by 15 dB by attenuating the response to an established environmental stressor. your notes
  • Tailor interactions to the individual dog and, where possible, run them OUT of the kennel away from environmental stressors, or in a foster home. your notes

Conspecific Social Interaction

Group housing is the under-used welfare lever. Solo housing drives isolation vocalisations and front-of-pen aggression, and breaking a co-housed pair has measurable knock-on effects on the dog left behind.

Solo versus group housing

Solo-housedPair or group-housed
Bark, whimper, and howl moreLess noisy, better behaved overall
More likely to show aggression at the kennel front toward other dogsReduced front-of-pen aggression
10% of solo-housed dogs displayed stereotypies in a 3-month study0% of group-housed dogs displayed stereotypies
More trotting, walking, and standingMore lying down (pair-housed dogs spent more time resting)
Safety data for group housing In a 3-month study of 211 dogs in group housing, only 5 incidents occurred, classified by the researchers as light wounds. Group housing is safer than commonly assumed and abolishes stereotypy in this dataset. your notes

Pair separation effects

  • When one dog was removed from a co-housed pair, the remaining dog's running, posture changes, stretching, circling, and figures-of-eight all increased, signalling restlessness. your notes
  • Many of these behaviours declined over the 6 day post-separation period, but play stayed reduced. your notes

Visual contact without interaction

Solo housing with nearby visual conspecific contact but no social interaction may have more complex welfare effects than is currently understood, and further empirical work is needed before this very common shelter arrangement can be called optimal. your notes

Cortisol nuance

No main effects of housing or social contact were found on cortisol values. Cortisol was HIGHEST in dogs in NOVEL housing receiving no social contact, and LOWEST in dogs in TRADITIONAL kennels receiving no social contact. your notes

Playgroup eligibility

Exam trap: less than one third In typical shelters, fewer than 1/3 of resident dogs are suitable and available for daily off-leash interaction with other dogs. Playgroups are useful but cannot be the primary social plan for the whole population. your notes

Object Enrichment

Toys have varying functional value to the species AND to the individual. Choose by the dog's history and preferences, and rotate to avoid habituation.

What dogs actually use

Toy typeWhat the data show
NylabonePreferred over other in-kennel toys in a kennel-preference study
Robust toys (Nylabone, hard rubber)Only about 35% of shelter dogs interacted with robust toys, for an average of about 2 minutes
Rope toyDogs interacted longest among the robust selections
Soft, destructible toys (squeaky bone, plush)More than 3/4 of dogs spent 25% of a 15 minute session interacting with them, mostly ignoring robust options
Tennis ballOf the soft selections, the LEAST interaction time
Exam trap: soft beats robust for engagement Shelter dogs largely IGNORE robust selections in favour of soft, destructible toys, with the longest engagement on squeaky bones and plush toys. Rotate items if usage drops, since habituation is likely. your notes

Sensory Enrichment

Auditory, olfactory, and visual interventions have small to moderate effects, with one big exam trap: barking spikes when music stops.

Auditory

  • Classical music: more resting, less standing, more quiet behaviour in the kennel, compared with other genres, human conversation, or no auditory enrichment. your notes
  • Heavy metal: increased body shaking, increased barking, less time resting. your notes
  • Audiobooks: dogs spent more time resting and less time walking, sitting, or standing; promising for variety in the rotation. your notes
  • Habituation is rapid: rotate multiple classical compilations daily, and integrate audiobooks, to keep effects fresh. your notes

Olfactory

ScentEffect (scented-cloth studies)
Lavender, chamomileIncreased resting; decreased movement and vocalisation
Coconut, gingerDecreased vocalisation and movement; more resting with eyes closed (versus no-cloth control)
Rosemary, peppermintLess resting; more standing, moving, and vocalising

Dog appeasing pheromone (DAP)

  • Produced by lactating queens shortly after birth, processed through the canine vomeronasal organ as a non-volatile chemosignal. your notes
  • Average barking AMPLITUDE was lowered by about 30% in the DAP group versus placebo, but PEAK barking amplitude did not differ between groups, and barking frequency and behaviour did not differ either. DAP softens the noise floor without preventing loud peaks. your notes

Visual

  • Televisions: regardless of image type, vocalising and movement decreased, with more time at the front of pen. But dogs spent only about 10% of their time looking at the monitor, and interest waned over time. your notes

Assessment and Measurement

An enrichment program without measurement is just a hope. The exam tests the timeline rules and the practical measurement methods, not theoretical perfection.

How to assess enrichment

  • Population level: What percentage of dogs engage with the enrichment? your notes
  • Individual level: Does this particular dog engage, and does the enrichment shift behaviours that matter for that dog (more resting, less barking)? your notes
  • How and how long? The duration and quality of engagement determine whether to continue or modify the enrichment. your notes
  • Observe from a distance, or have a staff member to whom the dog is habituated record the behaviour. Use paper or electronic records. your notes
  • Evaluate by measuring behaviours BEFORE the enrichment and again AFTER, either shelter-wide (kennel noise) or per individual (barking frequency and duration). your notes
Exam trap: 2 days and 1 week Two stop-rules. If an individual dog shows no direct engagement with a specific item after 2 days, change the type of enrichment. If the broader program produces no positive behavioural change within 1 week at most, modify the strategy. your notes

Measurement methods

MethodWhat it isPracticality
ContinuousAll instances of the behaviour and their duration are recordedUsually unrealistic in shelters
Focal animal samplingObserver focuses on a single animal for a defined periodDetailed; labour-intensive
Instantaneous scan sampling (ISS)Observer notes what one dog is doing in that INSTANT, then immediately moves to the next dogFast across many animals; very useful in sheltering
Interval recordingBehaviours are recorded as present or absent within fixed time intervalsModerate effort
Momentary time sampling (MTS)A blend of ISS and interval recording: observe one animal for an INSTANT, then observe the SAME animal again after a set INTERVALPractical and informative for individual dogs
Permanent productsThe tangible result of a behaviour acts as a proxy (faeces or urine in the kennel, KONGs emptied, chew marks or strings pulled from a rope toy, an object moved from the kennel)Most practical: the observer does not need to be present when the behaviour occurs
When permanent products work Only when the behaviour leaves a tangible trace. A KONG that has been emptied tells you the dog engaged; a chewed rope tells you it was used. Subtle behaviours like quiet resting do not leave a permanent product. your notes

Ensuring programs reach goals

LeverPractical implementation
Train the peopleA simple written task list with visual aids and a short video. More training only for complex behaviours.
Arrange the environmentMap a typical day of the staff and volunteers involved, identify opportunities and constraints, and talk to the people delivering enrichment for creative fixes.
Arrange consequencesKnow each DOG'S reinforcer (food, praise, social stimulation). Use reinforcement contingencies for STAFF and VOLUNTEERS too: a $2 store voucher after prepping 50 kibble-filled toilet rolls, an extra lunch break for hitting a target, or shirts and goodies for milestones.
Engage the publicSchool programs, walking programs, in-kennel reading programs, rope-toy braiding, and snuffle-mat building tap volunteer capacity and raise public profile.
Record into the dog's fileEnter enrichment delivery and engagement into the individual dog's record so decisions are data-based and continuity is preserved across staff.
