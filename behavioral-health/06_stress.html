Stress Physiology

Stress triggers two neuroendocrine cascades: the fast SAM system for immediate fight-or-flight and the slower HPA axis for sustained cortisol release. The exam tests the timeline, the hormones, and the chronic-dysfunction paradox.

Cortisol kinetics

  • Cortisol takes 20 to 30 minutes to peak following a stressor and remains elevated for 90 minutes or more. your notes
  • Low to moderate acute stress is considered beneficial (helps the animal cope). Extreme acute stress or chronic stress has detrimental effects on physical and emotional health. your notes

Chronic HPA dysregulation

Exam trap: low cortisol does not equal low stress Chronic stress causes adrenal hypertrophy and increased glucocorticoids, but ACTH remains unchanged. The HPA axis becomes dysregulated and stops responding appropriately, so a normal or low resting cortisol in a long-term resident may indicate a malfunctioning stress-response system, not a relaxed animal. Kennelled dogs in this state still remain responsive to acute stressors such as restraint and venipuncture. Cortisol also depends on when it was collected, neuter status, sampling method, and only indicates activation of stress, not the type (good, tolerable, or toxic). your notes

Systemic effects of stress

Stress increases maladaptive behaviour and displays of aggression, impairs social behaviour, memory, and cognition, increases disease susceptibility, slows healing, and shortens lifespan. The HPA effects include increased energy availability, increased oxygen intake, decreased blood flow to non-critical areas, and inhibition of digestion, growth, immune function, reproduction, and pain perception (memory and sensory functions are enhanced). Acute stress can enhance memory for threatening events. Chronic stress can lead to structural and functional brain changes. your notes

Stress Taxonomy and Dynamics

Three categories of stress, two types of stressor, and the shelter-specific concepts (trigger stacking, context learning, the first-week rule) that the exam tests.

Categories of stress

CategoryDefinition
Good stressThe animal faces a challenge and copes successfully with no long-term harm
Tolerable stressThe challenge is distressing at the time, but the animal can cope and does not suffer long term
Toxic stressDistress exceeds the animal's ability to cope, causing long-term consequences to emotional and physical health

Types of stressor

  • Physical: going for a run, hit by a car, catching a virus, hunger, thirst, pain, extreme temperatures, disease, sleep deprivation. your notes
  • Psychological: social or environmental factors with no direct physical perturbation (meeting a new person or dog, being attacked without injury, social isolation, exposure to novelty, unpredictable environments, chronic frustration). your notes
Exam trap: most potent psychological stressors Novelty, the withholding of an expected reward, and the anticipation of punishment are the most potent psychological stressors. your notes

Trigger stacking and context learning

  • Context learning: an aversive experience in a specific environment shapes the animal's future behaviour in that same context. your notes
  • Trigger stacking: multiple stressors in rapid succession accumulate with no window to return to baseline, producing an amplified response or a behavioural explosion. your notes

The first-week rule

  • Stress is highest during the first week. Owner-relinquished dogs show intense psychological stress without adaptation, whereas strays and return dogs show a lower baseline. your notes
  • Defer non-essential stressful activities during the first week to allow adaptation and relationship building. your notes
  • Prior kennelling experience reduces the activated stress response when the dog enters a new kennel. your notes
  • Petting interaction over 15 minutes per day does not change stress levels in owner-relinquished dogs, but it does reduce stress in strays. your notes
  • Research on wild animals shows translocation stress via four routes: capture, captivity, transport, and release. your notes

Maternal and early-life stress

Experiences during the first weeks of life profoundly affect the animal's ability to cope. Maternal stress during gestation can alter pup behaviour, and offspring often show learning impairment and increased susceptibility to conditioning of fearful responses, especially to auditory stimuli. your notes

Measuring and Identifying Stress

Cortisol is the most-tested stress marker but it comes with caveats. The exam also tests the stress leukogram and the sampling-matrix decision.

Glucocorticoid measurement

MatrixTime frameUse
Blood and salivaMomentaryCondition of the animal at that moment; confounded by handling and venipuncture stress
Urine, faeces, and hairLong-term (days to weeks)Population-level welfare audits, housing-design evaluations; non-invasive
Exam trap: sampling decision To evaluate a shelter's housing over months, faecal or hair cortisol metabolites are correct; blood or saliva cortisol would be a diagnostic error due to the acute stress of collection. your notes

Other endocrine and haematological markers

  • ACTH and luteinising-hormone-releasing-hormone stimulation tests measure adrenal and pituitary sensitivities. Increased HPA responsiveness with reduced pituitary sensitivity suggests HPA dysfunction from chronic stress. your notes
  • Stress leukogram: decreased peripheral lymphocyte numbers and increased neutrophil counts. In a long-term shelter animal with a normal cortisol, a persistent lymphopenia and neutrophilia on CBC provides evidence of chronic stress and HPA dysregulation. your notes

Feline Stress

Cats mask stress with feigned sleep and defensive displays. The five pillars, the FURI link, and the trapped-tame-versus-feral distinction are all high-yield.

Signs of stress in cats

  • Hypervigilance, feigned sleep, constant hiding, activity depression, and loss of appetite. your notes
  • Defensive behaviours: front-of-pen aggression, arched back, ears back, dilated pupils, vocalising. your notes
Exam trap: feigned sleep Feigned sleep is a coping mechanism in highly stressed cats, not evidence of relaxation. Recording "the cat is sleeping and relaxed" is an anthropomorphic error. your notes
Exam trap: trapped tame versus feral Even the tamest house cat, when trapped and stressed, can exhibit defensive behaviours indistinguishable from truly feral cats. Allow frightened cats to calm before assessing personality. Some cats may never adjust to the shelter. your notes

The five pillars of feline environmental welfare

  • 1. A safe place. 2. Multiple and separated key environmental resources. 3. Opportunities for play and predatory behaviour. 4. Positive, consistent, and predictable human-cat interaction. 5. An environment that respects the cat's sense of smell. std ref

Stress, disease, and adaptation

  • High stress score is linked to high rates of FURI. your notes
  • Stress levels generally reduce within the first two weeks, but about 4% of the feline population maintain a high stress level and may not be suited to shelter housing. your notes
  • Feral cats require the same minimum floor space as friendly cats (11 sq ft, per ASV) in short-term housing, though they may use hiding spaces only at night. ASV
Overlaps: feline housing as stress mitigation The detailed feline micro and macro environment specifications (8 to 11 sq ft individual floor space, 18 sq ft per cat group minimum, perch 12 inches wide and raised 13 to 14 inches, cages at least 18 inches off the floor, fill top cages first, temperature per ASV 18 to 26.6 C, quiet latches, horizontal bars, colour palette favouring blues, greens, and violets over fluorescing whites, hiding boxes for all cats at entry, group-housing selection and removal criteria) are built in full in the Facility and Environment hub. The key principle here is that each specification is a stress-mitigation tool: hiding lowers Cat Stress Score without increasing length of stay or reducing adoption rates; elevated cages reduce stress and improve monitoring; less cage space directly increases FURI risk and behavioural concerns; and small groups are preferred over warehouse-style rooms for welfare, stress, and disease reasons. your notes
UV vision clarification The notes state cats see in the UVB spectrum. Cats do transmit some short-wavelength and ultraviolet light through their lenses and are dichromatic (sensitive to blue-violet and green-yellow; reds and oranges appear greyish), which is why the practical advice (avoid bright white fluorescing plastics, choose matte blues, greens, and violets) is sound even though the "UVB" label is a simplification. your notes

Adaptation, Deterioration and Management

The first three days see cortisol rebalance, but prolonged confinement drives behavioural deterioration. Tracking whether the animal is sensitising, habituating, or developing a depression-like state is the clinical lever.

Behavioural adaptation

  • Cortisol decreases during the first three days, likely due to the animal regaining homeostatic balance after transport and introduction to a novel environment. your notes
  • By day six, dogs begin spending more time at the back of the kennel and less time active, which may indicate growing comfort. your notes
  • Dogs transferred to short-term foster rested significantly more than in the shelter, likely an artifact of a less stimulating environment rather than sleep disruption per se. However, when humans were absent, shelter dogs were more active than pet dogs during the latter's least active period. your notes

Behavioural deterioration

  • Barrier aggression, high arousal, worsening reactivity, increasing fear, and onset of abnormal behaviour are commonly cited examples. Chronic stressors include spatial and social restriction and a lack of agency and predictability. your notes
  • Increased length of stay may produce escape-related behaviours (door scratching, floor digging, whining) and reduced rest. your notes
  • Age differences: younger dogs tend toward bedding chewing and play bouncing; older dogs toward panting and barking. your notes
  • Shelter dogs show less variation in activity than pet dogs (temporal monotony from the fixed schedule). Boredom initially increases activity but over time can lead to wakeful inactivity, fragmented sleep, and a depression-like affective state characterised by loss of responsiveness to opportunities for reward. your notes
Exam trap: three states to distinguish Track changes from intake to differentiate sensitisation (increased activity), habituation (increased or decreased activity), and depression-like affective states (wakeful inactivity, loss of reward responsiveness). your notes

When you identify a stressed animal

  • Stop and reassess: what is causing the stress? What options exist to alleviate it? Is the procedure absolutely necessary right now? Assess needs versus wants and prioritise the need. your notes
  • If stress cannot be avoided: can it wait? Can the dog undergo an anaesthetic event? Is there a different approach? your notes

Long-term stays

  • Keep length of stay as short as possible. Animals staying more than a few days require daily enrichment. your notes
  • Chronic stress from prolonged stays reduces coping, increases fear, anxiety, and frustration, and underlies social withdrawal, repetitive behaviours, and aggression, jeopardising placement. your notes
  • Options: increased enrichment, additional space and choice, foster care. your notes
  • Reproductive stress (oestrus, sex drive) increases urine spraying, marking, fighting, and emotional stress; animals housed long term should be spayed and neutered. your notes
  • Long-term confinement without basic care (daily enrichment and exercise) or where basic care induces stress or compromises safety is unacceptable. ASV
  • Feral animals unable to express natural behaviour, play, or form social bonds: euthanasia is humane when a live outcome (return to field) is not possible in a timely manner. your notes
  • Very fearful dogs are less likely to acclimate and are at high risk for deterioration. For pro-social animals, group housing should be seriously considered; concerns about disease and aggression are minimal in well-managed programmes, and behaviour stabilises within one to two days of adding a new dog. your notes
