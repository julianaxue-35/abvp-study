Definitions: Anxiety, Fear, and Phobia

The single most testable item in this subdomain is the three-way distinction. The presence or absence of an identifiable stimulus, and the proportionality of the response, separate the three states.

StateDefinitionStimulusAdaptive value
AnxietyA reaction of apprehension or uneasiness to an ANTICIPATED danger or threat. Signs are physiologic (autonomic arousal: increased HR and RR, trembling, salivation, GI signs, hypervigilance) and behavioural (freezing, lip licking, yawning, pacing, stress vocalisation, restlessness).MAY have no identifiable stimulus; can become generalised, or specific to certain contexts of perceived threatMaladaptive when chronic; in low doses can be adaptive vigilance
FearAn emotional response due to the PRESENCE or PROXIMITY of a specific stimulus (object, noise, individual, social situation) that the pet perceives as a threat. Psychological and physiological state with somatic, emotional, cognitive, and behavioural components.IDENTIFIABLE stimulus differentiates it from anxietyCan be a NORMAL adaptive response; appropriateness judged by magnitude and context
PhobiaA profound, excessive, abnormal fear response that occurs WITHOUT a true threat, or is OUT OF PROPORTION to the actual threat. Persists or INTENSIFIES with repeated exposure. Panic attacks, hysteria, and catatonia can be components.Either none, or grossly disproportionate to the stimulusMALADAPTIVE and interferes with normal function
Exam trap: anticipated versus present Anxiety is to the FUTURE what fear is to the PRESENT. A dog scanning constantly in a quiet home with no trigger is anxious. A dog cowering when a delivery truck appears is fearful. A dog that has a hysteria attack when a plastic bag rustles is phobic. your notes

Pathological anxiety

An uncontrollable, excessive, inappropriate, generalised, dysfunctional, and aversive emotion that triggers physiological and behavioural responses lacking adaptive value. The response is to the EXAGGERATED ANTICIPATION OR PERCEPTION of threats, INCOMMENSURATE with the actual situation. your notes

Signs of anxiety to recognise clinically (Box 12.3)

  • Hypervigilance, scanning. your notes
  • Increased motor activity: restlessness, pacing, circling. your notes
  • Vocalisation, whining. your notes
  • DISPLACEMENT behaviours: out-of-context grooming and scratching, yawning, lip licking, whining and barking, destructiveness, digging. your notes
  • Changes in social-soliciting behaviour: increase OR decrease in attention seeking. your notes
  • Hiding, escape attempts. your notes
  • Physiologic signs: trembling, dilated pupils, hypersalivation, increased RR, increased HR, urination, defaecation, vomiting. your notes
  • Decreased appetite. your notes

The freeze response and behavioural shutdown

A dog standing rigidly, panting heavily, with dilated pupils, lip licking, and a tucked tail is in a FEAR state with behavioural shutdown (the freeze response), often misread as "calm" or "stubborn." Fear responses can be aggression, cowering, shaking, freezing, or escape. your notes

Neuroanatomy of the Fear Response

Five structures and one cascade. The amygdala triggers it. The locus ceruleus broadcasts noradrenergic alarm. The hippocampus contextualises and is damaged by chronic cortisol. The raphe nuclei supply forebrain serotonin and modulate the response. The forebrain enables unlearning.

The five structures

StructureRole in fear
AmygdalaPRIMARY site for processing fear-evoking triggers (visual, auditory, odour, hypoxia, pain). Inputs from sensory organs through the THALAMUS. Triggers an immediate physiologic startle BEFORE the slower cortical pathway can analyse whether the threat is real, and once the emotional stage is triggered it is difficult to inhibit. Signals the paraventricular nuclei of the hypothalamus to release corticotropin-releasing factor (CRF), stimulating the HPA axis and NE release from the locus ceruleus. CENTRAL SITE for fear conditioning (integrating prior memory).
Locus ceruleusBrainstem nucleus in the gray matter of the PONS. Contains the HIGHEST concentration of noradrenergic neurons in the brain, with projections to the cortex, hypothalamus, hippocampus, and median and dorsal raphe nuclei. Receives sensory and visceral input plus excitatory afferents from the central nucleus of the amygdala. Stimulation triggers NE release. Dysregulation produces panic attacks, ADHD, sleep and arousal disorders, and affective disorders.
HippocampusMajor nucleus of the limbic system; memory storage; anatomical connections with the amygdala and hypothalamus. Processes CONTEXTUAL information and differentiates safe from dangerous. Normally SUPPRESSES the HPA axis. In chronic stress, sustained cortisol DAMAGES the hippocampus, downregulates the HPA-axis brake, and produces excessively high cortisol, a vicious cycle. Reduced hippocampal volume is documented in PTSD and in animals exposed to chronic stress.
Dorsal and medial raphe nucleiTwo functional neuron clusters in the centromedial brainstem, part of the reticular formation. Provide VIRTUALLY ALL serotonin input to the forebrain. Help modulate fear, anxiety, and autonomic responses, AND the cognitive and motor components that INHIBIT the fight or flight response.
ForebrainModulates the limbic system and hypothalamus. A functioning forebrain is necessary to UNLEARN fear responses (extinction).
Exam trap: cortisol-hippocampus vicious cycle Hippocampus normally SUPPRESSES the HPA axis (negative feedback). Chronic stress damages hippocampal neurons, removing that brake, which produces even higher cortisol, more damage, and reduced hippocampal volume. This is the human PTSD parallel. your notes

The fear cascade (sympathetic and HPA)

  • SNS releases NE and epinephrine (from subcortical brain and adrenal glands), driving fight, flight, or freeze. your notes
  • Physiologic response: increased HR, BP, RR, and vasoconstriction to internal organs. your notes
  • Epinephrine breaks down glycogen and fat (lipolysis), and increases gluconeogenesis to fuel fight or flight. your notes
  • HPA stimulation releases CORTISOL. Chronic stimulation drives long-term medical and behavioural consequences. your notes

Neurotransmitter targets for treatment

Dysregulation of fear pathways involves alterations in SEROTONIN (raphe to forebrain), NORADRENALINE (locus ceruleus), and GABA. These are the targets of SSRIs, TCAs, alpha-2 agonists, and benzodiazepines respectively. your notes

Determinants of Fear and the Learning Principles

Fear has genetic, environmental, and learning determinants. One unpleasant event can produce lasting fear (one-event learning). Fear can stay specific or generalise. The exam tests whether you understand kindling, sensitisation, discrimination, and generalisation.

Determinants of fear (Box 12.2)

CategoryDetail
GeneticsSPECIES: unconditioned stimuli for fear (predators, environmental danger, novel situations, social threats). INDIVIDUAL: temperament.
EnvironmentalInadequate socialisation or habituation; sensory isolation or nutritional deprivation or illness during development; TRAUMATIC or aversive experiences (conditioning); consequences and learning (negative reinforcement by stimulus retreat each time the fear display "works").
Components of fearPHYSIOLOGY: autonomic and neuroendocrine arousal, cardiovascular changes, pupils, piloerection, glucose metabolism. BEHAVIOUR: type determined by genetics, breed, individual, experience, environment, type and intensity of the stimulus, and the presence or absence of conspecifics or family. EMOTION: observation of the emotional state of stimulus, family members, and other dogs (social transmission).
Exam trap: one-event learning, discrimination, and generalisation An intensely aversive event can produce an intensely fearful and LASTING memory. The dog may stay fearful of the specific stimulus (DISCRIMINATION, e.g. a particular noise, place, or person), OR generalise from one example to many similar stimuli (e.g. fear of one child becomes fear of all children). your notes

Kindling and sensitisation

The kindling effect Repeated, intermittent, sub-threshold or threshold stimulation of fear circuits progressively LOWERS the threshold for future stimulation, so phobic reactions intensify with unmanaged exposure over time. Originally a neuroscience term from the epilepsy literature (Goddard 1969), extended to behavioural sensitisation in fear and PTSD models. Operationally, this is why repeated unmanaged exposure to a phobic stimulus (e.g. a noise-phobic dog enduring storm after storm without intervention) makes the next exposure WORSE, not better. std ref

Arousal and the loss of conscious choice

High states of arousal or anxiety place the dog in a state of HYPERVIGILANCE in which fear responses become AUTOMATIC and preclude conscious decisions about how to respond. This is why a panicking dog cannot "think its way out" of the situation, and why training must occur in calm states well below the fear threshold. your notes

Excessive fear: contributing factors

  • Genetic factors and breed predisposition. your notes
  • Inadequate early environmental experience and early socialisation. your notes
  • One or more unpleasant experiences producing conditioned fear. your notes
  • Medical or behavioural pathology. your notes
  • Loss of maintained social and environmental exposure later in life (even adequately socialised dogs can become fearful if exposure lapses). your notes
  • Unpleasant and stressful experiences during early adolescence, INCLUDING punishment-based training, can contribute to fear conditioning and increased avoidance. your notes

Prognostic factors

Good prognosisPoor prognosis
Short duration; stable temperament; adequately socialised to people; owner can control further interactions for safety and success; dog shows fear WITHOUT aggressionStrong genetic component; early age of onset; grossly inadequate early socialisation; environments where control of fear-eliciting stimuli is difficult; pet has concurrent fears or phobias

Behavioural Modification

Two principles, six techniques, one threshold rule, and one absolute prohibition. Punishment of fear ALWAYS makes the problem worse.

The two principles

  1. Avoidance of any stimuli or situations that evoke a fear response outside of training sessions. Every uncontrolled exposure FURTHER INCREASES the fear (kindling). your notes
  2. Controlled exposure at intensities below the fear threshold, paired with positive outcomes, so the pet relaxes in the presence of the stimulus and it no longer elicits a fear response. your notes

The six core techniques

TechniqueMechanism
Controlled exposureThe umbrella concept; deliberate, graded contact with the fear-eliciting stimulus under conditions the trainer manages.
HabituationRepeated exposure to a mild stimulus until the pet stops responding. Effective only when the fear is mild and the pet can adapt at its own pace.
Systematic desensitisationControlled exposure at an intensity (or distance, volume, familiarity) that is WEAKER than what triggers fear, so the pet remains SUBTHRESHOLD throughout.
CounterconditioningChange the underlying emotional response (the "feeling") about the stimulus by PAIRING it with HIGH-VALUE rewards (food, toys, play) at each subthreshold exposure. Reward must be reserved for stimulus exposure, otherwise the value drops.
Response substitutionTrain an alternative, desirable, calm behaviour that is physically INCOMPATIBLE with the fear response (sit-stay, look or watch, go to a mat) and reward it heavily on exposure.
ShapingReward successive approximations of the desired calm behaviour, increasing criteria gradually.

Designing the exposure gradient

AxisHow to grade
DistanceStart with the stimulus FAR (e.g. 20 m), progress to closer (a few metres at a time)
Volume / reproductionFor sound-based fears, start with audio recordings at barely perceptible volume (storms, fireworks, vacuum cleaner) and increase incrementally
Appearance and familiarityTrain with family members or less-threatening individuals before introducing strangers or specific triggers (Box 12.6 example for fear of men: familiar women → unfamiliar women → familiar boys → unfamiliar boys → familiar men → unfamiliar men)
Activity levelLow to high; quiet still child to running playing child
LocationCalmest environment first; train at home or in a calm setting before progressing to public spaces
Exam trap: the threshold rule If at any step the pet shows fear, anxiety, or aggression and refuses the high-value reward, the exposure MUST stop immediately. Move to the previous (lower) distance, volume, or intensity where the pet was calm, allow full recovery, and resume at that level. Forcing exposure when the pet has crossed its threshold STOPS learning and causes SENSITISATION and panic. your notes
Never punish a fearful display Punishment, anger, frustration, or even just an upset emotional state in the owner AGGRAVATES the fearful emotional state. While punishment might briefly reduce a visible undesirable behaviour, fear is an emotional state, and punishment ADDS to it; the underlying problem worsens and defensive aggression risk rises. your notes

Controlled flooding: narrow indication

Controlled flooding (continuous exposure until habituation) is most likely to be effective ONLY IF the stimulus does NOT genuinely threaten, owners can remain CALM, and the pet is restrained until it habituates AND THEN rewarded. It is appropriate only when the pet's fear will quickly dissipate; if fear increases, it becomes a welfare issue and FURTHER SENSITISES the pet. Confinement crates or head halters help with safety. your notes

Diagnosis and conflict signs

  • Fear cluster: aggression, cowering, shaking, freezing, or escape. your notes
  • Signs of CONFLICT indicating discomfort: displacement behaviours such as yawning, lip licking, whining, out-of-context grooming, or circling. your notes
  • Physical signs: tail held low or tucked, ears back, avoidance of eye contact, lowered body position, leaning away, lateral recumbency, submissive urination. your notes

Noise and Storm Phobia Protocol

Noise phobia affects 40 to 50% of dogs. The storm phobia and noise phobia overlap is asymmetric: about 90% of storm-phobic dogs also have noise phobia, but only 75% of noise-phobic dogs have storm phobia. Storm phobia is harder because the natural stimulus (barometric pressure, ionisation, lightning, rain on the roof) is multifactorial and not under owner control.

Prevalence and risk

  • Noise phobia is one of the most common concerns of dog owners, affecting 40 to 50% of dogs. Triggers include fireworks, engine noises, banging doors, vacuum cleaners. your notes
  • Protective factor: exposure to LOUD VOICES before 6 months may have a protective effect. your notes
  • Contributors: genetic, previous experience, SOCIAL TRANSMISSION (learned or copied from other fearful dogs), owner responses. your notes
  • Dogs with generalised anxiety may be fearful of a broad range of stimuli. your notes
Exam trap: the 90 / 75 asymmetry Approximately 90% of dogs with STORM phobia also have NOISE phobia, but only about 75% of dogs with NOISE phobia have STORM phobia. Storm phobia is the more complex condition with broader triggers (pressure, ionisation, light, rain, noise). your notes

Clinical signs

Tremble, salivate, hide, attention seek, attempt to escape. Owners often describe the dog as "becoming a different dog" during events. your notes

Management: layered approach

LayerWhat it looks like
Safe spaceAn indoor room or confinement area where stimuli can be minimised (closet, furnace room, crate with sound-reducing cover, "Thunder Hut" nested cardboard or blanket arrangement). Allow the pet to RETREAT here, not forced.
Sight and sound barriersLight-proof window shades or storm shutters to seal out lightning. Mask external noise with a TV, radio, white noise machine, fan, or MUSIC WITH A DISTRACTING BEAT (drums, rap).
DistractionsFavoured chews, food-filled toys, companionship of a person, and the background sounds described above. Leash and head halter can help focus and settle some dogs.
Commercial calming aidsMutt Muffs (ear), Doggles and Calming Caps (visual), Anxiety Wrap and Thundershirt (gentle constant pressure), Storm Defender Cape (claimed to reduce associated electrical charges; one preliminary lab study showed effect, one study found no difference vs placebo though both groups reported improvement).
Never punishPunishment converts fear into a worse emotional state and adds risk of aggression. your notes
Ignore mild signs ONLY ifThe dog can recover SPONTANEOUSLY. For more intense distress, ACTIVE assistance is better (see below).
Assist the phobic dogIf the reaction is extreme, distract with a head halter, novel chews or toys or treats, or stage a "STORM PARTY" (excitedly toss treats and toys around the home).

Desensitisation and counterconditioning protocol

  1. Train a SETTLE CUE first: in a completely calm environment, reward-train the pet to voluntarily go to its safe space and relax on command. You cannot teach a complex behaviour to a panicking dog. your notes
  2. Use a CD recording of the storm or firework noise at SUBTHRESHOLD volume. your notes
  3. Pair the noise with the dog's favoured rewards (RESERVED only for noise-exposure sessions to keep their value high). your notes
  4. Increase volume only when the dog stays relaxed; drop back if any sign of anxiety. your notes

Anxiolytic support

  • For year-round or seasonal cover: SSRI (e.g. fluoxetine) or clomipramine. your notes
  • Adjunctive as-needed BEFORE the storm: benzodiazepine, clonidine, trazodone, or propranolol. your notes
  • SELEGILINE is sometimes used for noise phobia associated with chronic anxiety, but ONLY after complete withdrawal of any SSRI or TCA (serotonin syndrome risk). your notes
The myth that comforting reinforces fear Comforting a panicking dog does NOT reinforce the fear, because fear is an involuntary EMOTION, not an operant behaviour. Reinforcement principles apply to behaviours, not emotions. Active intervention (distraction, structural support, a "storm party") helps break the panic cycle; ignoring only works if the dog will recover spontaneously. std ref

Prevention

  • Expose early. your notes
  • Habituate to a variety of stimuli during the socialisation window. your notes

Pharmacology of Fear, Phobia, and Anxiety

Two tempos: daily MAINTENANCE drugs that take 2 to 4 weeks (up to 8 weeks) to produce maximal clinical effect, and as-needed EVENT drugs given 1 to 2 hours before a known trigger. The exam tests combinations, dose adjustments, washouts, and one classic contraindication. Full doses live in the Behaviour Medications hub.

Long-term daily maintenance

ClassExamplesNotes
SSRIsFluoxetine, paroxetine, sertraline, citalopramDecrease overall arousal, impulsivity, reactivity. Onset 2 to 4 weeks, maximal effect up to 8 weeks. First line for chronic anxiety, generalised anxiety, compulsive disorders, separation anxiety, seasonal noise phobias.
TCAsClomipramine, amitriptyline, doxepinCLOMIPRAMINE is the most selective serotonin reuptake inhibitor of the TCAs and is the standard for compulsive disorders, stereotypic behaviours, and ALD. Other TCAs are less effective for compulsive disorders because they have less SSRI effect.
MAOISelegilineUsed for noise phobia associated with chronic anxiety, or for CCDS. STRICTLY contraindicated concurrent with SSRIs or TCAs due to fatal serotonin syndrome risk. Switching FROM an SSRI requires a full washout (see below).

Short-term as-needed (event) medications

  • Benzodiazepines (alprazolam, diazepam, clonazepam): excellent for acute panic or situational anxiety. Give 1 to 2 hours pre-event. Side effects: sedation, INHIBIT learning and memory (so as the pet improves, lower doses), DRUG DISINHIBITION (may make a fearful animal more confident and unexpectedly aggressive), rebound on rapid withdrawal, paradoxical agitation, hepatotoxicity reports in cats. your notes
  • Alpha-2 agonist (clonidine): decreases central noradrenergic output; effective as a situational tool OR as a daily adjunct to SSRI or TCA. your notes
  • Trazodone: serotonergic; can augment daily SSRI or TCA therapy as an event medication. your notes
  • Propranolol: beta-blocker; an option for storm-event cover. your notes

Safe combination rules

CombinationAdjustmentRationale
TCA + FLUOXETINEReduce TCA dose by 10 to 25%Fluoxetine inhibits cytochrome P450 enzymes, slowing TCA clearance and raising serum levels
TCA + SERTRALINEReduce TCA dose by 50%Even larger pharmacokinetic interaction; still use with caution given serotonin syndrome potential
SSRI + TCA, traz, buspirone, tramadol, SAMe, tryptophanMonitor for serotonin syndromeAll add serotonergic load
MAOI (selegiline) + SSRI or TCACONTRAINDICATEDMAOI prevents serotonin breakdown; SSRI prevents reuptake; combined effect is uncontrolled serotonin accumulation, which can be fatal
Exam trap: signs of serotonin syndrome Tremors, HYPERTHERMIA, rigid muscle hypertonicity, mental status changes, and DIARRHOEA. Neuromuscular plus autonomic plus cognitive. your notes

Washout requirements

  • Switching FROM fluoxetine TO an MAOI (selegiline): washout of 5 to 6 weeks required because of fluoxetine's long-lived ACTIVE metabolite NORFLUOXETINE. your notes
  • Switching FROM other SSRIs or TCAs: shorter, drug-specific washouts apply (cross-reference Behaviour Medications hub). your notes
  • Switching FROM an MAOI TO an SSRI or TCA: also requires a washout before starting the second drug. your notes

Treatment duration and tapering

  • If the behavioural problem improves on medication, continue for AT LEAST 6 months; in chronic or compulsive cases, lifelong maintenance is often required. your notes
  • If tapering is attempted, do so GRADUALLY over several months; abrupt discontinuation triggers immediate relapse and behavioural regression. your notes
Acepromazine for noise or storm phobia: contraindicated as monotherapy Acepromazine provides chemical TRANQUILISATION and motor immobilisation but does NOT provide ANXIOLYSIS. The animal remains terrified internally while unable to move or escape, and acepromazine can heighten auditory sensitivity. Modern veterinary behavioural medicine treats this as a welfare problem. Acepromazine is not in your PDF directly but is standard clinical teaching for noise and storm phobia management. std ref

Augmentation and adjuncts

  • BUSPIRONE: additional anxiolysis without sedation; can cause disinhibition. your notes
  • GABAPENTIN: anxiety AND pain combinations with SSRIs. your notes
  • MIRTAZAPINE: for insomnia and agitation. your notes
  • MELATONIN: for chronic anxiety and phobic disorders such as storm phobia. your notes
  • THYROID supplementation: with SSRIs or TCAs as augmentation for mood elevation (human data). your notes
  • PHEROMONES (Adaptil, Feliway): may reduce anxiety in dogs and cats respectively. your notes
