Intake, Environment and Housing

The ASV Guidelines frame assessment as a continuous process that begins before admission and runs through the whole stay: collect history, manage the environment to minimise fear and stress, house appropriately, keep routines predictable, and treat enrichment as essential, not optional.

ASV: intake Collecting information before admission lets the shelter offer services that may prevent intake (outpatient behavioural care, rehoming resources, spay-neuter, return to field). Personnel must collect a thorough behavioural history at or near intake, for every animal regardless of source, including the reasons for surrender and previously observed behaviour. Available information about aggression must be recorded with an objective description of the actions and circumstances. ASV
Exam trap: intake communication Intake staff should be trained in communication techniques that reduce social desirability bias: asking open-ended questions, using objective language, and active listening. Expect a stem testing the open-ended plus objective-language pairing. ASV

Environmental management and housing

  • Create an environment that minimises stimuli that induce fear, stress, and frustration; shelters must have written policies supporting mental wellbeing. ASV
  • Housing has a large impact on welfare; novel environments are especially stressful for shy, under-socialised, or geriatric cats and dogs. ASV
  • Prey species must be housed away from predatory species at all times (olfactory, auditory, and visual separation). Note that cats are predators but may also be prey to dogs. ASV

Daily routine and predictability

A consistent, structured environment that minimises reassignment of enclosures, caregivers, and schedules protects against chronic fear and anxiety. ASV

Exam trap: drivers of unpredictability The three named sources are lack of routine in daily care, frequent disruption of enclosure set-up, and irregular patterns of continuous light or darkness. The light or darkness item is the one most often omitted. ASV

Enrichment and socialisation

Enrichment provides social interaction, physical and mental stimulation, opportunities for species-typical behaviour, and choice and control over the environment. ASV

  • Successful programs promote emotional wellbeing and reduce undesirable behaviours. ASV
  • Enrichment must be given the same significance as nutrition and medical care, and is never optional. ASV

Dog Behaviour Assessment and Information Sources

Behaviour assessment is the process of collecting information about an individual animal's behaviour to understand its needs, address welfare concerns, and match it to the right outcome. Over the past two decades, formal evaluation tests have been shown to predict future behaviour poorly, so the current recommendation is a mixed, longitudinal model: objective history plus objective observation across many contexts.

Why formal testing for every animal is no longer recommended Behaviour evaluation tests fail to reliably predict future behaviour, particularly aggression, in a new home. Running one stressful subtest after another can worsen results and harm welfare, and formal testing consumes time and resources and can raise length of stay. Named test batteries: SAFER, Assess-a-Pet, Match-up II. your notes

History from the owner

FindingDetail
Confidentiality (103-item C-BARQ)When told responses were confidential, owners more readily disclosed aggression toward family members and fear of strangers. No difference for stranger-directed aggression, dog-directed aggression or fear, non-social fear, separation-related, or attachment behaviours.
Confidentiality (42-item C-BARQ)No difference between confidential and non-confidential groups. Self-administered questionnaires may reduce social desirability bias, but more research is needed.
Surrender at intakeOwners told their information would inform adoption decisions were less forthcoming about owner-directed aggression and stranger-directed fear.
Exam trap: post-adoption correlations over time At 2 weeks, surrender reports correlate with aggression toward unfamiliar people, urination when left alone, and destructive chewing. At 6 weeks there are fewer correlations overall. Dogs reported by owners NOT to guard resources were similarly unlikely to guard at home. your notes

Observation across contexts

Medical examination

  • Vet exams are stressful for most dogs: close to 80% of owned dogs show fear during a standardised exam, and dogs without their owners are significantly more stressed. your notes
  • A dog reported fearful at the vet (55%) is also likely to show fear in new situations (68%), pain or touch sensitivity (35%), and non-social fear (37%). your notes
  • Exam observation is most useful for learning how the dog responds to stress and for building a handling or behaviour-modification plan, interpreted alongside other contexts. your notes

Daily care staff and volunteers

  • Untrained daily-care observation can be inaccurate: staff correctly predicted only about one third of problems later reported by adopters, while behavioural tests predicted almost 75%. your notes
  • A likely reason is reluctance to disclose negative information that could hurt a dog's adoption chances. your notes
  • Close to half of staff mistook aggression for excitement or frustration, but rarely the reverse, so people tend to judge dogs positively. Fearful dogs were recognised well by experts and non-experts alike. your notes
  • Experts and non-experts were equally poor at telling play from aggression. Non-experts focus on the head; trainers attend to the body and read motivation and emotion better. Training improves non-expert proficiency, and video improves learning more than photos and text. your notes

Walks, socialisation, in-kennel, playgroups, foster

  • Walks and field trips reveal kennel-exit enthusiasm, leash and harness tolerance, interest in handlers and environment, and reactions to passers-by, children, joggers, cyclists, and skateboarders. your notes
  • In-kennel: even friendly dogs may show aggression when confined. Aggression toward people is HIGHEST when the dog is in its kennel; aggression toward dogs tends to be lowest there. Monitoring in-kennel behaviour gives early warning. your notes
  • Playgroups: dogs that joined a playgroup before evaluation reacted more positively to a stimulus dog, and regular adult interactions reduced aggression in evaluation. your notes
  • Foster and returns are the most accurate reflection of home behaviour. Dogs returning from foster were about 20 times more likely to be adopted or transferred, and about 85% of dogs returned within 30 days were quickly re-adopted. Sleepovers reveal little, since most dogs just sleep. your notes

Behaviour Evaluation Science

If a shelter chooses to use a standardised evaluation, it should fare well on feasibility, reliability, and validity, and the results must be read against the prevalence of the behaviour of concern. The mathematics of low-prevalence testing is the single most testable idea in this section.

Three criteria of a good evaluation

CriterionWhat it means
FeasibilityBalance information against throughput: no longer than 30 minutes, no more than 2 handlers and a stimulus dog, a documented step-by-step protocol, and an easy scoring system.
ReliabilityIntra-rater, inter-rater (IRR), test-retest, and internal consistency. Good IRR implies good intra-rater reliability. Evaluator sex and position (standing, kneeling, sitting) change the dog's response.
ValiditySensitivity, specificity, and positive and negative predictive values, all of which depend on prevalence in the tested population.
Exam trap: choosing predictive value by cost of error For aggression, favour a test with higher NEGATIVE predictive value, because adopting out a missed aggressive dog is unacceptable. For low-cost problems (mild fear, mouthiness), balance positive and negative predictive value. At low prevalence most errors are false positives; at high prevalence most are false negatives. your notes
Reference: the coin-flip paper Patronek and Bradley simulated that as many as 78% of dogs flagged aggressive on an evaluation are likely false positives, and even at 85% sensitivity and specificity the false-positive share is about 48%. The paper recommends discontinuing standardised behaviour evaluations and underlines that aggression must be safely corroborated in additional contexts. std ref

Subtests

  • Handling: no procedure proven superior. Begin with friendly, non-invasive touch (shoulders, side, rump), then advance to less pleasant contact (paw lift, toe-webbing pinch, muzzle hold, mouth open, thigh pinch) to mimic grooming or a vet visit. your notes
  • Play: short bursts of tag or tug reveal whether arousal becomes unruly play, mouthiness, sexual behaviour, or aggression. Dogs never kept as pets may not know how to play. your notes
  • Food guarding (assess-a-hand): controversial. The test predicts ABSENCE better, with 78% of non-guarders in the test also non-guarding at home. Many shelter food-guarders did not guard in the home, adopters cared little, and suspending the test did not change the roughly 9% return rate. your notes
  • Doll test: a toddler-sized doll simulates a child approaching. Controversial; mimics tethering and child bites. your notes
  • Threatening stranger: sensitive to tester sex, and most informative with a MALE tester, since dogs tend to be more reactive to men. your notes
  • Dog-dog: the stimulus dog must be moderately social and tolerant; watch for a friendly stimulus dog turning defensive after repeated tests. your notes
  • Model (fake) dog: good agreement for friendliness, moderate for fearfulness, POOR for aggressiveness. Useful as an initial screen, not a sole predictor. your notes

Cat Behaviour Assessment

For cats the first problem is telling a frightened pet cat from an unsocialised one, then predicting how a socialised cat will behave at home. Several frameworks exist, each with trade-offs between data quality and resource cost.

The discrimination problem

  • Minimum hold times to decide feral versus frightened range from 1 day to more than 7 days, with potential euthanasia for feral cats after the hold. your notes
  • A good predictive assessment could cut animal-care days, improve welfare, and raise live-release rates. your notes

Trait ratingExperienced raters score traits (agreeableness, neuroticism) on a scaleCaptures subtle behaviour, but needs long familiarity and is subjective

Exam trap: structured feline test metrics A structured test yields a rating of severity (1 to 5), frequency, duration, proportion, and latency (for example, meowed 4 times, hid in the box for 60% of the period, approached after 37 seconds). your notes

Exam trap: Feline-ality validity Despite its operational benefits, the caveat is that as of yet no studies have evaluated the reliability or validity of the Feline-ality scales in shelters. your notes

Hiding box

Providing a hiding box facilitated a quicker reduction in stress after arrival; cats with boxes were more likely to approach the cage front and spent less time behind the litter box. Optimising shelter conditions is one of the most impactful ways to improve cat wellbeing. your notes

Risk, Behaviour Modification and Outcomes

When behaviour or welfare concerns arise, shelters develop an individualised plan, provide behavioural support, and make timely outcome decisions. Training and modification must follow least-intrusive, minimally-aversive principles, and risk to people must be addressed promptly.

ASV: training and behaviour modification Training MUST follow Least Intrusive, Minimally Aversive (LIMA) principles and the Humane Hierarchy of Behaviour Change. Positive reinforcement improves health, welfare, and adoptability; punishment-based methods can increase fear, anxiety, and aggression and compromise safety and welfare. It is unacceptable to use physical force as punishment. ASV

Behaviour medication

  • Must be strongly considered for emotional-health welfare concerns, for immediate intake or housing stress, or for long-term problems (separation-related, fear of people, chronic shelter stress). ASV
  • Concerns must be objectively assessed and diagnosed, with clear treatment goals, under veterinary advice or written protocols, and dispensed per regulation. ASV
  • Goal: improve welfare, reduce stress and anxiety, and support the behaviour plan, as part of a comprehensive plan (assessment, environment, routine, foster, enrichment, training, monitoring). ASV
ASV: risk assessment of aggression Shelters MUST respond promptly to behaviour posing a significant safety risk and must have protocols to identify and manage animals at high risk of harm. Dogs surrendered with credible reports of severe aggression toward people or other dogs should be euthanised as quickly as legally possible, since there is no benefit to holding them. Euthanasia is the appropriate outcome for animals at high risk of serious harm to people, and legal consultation can help when writing these protocols. ASV
Exam trap: single-incident euthanasia and thin slicing A euthanasia decision should rest on a single aggression incident only if it is truly egregious (a severe attack on a person or dog); otherwise confirm the behaviour safely in other contexts. Experts can decide accurately from a few observations (thin slicing), but non-experts who thin-slice are prone to error. Fast-track dogs are those with a good surrender report and high sociability to people and dogs. your notes

Most common shelter behaviours

  • Anxiety or fear about unfamiliar people, handling, or other dogs, often shown as avoidance including aggression. ASV
  • Frustration about other dogs, withdrawal of attention or play, or limited activity and stimulation. ASV
  • Anxiety or fear about resources (toys, food) or about separation from people. ASV

Assessment timing

Rehoming, adoption criteria, and follow-up

  • Provide resources and a safe, gradual, controlled introduction to children and resident pets, and communicate any behaviour concerns to reduce returns. your notes
  • Basic adoption criteria: the animal must be desexed; no adoption to anyone with a known history of animal or child abuse, anyone suspected of being intoxicated at application, or for use as a food source. your notes
  • Guiding placement values: the match suits the animal and family, appropriate veterinary care, social and companionship needs met, a liveable environment, and the pet respected and valued. your notes
  • Follow-up: more than one behaviour problem was reported by about 51% of adopters at 1 week and 1 month; house training was the top dog issue and chewing or scratching the top cat issue. Conversation-based adoptions and good interpretation reduce mismatches. your notes

Welfare and Ethical Decision Making

Welfare is multidimensional and no single measure is a gold standard, so shelters combine input-based and animal-based measures within a recognised framework, monitor over time, and use structured ethical reasoning to make and defend outcome decisions.

Three viewpoints of welfare

FrameworkComponents
Five FreedomsFrom thirst and hunger; from distress and fear; from injury and pain; from physical discomfort; to express normal behaviour
Welfare Quality (4 principles)Good feeding, good housing, good health, appropriate behaviour
Five DomainsNutrition, environment, health, behaviour, mental state

std ref Input-based measures (sound, air quality, temperature, humidity, housing, resources, management) sit alongside animal-based measures. Qualitative Behaviour Assessment (QBA) rates the expressivity of the whole animal as it engages with its environment and shows high inter-rater agreement.

Cortisol and monitoring caveats

Exam trap: cortisol and behaviour as welfare markers Plasma cortisol is highest in the first 3 days; urinary cortisol peaks around day 17; dogs with prior kenneling experience have LOWER urinary cortisol than naive dogs. A dog's behaviour cannot be used as a sole predictor of stress, since behavioural stress is expressed many ways and correlates poorly with plasma cortisol. Some dogs performing stereotypies had better welfare than those that did not, and stereotypy can persist as a behavioural scar. your notes

Daily rounds

Daily rounds are a systematic monitoring process and one of the most effective tools for protecting welfare, helping create urgency, move animals through the system, reduce LOS, and prevent crowding. your notes

  • Team includes shelter operations, medical, and behaviour perspectives, led by a population manager, with protected time. your notes
  • Conduct rounds before cleaning and feeding to catch inappetent animals; for group housing, sometimes during feeding to confirm access. your notes
  • Six questions: Who are you? How are you? Are you where you should be? What can we provide today? Where are you going? Are you meeting the Five Freedoms or domains? your notes

Ethical decision making and welfare threats

  • Respond to welfare threats by addressing fundamental causes (reduce stressors, change the environment) rather than ad-hoc treatment of signs; pharmacology should not replace environmental change. your notes
  • Behavioural restriction is a powerful psychological stressor; increasing an animal's ability to predict and control its environment protects wellbeing. your notes
  • A structured ethical approach makes decisions inclusive, transparent, and evidence-based, and easier to justify to those with opposing views. your notes
  • Moral stress, the stress of being unable to do what one believes is right, can drive medical error, compassion fatigue, and burnout; organisational structures help more than individual resilience techniques alone. your notes
