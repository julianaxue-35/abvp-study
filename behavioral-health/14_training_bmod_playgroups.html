Training and Adoptability

Reward-based training increases adoptability. The data points to know are the 1.4x adoption effect of sit-at-the-front, the 27.5% counseling lift, the praise-as-ineffective-reinforcer finding, and the tiered volunteer model.

Target behaviours for adoptability

  • Name recognition, eye contact, hand targeting, trick training, stay, walking past other dogs, crate training, drop it, leave it, and sitting at the front of the kennel when people approach. your notes
  • Adopter research shows people prefer dogs that come to the front of the pen, sit, interact, and refrain from barking. your notes
Exam trap: 1.4x adoption but no LOS effect Teaching dogs to approach the front of the kennel and sit when people approach made them 1.4 times more likely to be adopted, but had no effect on overall length of stay. your notes

Tiered volunteer model

  • Lower tier: socialisation, simple tasks (sit, down). Higher tier: agility, nosework, cooperative care for low-stress handling, work with behaviourally challenged dogs. Develop relationships with local dog trainers. your notes

Reward-based training and reinforcers

  • Use reward-based (positive reinforcement) techniques. Regular human interaction via socialisation, play, and obedience training is clearly beneficial. your notes
  • Focus behaviour efforts on older adolescents and adult dogs. your notes
Exam trap: praise is an ineffective reinforcer in shelters Social interaction (praise and petting) has been shown to be an ineffective primary reinforcer for shelter dogs compared with food rewards. your notes

Pre and post adoption counseling

Implementing pre and post adoption counseling resulted in 27.5% more adopted dogs than a comparable shelter without the programme. your notes

Behaviour Modification

BMOD for bite histories, intraspecific aggression, excessive arousal, and fear. The high-yield numbers: 87% success for bite-history DSCC, 20 to 49% intraspecific aggression prevalence, 1% return rate for BMOD dogs versus 16% general, and the ASPCA BRC fear programme outcomes.

Bite-history rehabilitation

  • Requires DSCC to triggering stimuli, teaching safety and warning cues, training alternative behaviours, and bite inhibition. 300 dogs, shortest LOS one year, longest three years, 87% success rate (defined as adoption with no subsequent report of biting). your notes

Intraspecific aggression

  • In 43 Canadian shelters, 20 to 49% of dogs exhibited intraspecific aggression. BMOD focused on walking calmly on leash and maintaining eye contact with a handler in the presence of other dogs. Playgroup programmes can also help (see Playgroup Models tab). your notes

Excessive arousal

  • Can increase LOS and reduce adoption likelihood. Characterised by hyper-responsiveness, exaggerated emotionality, and reduced behavioural inhibition. your notes
  • Signs: in-kennel pacing, circling, spinning, bouncing, barking; out-of-kennel jumping, mouthing, leash tugging, clothes grabbing, snatching treats, and sometimes redirection. your notes

Fear in shelter dogs

  • Social and non-social fear: fear of handling, restraint, people, dogs, being alone, environments, objects, loud noises, or anything unfamiliar (neophobia). Responses include trembling, panting, freezing, withdrawal, hiding, escape attempts, and defensive aggression. your notes
  • Interventions: DSCC (gradual exposure below threshold paired with something positive), operant conditioning. Flooding (intense stimulus, no escape) risks learned helplessness. your notes
  • ASPCA BRC programme: specialised kennels, restricted handling, designated low-stress handlers, daily enrichment, standardised BMOD, fluoxetine and gabapentin. 66 fearful dogs, 36% met adoptability guidelines, 44% improved but needed further work. Four target categories: fear of people, fear of handling, fear of leashing and walking, fear of novelty. your notes

BMOD success and return rates

  • Most adopters report concerns in the first weeks but rarely after three months; few regret adopting behaviourally rehabilitated dogs. your notes
  • Shelters screen adopters, provide specialised counseling at adoption, and offer post-adoption support. 1% of BMOD dogs were returned versus a general return rate of 16%. your notes

Playgroup Models and Operations

Two main models, one maximising efficiency (DPFL) and one minimising aversives (SPA). The exam tests the group sizes, handler ratios, cortisol outcomes, and equipment rules.

DPFL versus SPA

ParameterDPFL (Dogs Playing for Life)SPA (Shelter Playgroup Alliance)
PhilosophyLarge-scale enrichment, operational efficiency, and real-time behavioural assessmentLIMA-based: safety, choice, control, and consent
Group sizeLarge groups (6+ dogs)Low-risk: 2 to 3 dogs; high-risk: 4 to 6 dogs
Handler ratiosNo general restriction; hands-off styleLow-risk: 2:1 dog to handler; high-risk: 3:1 dog to handler
ToolsAirhorns, break sticks, spray bottles, shake cans; accepts higher conflict riskTreats, vocal praise, protected contact intros, muzzles, longlines; restricts aversives
DisadvantagesHigher conflict and flooding riskReduced efficiency, high staff demand, difficult for high-intake shelters
Cortisol outcomeLarge groups with aversives: decreased cortisolSmall groups with treats and praise: decreased cortisol; small groups with aversives: increased cortisol
Exam trap: cortisol and aversives Post-playgroup salivary cortisol increased only in dogs participating in small playgroups that regularly used aversives. Dogs in both the large-group (with aversives) control and the small-group (with treats and vocal praise) showed decreased cortisol. The LIMA-based approach improved in-kennel welfare and adoptability indicators and produced significantly lower cortisol than aversive-using groups. your notes

Veterinary clearance and candidate selection

  • All candidates must be cleared by veterinary staff. Play is species-typical but not universally reinforcing or safe. your notes
  • Consent tests (choice tests, preference assessments) confirm willingness. On-leash reactivity and barrier reactivity are not accurate predictors of off-leash aggression or sociability. your notes
  • Average participation: about 50% of the shelter dog population. Higher in low-intake shelters. your notes
  • Helper dogs can be used but should be rotated to avoid fatigue, elevated stress, and injury risk. your notes

PE and biosecurity considerations

  • Suspend or modify playgroups during outbreaks. Disease stats: CPV less than 1%, distemper 0%, CIV 1%, CIRDC 19%. Dogs with contagious illness, injuries, or pain are excluded. your notes
  • Psychotropic medications, steroids, and analgesics may affect behaviour and should inform group composition. Neuter status, age, weight, size, and sex should be considered. Puppies over 20 weeks (fully vaccinated) may join; puppy-only pairings should be supervised. your notes

Physical environment

  • High, secure fencing; multiple connected zones with separate entrance and exit pens and two-way gates; elevated platforms, ramps, benches, kiddie pools; rest and avoidance areas; regular inspection. your notes
  • Flat, washable collars with quick-release snaps (no buckles); basket muzzles if needed; 6-foot leashes or biothane longlines. Gentle Leader headcollars are not recommended. your notes
  • Interruption tools (airhorns, compressed air, citronella spray, shake cans, water spray bottles, steel bowls for noise, squeaky toys) in weather-resistant containers. Toys and treats may trigger resource guarding. your notes
ASV guideline Optimise human and animal safety by limiting the number of dogs in playgroups based on personnel training, skillset, play-yard size, individual dog behaviour, and shelter resources. ASV

Play, Conflict and Evaluation

Healthy play has specific ethological markers. The exam tests self-handicapping, the play-bow pause, which behaviours are misread as play versus aggression, and why play wrestling carries the highest conflict risk.

Healthy social play: definition and components

  • Defined as simultaneous, reciprocal affiliation between conspecifics that includes at least one of: self-handicapping, inhibited biting, play wrestling, play chasing, or exaggerated low-intensity predatory behaviours. your notes
  • Self-handicapping: one dog voluntarily uses less advantageous strategies to maintain the interaction. your notes
  • Inhibited biting: oral contact on a conspecific's body without causing injury or distress vocalisation. your notes
  • Play wrestling: continuous bodily contact with inhibited biting, shaking, growling, and forceful movement, reciprocated by the partner, without injury or obvious distress. your notes
  • Play chasing: running alongside or behind a conspecific with no observable aggression, fear, or distress in the dog being chased. your notes
  • Reliable play signal: a play bow with a distinct pause, often followed by more active behaviours. your notes

Commonly misinterpreted behaviours

Direction of errorBehaviours
Incorrectly read as aggressive (actually can be play)Piloerection, inhibited biting, herding, standing over, showing teeth
Incorrectly read as play (actually can be arousal or conflict)Barking, bouncing, ears erect and forward, tail wagging

Dog-to-dog conflict

  • Play wrestling carries the highest risk of escalating into play-induced conflict and aggression. your notes
  • True conflict signs: tucked or raised elevated tail, full-body hackles with a visibly stiff body, fixed stare, low growls, lunging, bite attempts. your notes
  • One dog should not be playing at the expense of another. Watch for cowering, hiding, trembling, or one dog repeatedly displacing others. Redirect both dogs simultaneously. your notes
  • Inter-dog play most often occurs between two dogs. Aggression risk increases when more than two dogs join an interaction, but there is no correlation between average playgroup size and increased fights or injuries. Average playgroup size across shelters is about 8 dogs. your notes

Handler roles

  • Human interaction significantly increases positive social interactions, including social play. Handlers should provide verbal praise and appropriate petting to facilitate healthy play, especially among familiar dogs. your notes
  • Handlers who bow, lunge, clap, pat the floor, or chase can evoke human-dog play invitations that compete with dog-to-dog play. If the session's goal is assessing conspecific sociability, handlers should remain neutral. Handlers can also overmanage interactions and disrupt healthy play. your notes

Mounting as displacement

Mounting can manifest as a displacement behaviour induced by frustration or a high motivation to engage in a different behaviour that is not currently possible. It is not necessarily sexual or aggressive. your notes

Documentation and evaluation

  • Use the SPIDER framework: Setting goals, Planning, Implementation, Documentation, Evaluation, and Readjustment. your notes
  • Monitor goals, behavioural dynamics, and incidents. Surveillance cameras are valuable for replaying fights to identify antecedents and consequences. Voice-to-text apps can facilitate real-time documentation. your notes
  • A conflict and fight management plan must be planned, prepared, and articulated among staff before playgroups begin. Everyone involved must have reviewed protocols and had the opportunity to contribute. your notes
