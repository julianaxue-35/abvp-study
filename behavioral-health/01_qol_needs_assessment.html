QOL and Welfare Foundations

Quality of life is broad, complex, and deeply individual. The shelter mandate is to make every attempt to sustain it, which means understanding what shapes each animal's needs and recognising that physical and emotional health move together.

The six determinants of emotional and behavioural needs

  • Emotional and behavioural needs are determined by environment, species, genetics, personality, prior socialisation, and life experiences. your notes
  • These overlap and are non-hierarchical: a deficit in one (for example poor prior socialisation or genetics) changes how the animal responds to the others (for example its environment). your notes

Physical and emotional health are bidirectional

Emotional and behavioural health affect physical health, and physical health affects emotional health. A decline in physical welfare, such as undiagnosed chronic pain or disease, degrades affective states, while chronic negative emotional states, such as shelter-induced distress, impair immune function and slow medical recovery. your notes

What quality of life is

  • Every attempt must be made to sustain QOL for shelter animals, even though QOL, like happiness, remains difficult to define universally. your notes
  • Both physical and emotional factors contribute to QOL, wellbeing, or welfare; these factors are broad, complex, and very individual. your notes
  • QOL is comprised of an array of affective states, broadly classified as comfort-discomfort and pleasure states. In general, the greater the pleasant and the lesser the unpleasant effects, the higher the QOL. your notes
Why QOL is individual Because needs are individual, an environment that produces pleasure in one animal may produce fear or discomfort in another. Assess the individual in front of you, not the species average. your notes

Staff competency: the three pillars

  • Read animal body language. your notes
  • Describe behaviour objectively, using factual, non-anthropomorphic terminology. your notes
  • Interpret and respond to body language and behaviour, adjusting the animal's care plan or handling. All relevant personnel must be trained, not only behaviour staff, so that every interaction protects QOL. your notes
Exam trap: objective versus anthropomorphic Objective: the dog is cowering in the back corner with its tail tucked below the pelvic midline. Anthropomorphic and unacceptable for the record: the dog is spiteful, the cat is furious, the kitten is sad. your notes

Welfare Frameworks

The notes point to the Five Freedoms. The ASV actually frames shelter welfare through the Five Domains, which is derived from the Five Freedoms and, crucially, adds positive welfare states, matching the notes' emphasis on pleasure as well as comfort.

The Five Freedoms

  • Freedom from hunger and thirst; freedom from discomfort; freedom from pain, injury, or disease; freedom to express normal behaviour; and freedom from fear and distress. ASV
  • These define essential welfare but focus on avoiding negative experiences. Positive experiences are also needed for a life worth living, which is why the framework was extended. ASV

The Five Domains

The Five Domains model is derived from the Five Freedoms and does two new things. First, it gives a spectrum for each domain, including not just the absence of pain but the presence of comfort, fitness, and other positive experiences. Second, it shows that a positive welfare state can occur even when a need is not fully met, and that a negative state can result from a single unmet need. ASV

DomainPositive experiencesNegative experiences
1. NutritionEnough fresh water and balanced, varied foodRestricted or poor-quality, monotonous food and water
2. EnvironmentComfortable, temperate, clean, routine, with interest and varietyToo hot or cold, too bright or dark, too loud or quiet, unpredictable, soiled, monotonous
3. HealthPhysical health, good function and body condition, restful sleepDisease, pain, body dysfunction or impairment, poor fitness
4. OpportunityChoice of environment and interaction, behavioural variety (play, forage, rest), noveltyBarren cage, confined space, restraint, separation from people or species, unavoidable sensory inputs
5. Mental stateSatisfied, engaged, comfortable, playful, confident, calmFearful or anxious, frustrated, bored, lonely, exhausted, in pain or discomfort
The two ASV worked examples A stray cat on cage rest with a healing pelvic fracture (restricted agency and pain) can still have an overall positive welfare state when appropriately treated and housed in an enriched foster home. Conversely, a well-fed, physically healthy dog confined long-term to a kennel (restricted agency) may have profound distress and overall negative welfare. A single unmet need can drive welfare either way. ASV

How the ASV ranks its statements

  • Unacceptable: avoid or prevent without exception. Must: necessary to ensure humane care. Should: strongly recommended, expected in most circumstances. Ideal: implemented when resources allow. ASV

Assessing Needs and QOL

Needs and QOL are read through ongoing, objective behavioural observation, set against the Five Domains and the shelter's capacity for care. When QOL is profoundly and unresolvably compromised, the outcome pathway must be reconsidered.

Assessment versus evaluation

TermWhat it is
Behaviour assessmentAn ongoing process of observing and interpreting an individual animal's behaviour throughout its shelter stay, to understand its needs, address welfare concerns, and guide handling, outcome, and placement decisions
Behaviour evaluationA structured procedure or test in which responses to a series of subtests are observed one after another (for example SAFER, Assess-a-Pet, Match-up II)
Timing Behavioural information is most reliable once acute intake stress has settled, rather than in the first 24 to 48 hours, when arousal distorts behaviour. This connects to the Assessment and Decision Making and Learning Theory hubs. ASV

Objective description protects the record

All relevant personnel must record behaviour with factual, observable terms (body position, posture, distance, specific actions) rather than anthropomorphic labels. Objective data lets the next staff member interpret and respond consistently, which is the point of training everyone, not just behaviour specialists. ASV

Capacity for Care, defined by the Five Domains

Capacity for Care is the total resources (humane housing, trained personnel, medical care, and appropriate outcomes) required to promote positive welfare, as described by the Five Domains, for all the animals in or coming into the shelter's care. Exceeding capacity for care degrades welfare across the population. ASV

When QOL is profoundly compromised

Outcome implication A prolonged, severe negative emotional state that cannot be mitigated means QOL is profoundly compromised, and the animal's care or outcome pathway must be re-evaluated. Euthanasia may be the appropriate outcome to prevent unresolvable suffering. This connects to the euthanasia position statements. ASV

Behavioural welfare indicators — what to watch for notesASV

Behavioural observation is the primary welfare monitoring tool for shelter animals. The indicators below, assessed during daily rounds, translate the Five Domains into what is actually visible at cage-side.

IndicatorWhat it signals
Hiding durationCats spending most of the housing period in the hide box, or dogs wedged unresponsively in the back corner, signal fear, chronic stress, or acute pain. Persistent, unbroken hiding in a cat beyond 24–48 hours warrants welfare review.
Voluntary appetiteEating voluntarily is one of the clearest welfare signals: reduced intake indicates pain, illness, chronic stress, or food aversion. Partial eating is better than none. Total anorexia in a cat beyond 48–72 hours is a veterinary emergency.
Self-groomingAbsent grooming indicates depression, pain, or severe stress. Excessive or focal over-grooming indicates anxiety, pain at a specific site, or a compulsive disorder. Normal self-grooming is a positive welfare indicator.
Social responsivenessWillingness to approach familiar staff, make eye contact, solicit play, or accept handling is a sensitive real-time welfare indicator. Marked withdrawal from previously sociable animals is a red flag even in the absence of physical illness.
Stereotypies and repetitive behavioursPacing, circling, weaving, wall bouncing, or bar chewing are not "shelter boredom" — they indicate chronic stress, understimulation, or frustration that has exceeded the animal's coping capacity. These behaviours warrant welfare intervention, not observation alone.
Posture and resting qualityHunched, rounded back, or guarded posture suggests pain or fear. A cat pressed into the back of the cage in a compact "loaf" with wide eyes is showing defensive withdrawal, not relaxation. Lateral, unguarded sleep is a positive welfare indicator.
Play and exploratory behaviourPlay, investigation of enrichment, and soliciting interaction are among the most reliable markers of positive welfare. Sustained absence of any play or exploration — even when enrichment is offered — indicates compromised QOL requiring action.
Exam trap · absence of distress ≠ good welfareAn animal that stops barking, stops pacing, and stops seeking contact is not "settling in." Behavioural depression is a late-stage deterioration response that can superficially resemble calm. A dog that was highly reactive at intake and now lies flat at the back of the kennel without responding to its name is not improving — it may be in a learned helplessness state. Welfare assessment requires tracking positive indicators (voluntary engagement, appetite, play) alongside the absence of distress behaviours.
