Medical and Neurological Contributors, and Senior Pets

Behavioural change often precedes severe neurological signs by weeks to months. Rule out medical and neurological causes BEFORE labelling a problem behavioural. The senior-pet section anchors on CCDS and its DISHA acronym.

Brain regions that drive behavioural signs when damaged

RegionFunctionsSigns when damaged
Limbic systemMemory, emotions, basic drives (sexual, anxiety, pleasure)Personality change, increased fearfulness and aggression, seizures
Forebrain (incl. prefrontal)Cognitive behaviour, motor planning, thought, perception, awarenessTemperament change, loss of previously learned behaviours, failure to recognise or respond appropriately, altered consciousness and mentation
BrainstemArousal and conscious response to stimuliAltered response to stimuli, dullness and stupor, coma

Specific contributors

CategoryKey points
Intracranial neoplasiaPRIMARY tumours (meningioma, astrocytoma, neuroblastoma, oligodendroglioma, ependymoma) cause INSIDIOUS, slowly progressive signs (dementia, aggression, altered habits, seizures, head pressing, housesoiling, circling, pacing, panting). Usually dogs over 7 years; age alone cannot rule out a brain tumour, the brain is the second most common neoplasia site. SECONDARY metastatic tumours produce ACUTE changes. Cats: meningioma presents as "not being themselves," reduced play, episodes of lethargy and aggression.
SeizuresFocal motor (involuntary movement of one body part) or focal sensory (tingling, pain, or visual hallucination, e.g. fly biting or fly snapping). Other signs: head pressing, vocalising, aimless walking or running. Cats: hippocampal necrosis is one cause; also metabolic disease (DM), hepatic encephalopathy, neoplasia, meningoencephalitis.
ToxicosisNeurological signs: ataxia, stupor, seizures, or death.
DegenerativeHeritable, first weeks to months of life. Cerebellar abiotrophy: normal at birth, signs appear 2 to 9 weeks (ataxia, intention tremors, swaying, hypermetria, head tilt, broad-based stance); onset before 4 months; reported in many breeds; less common in cats. Lysosomal storage disease: rare genetic defects, purebred animals, history of inbreeding, signs in the first year (ataxia, disorientation, weakness, behavioural change); neuronal ceroid lipofuscinosis is one example.
InflammatoryDiffuse, focal, or multifocal. Encephalitis or parenchymal CNS inflammation: depression, stupor, coma, or altered consciousness.
UrogenitalUrinary incontinence (conscious vs unconscious voiding); urethral sphincter mechanism incompetence (USMI) is a risk in female dogs over 20 kg neutered before 3 months. Bladder storage dysfunction (detrusor instability, UTI, neoplastic infiltration, ectopic ureters). Urine marking in dogs often reflects conflict, frustration, or anxiety. Submissive or excitement urination must NOT be punished, even acting upset worsens it.

Canine Cognitive Dysfunction Syndrome (CCDS)

  • Neurodegenerative disorder of senior dogs and cats with progressive brain pathology and cognitive decline; similar findings to Alzheimer's in humans. your notes
  • Pathology: reduced frontal lobe volume with increased ventricular size, meningeal calcification, demyelination, increased lipofuscin and apoptotic bodies, neuroaxonal degeneration, reduction in neurons. Functional: catecholamine depletion, increased MAOB activity, cholinergic decline, altered REM sleep, increased oxidative damage from reactive oxygen species. your notes
  • Onset: traditionally diagnosed in dogs at 11 years and older. Spatial-memory impairment can appear as early as 6 to 9 years, because the frontal lobe atrophies and accumulates beta-amyloid before other regions. Cats start around 10 to 11 years. your notes
  • Reassess every 6 months; progression is typical over 6 to 12 months. your notes
Exam trap: DISHA The five letters used to screen for CCDS: Disorientation, altered Interaction with people and other pets, Sleep-wake cycle alteration, Housesoiling, Altered Activity level (with decline in memory and learning). your notes

Treatment of CCDS

InterventionNotes
SelegilineMAOB inhibitor in dogs; enhances dopamine and other catecholamines in cortex and hippocampus; demonstrated cognitive enhancement in lab and clinical settings.
Adrafinil, modafinilNoradrenergic-enhancing drugs that may improve alertness and help maintain sleep-wake cycles in older dogs.
DietHill's Prescription Diet b/d has been shown to improve signs and slow progression in dogs. Generally, fruits, vegetables, nuts, whole grains, and vitamins E and C may delay onset.
EnrichmentAlways combine diet and drugs with environmental enrichment.
Critical drug interaction in senior pets Selegiline must NOT be used concurrently with other MAO inhibitors, OR with SSRIs or TCAs (serotonin syndrome risk). Tramadol, SAMe, tryptophan, and buspirone all add to serotonin and add risk. Senior pets are also more sensitive to anticholinergic or sedating drugs; anxiolytic disinhibition can paradoxically increase aggression in situations where the pet would have otherwise retreated. your notes

Compulsive Disorders and Stereotypies

An obsessive-compulsive disorder is an abnormal, invariant repetition of a motor pattern that serves no apparent function. Most arise prepubertal (3 to 6 months) or before social maturity (12 to 36 months) after a period of conflict, change, or distress. Mechanism is abnormal serotonin transmission, which is why SSRIs and clomipramine are first line.

Origins: normal behaviour becomes maladaptive

Risk factors

  • Problems with early environment, maternal care, and handling. your notes
  • Socialisation and environmental localisation problems. your notes
  • Inadequate enrichment and outlets for normal behaviour (social interaction, object play, exercise). your notes
  • Unpredictable schedule or routine; insufficient or inconsistent training and communication. your notes
  • Excessive, unnecessary, or unpredictable positive punishment. your notes
  • Frustration and inability to achieve goals; conflict and competing motivation. your notes
  • Social anxiety (family changes, new or lost pets); household anxiety (moving, renovation). your notes
  • Intense or recurrent fear or anxiety (e.g. noise sensitivity). your notes

Pharmacology highlights (cross-reference: Behaviour Medications hub for full formulary)

  • Clomipramine is the most selective serotonin-reuptake inhibitor of all the TCAs; effective for ALD, spinning, tail chasing, and feline psychogenic alopecia. TCAs OTHER than clomipramine have NOT been found as effective because they have less SSRI effect. your notes
  • SSRIs (paroxetine, fluoxetine, sertraline, fluvoxamine, citalopram) are often effective. SSRI or TCA monotherapy reduces compulsive behaviour by 50 to 75% within 4 weeks. your notes
  • If response is insufficient at 4 to 6 weeks without adverse effects, doses can be DOUBLED, with monitoring for serotonin syndrome. your notes
  • NMDA antagonists (e.g. memantine) block glutamate-sensitive NMDA receptors; 64% effective within 2 weeks, synergistic with fluoxetine. your notes
  • Opiate antagonists: naloxone 0.01 mg/kg has been reported effective for compulsive tail chasing within 20 minutes (lasting 3 hours); oral naltrexone 2.2 mg/kg SID or BID for ALD showed improvement in 7 of 11 dogs, but all relapsed. your notes
  • Dopamine AGONISTS (apomorphine, amphetamines) INDUCE stereotypic behaviour; antipsychotics block them. your notes
  • Combination adjuncts: buspirone or tryptophan; gabapentin, pregabalin, or carbamazepine; clonazepam; tramadol; phenobarbital, potassium bromide, levetiracetam. your notes
Exam trap: minimum 6 months of treatment If a compulsive disorder improves on medication, the drug must be continued for AT LEAST 6 months, often lifelong. Premature taper triggers immediate relapse. your notes

Later: hissing, growling, biting the tail or sides, exaggerated tail wagging, some cats vocalise, suddenly run, hide, and defecate while running. Hallmark: rippling or twitching of the THORACOLUMBAR skin and spasm of the EPAXIAL muscles, can be induced by rubbing the back, impossible to interrupt. Rule out medical, then neurological diseases (neuritis, cranial disease, partial seizures, spinal disease). Treatment: avoid triggers, predictable environment, enrichment; meds: clomipramine or fluoxetine, plus or minus lorazepam or clonazepam (anxiolysis and muscle relaxant).
Canine hyperesthesiaTwitching of skin and epaxial muscles, may be associated with circling or self-trauma such as licking or biting.

Behavioural treatment principles

  • Consistent, predictable environment and daily routine over which the pet has control to engage in desirable activities (resting, perching, object play) and prevent undesirable behaviours. your notes
  • Consistent, predictable consequences that use REWARDS to encourage desirable behaviour, NOT PUNISHMENT to discourage undesirable behaviour. your notes
  • Sufficient enrichment and outlets; ensure owner responses do not reinforce or aggravate the response; manage environment to prevent recurrence. your notes
  • Identify and remove stressors that lead to conflict; identify and avoid triggers; teach response substitution using reinforcement-based techniques. your notes
  • Teach owners to identify triggers and read facial and body language to pre-empt the behaviour. your notes

Separation Anxiety and Destructive Behaviours

Separation anxiety is a clinical diagnosis of exclusion built on three classic signs in the owner's absence: vocalisation, destructiveness, and housesoiling. Destructive behaviours when the owner IS home usually have a different cause. The exam trap is differentiating separation anxiety from confinement frustration, noise phobia, and barrier escape.

Separation anxiety: diagnosis

  • Exaggerated anxiety when the dog does NOT have access to family members. Must rule out medical conditions and age-related cognitive decline first. your notes
  • History clues: hyper-attachment to one or more family members; anxiety at the time of departure; owner-absent behaviour problems with other causes excluded. your notes
  • Most common signs: vocalisation, destructiveness, housesoiling during the owner's absence. Also: depression and anorexia during departure, hypersalivation, vomiting and diarrhoea, increased motor activity, excessive licking or grooming, ALD, restlessness, shivering, shaking, stereotypic behaviours (ritualised circling, pacing), occasional aggression as the owner prepares to depart. your notes
  • Risk factors: genetics, changes in routine or household or environment, other anxiety disorders, cognitive dysfunction, visit to a new environment, recent move, or recent kennel stay. your notes
  • Dogs adopted after abandonment, or who lost an attachment figure, may have less secure bonds and more anxiety than puppies raised in the same home from puppyhood. your notes
Exam trap: differentials Destructive behaviour, vocalisation, and housesoiling can arise from confinement frustration rather than true separation anxiety. Separation anxiety is consistent and OCCURS ONLY when the owner departs. Consider other differentials if the behaviour occurs while the family is home, or only randomly rather than consistently on departure. your notes

Prognosis and pharmacology

  • Good prognosis: recent onset, no concurrent anxiety disorders, motivated owners, pet responsive to psychotropic drugs. your notes
  • Meds (cross-reference: Behaviour Medications hub for full doses): pheromones, daily SSRI or TCA, combined with a benzo (alprazolam, diazepam) or clonidine given 1 hour PRE-departure. your notes
  • Effectiveness comparison: amitriptyline worked in 56% of cases versus fluoxetine or clomipramine at 75%. your notes

Four-pillar management program

PillarWhat it means in practice
1. Shape independenceGradually shape more relaxed and independent behaviours; remove rewards for ALL attention-seeking. Provide structured interactions: attention only when the pet is calm. Use the same rule structure for all rewards (sit, down, or stay first).
2. Reduce departure and homecoming anxietyAdapt the dog to the departure routine while present, so it no longer predicts departure. Depart when the dog is occupied for 15 to 20 minutes (special toys, mat, music). Avoid exposing to typical cues. Keep homecomings low-key; ignore the pet until calm.
3. Decouple departure cues from actual departuresExpose the dog to keys, coat, garage door, car start without leaving. Add a unique non-departure cue (a song, a sign on the door) for short training sessions of staying calm; mock departures progressively lengthened, on a variable schedule, ALWAYS shorter than the dog's anxiety latency.
4. Anxiety reductionAvoid punishment; give the pet more control with structure, predictability, and consistency. Confinement (crate, room, pen, tied down) only if introduced gradually and the pet is calm in it. Use food, toys, chews as rewards and distractors. Pheromones (Adaptil) on or off label. Selegiline considered for chronic anxiety but NOT concurrent with SSRI or TCA.

Canine destructive chewing

  • Inappropriate chewing in young dogs is often normal (exploration, play, scavenging, hunger, teething, escape attempts); just unacceptable to owners. Persistent chewing into adulthood may be reinforced by the owner or self-reinforcing. your notes
  • Causes to differentiate: exploration and toy retrieval, attention-seeking, inappropriate toys or tug games, inadequate enrichment, hunger, predatory, territorial, separation anxiety, noise phobia, barrier escape frustration, compulsive disorder, medical. your notes
  • Management: regular daily routine with exercise, training, and play; durable appealing chew items rotated every few days; reward chewing of toys with affection or a treat; reduce appeal of previously chewed items with cayenne pepper or commercial anti-chew spray; if hunger drives it, increase the amount, switch to higher-bulk diet, or shift feeding to match the chewing window. your notes

Canine digging

  • Innate trait, often arising in the same contexts as other destructive behaviours when stimulation is insufficient. Terriers and other breeds bred to flush prey are especially driven. Differentiate escape digging, separation anxiety digging, noise-phobia digging, and predatory digging. your notes
  • Hole locations: predatory digging is at single or multiple sites in the yard. your notes
  • Management: appropriate digging area; fill holes with rocks, gravel, water, or chicken wire; supervise outside; remote deterrents (motion-activated sprinkler, spray collar). Some dogs require supervision or confinement to prevent undesirable digging. your notes

Feline destructive scratching

  • Scratching provides BOTH a visual mark and a SCENT (interdigital pheromone). Cats punished by the owner often continue when out of sight. your notes
  • Management: provide one or two appealing locations and substrates near where the cat already scratches; stable, tall enough for the cat to stretch on its hind legs; reward desirable scratching (clicker training works well); a synthetic feline interdigital pheromone (Feliscratch) can encourage post use. your notes
  • Remote deterrents (double-sided tape, motion-activated alarm or spray) can teach avoidance without owner association, only if they do not cause fear or anxiety. your notes

Housesoiling and Marking

Punishment makes housesoiling WORSE in both species. The exam trap is the feline three-way split: medical, inappropriate toileting, and marking, each with a distinct management approach.

Canine housesoiling

  • Avoid PUNISHMENT. Punishment teaches the dog to: avoid eliminating where the owner is present; avoid all elimination in the owner's presence; avoid or become aggressive toward the owner from fear. your notes
  • Causes (also the DDx list): medical; inadequate training; submissive or excitement urination; marking; separation or confinement anxiety; cognitive dysfunction syndrome. your notes
  • Treatment summary: diagnose and treat medical; treat underlying emotional issues; choose a desirable location and substrate and reinforce there; control feeding schedule to control elimination schedule; supervise to train and to interrupt; prevent re-soiling at previously used areas (block access, change substrate, eliminate odour, avoidance condition the area); REWARD desirable behaviour, do not punish. your notes
Exam trap: submissive and excitement urination Identify triggers, interactions, or situations. Greet calmly and predictably; reinforce only behaviours incompatible with submissive, avoidant, or excitable responses (use response substitution). Confine briefly when visitors arrive; greet on leash only when calm; ignore the puppy until it has settled. Pharmacology if needed: phenylpropanolamine (alpha-adrenergic) or imipramine (TCA). Almost always improves with age. your notes

Feline housesoiling: the three-way split

1. Medical causes of feline housesoiling

  • Urinary: FIC, urolithiasis, UTI, DM, neoplasia, incontinence, conditions that limit locomotion. your notes
  • Faecal: increased frequency or urgency (IBD, colitis, diarrhoea); pain on defaecation (anal sacculitis, obstipation, constipation, neoplasia); locomotion; neurological incontinence. your notes

2. Inappropriate toileting

  • Substrate preference and aversion; location preference and aversion; aversion to the box, an unpleasant event there, or cleanliness. your notes
  • Litterbox rules: at least 1 box per cat plus 1; boxes at least 1.5 times the cat's length; scoop once or twice daily; clean weekly with hot water (no strong disinfectants); offer 3 to 4 substrate types if space allows. A healthy cat urinates 2 to 4 times daily and defaecates at least once daily, so cleanliness is paramount. your notes
  • Treatment: reward in the box; remove cause (substrate aversion, location aversion); confine to a small area with the box until the habit re-establishes; prevent return to previously soiled areas (move furniture, block access, eliminate odour with enzymatic cleaner, change the area's function with food bowls or a scratching post). your notes

3. Marking (urine spraying)

  • Posture: VERTICAL surface, cat stands with TAIL ERECT, backs up, TREADS with the rear legs, sprays urine. your notes
  • Triggers: territorial competition, anxiety, novel sights, sounds, or odours (especially from other cats); typically POSTPUBERTY. your notes
  • Risk factors: hormones, genetics or temperament, cat population density, scents from other cats, environmental change, new owner schedule, owner absence, inappropriate punishment. your notes
Exam trap: surgery for marking Castration reduces spraying in 90% of males; spaying reduces spraying in 95% of females. your notes

Marking treatment

  • Reduce stimuli (window coverings, white noise, block view of outdoor cats, remove bird feeders, reduce cats in home). your notes
  • Environmental management: increase litterbox count and location options, scoop at least once daily, weekly clean, odour counteractants. your notes
  • Pheromone therapy: Feliway spray or diffuser may reduce spraying within 1 to 4 weeks. your notes
  • Pharmacology (in Australia ONLY clomipramine 0.25 to 0.5 mg/kg PO q24h is registered for cats): fluoxetine effective at 1 mg/kg with significant improvement by week 2 and continued decrease to week 8; cats with the greatest baseline marking are most likely to recur after drug withdrawal. Buspirone reduces spraying in 55% of cats, ceases in 33%; relapses about 50%; no sedation or ataxia or polyphagia. Treatment usually 8 weeks for maximal effect; if cessation for at least 2 months, attempt slow dose reduction over months. your notes
Feliway versus Felifriend Feliway uses the feline FACIAL pheromone associated with environmental marking; reduces stress and anxiety. Felifriend uses the feline ALLOMARKING pheromone for cat-to-cat familiarisation; applied on the unfamiliar person or animal BEFORE introduction. The two have different indications. your notes

Canine Aggression

Aggression is treatable but rarely curable. Always classify before treating. The three umbrella categories are affective or social (offensive or defensive), predatory, and play. The 13 specific types within these matter on the exam.

Three umbrella categories

CategoryDefinition
Affective or social aggressionSympathetic arousal present; can be OFFENSIVE (confident) or DEFENSIVE (fearful)
Predatory aggressionHunting behaviour directed at a moving stimulus; little to no sympathetic arousal or prior signalling
Play-related aggressionNormal puppy play behaviour that has become problematic in intensity or target

The 13 canine aggression types

TypeHallmarks and key facts
Conflict-relatedCompeting motivations, frustration over an inability to perform a desired behaviour. Fearful or anxious dogs, unsure how to avoid disagreeable interactions when owners give inconsistent signals. Progression: submission → anxiety → uncertainty → fear → aggression. Often exhibited toward social group members during physical manipulation or around valuable resources, with ambivalence or submission after biting. Management: identify and avoid eliciting situations. Prevention: consistency and positive reinforcement from puppyhood.
Resource guardingPossessive aggression; normal behaviour that becomes maladaptive. Sympathetic arousal (dilated pupils, tense body); stems from anxiety that items will be removed. AGE OF ONSET about 16 weeks; adult onset in dogs on reducing diets or appetite-stimulating drugs or with conditions that increase appetite (e.g. hyperadrenocorticism). Control achievable in most cases within 2 to 3 months. Counter-condition by giving something better in return, or wait until the item is dropped and reward.
Fear-relatedTriggered by a perceived threat. An underlying factor in MANY forms of aggression. Body language: tail DOWN, ears BACK, crouched body, weight shifted AWAY, attempts to escape, piloerection, dilated pupils, tachypnoea, defaecation, urination. Posturing can look offensive when the dog is behind a barrier or the stimulus is at a distance. Particularly susceptible to chronic stress neurochemistry. Treatment: safe management, structure, coping tools, desensitisation and counterconditioning, replace fear response with a calm response. Prevention: early socialisation.
Territorial and protectiveDefined by TARGET, target behaviour, and LOCATION. Territorial is in the home, yard, or car, directed at unfamiliar approachers. Protective requires the PRESENCE of a family member, so a protective dog may be friendly to strangers when the family is absent. Negatively reinforced every time the person or dog passes by, the dog learns the display works. Prognosis is good with environmental control, guarded if the dog has learned the behaviour drives the stimulus away.
PredatoryNormal instinct; behaviour pattern: stalking, chasing, catching, biting, killing, eating. Stimulated by ANY movement (animals, joggers, cyclists, playing children, cars). Diagnosis: a MOVING stimulus is the target, typically smaller than the dog. Dogs with unwavering focus on a baby's movements or vocalisations are highest risk. Prognosis poor for resolution but management possible with head halter, basket muzzle, leash, response substitution.
Pain-induced and irritableHandling or anticipation of handling (approach, reaching) triggers aggression. Even gentle patting, grooming, or medication application of a painful area can elicit it. Hypothyroidism is one differential cause.
PlayNormal in young dogs, dangerous when intense or uninhibited. Diagnosed by playful postures and behaviours; classic sign is the PLAY BOW. Inconsistent owner responses including punishment can progress play to fear and conflict. Good prognosis with early proper intervention.
Dominance-relatedThe MOST mis-diagnosed and OVER-diagnosed type. Three required elements: confident temperament, assertive signalling, AND aggressive behaviour toward social group members in the ABSENCE of fear. Owner-directed aggression in situations where the dog's control of resources or social position is challenged. Lifelong management with guarded prognosis.
MaternalBitch directed at people or animals approaching her puppies. Good prognosis with spontaneous remission as puppies mature or wean. Management: gentle positive handling by trusted family members; muzzle or separate from puppies if needed.
RedirectedAggressive behaviour directed at someone or something that is NOT the stimulus. Arises from frustration or interruption of other aggression or arousal. The primary aggression may be territorial, protective, or fear-related; the secondary aggression is redirected. Resolves with treatment of the primary cause unless the relationship has been damaged by the owner's response.
Intraspecific miscommunicationFailure to communicate; arises in dogs without sufficient socialisation to other dogs, weaned early, or hand-raised. Appeasement behaviours cannot be effectively conveyed. Worsened by altered physical appearance (docked tail, cropped ear, brachycephalic face).
Intraspecific dominance / status (household)Aggression between dogs in the same household. Most often SAME-SEX pairs, especially FEMALES. SPAYED FEMALES are over-represented. Younger dogs, new arrivals, and rehomed dogs are most likely to start fights. Intact male and female intra-pair fighters begin at 1 to 3 years. Bites may be uninhibited and severe; prognosis fair to poor. Dogs are roommates, not a pack; giving priority access to the senior dog beats arbitrary preference.
PathophysiologicalAggression from underlying physiologic or physical abnormalities: abnormal neurotransmitter or receptor activity, CNS anatomic abnormalities, neoplasia, metabolic disease, infection (rabies), trauma, toxicity.
Exam trap: dominance is rarely the answer A diagnosis of dominance-related aggression requires confident temperament, assertive signalling, and aggression toward family members in the absence of fear. Most owner-directed aggression is in fact CONFLICT-RELATED or RESOURCE GUARDING, not dominance. your notes

Behavioural pathology criteria

An aggression problem is considered behaviourally abnormal (a mental health disorder) when ANY of the following apply: exceptionally fearful or anxious, poor impulse control, out of context, excessive for the level of threat, aggressive to benign social signals, lacking predictability or consistency, or an unstable temperament. Drug therapy is helpful in these cases: a daily SSRI (fluoxetine, paroxetine, sertraline) plus an as-needed second agent (trazodone, clonidine, or a benzo). your notes

Risk assessment and prognosis

  • Mixed breeds are over-represented, and so are English springer spaniels. The majority of dogs who bite are neutered. your notes
  • Large dogs over 18.2 kg with a short latency to arousal (immediate response to minimal stimuli) and unpredictability are more likely to be euthanised than smaller dogs. your notes
  • Six prognostic factors: latency to arousal (impulsivity), environment, complexity of the situation, potential to do harm, predictability, emotional stability. your notes

Treatment plan (7 steps)

  1. Medical evaluation and treatment as necessary (rule out pain, hyperadrenocorticism, hypothyroidism, seizures, hepatic encephalopathy). your notes
  2. Educate the family about all aspects of the problem. your notes
  3. Safety recommendations, especially with children in the home. your notes
  4. Management changes. your notes
  5. Behavioural modification: operant conditioning first, then classical counter-conditioning to shift emotional valence; built on obedience training, structure, predictability, consistency, and resource control. AVOID all physical punishment, which increases aggression and avoidance. your notes
  6. Management tools (head halter, muzzle, leash). your notes
  7. Medication, supplements, and diet changes. Low-protein diet with tryptophan supplementation is partially effective for territorial aggression. Recheck every 2 weeks by phone and in person at 1 to 2 months. Desexing males reduces territorial and intermale aggression. CAUTION in females: increased reactivity to humans, increased reactivity to unfamiliar dogs, and increased aggression toward family members have been reported AFTER ovariohysterectomy. your notes

Feline Aggression

Ten distinct types. Play aggression is the most common toward owners. Petted-induced is the second most frequent referred. Redirected aggression is the classic cause of acute-onset hostility between previously friendly housemate cats.

The 10 feline aggression types

TypeHallmarks and key facts
PlayThe MOST COMMON aggression by cats toward owners. Predation is highly motivated and instinctive; play attacks start with SILENT STALKING (no vocal or postural threats). Peaks at 9 to 12 months, wanes with maturity. Risk factors: kitten age, hands-and-feet play as a kitten, solo-housed cat, rough or finger-toe play with owners, long periods alone. Management: chase toys (NOT hands); food puzzle toys; redirect to appropriate moving objects; avoid all physical punishment; another social cat of similar age may help (one of the few problems where adding a pet is curative). Prevention: early socialisation, appropriate object and social play; hand-raised kittens are at greatest risk.
Fear-related (defensive)Cat perceives a threat with no opportunity to escape. May also redirect after being frightened by an inanimate stimulus. Body cluster: defensive (dilated pupils, ears flat, tail tucked, lowered body, leaning away) PLUS aggressive (piloerection, hissing, teeth bared, growling, swatting, biting, scratching). Good prognosis if adult-onset, short duration, mild arousal, high threshold, defined stimuli, controllable exposure, inhibited bites, adult owner willing to commit.
Petted-inducedThe SECOND MOST FREQUENT form of aggression in referred cases. Cat appears to enjoy attention then bites when stroking exceeds its tolerance. Classically reactive when stroking occurs over the LOWER BACK. Warning signs: fidgeting, tail twitching, tenseness, leaning away, ears flat, lips retracted, hissing, or running. Management: respect the threshold; desensitisation and counterconditioning with high-value food; NEVER restrain or confine during training; no physical punishment.
RedirectedA common cause of ACUTE-onset aggression between cats in the same household who have lived together amicably for years. Target is NOT the stimulus. Aroused-cat signs to recognise BEFORE the attack: hypervigilance, agitation, nervous pacing, piloerection, fixed gaze, tail flipping, dilated pupils, low vocalisation. Attacks are acute, intense, loud (growling, yowling), with vigorous lunging. Settling may take HOURS to DAYS. Triggers: sight, sound, or odour of another cat; unusual noises; unfamiliar people, animals, or environments.
Pain-induced and irritableLiver disease, pancreatitis, renal disease, endocrine disorders, CNS disorders, sensory decline. Diagnose with behaviour change plus medical workup.
TerritorialCats recognise colony versus non-colony members; aggression by colony members toward unfamiliar cats. Occurs in both sexes; particularly intense during breeding season. Approach may be slow and stalking or immediate and aggressive. Closely associated with fear-related aggression: a territorially aggressive cat may retreat if the encounter occurs OUTSIDE its territory.
Intercat householdDifferentials: territorial, fear-related, redirected, pain-induced, hormonal. May arise after a single event (e.g. a redirected attack) altering a previously stable relationship. Mild encounters may resolve with hiding places allowing recognition and re-establishment. Spontaneous recovery in hours to weeks. Management: SEPARATE first, then GRADUAL reintroduction (semiochemical exchange with cloths, scent before sight, then visual at distance, then proximity with treats, finally same-room access). Cats should NOT be allowed to "fight it out."
PathophysiologicalUnderlying physiologic or physical abnormality outside the standard categories (e.g. feline hyperesthesia, impulse dyscontrol, chronic anxiety, compulsive disorders, inability to form stable social relationships).
Social status-related (social stress)Confident cat asserts status with bold posturing, biting, or threatening to acquire or maintain resources. May initiate interaction then bite during stroking or when lifted or moved. NOT motivated by fear, so may train well to commands when desired resources are the reward. Punishment quickly raises arousal and adds a defensive component since these cats are more likely to fight than back away.
Hormonally mediatedNormally affiliative queen displays aggression to familiar people or animals approaching her kittens or nest. Function of hormonal lactation state plus the presence of young. Management: do not approach the nest; high-value food rewards at a safe distance.
Exam trap: redirected aggression in cats Two previously amicable housemate cats suddenly fight. The MOST LIKELY cause is redirected aggression: one was aroused by an outside trigger (another cat through a window, a strange noise, an unfamiliar visitor) and attacked the bystander. Settling may take HOURS to DAYS. Triggers must be identified and exposure prevented; gradual reintroduction follows. your notes

Feline aggression pharmacology (cross-reference: Behaviour Medications hub)

  • SSRIs (fluoxetine, paroxetine, sertraline) for impulsive, explosive aggression, or for the VICTIM cat with excessive fear of the aggressor. your notes
  • TCAs (clomipramine, amitriptyline) as alternatives. your notes
  • Buspirone for MILD anxiety, but can cause disinhibition and INCREASE aggression. your notes
  • Benzos for refractory cases: rapid anxiolysis, appetite stimulation useful for counterconditioning. Side effects include sedation, incoordination, disinhibition, rebound on rapid withdrawal, paradoxical agitation, and reports of HEPATOTOXICITY in cats. your notes
  • Pheromones: Feliway (environmental) and Felifriend (allomarking with unfamiliar person or animal); alpha-casozepine and L-theanine as adjuncts. your notes

Behaviour Forensics and Counselling

Two distinct exam topics live here. Behaviour forensics is the evaluation of dogs from fighting, hoarding, and puppy-mill backgrounds for disposition decisions. Behaviour counselling is the structured history-taking that frames every behavioural consultation.

Behaviour forensics: fighting dogs

  • Dogs from fighting environments are typically LESS aggressive toward PEOPLE than the general shelter population, but must be extensively evaluated for aggression toward OTHER DOGS. your notes
  • Physical scarring is evidence of a fight history but does NOT prove the dog was a willing fighter. Some fight-stock dogs attack on first opportunity even without fighting experience; others require encouragement. Inter-dog aggression can emerge as early as 10 weeks. your notes
  • Stimulus dogs must be CAREFULLY SELECTED and UNRELATED to the case (dogs tethered next to each other in the fighting yard are less reactive). your notes
  • Order of testing: OPPOSITE-SEX stimulus dog FIRST (fighting dogs often react more favourably or non-aggressively to opposite-sex), then SAME-SEX. your notes
  • Initial introduction through a chain-link FENCE BARRIER, both on leashes (ears, paws, tails, lips can still be bitten through the fence). If neutral or friendly, move away from the barrier and allow a direct nose-to-nose at 0.6 to 1.5 m. your notes
  • Dog handling: THICK BUCKLE COLLARS to avoid discomfort; leashes slightly TAUT to avoid frustration-elicited aggression; handler stays close behind the dog's rear to enable a clean pull-away. your notes
Exam trap: the fake-dog test Fake dog must be standing erect, short hair, visible eyes, wearing a leash and collar, with a handler. Test dog approaches on a loose lead; if it postures, the fake dog is moved or made to fall in front of the test dog (this may elicit aggression). True aggression toward the fake dog: lunging, intense focus, slow stalking, growling and snapping, excited barking (some are silent), attack with no hesitation or once the fake dog rears or falls, FULL-MOUTHED bites with hold and shake. These dogs are UNSAFE around other animals; with no evidence on behaviour-modification effectiveness, EUTHANASIA is the recommended outcome. your notes

Hoarder and puppy mill dogs

  • Standard behaviour evaluation PLUS additional fear and sociability subtests. your notes
  • These dogs can be extremely social with OTHER DOGS but highly abnormal with PEOPLE and novelty. Impoverished or unenriched environments produce excessive fear of strangers, defensive aggression toward people, catatonia, frenetic activity, and impaired learning. your notes

Evaluation protocol

PhaseWhat happens
AcclimatisationTester seated in a room with the dog loose, NOT soliciting interaction (uses a book or phone). 1 to 2 minutes.
Active interactionTester then actively engages the dog. The earlier passive phase indicates how motivated the dog is to socialise.
Remote-control car testMeasures response to novelty; slowly moving car observed for 60 seconds for investigation versus avoidance.
Umbrella testPlace a food bowl, open and close an umbrella while the dog is eating, observe 60 seconds. Dogs that are too frightened will NOT eat. Some associate the umbrella with the tester and become fearful of the tester. Skip both novelty tests if the dog is too fearful for the rest of the evaluation.
Disposition decision Dogs with no sociability toward humans are unlikely to make suitable pets. Dogs that panic to the point of self-injury or are catatonic in novel environments should be humanely euthanised, even if they can be tamed in home-like surroundings, because they rarely cope with everyday stressors. std ref
Exam trap: long-term outcomes in puppy-mill survivors Even after rehoming, 83% of former puppy-mill BREEDING dogs still exhibit behaviour problems (fear, housesoiling, sensitivity to touch, compulsive behaviour with compromised quality of life). your notes

Behaviour counselling and history-taking

  • Undesirable behaviour is the owner's inability to manage an otherwise normal behaviour (resource guarding, protective territorial aggression at visitors, digging, chewing, intraspecies aggression). your notes
  • Abnormal behaviour is dysfunctional, anxiety-loaded, out of context, excessive, uninhibited, or impulsive (compulsive disorders, excessive fear, panic and phobias, aggression from dyscontrol, learning deficits, mental health disorders). your notes

Chronic anxiety: clinical signs to look for

Tachycardia, tachypnoea, salivation, GI disturbance, skin disease, self-injurious behaviour, stress- or excitement-induced elimination, PUPD, change in appetite, marked weight loss, obesity, altered grooming, diminished responsiveness, altered sleep pattern. your notes

History-taking protocol

A complete behavioural history takes 30 to 90 minutes; the primary caretaker and family members should be present. Four pillars:

PillarWhat to ask
1. Family and environmentHouse and yard size, human and animal foot traffic, family members, ages, schedules, primary caretaker, other pets and their interactions.
2. The petSexual status, age, personality, why, when, and where adopted, age at adoption, average daily schedule, location during home, away, sleeping, exercise, interactions with people and pets.
3. The problem (5 Ws)WHAT occurs, WHERE, WHEN, WHO is present, WHY does the owner think it occurs. When did it first appear, initial circumstances, frequency and intensity, full body and facial language before, during, and after each incident. Walk back chronologically, most recent first. What corrections have been tried.
