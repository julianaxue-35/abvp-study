Canine Development

Behaviour starts before birth and is shaped through windows that close. The exam tests the sequence (prenatal, neonatal, transition, socialisation, juvenile, senior), the peak sensitivities and fear periods, and the consequences of getting the windows wrong.

The periods

PeriodAgeKey points
Prenatalin uteroNeurons generated and basic patterns develop; maternal stress raises fetal corticosteroid load and produces more reactive pups; intrauterine androgens promote masculine traits in female offspring (rodent data); gustatory receptors develop before birth and shape food preferences. Keep the dam in a low-stress environment on varied, nutritious food.
Neonatal0 to 2 wkTouch, smell, and taste only; daily 3 minute handling with mild stressors (cool surface, gentle rocking) produces lifelong HPA-axis adaptation, better stress resilience, and easier learning.
Transition10 to 16 days for about a weekRapid shift from neonatal to mature patterns; motor skills appear; some early human memories may form.
Socialisation3 to 12 wk; peak 6 to 8 wkHearing and sight come in at 2 to 5 wk with cortical myelinisation; pups actively approach novel objects until about 5 wk and become wary after, with wariness peaking at 12 to 14 wk. First fear period at 8 to 10 wk. About 20 minutes per week of varied, controlled exposure is enough.
Juvenile12 wk to sexual maturityLess tolerant of change but socialisation must continue; predatory motor patterns (eye, stalk, chase, bite) appear about 7 months in most breeds, as early as 8 weeks in Border Collies; puberty 6 to 18 months with a testosterone surge in males around 6 to 8 months. Dogs are socially mature about 18 months and fully mature about 2 years.
OldergeriatricReduced social interaction and reduced learning and memory.
Exam trap: what is learned in the socialisation window Attention, care-soliciting, bite inhibition, and primary social attachment are all learned here. A pup not socialised in this window is socially handicapped for life and can become neophobic. Livestock guarding dogs housed with sheep in this period form a lifelong social bond with sheep. your notes

Fear periods, play, and bullying

  • During fear periods, reduce novel exposure to a level the pup can recover from quickly, and avoid scheduling traumatic events (adoption, vaccination) inside them. your notes
  • Puppy play needs supervision: a pup bullied even a few times can become fearful, and a pup that bullies successfully can learn to bully more efficiently. your notes
  • Early weaning is linked to destructive behaviour, possessiveness, excessive barking, and fearfulness later. your notes
AVSAB: socialisation before 12 weeks takes precedence over completing the vaccine series The AVSAB position statement (often tested on the ABVP) is that the benefits of careful socialisation in controlled, low-risk environments before 12 weeks outweigh the small infectious-disease risk of attending well-run puppy classes before the vaccine series is complete. std ref
Flagged for your approval: minimum age at adoption Your notes read "5 wk start handling, 6 wk OK to adopt out, 8 to 12 wk wider exposure." Current AVSAB and US best-practice guidance is to keep puppies with the dam and littermates until at least 7 to 8 weeks, to preserve bite inhibition and littermate-mediated social learning (many US states set the legal minimum at 8 weeks). Canine Communication and Body Language

Read the dog in clusters, not in single signs. The exam tests whether you can tell offensive aggression from defensive aggression and submission from a submissive grin, and whether you know the autonomic correlates of "hard eyes" and tail lateralisation.

Visual signals

ChannelWhat to read
PostureLean forward shifts weight onto the front, signalling offensive intent; lean back unloads the front and signals defensive intent. Limbs and weight distribution drive movement.
PiloerectionHackles up anywhere on the body indicates AROUSAL, not a fixed emotion; can occur in play, conflict, or fear.
Tail heightHigh tail signals arousal or excitement; low tail signals fear, submission, appeasement, or affiliation.
Tail movementLong and wagging is engaged; long and still is absent of communication; short tail length adds little.
Tail speedLoose side-to-side is greeting and excitement; low and quick suggests nervousness or timidity; high and fast signals arousal (excitement, interest, or aggression).
Tail-side lateralisationWagging more to the dog's RIGHT side reflects positive valence (processed in the left hemisphere); more to the LEFT side reflects negative valence or withdrawal (right hemisphere).
MouthOpen relaxed mouth is comfortable; tight mouth is emotional or physical discomfort; yawns can be soporific or stress-related.
Commissure"Long lip" pulled back signals fear, distress, or appeasement. "Short lip" pushed forward with a C-shaped mouth and wrinkled muzzle signals aggression.
Submissive grinLips retracted, teeth visible, eyes squinty, forehead smooth, ears pulled back. A subthreshold appeasement gesture, NOT aggression.
EyesHard eye (direct, prolonged gaze with dilated pupils) activates the sympathetic nervous system and signals threat and arousal. Whale eye (sclera visible) signals discomfort or nervousness.
EarsRead the BASE: pressed back means fear, submission, defensive, or aggression depending on the rest of the cluster; forward means interest, attention, or approach.

Emotional state clusters

StateCluster (read together, not in isolation)
Offensive aggressionLean forward; fixed hard eye; raised tail; stiff or frozen; closed mouth; offensive pucker (short lip, C-shape, wrinkled muzzle)
Defensive aggressionPosture pulled back; ears back; tail tucked; increasing distance
Timidity / fearfulnessLow tail (may wag low and quick); tight mouth or yawns; panting or whining; long-lip commissure; whale eye; ears back; trembling; lowered body; cowering or hiding; barking, howling
Alert / attentionEars forward; tail down but not tucked; prolonged focus on the person or object
ArousalPiloerection; tail high and fast (often left-biased); tight mouth and short-lip commissure; panting with hard eyes locked; ears back
Exam trap: submissive grin versus offensive pucker A submissive grin is teeth-visible but with SQUINTY eyes, smooth forehead, and ears back. An offensive pucker is teeth-visible with a wrinkled muzzle, C-shaped mouth, and HARD open eyes. The eyes and the muzzle settle the question. your notes

Acoustic

  • Whine, yelp, growl, howl, bark. Barks are short-range communication and meaning varies with context. your notes
  • Low-pitched, harsh, low-amplitude barks signal aggression (low frequencies are distance-increasing across mammals). your notes
  • High-pitched barks signal fear or desperation. your notes

Olfactory and the vomeronasal organ

  • The vomeronasal organ (VNO) sits below the nasal cavity and is a chemosensory organ specialised for pheromones in faeces, urine, and saliva. Dogs collect samples via the head or anogenital area and route them through the VNO to the olfactory bulb. your notes

Modified-signal breeds: reading dogs with altered anatomy notesstd ref

Selective breeding has altered or eliminated key visual communication signals in some breeds. These animals are not communicating less — their signals are constrained or absent, and the reader must compensate by weighting intact channels more heavily.

ModificationSignals lost or alteredWhat to use instead
Brachycephalic faces (Bulldogs, Pugs, Boxers, French Bulldogs)Lip and muzzle position is permanently compressed. Offensive pucker vs. submissive grin distinction is unreliable; C-shape muzzle and lip tension are masked.Focus on eye expression (hard eye, whale eye, blink rate), ear base position, body weight distribution, and tail. Read the cluster more broadly, giving more weight to non-facial cues.
Docked tails (Dobermans, Boxers, Rottweilers, some working breeds)Tail height, lateralisation, and wag speed are eliminated or severely reduced. Tail-based social signalling is functionally absent.Compensate with full body posture, ear position, piloerection extent, and head orientation. Studies show docked-tail dogs receive more aggressive approaches from intact-tail dogs, confirming the signal loss is real and bidirectional.
Cropped ears (Dobermans, Cane Corsos, some Boxers)Natural ear mobility is surgically fixed in an erect position, eliminating pressed-back (fear/appeasement) and lateral (relaxed) ear signals.Rely on forehead tension, eye expression, and overall body posture. Cropped ears often create a more alert or threatening appearance regardless of the animal's actual emotional state.
Exam trap · body language requires knowing the breed anatomyAssigning an emotional state based on muzzle or lip position in a brachycephalic dog, or based on tail lateralisation in a docked-tail dog, is unreliable. On exam scenario questions involving these breeds, the correct answer will privilege eye expression, weight distribution, and body posture over the absent or reduced signal channel.

Feline Development

The feline socialisation window closes early (2 to 7 weeks), which is the single highest-yield fact in this tab. Genetics, queen nutrition, and queen handling all leave permanent imprints.

Genetics

  • Genetics influence neurodevelopment and neurotransmitter systems, shaping emotional and motivational states. your notes
  • Personality is a confluence of environment and genetics, but the SIRE'S personality is a strong independent predictor: kittens sired by outgoing, friendly toms show more approach and affiliative behaviour with people, less stress around strangers, and more exploration of novel objects. your notes

Prenatal (gestation 63 days)

  • Kittens show preferences for flavours fed to the queen in pregnancy. your notes
  • Protein and calorie restriction (even in the second half of gestation) produces offspring with elevated emotional reactivity and impairments in social interaction, environmental exploration, and learning. your notes

Neonatal (0 to 14 days)

  • Kittens suckle within an hour of birth; they suckle and sleep. They cannot thermoregulate. your notes
  • With eyes and ears closed, kittens orient to the queen and littermates by TACTILE, THERMAL, and OLFACTORY cues. Forelimb paddling predominates; hindlimbs are weak. your notes
  • Hearing comes in by about day 5; eyes open in week 2 (earlier in kittens born to young queens, and earlier in females than males). your notes
  • Kittens hand-reared from 2 weeks (separated from queen and littermates) are more fearful and aggressive toward people and cats, more sensitive to novel stimuli, with poor social and parenting skills and poorer learning. your notes

Socialisation (2 to 7 weeks)

Exam trap: the feline socialisation window closes at 7 weeks Kittens handled by humans between 3 and 14 weeks were more likely to approach humans and accept handling for longer than kittens handled only after 7 weeks or not at all before 14 weeks. Exposure to human handling by 7 weeks is critical for a cat to enjoy human interaction in the future. your notes
  • Shelter-born kittens handled for more than 5 minutes per day were more likely to approach novel toys and unfamiliar people than kittens not handled. your notes
  • Full motor coordination is reached by 7 weeks. your notes
  • Kittens have ultrasonic vocalisations; pitch falls with age. your notes
  • Visual acuity and binocular vision continue to develop into this period. your notes

Feline Communication and Body Language

Cats use vocal, scent, and visual channels and signal differently to humans than to each other. The clusters worth knowing: amicable approach, distress, and the "polite decline" of the wrapped tail.

Vocalisation

  • Used for agonistic, sexual, and queen-kitten encounters, and cats vocalise much more to humans than to other cats. your notes
  • Purr: a care-soliciting behaviour heard in kittens at the nipple, in friendly interactions, AND in mildly anxious or ill cats. The dual meaning matters clinically. your notes
  • Chirr or trill: a closed-mouth meow used as a friendly greeting (often to preferred associates). your notes
  • Agonistic communication is usually OPEN-mouth: hiss, spit, growl, snarl, yowl, shriek. your notes

Scent

  • Urination, defaecation, and scratching deposit chemical information. your notes
  • Sebaceous glands on the head, perianal area, and between the digits leave rich information. Head rubbing (allorubbing) of objects or social targets uses the glands on the temporal head, under the chin, and around the lips. your notes
Social terms worth knowing Allorubbing (rubbing one cat's body or head against another, including humans), allogrooming (grooming another cat), and preferred associates (cats that consistently exchange these affiliative behaviours and rest near each other) are the formal terms used when assessing multi-cat household tension. std ref
Exam trap: marking versus normal voiding Stress-related urine MARKING is usually a VERTICAL surface, in a standing posture, with a quivering tail and little to no scratching. Normal voiding is in a HORIZONTAL squat with substrate scratching afterwards. std ref

Pain-related behavioural indicators notesstd ref

Pain produces specific, observable changes in posture, movement, and behaviour. Recognising them prevents misclassifying pain-driven defensive behaviour as a primary behavioural problem.

Postural and movement signs

  • Hunched or rounded back: abdominal or spinal pain. Often paired with reluctance to move and a hindquarter tuck.
  • Guarded posture: the animal holds a specific body region away from contact — a painful limb held up, a flank held tightly, or the head and neck kept stiff. Attempting to touch the guarded area provokes rapid withdrawal or snapping.
  • Altered weight-bearing: shifting weight off a painful limb; reluctance to jump, climb, or use stairs; hesitation before sitting or rising; short-strided asymmetric gait.

Facial and ocular signs

  • Reduced eye aperture (squinting or partial eye closure): a characteristic indicator of pain in both dogs and cats, particularly head, ear, or dental pain. Validated in the Feline Grimace Scale and the Canine Grimace Scale.
  • Whisker position (cats): whiskers pulled flat or held rigidly back toward the cheeks are a pain indicator included in the Feline Grimace Scale.
  • Ear position in pain (cats): ears rotated outward and slightly lowered — distinct from the fully flattened fear ear — is included in the Feline Grimace Scale.
  • Orbital tightening: squished periocular soft tissue appearance present in both feline and canine grimace scales.

Behavioural signs

  • Sudden onset of aggression or snapping in a previously non-aggressive animal should always prompt a pain workup before a behavioural diagnosis.
  • Decreased appetite, reduced voluntary activity, withdrawal from social contact, and reluctance to interact overlap with stress — if onset is acute or linked to a medical event, pain is the priority differential.
  • Self-directed licking, biting, or scratching at a specific site indicates local pain or discomfort at that location.
Exam trap · pain mimics behaviour problemsA cat presented for "aggression that started last week" whose physical exam reveals dental disease, a fractured tooth, or chronic otitis is not showing a primary behavioural disorder — it is showing pain-elicited defensive aggression. Treating the behaviour without treating the pain will fail. Rule out pain, metabolic disease, and neurological abnormality before attributing behaviour change to a primary behavioural disorder.
