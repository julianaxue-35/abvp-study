How Animals Learn

Learning is how experience reshapes behaviour. For the shelter exam the practical core is that repetition physically changes the brain, that memory is fragile while it forms, and that these facts shape how we train and house animals.

What learning is

  • Learning is a process by which experience leads to long-lasting changes in behaviour. As an adaptive mechanism it lets an animal adjust to predictable and unpredictable circumstances across its lifetime. your notes
  • Innate behaviours occur without any history of learning and are intrinsically reinforcing (usually genetic), although they can still be modified by learning. your notes

The five aspects of learning

  • Attention, then contiguity (events close in time), reinforcement (a consequence that strengthens a behaviour), repetition and association (to consolidate, store, and retrieve learning as memory), and stimulation (neural activity that drives neural growth). your notes

Working versus long-term memory

  • Long-term memory holds information stored innately or from previous learning. your notes
  • Working memory combines retrieved long-term information with current internal and external sensory input, is processed in the frontal lobes, and is only temporary. your notes
Exam trap: one behaviour at a time Working memory is easily disrupted during its transition to long-term memory, so it is better to teach only one new behaviour at a time. your notes

Neurology in brief

Each time a behaviour occurs, neural circuits reorganise to support it. Neurons that become more positively charged are depolarised (excitation, driven by glutamate); those that become more negatively charged are hyperpolarised (inhibition, driven by GABA, which raises the glutamate needed to fire). Glial cells recycle or destroy leftover neurotransmitter so excitation does not continue indefinitely. The more often a circuit fires, the more permanent the learning. your notes

Working memory uses proteins already present in the cell; building long-term memory requires synthesis of a specific protein (CREB), which drives long-term potentiation (new dendritic spines, more receptors, synaptic widening). Consolidation occurs during sleep, with REM assisting. Mastered simple motor behaviours shift from the hippocampus to the basal ganglia, brainstem, and cerebellum, freeing the hippocampus for new processing. your notes

Equipotentiality: categories of information are stored redundantly across circuits, so if a few cells die the rest carry on, protecting the memory. your notes

Clarification: the calcium step Your notes compress calcium into one cascade. Strictly, presynaptic calcium drives neurotransmitter release, while postsynaptic calcium (through NMDA receptors) drives the kinase and CREB cascade behind long-term potentiation. The practical takeaway, that repetition plus sleep builds durable memory, is unchanged. The fuller molecular detail in your notes was kept light here for ABVP scope. your notes

Non-Associative Learning

A single stimulus, with no paired event. Three forms, and the high-yield point is the direction of the response change.

FormWhat happensUse or note
HabituationThe animal reduces or stops its response to a stimulus after repeated exposureUsed widely to reduce fear responses to harmless stimuli
SensitisationThe opposite of habituation: repeated exposure increases the responseA rising reaction to a repeated trigger
ImprintingAn irreversible attachment that can form only during a short, definite sensitive period early in life, and can generalise to all objects of that classMost commonly described in birds and neonatal foals
Exam trap: direction Habituation lowers the response, sensitisation raises it. Both follow repeated exposure to a single stimulus with no pairing. your notes

Classical (Respondent) Conditioning

Involuntary, reflexive learning: a neutral stimulus comes to elicit a response because it reliably predicts an unconditioned one. This is also where emotional associations, and the pitfalls of poisoned cues, are built.

The three phases and the core terms

  • Before: an unconditioned stimulus (US) automatically elicits an unlearned, involuntary response. your notes
  • During: the neutral stimulus and US are repeatedly presented together. The association forms less effectively if the neutral stimulus occurs after (backward) or during (simultaneous) the US, or if there is a long delay. your notes
  • After: the neutral stimulus becomes a conditioned stimulus (CS) and elicits the response by itself. your notes
  • A CS that predicts something the animal wants can act as a secondary reinforcer for operant behaviour (for example a clicker). your notes
  • A conditioned emotional response (CER) is created by pairing an object, place, or person with something that elicits emotion (joy, fear, anger, anxiety). your notes
  • Success factors: novelty and salience of the CS, salience of the US, CS to US contiguity, the training context, and biological preparedness. your notes
Exam trap: cue poisoning If punishment is delivered alongside environmental stimuli (for example a leash jerk and a shouted cue when a dog forges to greet another dog), the dog may associate any of those stimuli with fear and pain. Cues, including contextual cues, become poisoned, producing fearful or aggressive responses later. your notes

How animals associate

  • Associations form between stimuli and between the absence of stimuli, and include contextual stimuli (the hippocampus tracks unattended background detail). your notes
  • Associations are learned more easily when stimuli are contiguous, similar or contrasting, frequent, and salient (such as when reinforcers result). your notes
  • One-trial learning: a single experience, often a perceived life-threatening one, can produce lasting learning. your notes
  • Generalisation: an association extends to other members of the same stimulus or response category. your notes
  • Biological preparedness and contrapreparation: animals learn survival-relevant behaviours easily, but contraprepared behaviours clash with innate loops (for example a static sit instead of chasing fast movement) and are harder to train. your notes
  • Mapping: cognitive mapping (spatial), sequential mapping (chains of stimuli), serial learning (the exact order of events, important for motor sequences such as agility or flyball), and latent learning (acquired without showing until needed). your notes

Memory retrieval and state

  • Retrieval is cued by context (lighting, scent, background noise) and by internal state (body position, physiological state, hormones, neurotransmitters), which all join the retrieval-cue package. your notes
  • State-dependent memory: a memory learned in one state may not retrieve in a different state, so antecedent control affects learning. To avoid context-bound cues, train the cue in varying contexts. your notes

Operant Conditioning and Humane Training

Operant learning is about consequences: the animal acts on its environment, and the consequence makes the behaviour more or less likely. For the shelter exam the essentials are the four quadrants, the preference for positive reinforcement, and why positive punishment is avoided.

Operant basics

  • Operant behaviours are goal-directed: they operate on the environment to produce consequences, and the behaviour is strengthened or weakened by those consequences. your notes
  • Operant conditioning runs on the three-term contingency: antecedent, then behaviour, then consequence. your notes

The four quadrants

QuadrantWhat you doEffect on behaviour
Positive reinforcementAdd something pleasantIncreases std ref
Negative reinforcementRemove something aversiveIncreases std ref
Positive punishmentAdd something aversiveDecreases std ref
Negative punishmentRemove something the animal wantsDecreases std ref

Humane training: what to use and what to avoid

ASV: positive reinforcement preferred Training should follow Least Intrusive Minimally Aversive (LIMA) principles and the Humane Hierarchy. Positive reinforcement programs improve health, welfare, and adoption likelihood. Methods that incorporate punishment can increase fear, anxiety, and aggression, and personnel should not use anything other than mildly aversive methods except when safety is an imminent concern. ASV
Why not positive punishment It is aversive and painful and harms welfare; it does not address the underlying cause or emotional state; the behaviour may worsen or resurface; it can increase anxiety or fear and cause confusion about how to avoid the punishment; the animal may form a different negative association (cue poisoning, or fear of the handler); it carries safety risks; and it can reduce the animal's ability to learn. your notes

Negative punishment (removing something the animal wants, for example briefly withholding attention or a treat) is generally considered acceptable in force-free training and is distinct from positive punishment, which adds an aversive. std ref

Flooding and the humane alternative

  • Flooding: presenting a threatening stimulus at high intensity with no ability to escape. This can result in learned helplessness. your notes
  • Desensitisation and counter-conditioning is the humane alternative: gradual exposure kept below the fear threshold and paired with something positive, changing the underlying emotional response rather than suppressing the behaviour. ASV

Putting It Into Practice

The same principles drive prevention and shelter handling: build good associations early, set animals up to succeed, and read behaviour at the right time.

Preventing behaviour problems through socialisation

  • Early gentle handling by the breeder. your notes
  • Maternal rearing in a domestic environment with exposure to humans and varied animate and inanimate stimuli (sights, sounds, smells, movement, contact), leading to habituation. your notes
  • Adoption into the new home during the socialisation period. your notes
  • A variety of positive social experiences and new environments throughout the socialisation period, continued through the juvenile period. your notes
  • Avoidance of negative social experiences. your notes

Setting the pet up for success

  • Reward-based training (positive reinforcement). your notes
  • Supervision. your notes
  • Provide for the pet's needs: chewing, scratching, climbing, perching, bedding, food, elimination, physical and mental exercise, and social interaction. your notes
  • Prevention and confinement. your notes

Shelter application

Assess behaviour at the right time Behavioural evaluations performed within the first 24 to 48 hours of intake often mislead, because acute situational stress and altered arousal distort behaviour. Let the animal settle first. This connects to the Assessment and Decision Making subdomain. ASV
