Feline Shelter Vaccination: Science and Protocols

Speed

Blueprint placement

  • Physical Health of Animal = 29% of the exam. Vaccination = 12% of that domain, so roughly 12 of 350 exam questions.

Immune status on intake notes

  • Naive and susceptible: roughly 50% (plus or minus 20%) of dogs are naive for CDV and about 30% (plus or minus 15%) for CPV-2; about 64.5% are unprotected against CPV-2, CDV or both on intake. Around 50% of cats are naive for FPV, while over 75% already have antibody to FCV and FHV.
  • Already immune by natural infection or prior vaccination.
  • Already infected: may show signs, or incubate and shed, infecting other susceptible animals.
  • A vaccine can never be expected to produce more effective or longer-lasting protection than natural infection.

What core vaccines can and cannot do notes

  • Properly administered MLV core vaccines (CDV, CPV-2, CAV-2, FPV) can completely prevent infection and clinical disease when given an adequate time before exposure.
  • Vaccines cannot prevent the multifactorial respiratory complexes (FURI, CIRD); together with good husbandry they reduce severity and duration in some, not all, animals.
  • Vaccinating an already infected animal (for example CPV) may not protect it, but it will not make disease worse.

Shelter strategy and combination rules notes

  • Owned-pet vaccination is tailored to the individual's age, environment and lifestyle. The shelter goal is different: prevent the diseases most likely to be contracted or transmitted within the shelter, favoring vaccines with a rapid, effective response.
  • Combination products to prevent or reduce disease in shelters should contain only MLV core components. Rabies is a monovalent killed product and is not mixed with other core vaccines.
  • Core combinations should not routinely contain Chlamydia felis, feline or canine Bordetella, Leptospira serovars, canine coronavirus, or Lyme components.
  • Short-term non-core use can be warranted with diagnostic confirmation (for example feline Bordetella in a documented outbreak). When used, give non-core vaccines separately (different time and site). Never combine vaccines in ways the manufacturer did not intend.

Response, efficacy and failure notes

  • Vaccine efficacy is expressed as the preventable fraction: the proportion of vaccinated animals that do not develop disease after challenge compared with unvaccinated animals that do.
  • Factors reducing an individual's response: MDA interference, congenital or acquired immunodeficiency, concurrent disease, poor nutrition, chronic stress, and very young or old age. Do not rely on vaccination alone; reduce exposure too.
  • Causes of vaccine failure: failure to mount an adequate response, exposure before full immunization, MDA interference, improper storage or handling, waning immunity, and manufacturing errors (instability, expiry, potency loss).
  • DOI (duration of immunity) for core vaccines is years; DOI is not required for USDA licensure, but efficacy is.

Maternal Antibody

The single most tested immunology concept in shelter vaccination: why juveniles get serial boosters, why the window of vulnerability exists, and how to protect neonates that cannot yet be immunized. All claims from your notes.

The window of vulnerability notes

  • A virulent virus overcomes MDA better than vaccine virus does. There is a period when MDA has fallen low enough to allow field infection but remains high enough to block active immunization from vaccine. That gap is the window of vulnerability.
  • The window may persist as long as 12 weeks; with very high MDA, some puppies are not immunizable until 20 to 22 weeks. With current MLV CPV-2 and FPV vaccines the window per species rarely exceeds 2 to 4 weeks.
  • When the last core dose is given at 18 to 20 weeks, few if any animals still have MDA high enough to block the response.
  • Persistence of MDA is one of the most common reasons for vaccine failure, and MDA level cannot be predicted (it depends on the dam's titer and colostrum intake).

Killed vaccines and MDA notes

  • Killed vaccines can also be blocked by MDA (rabies excepted). If the first dose is blocked, the second only primes and a third is needed to immunize. If the second is also blocked, a fourth is required (the third primes, the fourth immunizes).
  • A single MLV dose, when MDA is absent or low enough not to block, gives complete immunity against CDV, CPV and FPV.
  • MDA can prolong infection within a litter: some pups are infected and die while higher-MDA littermates stay protected, then become infected later if not immunized in time.

Neonates, colostrum and passive transfer notes

  • Neither MLV nor killed vaccine should be given in the first 2 weeks of life: the immune system cannot respond effectively and vaccine virus can cause disease and death at that age.
  • Colostral IgG blocks active immunity during the first 3 days after birth. Nursing longer does not add protection, and juveniles get no protection from vaccinating the nursing mother.
  • Failure of passive transfer leaves neonates at very high risk in the first 4 weeks; it likely drives much orphan mortality. MDA also protects against bacterial sepsis, not only viruses.
  • Do not delay vaccination to conserve MDA. Vaccinating while MDA is present does not make it decline faster.
  • Feline timing: MDA is lowest around 3 to 4 weeks, with IgG and IgA rising at 5 to 7 weeks; kittens may be susceptible at about one month, roughly 2 weeks earlier than puppies.

Colostrum replacement and hyperimmune serum notes

  • Replacement colostrum must be provided within 72 hours of birth and before other proteins are ingested. Donor serum should come from healthy, well-immunized animals with robust quantitative titers to CPV-2, CDV and FPV; donor cats must be FeLV and FIV negative; serum can be frozen at minus 20 C.
  • Parenteral (SQ, preferably, or IP) serum extends the window for passive transfer because it does not depend on gut absorption (often 3 doses: at birth, 12 hours, and 24 hours).
  • Hyperimmune serum can prevent infection in naive animals exposed to CDV, CPV-2, CAV-1 and CHV-1 (and FPV in cats) when given before or at exposure, but cost and availability mean it is rarely used today.

Canine Vaccination

Core protocol for shelter dogs and the disease-by-disease detail behind it. All claims from your notes (2022 AAHA canine guidelines and shelter materials).

Core vaccines and shelter schedule notes

  • Core for shelter dogs: MLV DA2PP (distemper, adenovirus-2, parvovirus, parainfluenza), intranasal Bordetella and CPIV (with or without CAV-2), and parenteral rabies.
  • All dogs and pups 4 weeks and older receive MLV DA2PP at or before entry, with boosters every 2 to 3 weeks until 18 to 20 weeks. Prioritize foster placement or return for animals over 18 to 20 weeks.
  • Dogs over 18 to 20 weeks get an initial MLV DA2PP dose followed by a booster 2 to 3 weeks later.
  • All dogs, unless too ill to be housed in the shelter, should be vaccinated on entry with MLV for rapid onset. Pregnancy and mild illness or injury are not contraindications.

Respiratory vaccines and routes notes

  • Intranasal Bordetella and CPIV stimulate a rapid local innate response and mucosal IgA and avoid MDA interference, so they can be given early.
  • Avoid an intranasal Bordetella-only product: a dog at risk for Bordetella is also at risk for CPIV, so use the two-way product.
  • Injectable Bordetella should be avoided in shelters because of delayed onset and little local URT immunity; oral or injectable routes are only for when a two-way intranasal product cannot be used.
  • Intranasal and oral canine respiratory vaccines are live attenuated, not interfered with by MDA, and can be given at 4 to 8 weeks with no revaccination usually required.

Disease detail notes

CDV (distemper)

  • MLV and recombinant canarypox-vectored products give equivalent, highly effective immunity; minimum age 6 to 8 weeks; MDA usually absent by 12 to 14 weeks.
  • Almost immediate immunity after a single dose if not MDA-blocked; ideally vaccinate 2 to 3 days before exposure. Early partial immunity is possible (unique to CDV). Protect pups under 5 months by separation.

CPV (parvovirus)

  • Most common cause of canine viral enteritis; very stable in the environment; variants 2a, 2b, 2c are about 99% similar and the vaccine protects against all three.
  • Maternal antibody can persist 13 to 15 weeks, so all pups under 18 to 20 weeks should be considered susceptible regardless of vaccines received. Parvovirus immunity is complete or non-existent, unlike the early partial immunity of CDV.

CAV-2

  • Cross-protects against CAV-1 (DOI for CAV-1 is lifelong, for CAV-2 about 3 years). MLV CAV-2 can be given intranasally to pups as young as 3 weeks because mucosal immunity is not MDA-blocked.
Exam trap · rabies timing in dogsRabies is not required on shelter entry because in-shelter exposure risk is low, but a single parenteral dose must be given to all dogs over 12 weeks before release for the public-health benefit. If a long stay is anticipated, give it on entry with the other core vaccines.

Non-core in the shelter notes

  • Leptospirosis, canine influenza and Lyme are not recommended in the shelter; they generally pose minimal in-shelter risk.
  • CIV (H3N8, H3N2) vaccines are killed, not core, with little cross-protection, and routine use in all dogs is not recommended.
  • Reduce the number of vaccines per visit (especially small dogs), allow about 2 weeks between vaccines, and do not reduce the labeled volume.

Feline Vaccination

Core protocol for shelter cats, the FVRCP components, retroviral and respiratory non-core vaccines, and injection-site sarcoma safety. All claims from your notes (2020 AAHA/AAFP feline guidelines and shelter materials).

Core vaccines and shelter schedule notes

  • Core: FVRCP (FHV-1, FCV, FPV), rabies, and FeLV for cats under 1 year.
  • Shelter cats: a single FVRCP dose at intake as early as 4 weeks, then boost every 2 weeks until 16 to 20 weeks. Cats over 16 weeks get a single dose at intake (or one week before entry) then a second 2 weeks later.
  • For FPV the last dose must be at 18 to 20 weeks, then a booster 1 year later, then no more often than every 3 years. Because up to one third of kittens fail the final 16-week dose, WSAVA recommends replacing the 1-year booster with revaccination at 6 months.

FVRCP components notes

  • FPV: rapid, long-lasting immunity; protection can be immediate in susceptible kittens, though oral or intranasal challenge needs vaccination at least 3 days before. Do not use killed or intranasal MLV FPV in shelters (less effective than parenteral MLV). Parenteral MLV FPV also prevents CPV-2, which can cause a panleukopenia-like disease in cats.
  • FCV: immunity develops in about 7 days, fuller after a second dose; DOI at least 3 years. It does not prevent infection, but reduces disease severity.
  • FHV-1 (FVR): immunity in about 7 days; DOI at least 3 years; never complete; nearly all infected cats become latently infected and can be reinfected. Vaccinated cats are more likely to be protected from severe disease.
  • Intranasal FCV plus FHV-1 combos give faster local immunity and work at an earlier age with MDA present; they have no advantage for FPV.

Retroviral and respiratory non-core notes

  • FeLV: recombinant non-adjuvanted product; test FeLV status before vaccinating (ideally vaccinate only negatives, though it will not harm a positive cat). Give 2 doses 3 to 4 weeks apart (this interval is required); a single dose gives no protection. Revaccinate at 1 year if still high-risk, then not necessary.
  • FIV: not recommended for shelter cats; the immune response is indistinguishable from natural infection on current tests; requires at least 3 doses.
  • Bordetella bronchiseptica and Chlamydia felis: not recommended for shelter cats unless lab-confirmed as outbreak contributors.
  • FIP: labeled from 16 weeks but generally not recommended; insufficient evidence of clinically relevant protection.

Injection-site sarcoma (FISS) safety notes

  • Recombinant non-adjuvanted products are associated with reduced injection-site sarcoma risk.
  • Site mapping (AAFP): FVRCP in the right forelimb, rabies in the right hind limb, FeLV in the left hind limb. Interscapular (scruff) injection is no longer recommended; placing vaccines distally on a limb or in the tail allows complete surgical margins by amputation if a sarcoma develops.
  • The 1-2-3 rule: biopsy a post-vaccinal mass if it persists for 3 months or more, is 2 cm or larger, or is still increasing 1 month after injection.
Exam trap · pregnant queen and FPVMLV FPV vaccine in a pregnant queen risks fetal cerebellar hypoplasia (intention tremors, ataxia). In a shelter panleukopenia outbreak the risk of abortion and maternal and kitten death generally outweighs that concern, but where immunization cannot be deferred a killed product is the safer choice in pregnancy.

Feline adverse reactions notes

  • Adverse reactions within 30 days occur in about 0.52% of vaccinated cats: lethargy, anorexia, fever, or local inflammation. Risk is greatest around 1 year of age and rises with the total volume and number of vaccines given at once.
  • Type I hypersensitivity signs: vomiting, diarrhea, respiratory distress, facial or generalized pruritus, facial swelling, collapse.
  • After a prior reaction, use a different formulation and pretreat with an antihistamine and glucocorticoid 20 to 30 minutes before, then observe for a few hours.

Rabies

A killed, monovalent, public-health-driven vaccine with its own legal rules and timing. All claims from your notes.

Product and immunity notes

  • Rabies vaccine is killed and adjuvanted (canine products are all inactivated; cats also have a non-adjuvanted 1-year recombinant option). Immunity is induced as early as 2 to 3 weeks after vaccination.
  • DOI is a minimum of 3 years for most products, and up to 4 years for one feline vaccine.
  • Best shelter choice is a 1-year or 3-year product; the 3-year is as safe or safer and is recommended, especially in shelters. The 3-year is acceptable for cats, as is the non-adjuvanted 1-year recombinant feline product.

Timing, intervals and the law notes

  • Rabies is not necessarily recommended on intake because in-shelter spread is unlikely, but it is recommended for long-stay animals and before adoption or release for public-health reasons. In dogs, give a single parenteral dose to all over 12 weeks before release.
  • Core vaccines should not be given at intervals under 2 weeks, but rabies is an acceptable exception to that spacing.
  • Rabies is also the exception to the killed 2-doses-within-6-weeks rule: most animals over 3 months are protected by a single injection, with the legally recommended second dose given 1 year later.
  • Animals with no history of prior rabies vaccination must legally be revaccinated within the year in most communities, regardless of whether a 1-year or 3-year product was used first. If not revaccinated after the first dose, a 3-year product may give a longer DOI than some 1-year products.
  • The age for the first dose varies by jurisdiction but is often over 3 months. Many jurisdictions require a licensed veterinarian to administer it. Antibody titers are not a substitute for vaccination, and exemptions should be documented.
Exam trap · rabies is the exceptionWhenever a question contrasts rabies with the other core vaccines, rabies usually breaks the rule: it is killed yet protective after a single dose, it may be given at an interval under 2 weeks from other cores, and it is timed to release rather than intake. Communicate the legal 1-year revaccination need to adopters.

Titers, Diagnostics & Special Situations

Serologic risk assessment for outbreak management, how recent vaccination confounds diagnostic tests, and the rules for sick, pregnant, repeat-admission animals plus cold-chain and records. All claims from your notes.

Serologic risk assessment (CDV, CPV, FPV) notes

  • Antibody correlates with protection for these three diseases, so a healthy animal with a protective titer is very unlikely to become infected or ill even if exposed. It helps minimize euthanasia and lockdown during outbreaks and should sit within overall risk assessment.
  • Serology for risk assessment is only interpretable in animals completely free of clinical signs; a positive titer in a symptomatic or recently recovered animal may reflect active or recent infection, and such animals may still shed (especially CDV).
  • Lab testing is quantitative (numerical); in-house kits are qualitative (positive or negative).

LowAnimals over 5 months with antibody; or known vaccination at over 16 weeks; and no clinical signs.Can remain in the general population and be adopted (low risk is not no risk).
  • In juveniles a qualitative kit cannot reliably separate active immunity from passive maternal antibody, so quantitative titer testing is the better (gold-standard) option in puppies and kittens. Passive titers degrade over weeks, so a juvenile's low-risk status can change rapidly as MDA wanes.

Sick, pregnant and repeat-admission animals notes

  • Sick or injured: vaccines are unlikely to harm, whereas exposure to a virulent virus is far more dangerous. Vaccinate mild to moderately ill animals, then repeat no less than 2 weeks after recovery. If an animal is too ill to vaccinate safely, it should not remain in the shelter unless it can be cared for in a separate isolation area.
  • Pregnant or nursing: vaccine virus may risk the fetus, but virulent infection is usually the greater danger. In legal cases, seek permission to vaccinate or confirm valid records; weigh each component of a combination product against the immediate viral risk.
  • Repeat admissions: do not routinely revaccinate. Waive vaccination if the previous dose was given within the timeframe the vaccine is labeled to protect.

Outbreak vaccination protocols std refnotes

Key principle: Vaccinating an already-infected animal does NOT worsen disease. Vaccinate all animals on entry — even during an active outbreak — unless too ill to house safely in the general population.

CPV (Canine Parvovirus) outbreak

  • Immediately vaccinate all dogs and puppies ≥4 weeks with MLV DA2PP at intake. Boost every 2–3 weeks to 18–20 weeks.
  • All pups under 18–20 weeks must be treated as susceptible regardless of vaccination history. MDA can block the vaccine response to 13–15 weeks; the vaccine cannot be assumed protective in these animals.
  • Risk stratification: titer test healthy animals (qualitative in-house kit or quantitative lab). No titer = high risk → separate immediately from the population. Positive titer + over 5 months + no signs = low risk → can remain in population (low risk ≠ no risk).
  • Foster placement is strongly preferred for pups under 18–20 weeks during an outbreak.
  • Disinfection is critical: CPV persists months to years in the environment. AHP 1:32 (10 min), potassium peroxymonosulfate 1% (10 min), or bleach 1:32 (10 min) are effective. Quats fail against CPV. See Infectious Disease hub for full protocol.

CDV (Canine Distemper) outbreak

  • Immediately vaccinate all dogs with MLV DA2PP. CDV is unique: early partial immunity can develop within days of a single MLV dose (this is not true for CPV or FPV). This makes rapid vaccination especially impactful in distemper outbreaks.
  • Consider the recombinant canarypox-vectored CDV vaccine (rCDV, Recombitek): it does not contain infectious CDV, so it does NOT interfere with PCR diagnostics, and it may perform better through maternal antibody.
  • Risk stratification: positive CDV titer + no clinical signs = low risk (can remain in population). Serology is useful for healthy adult dogs in outbreak management.
  • Disinfection: CDV is enveloped and fragile — readily killed by bleach, AHP, quaternary ammonium, and most disinfectants.

FPV (Feline Panleukopenia) outbreak

  • Immediately vaccinate all cats and kittens ≥4 weeks with parenteral MLV FVRCP. Intranasal MLV FPV and killed FVRCP are less effective for panleukopenia protection and should not be used in this context.
  • Kittens under 4 weeks: cannot be vaccinated — place in foster immediately (not in shelter).
  • Pregnant queens: MLV FPV risks fetal cerebellar hypoplasia (intention tremors, ataxia in surviving kittens). In an active outbreak, the risk of maternal and kitten death from panleukopenia usually outweighs the fetal risk. If a killed FVRCP is available, it is the safer choice in pregnancy.
  • Environmental control: FPV persists more than 1 year on surfaces. Only fully vaccinated cats should enter a previously contaminated area. Effective disinfectants: bleach 1:32, AHP, potassium peroxymonosulfate, or calcium hypochlorite. Quats and alcohol fail.
  • All exposed cats should be quarantined; no movement until cleared.

FPV in pregnancy with MLV risks cerebellar hypoplasia but this risk may be acceptable in a severe outbreak. Quats fail against both CPV and FPV. Recombinant CDV vaccine does not interfere with PCR; MLV CDV does for up to 2 weeks.

Cold chain monitoring protocol ASVnotes

Storage specifications

  • Store refrigerated vaccines at 2–8°C (36–46°F); thermostat set at approximately 5°C. Use a pharmaceutical-grade or dedicated stand-alone refrigerator — not a staff break-room fridge (door fluctuations cause excursions).
  • Position vaccines 2–3 inches from all walls, top, bottom, and door. Keep in original packaging. Never store on the door shelf. Do not put food in the vaccine fridge.
  • Frozen vaccines (rare in shelters): store at or below −15°C. Do not refreeze after any thawing.

Temperature monitoring equipment

  • Minimum: a min/max thermometer (not a dial gauge) placed in the center of the fridge — it records the highest and lowest temperatures since last reset, not just the current temperature.
  • Preferred: a digital data logger with continuous recording and date/time stamping; download records weekly.
  • Check and log refrigerator temperature at the start of every working day. If fridge temperature is outside range when checked: note the time discovered and estimated duration of excursion.
  • Calibrate or replace thermometers at minimum once annually; calibration records kept on file.

Temperature excursion response

  • Do NOT immediately discard affected vaccines — contact the vaccine manufacturer (not just the distributor) to request guidance on the specific lots affected. Manufacturers have stability data for specific time-temperature combinations.
  • Quarantine affected stock: label with "DO NOT USE — PENDING MANUFACTURER GUIDANCE" and move to a separate area of the fridge (not in a separate fridge that might freeze them).
  • Document: date, time discovered, temperature found, estimated duration out of range, lot numbers of affected vaccines, and action taken.
  • If vaccines were frozen: contact manufacturer — freezing damages adjuvants in killed vaccines and may over-attenuate MLV components; efficacy is compromised.

Power failure protocol

  • Immediately transfer vaccines to an insulated cooler with ice packs (not loose ice — direct contact can freeze and damage vaccines). Target cooler temperature: 2–8°C.
  • Contact the vaccine manufacturer and shelter leadership immediately. Do not use vaccines that reached unknown temperatures for unknown duration without manufacturer clearance.
  • Document the event: time power failed (estimated), temperature at discovery, time vaccines moved, and cooler temperature.

Reconstitution and use-time protocol

  • Reconstitute only the number of doses needed for immediate use. Use the diluent provided — do not substitute.
  • MLV reconstituted vaccine: use within 30–60 minutes; discard any unused portion. Do not return reconstituted vaccine to the refrigerator and reuse later.
  • Killed (inactivated) vaccines: more thermostable, but use within 4 hours of removal from the fridge; discard remainder.
  • If reconstituted MLV is left at room temperature (approximately 21°C) for over 1 hour, discard it.

Documentation requirements

  • ASV requirement: record batch and serial number for rabies and all other vaccines given, to support adverse-reaction, recall, and vaccine-failure tracking.
  • AAFP recommended fields per vaccination event: animal ID, date, vaccine name, manufacturer, serial number, lot number, expiry date, site, route, and administrator name.
  • Designate a primary Vaccine Manager and a trained backup; both should be proficient in cold chain monitoring, excursion response, and documentation.
Exam trap · cold chain failureA min/max thermometer found outside range in the morning does not mean all vaccines are compromised — it depends on the duration and the specific products. Contact the manufacturer before discarding. The key mistake is either using potentially compromised vaccines without manufacturer guidance, or discarding expensive vaccines that may still be viable.
