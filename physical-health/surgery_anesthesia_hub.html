Surgical program & dentistry notes

  • All surgical practices and protocols must be developed in consultation with a veterinarian familiar with the organization, its population and its facilities.
  • Non-sterilization surgeries performed in the shelter, including dentistry, must adhere to the ASV spay-neuter guidelines for surgical suite, anesthesia, analgesia, and principles of sterility for instrumentation and surgical practice.
  • Non-anesthetic dental probing, scaling and polishing is unacceptable: without sedation, significant dental disease can be missed or inadequately addressed, and the restraint required causes major stress and risks serious injury from sharp instruments or bites.
  • Ideally intraoral radiographs are taken for dental surgery, but treatment for a painful mouth is strongly recommended even when radiology is unavailable, because dental disease carries serious welfare implications.
  • The goal of balanced anesthesia, optimal peri-operative care, good technique and low-stress handling is not only survival but long-term health and vitality.
Exam trap · non-anesthetic dentistryIf a cost-saving proposal offers non-anesthetic dental cleaning, the correct answer is that it is unacceptable. Sedation is required to assess and treat disease and to protect both the patient and staff.

Preoperative assessment notes

Intake history to obtain before GA or surgery

  • Known medical history; current or recent medications, including parasiticides.
  • Behavior (lethargy, depression), appetite, food and water intake, vomiting or diarrhea, ability to urinate and defecate, sneezing or coughing, stress level.
  • Satisfaction of post-operative fasting requirements, and any other unusual behavior or medical concern. A standardized intake form is recommended.

Physical examination (vet performs the complete PE before any GA or analgesic agent)

  • A licensed or trained technician can perform the initial exam and vital-parameter assessment; the veterinarian then performs a more complete PE before drugs are given.
  • A preoperative PE must be performed before GA unless the animal is fearful, aggressive or feral.
  • Components: thoracic auscultation; pulse rate and rhythm; respiratory rate and pattern; hydration; airway patency; BCS and body weight; oral mucous membrane color; ambulation; attitude and handleability; sexual status; congenital defects affecting GA or approach; abdominal palpation; mental and neurologic status.

Bloodwork and reasons to defer notes

  • In the cited shelter data, blood work was considered unnecessary in over 84% of dogs; of the remaining 16%, surgery would have been postponed in less than 1% and the GA protocol altered in only 0.2%. Pre-GA blood work is therefore unlikely to add peri-operative information in healthy animals. Point-of-care tests (PCV/TP, blood glucose, BUN) can be considered.
  • Age should not be the determinant of whether blood work is needed. Concerns about health status, disease potential and the risk of GA or surgical complications should dictate testing.
  • Reasons to defer surgery: extreme stress; traumatic injuries; severe dermatologic disease; severe dehydration; severe debilitation; known or suspected intestinal or urinary obstruction; malnourishment or starvation; severe parasitism; severe or life-threatening respiratory disease; prolonged vomiting or diarrhea; jaundice; toxicity or other signs of severe systemic disease.

Breed, size & temperament risk AAHA 2020

  • MDR1 / ABCB1 mutation (herding breeds — Collies, Australian Shepherds, etc.): give reduced doses of acepromazine and potentially butorphanol because of altered CNS drug handling.
  • Sighthounds (Greyhounds): prolonged recovery after barbiturates (e.g., thiopental) from low body fat and altered metabolism; reported hyperkalemia associated with general anesthesia.
  • Brachycephalic dogs and cats: prone to upper-airway obstruction and have higher airway-related anesthetic complication rates — prioritize intubation, preoxygenate, and extubate late.
  • Breed cardiac predisposition: Cavalier King Charles spaniel (mitral valve disease), Maine Coon (HCM). Grain-free–fed dogs warrant echocardiography for diet-associated DCM before elective GA.
  • Very small / toy breeds and all cats: higher risk of hypothermia, harder to intubate and monitor, and easily overdosed with high-concentration drugs or large-volume fluids — use precise volume delivery (syringe pump, buretrol) and lean-bodyweight dosing.
  • Temperament (fear, anxiety, stress — FAS): aggression, hiding, fleeing or freezing limit exam and raise risk. Manage with at-home anxiolytics (gabapentin, trazodone) and low-stress handling before transport; fractious patients generally need higher premed doses to allow safe IV access and induction. Anesthetic risk & correctable conditions AAHA 2020

  • Baseline mortality: anesthesia-related death is estimated at ~0.05% in dogs (1 in 1,849) and ~0.11% in cats (1 in 895), with most deaths occurring in the first 3 hours postoperatively.
  • ASA status ≥3 raises the odds of anesthetic death, and cats carry a higher odds ratio than dogs — stabilize before elective GA.
  • Correct or minimize before induction: anxiety, pain, hypoglycemia (BG <60 mg/dL), hypothermia (<99°F), anemia, dehydration, life-threatening arrhythmias, electrolyte/acid–base derangements (K⁺ >6.0 mEq/L, pH <7.2), cyanosis, congestive heart failure, oliguria/anuria, pneumothorax.
  • Time of day: procedures late in the day or after hours carry higher risk (limited staff for stabilization, fatigue, more emergencies) — schedule critical cases early to allow supported recovery.
  • Use an anesthesia–surgery safety checklist as part of the medical record to prevent critical oversights, and treat anesthesia as a continuum of care from home through recovery, with client communication throughout.

Anesthesia & airway notes

  • Mask induction is not recommended: unnecessary stress, uncontrolled entrance into unconsciousness, gas waste to staff and environment, and it is sympathomimetic and bronchoirritating, with risk of aspiration of gastric contents through an unprotected airway.
  • Chamber induction should be avoided at all costs (unless a heavy risk-benefit analysis for patient and staff is done): severe waste-gas accumulation, prolonged and excessive sympathomimetic windup, and lethal high inhalant concentrations.
  • In a busy operation, brief mask inhalant supplementation on a small share of animals (under about 10 to 15%) for short periods is often necessary and appropriate. If mask maintenance is needed too often, consider: a protocol that does not use inhalant anesthesia; a protocol that uses intubation rather than masking; protocols with better analgesics and sedatives; and eliminating contributors to unsteady anesthesia (hypotension, hypoxia, hypercarbia, hypoglycemia).

Intubation

  • Benefits: a protected airway and reliable maintenance under GA.
  • Drawbacks: risks of intubation including regurgitation, vomiting, increased intracranial and ocular pressure, increased vagal tone, and post-operative complications.
  • Prioritize intubation in: most unfasted dogs or those with unknown fasting status; patients with severe upper respiratory or pharyngeal disease; patients with questionable regurgitation or vomiting; overweight dogs; brachycephalic dogs.

Induction & airway safety AAHA 2020

  • Preoxygenate with 100% O₂ for ~3 minutes before induction — this buys roughly 6 minutes of adequate hemoglobin saturation during apnea/intubation. Critical in airway disease, breathing difficulty, expected difficult intubation, and all pregnant patients (fetal oxygen delivery).
  • ETT cuff: inflate just to seal — deliver a manual breath to 15–20 cmH₂O and inflate only until the hissing leak at the mouth stops. Overinflation causes tracheal damage.
  • Tracheal tears are a significant risk in cats: when repositioning an intubated patient, disconnect the ETT from the breathing circuit so the tube does not rotate within the trachea.
  • Apply corneal lubricant after induction and every 2–4 h to prevent corneal ulceration. Supraglottic airway devices are an option in cats and rabbits.

Intraoperative monitoring notes

  • Whatever monitors are used, monitoring requires vigilance over CNS depression, circulation, oxygenation and ventilation; it improves recognition of worrisome trends and provides a legal record of significant events.
  • Reliance on a single variable can lead to an inadequate plane of anesthesia, or to excessive depth with accompanying risk of emergencies.
  • Track heart rate, pulse quality and oxygenation; blood pressure; and respiratory rate and pattern.
  • Capnography monitors expired CO2 and reflects the balance of CO2 production, circulation delivering CO2 to the alveoli, and CO2 removal by ventilation.
  • Judging depth (hands-on, AAHA): a surgical plane is typically indicated by an absent palpebral reflex, mild jaw tone, a central-to-ventromedial eye position, and no purposeful movement. Combine electronic monitors with this hands-on assessment — neither alone is sufficient.

Hypotension (MAP < 60 mmHg) std ref

Treatment ladder — in order

  • Step 1 — Reduce inhalant concentration. Inhalant-related hypotension is dose-dependent vasodilation; lowering the plane is the fastest reversible intervention.
  • Step 2 — IV fluid bolus. Isotonic crystalloid 5–10 mL/kg in dogs, 3–5 mL/kg in cats, over 10–15 minutes. Repeat once if needed; avoid volume overload, especially in cats.
  • Step 3 — Inotrope / vasopressor (if no response). Match the drug to the problem: a positive inotrope (dopamine, dobutamine) for poor contractility, a vasoconstrictor (phenylephrine, norepinephrine) for excessive vasodilation, or ephedrine for both. Full AAHA dose ranges are on the Anesthetic Pharmacology tab.
  • Investigate cause: hemorrhage, excessive depth, acepromazine, cardiac dysfunction, or sepsis. If also bradycardic, give an anticholinergic (atropine, glycopyrrolate).
Exam trapMAP below 60 mmHg impairs renal and hepatic perfusion and raises anesthetic mortality. Reducing inhalant is the first step — do not start vasopressors before addressing depth and volume.

Bradycardia std ref

  • First rule out: excessive anesthetic depth, hypoxemia, high vagal tone (visceral traction), dexmedetomidine, or opioid effects.
  • Atropine: 0.02–0.04 mg/kg IV (onset 1–2 minutes). 0.04–0.1 mg/kg IM if no IV access.
  • Glycopyrrolate: 0.005–0.011 mg/kg IV. Longer duration than atropine; does not cross the blood-brain barrier (preferred when CNS effects are a concern).
  • Mild bradycardia with adequate blood pressure in a deeply sedated animal: monitor first before treating.

Hypothermia std ref

  • Shelter surgeries carry high hypothermia risk; pediatric patients and cats are highest risk due to large surface-area-to-mass ratio.
  • Prevention: forced warm air blanket (Bair Hugger), circulating warm water blanket, warm prep solutions, warm IV fluids; minimize wet prep area.
  • Treatment: forced warm air blanket first-line. Warm IV fluids (38–40°C). In severe cases: warm water lavage. Do NOT use direct heat lamps or uninsulated heating pads (contact burns).
  • Monitor temperature every 5–10 minutes; target core temp above 37°C before recovery.
Exam trapHypothermia prolongs drug metabolism, extends recovery, depresses cardiac function, increases bleeding time, and impairs immune function. It is preventable and especially severe in kittens and small cats.

Hypoventilation and apnea std ref

  • Defined as ETCO2 above 55 mmHg, RR below 8 breaths/min, or apnea more than 30 seconds.
  • Step 1: Check airway patency and ET tube position (kinked, displaced, or endobronchial).
  • Step 2: Begin manual IPPV — tidal volume 10–15 mL/kg, rate 8–12 breaths/min.
  • Step 3: Reduce inhalant and opioid. Check anesthetic depth — may be too deep.
  • Step 4: Consider reversal agents — naloxone for opioids (0.04 mg/kg IV), atipamezole for alpha-2 agonists (100 µg/kg IM).
  • Doxapram (5–10 mg/kg IV) is a last-resort respiratory stimulant; short-acting, increases O2 consumption, avoid in neonates.

Cardiac arrest — RECOVER 2024 std ref

BLS — do this first, continuously

  • Compressions: 100–120 per minute; compress 1/3 to 1/2 chest width. Sternal for cats and dogs under ~7 kg; lateral recumbency for larger dogs.
  • Ventilation: 1 breath per 10 compressions (10:1 ratio); tidal volume 10 mL/kg; 100% O2. Do not over-ventilate.
  • Rotate compressor every 2 minutes. Minimize interruptions — check rhythm in less than 5 seconds.

ALS — add after BLS is running

  • Epinephrine every 3–5 minutes; vasopressin can alternate with epinephrine.
  • Atropine once for PEA or asystole.
  • Amiodarone (preferred) or lidocaine (dogs) for VF or pulseless VT.
  • Reversal agents (naloxone, atipamezole, flumazenil) if drug-induced arrest suspected.

Pain Assessment & Post-Operative Care

Validated pain scales with intervention thresholds, wound care, activity restriction, discharge criteria, and adopter instructions. Pain scoring should be completed before surgery, immediately post-op, then every 2–4 hours during recovery. Source: CMPS-SF from Glasgow University; UNESP-Botucatu from São Paulo State University; CSU scale from Colorado State University.

Recovery from anesthesia AAHA 2020

  • Recovery is the highest-risk phase: roughly 47–60% of anesthetic deaths occur postoperatively, most within the first 3 hours. Monitor HR/RR, SpO₂, BP and temperature with the same vigilance as during maintenance.
  • Extubate when the patient can protect its airway by vigorously swallowing; deflate the cuff first. After dental or rhinoscopy procedures, leave the cuff slightly inflated and position the nose down to clear blood/debris and reduce aspiration.
  • Express the bladder to improve comfort, especially in nonambulatory patients. Keep IV catheters in until extubation and sternal recumbency are reached.
  • Hypothermia and hypoglycemia prolong recovery — keep warming devices on through recovery and check blood glucose in small/neonatal patients. Optimal recovery is alert, normothermic and ambulatory within ~10–30 min of the end of anesthesia.
  • Dysphoria vs emergence delirium: if pain control is adequate, treat dysphoria with an anxiolytic-sedative — dexmedetomidine 0.001–0.005 mg/kg IV/IM (also provides analgesia). If a respiratory cause is suspected, give O₂ first. Small doses of alfaxalone (1–2 mg/kg) or propofol (1–2 mg/kg) IV to effect can be used; be ready to re-intubate.
  • Reversals are selective: reverse alpha-2 agonists only if excessively sedate or rapid recovery is needed; antagonize opioids only after other analgesia is on board, or the patient may experience intolerable pain.

Why validated pain scales? std ref

  • Pain is subjective. Multi-dimensional validated composite scales reduce observer variability, allow communication across staff, and give a numeric threshold for rescue analgesia decisions.
  • Any animal at or above the intervention threshold receives rescue analgesia immediately — without waiting for re-evaluation if pre-authorized by protocol.
  • In shelter and high-volume settings, staff training is the limiting factor. The CSU scale trains most rapidly; CMPS-SF and UNESP-Botucatu provide the validated thresholds most likely to appear on the ABVP exam.

CMPS-SF — Canine (Glasgow Short Form) std ref

  • Validated for acute post-operative pain in dogs.
  • Domains: vocalization (0–3), attention to wound (0–2), posture (0–4), activity (0–3), demeanor and response to touch (0–5), overall behavior (0–6).
  • Maximum score: 20 (6b short form variant).
  • Intervention threshold: ≥ 6/20. Rescue analgesia is indicated at this score or above.
Exam trapCMPS-SF intervention threshold is 6 out of 20 for dogs. A score of 5 is not sufficient to trigger rescue analgesia per the validated cutoff.

UNESP-Botucatu — Feline std ref

  • Validated for acute post-operative pain in cats; originally validated for OHE pain.
  • Domains: posture (0–3), comfort (0–3), activity (0–3), attitude toward examiner (0–3), response to palpation of wound (0–3), response to abdominal/flank palpation (0–3), arterial pressure (0–3), appetite (0–3).
  • Maximum score: 27.
  • Intervention threshold: ≥ 8/27. Rescue analgesia at this score or above.

CSU Scale — Dogs and Cats (Colorado State University) std ref

  • Visual analogue composite scale; simple 0–4 or 0–5 format combining physiologic and behavioral signs.
  • Separate validated versions for dogs and cats. Freely available from Colorado State University Veterinary Medicine.
  • Recommended for shelter and high-volume settings due to simplicity; staff can be trained in under 30 minutes.
  • Intervention threshold: ≥ 2/5 in most protocols.
ABVP exam focusKnow each scale by name and species: CMPS-SF = dogs (threshold 6/20); UNESP-Botucatu = cats (threshold 8/27); CSU = both, simplest, best for shelter training. The testable skill is selecting the right scale and knowing when to intervene.

Wound care and post-operative discharge std ref

Incision assessment schedule

  • Immediately on recovery; then at 2, 7, and 10–14 days (the last for suture removal if applicable).
  • Normal: mild swelling and bruising for 24–72 hours; suture line apposed.
  • Return immediately for: any dehiscence (gap in suture line), seroma or abscess formation, purulent discharge, erythema tracking beyond incision margins, persistent bleeding, or uncontrolled pain.
  • In shelter recovery protocol: check animals hourly for first 4 hours, then every 2 hours until discharge criteria are met.

Elizabethan collar (E-collar)

  • Apply before full recovery from anesthesia — while the animal is still in the immediate post-op phase — to prevent self-trauma to the incision.
  • Worn continuously until sutures are removed or wound is healed (minimum 10–14 days).
  • Fit: animal can lower its head to a bowl but cannot reach the incision.

Activity restriction

  • No running, jumping, rough play, or swimming for 10–14 days post-surgery.
  • Dogs: leash walks only. Cats: confined to a small room without furniture to jump on.
  • In shelter housing: use lower cage level; remove climbing structures and hammocks.

Discharge criteria

  • Alert and ambulatory without assistance; sternal or standing.
  • Core temperature above 37°C (98.6°F); stable heart rate and respiratory rate.
  • Pain score below intervention threshold (CMPS-SF < 6 dogs; UNESP-Botucatu < 8 cats).
  • Has urinated at least once post-op.
  • Tolerates small amount of water without retching.

Adopter discharge instructions (key points)

  • Return immediately if: incision opens, heavy bleeding, unable to urinate or defecate, collapse, persistent vomiting, or uncontrolled pain.
  • Complete prescribed analgesia course (typically NSAID 3–5 days; opioid 1–2 days).
  • No bathing or swimming 14 days; keep incision dry.
  • E-collar on at all times; remove only under direct supervision.
Exam trap · E-collar timingE-collar must be applied before full recovery from anesthesia — not after the animal is ambulatory. A fully awake animal can traumatize a fresh incision in seconds.

Anesthetic Pharmacology

The drug protocols from the 2020 AAHA Anesthesia & Monitoring Guidelines: premedication/sedation, opioids, injectable induction agents, local/regional blocks, and blood-pressure support. Doses are starting points for healthy dogs and cats — reduce for sick, sedated, neonatal or geriatric patients and always titrate to effect. Confirm every dose against a current formulary before clinical use.

Core principle · multimodal & pre-emptive analgesiaUse multiple drug classes acting at different points of the pain pathway: an anti-inflammatory (NSAID/grapiprant) + a local anesthetic + an opioid ± an alpha-2 agonist. Give analgesia pre-emptively (before the painful stimulus) to lower intra- and post-operative requirements. AAHA's headline recommendation: use local anesthetics, insofar as possible, with every surgical procedure. Analgesia also lowers inhalant requirement, improving cardiovascular stability.
