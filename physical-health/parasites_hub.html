Control Principles & Transmission

Parasite management in shelters protects the population, the public, and the adoptability of each animal. The core levers are a default intake deworming protocol, understanding which parasites can establish in a congregate setting, and rigorous sanitation against environmentally resistant stages.

The three shelter concerns and the intake mandate notesASV

  • Three concerns: protect the individual animal and the population (and limit environmental contamination); protect staff, visitors, and adopters from zoonotic agents; and ensure untreated parasites do not reduce adoptability or burden new owners.
  • ASV intake mandate: all dogs and cats must be treated for roundworms and hookworms at intake, starting at 2 weeks of age, because of the zoonotic risk. Whipworm and tapeworm treatment at intake is often cost-prohibitive and targeted instead.
  • At minimum, every dog should leave the shelter having received a product effective against hookworms and roundworms.
  • Broad-spectrum heartworm and flea spot-ons should not replace a primary dewormer. Choose products by availability, price, and required spectrum, as determined by the attending veterinarian.

Direct versus indirect transmission notes

Exam trapGiardia and Cryptosporidium are shed in an immediately infective form, so daily sanitation must be impeccable. Ascarids, hookworms, whipworms, and coccidia are shed in a non-infective form that needs environmental development, so infected animals do not need isolation, but post-treatment fecal output is heavily contaminating and demands prompt cleanup.

Environmental persistence notes

  • Ascarid (roundworm) eggs are extremely resistant; on hard surfaces, heat and disinfectants such as bleach or ammonia work mainly by disrupting the outer coat to increase desiccation. Once in soil, they are nearly impossible to destroy.
  • Hookworm eggs are less hardy and are killed fairly rapidly by desiccation on dry surfaces. Whipworm eggs are hardy but their polar plugs may make them slightly more susceptible to bleach than ascarid eggs.
  • Because eggs are not immediately infective, there is no need to isolate or quarantine infected animals; the priority is prompt, thorough sanitation after treatment.

Intestinal Endoparasites

The shelter-relevant intestinal worms (hookworms, ascarids, whipworms, tapeworms) and protozoa each have a characteristic transmission route, prepatent period, and zoonotic profile. Diagnosis centers on zinc-sulfate centrifugation.

DiagnosisThe test of choice for nematode eggs is a zinc sulfate centrifugation fecal examination. Because shedding is intermittent and prepatent periods are long, a single negative does not rule out infection; fecal antigen ELISA or empiric treatment may be warranted in a compatible case.

Hookworm and roundworm specifics notes

  • Transmammary A. caninum: larvae in the bitch's milk infect nursing puppies, and with a roughly 2-week prepatent period, puppies can shed eggs and develop life-threatening regenerative anemia and melena by about 14 days of age. This milk route is unique to A. caninum among the hookworms.
  • A. braziliense spends more time in the dermis, which is why it causes the majority of human cutaneous larva migrans cases.
  • T. canis can be minimized in puppies by treating the pregnant bitch with ivermectin or doramectin around day 40 to 50 of gestation, which greatly reduces, but does not eliminate, environmental contamination.
  • Recommended regimen: deworm puppies every 2 weeks from week 2 of life and kittens from week 3, then monthly to 6 months; treat pregnant and nursing animals too.

Tapeworms and protozoa overview notes

  • Dipylidium caninum uses the flea as its intermediate host, so a sound flea-control program controls it. Visible tapeworm segments are an indication that flea control needs review.
  • Taenia species use prey (intermediate hosts), so they are unlikely to cycle within a shelter. Echinococcus is a significant zoonosis and is not covered by fenbendazole; praziquantel is the tapeworm drug.
  • Protozoa: Giardia, Cystoisospora (coccidia), Cryptosporidium, and Tritrichomonas cause diarrhea. Work up feline diarrhea with fecal flotation, direct smear and cytology, and a Giardia ELISA as needed; if negative, treat empirically with a broad-spectrum anthelmintic.
Exam trapFenbendazole covers a broad range (hookworms, roundworms, whipworms, Giardia, and most tapeworms) but not Dipylidium or Echinococcus. For those, reach for praziquantel.

Tritrichomonas foetus in cats notesstd ref

  • Organism: Tritrichomonas foetus — a protozoan flagellate; the same species causes bovine trichomoniasis but in cats it colonizes the large bowel.
  • Epidemiology: young cats (typically under 2 years) in congregate settings — shelters and catteries. Fecal-oral transmission via contaminated litter boxes.
  • Clinical signs: chronic, intermittent large bowel diarrhea — fresh blood, mucus, semi-formed to liquid feces, tenesmus. Normal appetite, body weight, and activity. No systemic signs. Cats often look clinically well between episodes. It self-resolves over months to years in many cats.
  • Diagnosis:
    1. Direct fecal smear of very fresh stool (within minutes) — look for motile, pear-shaped organisms with a characteristic forward rolling movement and visible axostyle (stiff rod).
    2. InPouch TF culture — incubated 48–72 hours; gold standard for sensitivity.
    3. PCR — most specific; can detect T. foetus when other methods are negative.
  • Treatment: Ronidazole 30 mg/kg PO once daily × 14 days. Ronidazole is nitroimidazole-based but NOT interchangeable with metronidazole for Tritrichomonas. Metronidazole has POOR efficacy against T. foetus and will not resolve the infection.
  • Shelter management: segregate affected cats from kittens; rigorous litter box hygiene (prompt removal of feces); avoid shared litter boxes wherever possible; fomite transmission via hands, equipment, and litter scoops is possible.
Exam trap — Tritrichomonas vs. Giardia on smearBoth are flagellated protozoa seen on direct fecal smear. Distinguish them by movement and morphology:
T. foetus: forward rolling movement; single axostyle (stiff posterior rod); one nucleus; three anterior flagella and one undulating membrane.
Giardia: "falling leaf" tumbling motility; two nuclei (owl-eyes); concave ventral disc.
Critical treatment trap: metronidazole is effective against Giardia but NOT against T. foetus. Using metronidazole for suspected Tritrichomonas is a high-frequency error.

Cryptosporidium spp. notesstd ref

  • Organisms: C. parvum (dogs, ruminants, humans), C. canis (dogs), C. felis (cats). All are coccidian apicomplexans, closely related to Toxoplasma and Cystoisospora.
  • Transmission: fecal-oral. Oocysts are immediately infective when shed — there is NO required period of sporulation outside the host. This makes environmental contamination occur faster than with most other coccidia.
  • Zoonotic risk: C. parvum is a significant human pathogen — particularly dangerous to immunocompromised individuals (HIV/AIDS, organ transplant, pregnancy). C. felis has some zoonotic potential. Always treat cat feces as potentially zoonotic when Cryptosporidium is suspected. Handwashing is the single most important protective measure.
  • Clinical signs: small intestinal diarrhea, watery to mucoid, often self-limiting in immunocompetent adult animals. Young, neonatal, or immunocompromised animals can develop severe, life-threatening diarrhea.
  • Diagnosis:
    1. Routine fecal flotation: poor sensitivity — oocysts are very small (4–5 μm) and can be missed.
    2. Modified Ziehl-Neelsen (acid-fast) stain: oocysts stain pink to red against a blue-green background; the reference staining technique for Cryptosporidium.
    3. ELISA or direct IFA: better sensitivity for C. parvum; useful when ZN stain is negative and suspicion remains high.
    4. PCR: most specific; also specifies the Cryptosporidium species.
  • Treatment: no consistently effective treatment exists for immunocompetent dogs and cats. Supportive care (fluids, nutritional support, bland diet). Azithromycin has been used empirically. Paromomycin was historically used but is nephrotoxic in cats and no longer recommended. Focus on strict hygiene and reducing exposure rather than drug treatment in most shelter cases.
  • Disinfection — critical exam trap: Cryptosporidium oocysts are highly resistant to chlorine disinfectants including bleach at standard dilutions. Do not rely on bleach alone.
    • Effective: 6% hydrogen peroxide (30 min), steam cleaning (over 60°C is required), desiccation (dry surfaces, direct sunlight)
    • Ineffective or unreliable: standard bleach dilutions (1:32), quaternary ammonium compounds, most commonly used shelter disinfectants
  • Shelter protocol: isolate affected animals; restrict to dedicated staff where possible; thorough handwashing after any contact; use steam cleaning or 6% H₂O₂ for environment; counsel adopters on zoonotic risk.
Exam trap — bleach fails against CryptosporidiumUnlike CPV or Giardia, Cryptosporidium oocysts have an extremely tough outer wall that resists standard chlorine disinfection. A shelter using bleach as its primary disinfectant during a Cryptosporidium outbreak will NOT adequately decontaminate the environment. Steam cleaning or high-concentration hydrogen peroxide must be used.

Heartworm Disease

Dirofilaria immitis is largely clinically invisible, slow, and expensive to treat, which makes shelter protocols a balance of testing, prevention, and selective treatment. The ASV endorses the AHS guidelines, adapted for shelter resources.

Life cycle notesAHS

Diagnosis correctedAHS

  • Antigen tests detect circulating antigen of adult female worms. They are the primary screening test. Microfilariae are detected separately by concentration tests (Modified Knott).
  • Run both antigen and microfilaria tests, because a small fraction of infected dogs are antigen-negative and microfilaria-positive. Do not test before about 7 months of age, since the prepatent period is roughly 6 months.
  • Dogs on preventives may have a false-negative antigen test from antigen-antibody complex formation; heat treatment of serum can unmask antigen by breaking those complexes.
  • A "no antigen detected" result does not confirm freedom from infection; all positive antigen tests should be confirmed before any adulticidal therapy.
CorrectionYour notes stated the antigen test detects "circulating microfilaria." Antigen tests detect adult female worm antigen, not microfilariae. Microfilariae are found by concentration tests such as the Modified Knott. This distinction is exactly why AHS recommends running both tests.

Treatment: the AHS protocol AHSnotes

  • Melarsomine is the only FDA-approved adulticide, effective against worms aged 120 days and older, given by deep IM injection into the epaxial lumbar muscles (L3 to L5).
  • Wolbachia and doxycycline: Wolbachia is a gram-negative intracellular endosymbiont essential to heartworm development and reproduction, transmitted vertically from adult female to microfilariae. Doxycycline at 10 mg/kg twice daily for 28 days suppresses Wolbachia, reduces post-treatment pulmonary pathology, lowers microfilarial counts, and renders microfilariae non-infective to mosquitoes (limiting spread).
  • Slow-kill (macrocyclic-lactone-only) adulticide is not recommended except in specific situations (a prior anaphylactic reaction to melarsomine, a grave non-heartworm prognosis); it is contraindicated in performance dogs or dogs that cannot be exercise-restricted, and where ML-resistance is suspected.
  • Caval syndrome: heavy worm burdens partially obstruct the tricuspid valve, develop acutely, and are usually fatal within about 2 days unless worms are surgically extracted.

Feline and ferret heartworm correctedAHS

SpeciesKey points
CatLow worm burdens; an infected cat poses no transmission risk to other cats. Melarsomine is not recommended (high risk). Management is supportive, with prednisolone for respiratory signs and maintained prevention.
FerretHighly susceptible; even a single worm can cause catastrophic obstruction given the tiny heart. Microfilaremia is transient and antigen tests are frequently false-negative (low burden or single-sex male infections). Bilirubinuria is the clinicopathologic change most consistently reported.
CorrectionA draft statement said most feline infections "resolve spontaneously without shortening lifespan." That overstates safety. Many feline infections are self-limiting, but feline heartworm (HARD) can cause sudden death, and the death of even a single worm can trigger a fatal acute pulmonary reaction. The correct framing: no adulticide in cats, supportive management, and counsel adopters about the real risk of acute decompensation.

Shelter strategy and prevention ASV PS

  • The ASV endorses the AHS guidelines and urges year-round prevention for dogs, cats, and ferrets, since some transmission risk exists everywhere. Screen at-risk dogs; provide prevention even when screening is not done; treat infected dogs promptly to reduce pathology and limit transmission.
  • Heartworm management should not divert resources from the care of other animals. A shelter without treatment capacity may transfer infected dogs to a partner with capacity, or release to an informed adopter with clear follow-up expectations.
  • In high-prevalence areas it is more cost-effective to screen dogs by microfilaria first; in low-prevalence areas, antigen-test first. A treated dog is not considered heartworm-free until two antigen tests 6 months apart are negative.
  • Macrocyclic lactones (ivermectin, selamectin, milbemycin, moxidectin) are reliable preventives only against worms present 30 days or less; DEET is unsafe in dogs, cats, and ferrets.

Ectoparasites

External parasites are all arthropods. Most can feed on any host; only lice and Demodex are strictly host-specific. The management split is between parasites that live entirely on the host and those (fleas, myiasis) with environmental stages.

Host specificity and lice notes

  • Only lice and Demodex mites are strictly host-specific and do not infest a different species. All other arthropods are opportunistic feeders.
  • Lice: sucking lice (Anoplura) have a head about as wide as the thorax and feed on blood; chewing lice (Mallophaga) have a head wider than the thorax and eat skin debris. Cats carry one louse (Felicola subrostratus). Transmission is mainly by direct contact; lice survive only days off the host.
  • Nits are not killed by insecticides, so a population persists unless nits are mechanically removed; treat at least twice, one week apart, and separate infested animals.

Demodex in shelter dogs notesstd ref

  • Biology: Demodex canis is a cigar-shaped mite living in hair follicles and sebaceous glands. Pups acquire it from the dam during the first 1–3 days of nursing — this is the ONLY significant route of transmission for D. canis. It is NOT contagious between adults.
  • Contrast with D. gatoi (cats): Demodex gatoi in cats IS contagious between cats and can be transmitted by contact. This is a high-frequency exam distinction.
  • Localized vs. generalized:
    • Localized: 1–4 focal patches of alopecia and scaling, usually on the face or forelimbs. Fewer than 5% progress to generalized. Resolves spontaneously in most cases. Treatment may not be required.
    • Generalized: 5+ focal areas or involvement of an entire body region, or pododemodicosis (all feet). Generalized demodicosis is a flag for underlying immunosuppression.
  • Immunosuppression — why it matters in shelters:
    • Juvenile-onset (under 18 months): indicates a heritable defect in T-cell-mediated immunity. Neuter the dog before adoption to prevent passing on the predisposition. Recommend owner not breed related animals.
    • Adult-onset (over 18 months): always indicates an underlying disease. Investigate: hypothyroidism, hyperadrenocorticism, neoplasia, immunosuppressive drugs. In cats: FIV or FeLV status.
    • Shelter-specific risk: prolonged LOS animals (>2–4 weeks) are at elevated risk due to chronic stress-mediated cortisol elevation and secondary immunosuppression. Monitor these animals actively for early skin changes.
  • Screening in the shelter: perform a deep skin scrape (pinch the skin fold firmly before scraping, to express mites from follicles) or pluck hairs from the margin of a lesion, on any LOS dog with alopecia, scaling, follicular papules, or thickened skin. A negative scrape does not rule out demodicosis in heavily scarred or crusted lesions (PCR of follicular contents may be needed).
  • Treatment:
    • Isoxazolines (fluralaner, afoxolaner, sarolaner, lotilaner) are now the treatment of choice for generalized demodicosis: easy oral dosing, safe, highly effective, used monthly or as a single dose depending on the product.
    • Amitraz remains FDA-approved for canine demodicosis; whole-body dips every 1–2 weeks are effective but labor-intensive, require PPE, and carry toxicity risk for staff. Less practical in a high-volume shelter setting.
    • Ivermectin (daily oral) is effective and widely used but not FDA-approved; avoid in MDR1/ABCB1-mutation-risk breeds (collies, shelties, Australian shepherds, etc.).
  • Adoption criteria: generalized demodicosis should be fully resolved (or at minimum showing consistent improvement with a documented treatment plan) before adoption. Document the heritable status for juvenile-onset cases and provide a written neuter recommendation with the adoption file.
Exam trap — D. canis vs. D. gatoi contagiousnessD. canis is not contagious between adult dogs; the only transmission route is dam-to-pup during nursing. D. gatoi (cats) is contagious between cats. Getting these backwards on the exam is a common error. Generalized demodicosis in a dog is always an immunosuppression flag, and in adults it mandates investigation of underlying disease.

Fleas notes

  • Adults take blood (flea-bite anemia in the young, old, or debilitated) and cause flea-allergy dermatitis with lumbosacral and tail-base alopecia. C. felis transmits Dipylidium, Bartonella, Rickettsia, hemoplasmas, and Francisella tularensis.
  • Treat with an adulticide plus environmental control (washing and vacuuming). For an obvious infestation at admission, nitenpyram kills adult fleas immediately, alongside a product effective against developing stages.

Myiasis and isoxazolines notes

  • Primary myiasis requires living tissue (the screwworm Cochliomyia hominivorax is a reportable disease). Secondary myiasis is opportunistic invasion of devitalized tissue on soiled, matted, or wounded animals; forensic entomology can estimate the duration of neglect or time since death.
  • The isoxazolines (afoxolaner, fluralaner, lotilaner, sarolaner) are highly effective against sarcoptes, demodex, otodectes, and lynxacarus, and are often the treatment of choice for generalized demodicosis given ease of dosing, short course, and safety.
Exam trapFleas do not commonly jump from host to host; animals acquire fleas from a contaminated environment, and the pupa is the hardiest stage, persisting for months. Environmental control is therefore as important as treating the animal.

Chagas disease correctedstandard ref

  • Trypanosoma cruzi is endemic in the southern United States and along the Mexican border. It is transmitted by triatomine (kissing) bugs, which deposit infective stages in their feces near the bite wound, or it is acquired when a dog ingests the bug. Signs can include acute myocarditis and right-sided cardiomegaly.
CorrectionDraft figures cited "20.3% prevalence in Texas" alongside "8.8% of shelter dogs," which are inconsistent and not reliably sourced. Reported Texas shelter-dog seroprevalence is commonly around 8 to 9% (higher in some regions). The testable point is the vector and the stercorarian (fecal) transmission route, which are correct.

Babesia gibsoni standard ref

  • A tick-borne (and bite-transmitted) intracellular protozoan causing hemolytic anemia, with notable spread among Pit Bull-type dogs through bite wounds.
  • Therapy reduces parasitemia and clinical signs but rarely achieves complete clearance, so treated dogs are permanent subclinical carriers at risk of relapse and need lifelong tick prevention.
  • Atovaquone plus azithromycin is the more effective protocol; the older triple therapy (clindamycin, metronidazole, doxycycline) reduces but does not clear infection.
Adoption counselingThese dogs can ethically be adopted out with full disclosure of permanent carrier status, relapse potential, and the need for routine tick prevention. Note that bite transmission, not only ticks, is a major route in fighting-breed dogs.

Personnel safety notes

  • For sarcoptic and notoedric mange, staff handling affected animals during the first 3 to 5 days of treatment should wear gloves and arm- and leg-covering clothing, and clean leashes, collars, and grooming equipment.
  • Reduce environmental hazards around the shelter: remove standing water, garbage, decaying organic matter, and long grass to limit vector breeding.
