Cleaning vs Disinfection Principles

The conceptual backbone of the subdomain. Examiners test whether you can separate cleaning from disinfection from sterilization, sequence the steps correctly, and explain why organic matter and contact time decide success or failure. Content is drawn from your sanitation notes and verified against ASV Standards of Care 2022, Section 5.

Blueprint placement

  • Physical Health of Animal = 29% of the exam. Sanitation and Biosecurity = 12% of that domain, so roughly 12 of 350 exam questions.
  • Sibling subdomains in this domain: infectious disease/outbreaks, surgery and anesthesia, vaccination, facility/environment, euthanasia, medical (non-infectious), nutrition and husbandry, parasites.

Four definitions you must not blur ASV

TermASV definitionExam hook
CleaningManual removal of urine, feces, food waste, hair, bodily fluids and other debris.Physical removal, no killing implied.
DisinfectionApplying a chemical to a clean surface for a specified contact time to kill most remaining pathogens.Kills most, not necessarily all. Needs a clean surface and contact time.
SanitationThe combination of cleaning and disinfection.Two separate steps, even with a combination product.
SterilizationDestruction of all pathogens including spores; reserved for surgical instruments.Not achievable on cage and kennel surfaces.
Exam trap · sterilizationThe ASV states plainly that true sterilization of cage and kennel surfaces does not occur. If an answer choice claims a disinfectant "sterilizes" a kennel, it is wrong. Sterilization language belongs to autoclaved surgical instruments, not housing.

The three-step sanitation sequence notesASV

  • Step 1, mechanical removal of visible organic matter. Most detergents and disinfectants are completely or partly inactivated by organic material (food, feces, urine, soil), so gross removal first is essential.
  • Step 2, cleaning with soap or detergent and warm-to-hot water, then thorough rinsing. Detergents and degreasers break down oil and grime with soap-like action and can remove up to 90% of environmental pathogens. Rinse before disinfecting so residue does not inactivate the next product.
  • Step 3, disinfection of the now-clean surface at the correct concentration for the full labeled contact time, then rinse if indicated and dry.
  • Sanitation can be by chemical means (soaps, detergents, disinfectants) or physical means (heat, steam, UV, desiccation), but physical methods must never be the sole means in a shelter.

Order of cleaning through the building ASV

  • Recommended order, first to last: healthy puppies and kittens, then healthy adult animals, then unhealthy animals. The principle is to move from least to most likely to be shedding, protecting the most susceptible.
  • Customize for subpopulations (specific infectious diseases, immune-compromised animals) per the shelter's protocols.
  • Staff change outer clothing and gloves and wash hands before moving to a different area and after cleaning.
  • Industry guidelines recommend a minimum of 9 minutes per animal per day for routine enclosure cleaning (NACA), useful for staffing math.

Why each step matters mechanistically ASVstd ref

  • Organic matter physically shields microbes and chemically neutralizes many disinfectants, which is why removal precedes everything.
  • Dilution matters both ways: too weak may be ineffective, too strong may harm animals and people. Follow label or published shelter recommendations.
  • Residue removal is a true step. Quats and bleach can harm animals that contact or ingest them even at recommended dilution, so rinsing is essential.
  • Contact (dwell) time is the interval the product must stay wet on the clean surface to kill the target pathogen. Spray-and-quick-wipe defeats it.
  • A labeled detergent-disinfectant combination product still represents two conceptual steps; cleaning and disinfection do not merge into one.
  • Observe practices against written protocols regularly. Common failures: wrong disinfectant, under or over-dilution, and missed contact time.

Class-by-class detail notesASV

Sodium hypochlorite (bleach)

  • Inexpensive and effective against FPV, CPV, FCV and ringworm. Retains efficacy in hard water.
  • No detergent action and significantly inactivated by organic matter, so the surface must be cleaned first.
  • Loses potency in light or if left out; prepare daily or whenever organic matter contaminates the solution.
  • Corrosive to metal and chain-link; toxic gas if mixed with ammonia or acids. Routine dilution about 1:32.

Quaternary ammonium compounds (quats)

  • Broad label claims and detergent-plus-disinfectant action, non-irritating when properly diluted and rinsed.
  • The shelter limitation: multiple independent studies show quats do not reliably eliminate non-enveloped viruses (FPV, CPV, FCV) despite label claims. They can be effective against ringworm only if specifically labeled fungicidal against Trichophyton spp.
  • Inactivated by anionic soaps and detergents, so do not layer over un-rinsed soap.

Potassium peroxymonosulfate, calcium hypochlorite, accelerated hydrogen peroxide, NaDCC

  • Potassium peroxymonosulfate (Virkon, Trifectant): oxidizing agent that denatures proteins and lipids and disrupts permeability. Some detergent activity, relatively good in organic matter, low tissue toxicity, less corrosive than bleach, but costly. Effective against dermatophytes at 2%.
  • Calcium hypochlorite (Wysiwash): effective against FPV, CPV, FCV; no detergent activity; inactivated by organic matter; dry tablet form for hose-end applicators.
  • Accelerated hydrogen peroxide (Accel): good detergent activity, works in light organic soil, short contact time (1 to 5 min), long diluted stability, no rinse required.
  • Sodium dichloroisocyanurate, NaDCC (Bruclean): chlorine-releasing tablet, no detergent activity, inactivated by organic matter, less corrosive to metal than liquid bleach.
Corrected · peroxymonosulfate vs ringwormEarlier notes treated potassium peroxymonosulfate as ringworm-negative. It is effective against dermatophytes at 2%. Treat it as ringworm-active at that concentration.
Clarified · bleach dilution for ringwormOlder shelter teaching recommended a strong 1:10 bleach solution for dermatophytes. The gentler 1:32 dilution with a 10-minute contact time, after thorough cleaning, is also effective against ringworm. The trade is concentration for dwell time: the lower 1:32 concentration is less irritating and less corrosive, so it is safer for both patients and staff while a longer contact time still achieves dermatophyte kill. The operational rule is unchanged: animals out of the area, clean first, full contact time, ventilate before return.

Agents to avoid or restrict notesstd ref

  • Aldehydes (glutaraldehyde, formaldehyde): carcinogenic to humans, avoid.
  • Biguanides (chlorhexidine, Nolvasan, Virosan): toxic to fish and aquatic environments, and a narrow spectrum that performs poorly against non-enveloped viruses.
  • Phenols (Lysol, Pine-Sol): cats are highly sensitive; do not use where cats are housed. Mechanistically, cats are deficient in the glucuronyl transferase conjugation pathway needed to clear phenolic compounds.
  • Alcohols (hand sanitizer, more than 60%): inadequate against non-enveloped viruses and useless on visibly soiled hands.
  • Never mix products unless the manufacturer approves. Bleach plus ammonia or acid yields toxic gas; quats are inactivated by soaps.
Exam trap · quat toxicity mimics calicivirusImproperly diluted or inadequately rinsed quats cause oral and skin ulceration, ocular lesions, fever, and respiratory signs in cats that can be misread as a virulent systemic calicivirus outbreak. The tell is the operational source: review dilution, application and rinsing. Management is to bathe the animal with warm water and gentle shampoo and rinse and dry the environment to remove residue. Revising the protocol resolves new cases faster than chasing a virus.

Protocols & Workflow

How the principles become daily practice: what a written plan must contain, the deep-clean sequence, spot cleaning, laundry and dishes, group housing, and the housing and flooring that make cleaning possible without removing the animal. Verified against ASV 2022 Section 5, with one laundry correction from the dermatophyte literature.

What a written sanitation plan must specify notesASV

  • The areas to clean and disinfect (including foster homes and outdoor spaces), the frequency, the responsible staff, and the products and supplies per area.
  • A step-by-step guide that walks staff and volunteers through the process, flags hazards to people, animals and the environment, and covers PPE.
  • Plans must be dynamic: alter protocols when disease rates rise or a harder-to-kill pathogen appears, and during an outbreak review protocols and observe practice for efficacy.
  • Sanitation plans must cover every location where animals are present, not just kennels.

  • Enclosures must be completely sanitized between different occupants, even after brief use, even if not visibly soiled, and even if the animal seemed healthy, because animals shed pathogens without signs.
  • Spot cleaning is less stressful (less handling, familiar scent retained), saves time and chemical, and lowers fomite transmission. It is especially valuable for shy or under-socialized cats and for animals with stress-worsened disease such as feline respiratory infection.
  • Group housing: for 2 to 3 animals clean as usual; for larger groups consider spot cleaning with periodic deep cleaning, scaled to group size, age, vaccination status and length of stay. Remove animals and deep clean if disease, excessive hair, dirt or smell appears.
  • It is unacceptable to spray or hose a primary enclosure while the animal is inside; remove animals from nearby compartments when overspray is likely.

Tools, mopping and cross-contamination control ASV

  • Provide separate cleaning supplies (mops, rags, buckets) per shelter section, or sanitize before use in each area.
  • Avoid aerosolizing pathogens: use damp, electrostatic or dust mops rather than vigorous brooms and high-pressure hoses where animals are present.
  • Mopping is ideally avoided (mops harbor pathogens) but may be needed where there are no drains. Mop heads must be disposed of, or sanitized and dried, between uses, including between cleaning and disinfection products and between housing areas.
  • Transport cages, traps and vehicle compartments must be sanitized between animals. Mobile carts and rolling bins should be assigned to one area or sanitized between areas, wheels included.
  • Each animal housing room should have its own dedicated cleaning supplies and storage.

Dishes, bowls and laundry ASVstd ref

  • Dishwashers reach high temperatures that destroy most pathogens but may not destroy non-enveloped viruses such as parvovirus, so apply a disinfectant to dishes after the cycle. Sanitize food and water bowls separately in location or time from litter pans to avoid cross-contamination.
  • Automatic watering devices and water bottles should not be used if the valve cannot be sanitized before the next animal.
  • Laundry: remove heavy organic debris first, wash in hot water with soap and bleach (about one-half to one cup per standard washer), do not overload, and dry completely.
  • Items that cannot be readily disinfected (leather gloves, muzzles) can spread disease when used on ill animals or during outbreaks.
  • Bedding contaminated with non-enveloped viruses (CPV, FPV) or dermatophytes should be discarded or laundered with a pathogen-specific protocol, because routine laundering may not remove them.
Corrected · laundry temperature claimThe supplementary gap-analysis note that fabrics "must reach at least 60 C (140 F) paired with chlorine bleach" to achieve mycological cure overstates the role of temperature. Moriello's dermatophyte laundering studies found that the decisive factor is washing twice (mechanical washing and rinsing) without overloading the machine; water temperature and the addition of bleach were not the critical variables when items were washed twice. Keep hot water and bleach as reasonable practice, but the exam-defensible point is the mechanical double wash, not a temperature threshold.

Flooring and drainage notesstd ref

  • Polished sealed concrete suits high-traffic corridors but is not durable enough inside dog kennels. Resinous epoxy and urethane coatings over concrete generally meet kennel flooring needs; non-porous tile with epoxy grout is a costlier option.
  • Simple sealed concrete is not recommended in most animal-traffic areas unless resealed annually, because porous or damaged surfaces cannot be fully disinfected.
  • Dog kennels need a sloped floor toward an appropriately sized, covered drain with adequate flow, to prevent standing water and cross-contamination between runs.
Verify against source · slope figureThe specific "exactly one-quarter inch per foot toward a rear drain" figure in the supplementary note is a facility-design specification, not an ASV Section 5 sanitation requirement, and exact slope and drain placement vary by design reference. Detailed flooring and drainage standards belong to the Facility/Environment subdomain; treat the number as a design guideline rather than a fixed sanitation rule.

Exam trap · contact time assumptionsLabel contact times assume a clean surface at ~20 °C. In a cold-weather shelter (< 10 °C), effective contact times may approximately double. Outbreak-level terminal disinfection ASVstd ref

When to escalate to terminal disinfection

  • ≥ 2 animals in a room or housing area develop the same confirmed or suspected illness within one week, OR
  • Any single confirmed case of: parvovirus (CPV/FPV), canine distemper (CDV), canine influenza (CIV), ringworm (Microsporum canis), or any highly contagious pathogen with significant morbidity/mortality in shelter populations.

Terminal disinfection sequence

  • Step 1 — Depopulate: Move all animals out of the affected area to clean housing or medical isolation. Cohort exposed animals separately from unexposed animals.
  • Step 2 — Strip and gross-clean: Remove all bedding, toys, litter boxes, and food/water equipment. Discard porous items that cannot be reliably disinfected (rope toys, wicker hides). Double-bag contaminated waste.
  • Step 3 — Detergent wash: Scrub all surfaces (walls, floor, ceiling if accessible, cage fronts, drains) with a diluted detergent. This is the step that cannot be skipped — no disinfectant penetrates organic debris.
  • Step 4 — Rinse thoroughly. Remove all standing water and detergent residue.
  • Step 5 — Apply pathogen-appropriate disinfectant at outbreak concentration (e.g. bleach 1:10 for CPV/FPV, AHP at 1:16) and leave wet for the full contact time.
  • Step 6 — Rinse (products requiring rinse) and allow to dry completely.
  • Step 7 — Restock with clean items only after complete drying.

Reopening criteria

  • Minimum 2 full incubation periods after the last case leaves the affected area (e.g. CPV incubation 3–7 days → hold area empty for 14 days minimum).
  • No new cases confirmed in the interim. Introduce only vaccinated, fully assessed animals after reopening.
Exam trap · terminal disinfection step orderApplying a disinfectant before completing the detergent wash and rinse (Steps 3–4) is the classic error. Organic matter remaining on the surface will neutralize the disinfectant regardless of concentration or contact time. The sequence must be: gross remove → detergent → rinse → disinfect → rinse (if needed) → dry.

Biosecurity Infrastructure

The standing systems that interrupt pathogen movement between animals: fomite control, PPE, hand hygiene, foot traffic, pest control, and the housing and population conditions that make the rest work. This tab is the routine infrastructure side. Outbreak detection, surveillance triggers and response stay in your Infectious Disease hub and are cross-referenced, not repeated here. Verified against ASV 2022 Sections 5.4 to 5.6.

Fomites and the routes you are interrupting ASV

  • A fomite is an inanimate object that can carry pathogens and contribute to transmission. In a shelter the everyday fomites are hands, work clothing, medical equipment, food bowls, litter boxes, toys, and cleaning and handling equipment.
  • Foot traffic spreads pathogens across the shelter and grounds; gloves, clothes and shoes all act as fomites.
  • Objects with scratched, damaged or porous surfaces (plastic litter pans, airline carriers, unglazed bowls) are difficult or impossible to fully disinfect and should be used with caution or discarded between animals.
  • In outbreaks, or when supplies cannot be sanitized between animals, switch to disposable items.

PPE and hand hygiene ASVnotes

  • Select PPE by the specific pathogens and exposure risks in each population. Change or sanitize and dispose of PPE between areas, and change protective garments between animals when transmission risk is high. Wash hands after removing PPE.
  • Hand hygiene stations belong in or near every area where animal contact occurs. A sink with soap, water and single-use towels is ideal; the minimum is hand sanitizer with at least 60% alcohol.
  • Handwashing: wet with clean running water, scrub with soap for at least 20 seconds over all surfaces, between fingers and under nails, rinse, and dry with a fresh towel or forced air.
  • Hand sanitizer is for visibly clean hands only and is ineffective against parvovirus, calicivirus and ringworm, so it must not be the sole means of hand hygiene.
  • Shelter personnel are far more likely to move pathogens during daily care than visitors, so staff protocols matter most.

Footbaths, the classic exam target ASVnotes

  • The current ASV position is firm: footbaths must not be relied on for infectious disease control in the shelter. Adequate contact time is impractical, organic debris accumulates and inactivates the disinfectant, and poorly maintained baths encourage pathogen growth.
  • It is unacceptable for animals to walk through footbaths.
  • The preferred approach in contaminated or protected areas (isolation, surgery) is dedicated boots that can be sanitized, or disposable shoe covers.
Corrected · footbath emphasisYour notes describe how to use footbaths (step in for at least 10 minutes, change solution daily, bleach or 1% Virkon preferred over quats). Those operational details are not wrong, but they reflect older guidance. The ASV 2022 standard is stronger: footbaths are not a reliable control and should not be depended on. If a question frames footbaths as a primary biosecurity measure, the ASV-aligned answer is that they must not be relied on, with boot changes or covers preferred. Within a parvo or panleukopenia situation, bleach and potassium peroxymonosulfate are still more reliable than quats if a bath is used at all.

Environment, pests and population ASVnotes

  • Physical methods (UV, steam, heat, freezing, HEPA) reduce pathogen dose but lack proven efficacy against all shelter pathogens and must not be the sole means of sanitation.
  • Environmental sampling helps assess sanitation efficacy and investigate outbreaks, for example ringworm, which can be widespread and persistent.
  • Rodents and insects may harbor pathogens spread by ingestion or by contaminating food and the environment. Protect all food in sealed bins, clean spills promptly, reseal and refrigerate opened containers, and use an integrated pest management plan.
  • Do not allow standing water (mosquitoes breed and pathogens thrive). Outdoor gravel, dirt and grass cannot be disinfected, so rotate or replace surface covers and restrict access to vaccinated, dewormed, healthy adults or where benefits outweigh risk.
  • Individual housing should at minimum allow effective cleaning without removing the animal and limit the animal's exposure to cleaning. Double-compartment housing is preferred for short stays.
  • Even the best sanitation and building cannot compensate for a poorly managed population; capacity and flow are biosecurity, not just husbandry.
