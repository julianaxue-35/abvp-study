Planning & Needs Assessment

Good shelters are planned, not just built. Needs assessment matches the building to the population it will actually serve so the facility supports Capacity for Care (C4C) rather than driving overcrowding or overbuilding. This tab covers the planning sequence, the space-usage mix, retrofit and sustainability guidance, and drop-box infrastructure.

The animal intake profile (planning Step 3) textbooknotes

  • Within the textbook's multi-step needs-assessment process, your notes detail Step 3, developing the animal intake profile. This is the step that anchors how much and what kind of housing to build.
  • What it requires: designate intake by mode of entry (for example owner surrender, stray, transfer, seizure) and by animal type, recorded monthly for a minimum of 2 years.
  • Why 2 years: two full annual cycles capture seasonality (kitten season, holiday surges) so the design is sized to real demand, not to a single busy or quiet month.
  • The goal: optimize Capacity for Care and prevent both overcrowding (too little housing or flow) and overbuilding (capital wasted on space that sits empty).

Capacity for Care and key metrics std ref

  • Capacity for Care (C4C): the maximum number of animals a shelter can house while still meeting each animal's full standard of care (housing, medical, behavioral, and daily husbandry time). It is a welfare ceiling, not a physical cage count.
  • Live Release Rate (LRR): the proportion of animals leaving alive (adoption, transfer, return to owner or field) out of total outcomes. A common operational outcome metric, distinct from C4C.
  • The intake profile feeds C4C: knowing predicted intake and length of stay lets you calculate the housing and staffing the building must support.
Source noteC4C and LRR are standard shelter-medicine terms used across the field. They are included for board fluency and badged as standard reference, not as ASV 2022 text.

Retrofit and sustainability guidance textbook

  • Filtration on a budget: a pleated MERV 6 or 8 filter is sufficient for standard HVAC retrofits; full HEPA is cost-prohibitive and reserved for surgery suites, not facility-wide.
  • Lighting: limit lighting loads to less than 1 watt per square foot; use photosensors to dim or switch off artificial lighting within roughly 15 ft of windows when daylight is sufficient.
  • Natural light: retain windows where possible to support circadian rhythm rather than removing them to chase a heat-transfer number.
ASV cross-checkMERV and HEPA specifics are textbook design guidance, not ASV 2022 text. ASV cautions that air-filtration and purification technologies (for example UVGI) must not be relied on as the sole method of air quality or infectious-disease control. Use filtration as an adjunct to ventilation, not a substitute.

Drop boxes ASV

  • Unmonitored "drop boxes" for placing live animals for later intake are unacceptable.
  • The harms ASV cites: safety risks for humans and animals, animal suffering, infectious-disease exposure, and death.
  • Acceptable alternatives for after-hours need: posted on-call animal-services numbers, drop-off arrangements with police departments, and care agreements with local veterinary emergency clinics.
Exam trap"24-hour community access" or "reduces intake delays" is a distractor framing. Convenience does not redeem an unmonitored receptacle; drop boxes remain unacceptable regardless of monitoring retrofits.

Space Planning & Flow

How animals, people, air, and equipment move through the building is itself a biosecurity control. Directional workflow, isolation that is structurally separated, and sensible placement of medical and intake spaces break transmission chains that no amount of cleaning can fix afterward.

Cleaning and traffic flow ASV

Why direction mattersStaff are the most likely vector to move pathogens during daily care because they contact many animals. A fixed direction plus PPE and hand hygiene between zones is a primary fomite control, not a nicety.

Isolation placement and design ASVtextbook

  • Isolation rooms must not open directly into another animal-housing area. Use a corridor or vestibule, which also serves as the space to don and doff PPE.
  • Provide a handwashing sink and space for treatments, exams, and dedicated supplies. Label rooms with current use and precautions. Windows allow observation without repeatedly entering.
  • Different species must not share an isolation room. Provide separate areas for different highly contagious diseases to prevent coinfection (for example parvovirus dogs apart from infectious respiratory dogs).
  • Textbook operationalization: larger shelters benefit from separate isolation or quarantine rooms grouped by syndrome, for example dermatology, respiratory, and gastrointestinal cases.
  • Makeshift fallback when no isolation exists: house contagious dogs at least 25 ft (7.6 m) from unaffected dog enclosures and cover enclosure doors. This is less effective than true isolation.
Feline nuanceAir from canine isolation should be exhausted outside and not recirculated. Separate air exchanges for feline isolation are a lower priority because cats do not readily transmit respiratory pathogens through the air over distance; droplet spacing matters more for cats.

Medical and surgery placement textbook

  • Surgery and recovery should be located at a distance from isolation areas that house sick and injured animals, yet close to animal housing to minimize patient movement through the building.
  • This balances infection control (away from sick animals) against stress and logistics (short transport for surgical patients).

Designing around feline upper respiratory infection notesASV

  • FURI is better controlled through proper shelter design and air systems coupled with appropriate management policies, even when the absolute number of isolation rooms is low.
  • Design levers: droplet spacing of cat housing, ventilation, stress reduction (spot cleaning, hiding places), and population management.
Exam trap"Increase group-room capacity to build herd immunity" and "convert to outdoor wire cages for airflow" are distractors. Crowding and exposure worsen FURI; design plus management controls it.

TNR trap/hold area design notestextbook

Community cats arriving for TNR (trap-neuter-return) are held in humane traps, not kennels. Their holding area has specific design requirements to meet their welfare needs while protecting both the cats and general population animals from exposure and stress.

Placement and separation

  • The TNR trap/hold area must be physically separated from general population housing — ideally in a dedicated room or wing. Community cats have unknown vaccination, disease, and parasite status and should not have airborne or contact access to the general population.
  • Locate near a dedicated entry/exit that community cat colony managers and volunteers can use without walking through general population housing. This reduces stress for both the community cats and resident animals, and streamlines the TNR drop-off and pick-up workflow.
  • The area should not be adjacent to or share air with dog housing, where possible.

Physical requirements

  • Floor: nonporous, impermeable, and drainable. Trap washing involves water and disinfectants; the floor must withstand repeated wet cleaning (epoxy or sealed concrete).
  • Drainage: floor-level drain in the trap-washing area for hose-down cleaning of traps between uses.
  • Walls: water-resistant (standard sealed hard surface); adequate height to prevent cats from climbing out of traps or accessing other areas if a trap door fails.
  • Ventilation: minimum 10–15 ACH with fresh air. If the area is adjacent to general population housing, treat as semi-isolation and ensure no recirculated air shared with general pop.
  • Temperature: same standard as animal housing (64–80°F / 18–26.6°C). Community cats in traps cannot thermoregulate as effectively as free-roaming cats; they are more vulnerable to heat and cold.

Equipment and workflow

  • Trap storage: designated area for storing clean traps (standard sizes: approximately 10"×12"×36" for cats). Store off the floor, clean and dry, ready for next use.
  • Trap-washing station: dedicated sink or hose bay with adequate pressure and hot water. Traps should be sprayed down and dried between community cats to prevent cross-contamination.
  • Dedicated feeding station: community cat feeder volunteers need easy access to the trap/hold area without moving through the general shelter. A labeled feeding log helps ensure every trapped cat is offered water and food.
  • Water access: a small water bowl wedged against the trap door, or a drip-style water attachment, provides access without requiring direct handling. Water is required for any hold over a few hours.

Hold time and welfare limits

  • Standard TNR hold: overnight post-surgery for monitoring, then return to trap and field release the next morning. Holding community cats longer than necessary is a welfare harm — stress of confinement accumulates rapidly in unsocialized cats.
  • Cats that cannot be immediately returned (injury, illness) are managed in the trap/hold area or transferred to a medical holding area, not placed in the general population.
Exam trap · TNR area disease riskA common question asks which shelter population requires the most physical separation from general admission areas. Unsocialized community cats in traps are a top answer because (1) they are unvaccinated or have unknown vaccination status, (2) they are often flea- and parasite-positive, (3) they are highly stressed and stress-shedding, and (4) their behavior makes treatment difficult.

Primary Enclosures & Housing

The primary enclosure is where an animal spends most of its time, so its size, set-up, and materials drive both disease management and welfare. ASV anchors this with the postural-adjustment standard and specific minimums; the textbook adds practical dimensions and the "unacceptables" that are heavily tested.

Minimum size and the postural-adjustment standard ASV

  • Postural adjustments (ASV core rule): animals must be able to stand and walk several steps, sit normally, lie down at full body length, and hold the tail completely erect, all within the primary enclosure.
  • Individual adult cat: less than 8 ft² (0.75 m²) of floor space is unacceptable; 11 ft² (1.0 m²) or more is ideal. Your notes add a volumetric target of roughly a 2.5 ft cube per cat.
  • Dogs: minimum kennel dimensions vary widely by body size. Aim for housing wider than 4 ft so a dog's nose and tail do not hit the side walls when turning.
  • Larger pens help behavior: dogs given 9 m² or larger showed increased general activity and visual exploration, less time in stereotypies, and more positive interactions, versus dogs in pens half that size.

The "unacceptables" ASV

  • Housing in an enclosure that would require forceful animal-handling equipment (catch poles, squeeze gates) for daily cleaning and care. Fractious animals need multi-compartment, touch-free care instead.
  • Housing in spaces not intended for animal housing (bathrooms, hallways), except for a brief emergency. Planned multi-use spaces (for example an office set up for housing) are different from improvised kennels in unequipped areas.
  • Tethering as a method of confinement for any animal, even briefly during cleaning.
  • Wire-mesh or slatted floors, and enclosure sides that are entirely wire or chain-link (injury, stress, disease risk). Solid barriers are recommended where contact can occur.
  • Using airline crates, transport carriers, or live traps as primary enclosures.
  • Stacking or arranging enclosures so that stress increases, ventilation is compromised, or waste contaminates units below.
Exam trap"Acceptable if length of stay is under 7 days" and "acceptable with the right PPE" are classic distractors on the forceful-handling item. These are categorical welfare violations, not time-limited or PPE-fixable.

Feline primary enclosure set-up ASV

  • Cats prefer raised surfaces; house units elevated off the floor, ideally at human eye level, which lowers stress and eases monitoring and positive interactions.
  • All cats must have a hiding place (feral cat den, covered perch, box, or partial door covering). Cats with hiding places hide less and approach adopters more.
  • The litter box must fit the cat's entire body and allow proper posturing; a box that is too small harms welfare and can cause house soiling.
  • Enclosures must allow scratching, climbing, and perching.

Canine primary enclosure set-up ASVtextbook

  • All dogs need a hiding option (covered crate inside the enclosure or a visual barrier over part of the kennel front), especially young, small, fearful, or anxious dogs.
  • Provide a soft resting place elevated off the floor for comfort, dryness, and thermoregulation.
  • Off-centre drains: placing the drain off-centre keeps a dry space for the dog to rest while the floor finishes drying after wash-down.
  • Indoor-outdoor units are ideal for welfare but are costly to build, waste energy, can freeze shut at the guillotine door in cold climates, and trap humidity and promote mold in hot, humid climates.

Handling and transferring fearful cats notes

  • Transport fearful cats in covered carriers or traps so they feel hidden and secure.
  • Transfer them as soon as possible to the highest available accessible holding cage, consistent with the feline preference for elevation.

Cohousing

Cohousing can improve welfare through species-appropriate social contact, but only with deliberate selection, sizing, and monitoring. Random group housing is unacceptable. The numbers here are ASV-anchored and frequently tested.

Group size caps ASV

  • Cats: regardless of enclosure size, no more than six adult cats per primary enclosure.
  • Dogs: two to four adult dogs per primary enclosure, with pairs preferred for safety and biosecurity.
  • Cohouse the minimum number needed to achieve a social benefit; larger groups are harder to monitor and raise conflict and disease risk.
CorrectionYour notes and draft MCQ key listed a feline group cap of "6 to 8 cats per room." ASV 2022 sets a firm ceiling of no more than six adult cats per cohousing enclosure, irrespective of room size. The board-defensible cap is 6. The MCQ in this hub has been corrected accordingly.

Space per animal ASV

  • Provide a minimum of 18 ft² (1.7 m²) of floor space per adult cat for cohousing. This is more than the individual-housing minimum because cohoused animals need to maintain distance from roommates by choice.
  • Maximize functional space by spreading resources out, and provide a variety of elevated resting perches and hiding places for cohoused cats.
  • Provide enough resources (food, water, bedding, litter boxes, toys) spaced out to minimize competition and resource guarding.

Selecting animals for cohousing ASV

  • Random cohousing is unacceptable. Unfamiliar animals must not be cohoused until health and behavior are assessed.
  • Match intentionally for age, sex, health, and behavioral compatibility.
  • Feline prerequisites: individual health and behavior assessment, freedom from signs of contagious disease, a confirmed negative FeLV and FIV status, and adequate time to mount an immune response to vaccines before group entry.
  • Pediatrics: puppies and kittens under 20 weeks must not be cohoused with unfamiliar animals unless the benefits outweigh the risks for all animals involved (for example pairing a single orphan with a surrogate).
CorrectionYour notes used both "under 5 months" and "under 20 weeks" for the pediatric cohousing rule. ASV uses under 20 weeks. Since 20 weeks is about 4.6 months, "5 months" is a loose rounding; memorize and answer with 20 weeks.

Introductions and monitoring ASVresearch

  • Dogs are introduced outside their primary enclosures, in pairs or groups, to test compatibility before cohousing.
  • Monitor individuals and group dynamics, especially after a new animal is introduced and at feeding time; include regular physical exams and body-weight checks to catch unrecognized social conflict.
  • Minimize turnover within groups to reduce stress, conflict, and disease exposure.
  • Cats that fight at the time of initial introduction are nearly 40 times more likely to keep fighting in the following weeks and months, so early conflict is a strong signal to separate.
  • Cohousing animals that fight one another is unacceptable. Provide individual enriched housing for animals that are fearful, aggressive to others, stressed by company, need individual monitoring, or are ill.
Exam trapThe "40 times" figure is the high-yield distractor target. Watch for swapped values (10x, 50x) or the wrong outcome (vaccine failure, reduced length of stay). The claim is specifically about continued fighting.

All-in / all-out advantage ASV

  • Smaller cohousing spaces support an all-in / all-out approach: all animals leave before new ones are added.
  • This lets the enclosure be completely sanitized between groups and eliminates the stress and disease risk of repeatedly introducing new animals to an established group.

Environment & Engineering

Air, temperature, humidity, light, sound, and surfaces are the engineering controls that keep a population healthy. Several of these carry exact ASV numbers that are prime exam targets, and your notes contained a temperature-conversion error corrected below.

Ventilation and air quality ASV

  • Ventilation standard: between 10 and 20 room air exchanges per hour with fresh air. Rates may need to rise at full capacity, since animals add heat, humidity, and carbon dioxide.
  • Isolation air: air from canine isolation should be exhausted outside and not recirculated. Separate feline-isolation air exchanges are a lower priority.
  • Doors: barred enclosure doors are recommended over plexiglass or fully enclosed units because they improve airflow and allow interaction; fully enclosed units require individual-unit mechanical ventilation.
  • Adjuncts only: UVGI and other air-purification technologies may assist but must not be relied on as the sole method of air quality or disease control. CO₂ monitors can help verify ventilation performance.
CorrectionYour notes listed a baseline of "10 to 14 ACH" alongside the ASV figure. For the exam, the ASV standard is 10 to 20 room air exchanges per hour with fresh (non-recirculated) air. The 10 to 14 figure is a general engineering baseline, not the ASV standard.
Filtration cross-checkMERV 6 to 8 for retrofits and HEPA limited to surgery are textbook design points, not ASV figures. ASV's position is that filtration and purification are adjuncts, never the sole control.

Isolation room HVAC specifications notestextbook

Standard room ventilation (10–20 ACH) applies to general population housing. Isolation and critical care rooms require additional specifications based on their clinical purpose.

Negative pressure — infectious disease isolation

  • Purpose: contains airborne and respiratory pathogens by keeping air pressure lower inside the isolation room than in the adjacent corridor. Air flows INTO the room when the door opens, preventing contaminated air from escaping into general population spaces.
  • ACH target: ≥12–15 room air exchanges per hour for infectious disease isolation; some guidance recommends up to 20 ACH for highly infectious respiratory pathogens.
  • Exhaust: 100% exhaust directly outdoors — never recirculated into the general HVAC system. This is the ASV-specified requirement and applies most critically to canine isolation (CDV, CIRD).
  • Pressure differential: −2.5 to −8 Pascals relative to the corridor; verify with a pressure gauge (Magnehelic gauge) or monitoring strip on the door frame.
  • Door seal: door sweeps and gasketed frames maintain the pressure differential; gaps at doors or penetrations will compromise isolation integrity.
  • Anteroom/vestibule: ideal design places a small anteroom between the isolation room and the corridor where staff can don/doff PPE without breaking the pressure gradient.

Positive pressure — surgery, neonates, and immunocompromised patients

  • Purpose: protects highly susceptible patients (post-surgical, neonates, FIV/FeLV-positive cats in recovery) by keeping air pressure HIGHER inside the room than outside — air flows OUT when the door opens, preventing environmental pathogens from entering.
  • ACH target: ≥15 ACH; supply air should be HEPA-filtered (100% fresh outside air, HEPA-filtered before delivery).
  • Surgery suites: HEPA filtration is the standard for surgery suites; this is the one context where full HEPA is justified (not facility-wide).

Pressure monitoring and documentation

  • Install a permanently mounted pressure gauge or monitoring strip visible from outside the room door. Check and log daily when isolation or surgery rooms are in use.
  • CO₂ monitors in general population rooms confirm adequate ventilation performance as a secondary check.
Exam trap · negative vs. positive pressureInfectious disease isolation = negative pressure (air in, pathogens contained). Surgery/immunocompromised = positive pressure (air out, pathogens excluded). Getting these backwards is a common exam error. A disease outbreak in an immunocompromised ward often traces to incorrect pressure direction or HVAC failure.

Temperature and humidity ASV

  • Ambient temperature: maintain between 64°F (18°C) and 80°F (26.6°C). Breed, body condition, health, haircoat, facial conformation, and age all affect thermoregulation, so monitor individuals.
  • Relative humidity: maintain between 30 and 70%.
  • If an animal cannot be kept comfortable by adjusting the thermostat and airflow, take additional measures (extra bedding, frozen treats or ice, or relocation).
CorrectionTwo errors in your notes are fixed here. First, a unit-conversion error: 80°F equals 26.6°C, not 36.6°C (36.6°C is about 98°F, near body temperature). Second, an internal contradiction: your notes gave both "60 to 80°F" and "65 to 80°F." The ASV figure is a single range, 64°F (18°C) to 80°F (26.6°C).

Exhaust vent placement notes

  • Locate exhaust vents at the rear of individual cat units to help prevent cross-contamination of air between animals.
  • For dogs, position exhaust low, near floor drains.

Noise management design ASVtextbooknotes

Why noise matters — species hearing sensitivity

  • Noise must be minimized; cat and dog hearing is sensitive. Cats hear 20 Hz–65 kHz; dogs hear 40 Hz–60 kHz; humans hear 20 Hz–20 kHz. Sounds comfortable for humans are perceived at much higher intensity by cats and dogs.
  • Decibel context: chronic noise above approximately 80–85 dB causes physiological stress in animals. Dog kennels commonly reach 95–115 dB during barking events — well above harmful thresholds. Even brief peaks cause HPA axis activation (cortisol spike).
  • Barking is multifactorial: visual stimulation, social contagion, fear, territorial response, and boredom all drive it. Preventing visual contact between dogs should not be the sole strategy to reduce barking.

Structural and acoustic design strategies

  • Individual enclosed rooms with walls continuous to the ceiling and doors with laminated glass plus a sniff hole are the most effective noise-reduction design: they prevent sound propagation between kennels and reduce contagious barking. These rooms require individual-unit mechanical ventilation (conflict with ASV's barred-door preference — know this tradeoff).
  • Acoustic panels on ceilings and walls absorb sound reflection and reduce reverberation; especially valuable in open-ward kennels where individual rooms are not possible.
  • Sound-absorbing ceiling tiles reduce peak decibel levels in large dog wards by 5–10 dB.
  • Locate noise-producing equipment (laundry, dishwashers, compressors, HVAC units) as far from animal housing as possible.
  • Quiet latches and rubber bumpers on cage and kennel doors reduce startling metal-on-metal sounds.

Inter-species separation — acoustic design

  • Dogs and cats should not have acoustic lines of sight: feline housing areas should be physically separated from dog wards with a solid wall barrier, not just separated by a corridor. The smell, sound, and movement of dogs is a chronic stressor for cats, even if they cannot see them.
  • Cat areas: design for quiet (soft opening doors, no dog-zone auditory penetration). Cat hearing is more sensitive than dog hearing, making feline areas the priority for noise management.
  • Consider locating cat housing at the building's quietest end, away from the intake/admission area and dog wards.

Behavioral and management adjuncts

  • Quiet hours: implement a daily quiet hour (e.g., midday) with no transport, cleaning, or visitor traffic — documented to reduce cortisol levels in both cats and dogs.
  • Classical or species-specific music at low volumes (studies show classical music reduces dog vocalizations in kennels).
  • Feeding at predictable times reduces anticipatory barking.
Tradeoff to knowThe enclosed-room acoustic design (best for noise) conflicts with ASV's ventilation preference for barred doors (best for airflow). Fully enclosed units cut noise but require individual-unit mechanical ventilation. The exam may present this as a forced choice: pick enclosed rooms if noise is the priority, but identify that they require supplemental mechanical ventilation.

Lighting ASVtextbook

  • Lighting should support a safe working environment and animal circadian rhythms; provide natural light where possible.
  • Efficiency target: limit lighting loads to less than 1 watt per square foot and use photosensors to dim or switch off artificial light within roughly 15 ft of windows when daylight suffices.
Exam trap"Lights on 24 hours to prevent shedding" and "remove all windows to stabilize heat transfer" are welfare-violating distractors. Circadian rhythm and natural light are protected goals.
