Foundations & Capacity

Medical care in a shelter is proactive and population-aware: it starts at or before intake, balances the individual against the group, and is bounded by a defined capacity to provide care. These foundations frame every decision in the rest of the subdomain.

The health mandate and care timeline ASV

  • Health is defined (after the World Health Organization) as a state of complete physical, mental, and social well-being, not merely the absence of disease or infirmity.
  • Shelter medical care must begin at or before intake and continue throughout the stay. When a shelter admits an animal, it becomes responsible for all needed medical and wellness care, or for promptly finding an outcome that meets those needs.
  • Preventive pillars: vaccination, parasite control, good nutrition, and appropriate handling and housing location. (These have their own subdomains; here they are named as the framework, not detailed.)

Capacity to provide medical care ASV

Population versus individualIndividual care is balanced against population health. Population health suffers when disease spread is likely, when long lengths of stay push the shelter over its capacity for care, and when one animal's treatment costs drain the resources available to others.

Oversight, Records & Stewardship

A shelter's medical program runs on a formal veterinary relationship, written evidence-based protocols, disciplined prescribing, and complete records. This tab also covers the supervision definitions and telemedicine, which determine who may do what, and where.

Veterinary supervision ASV PS

  • Every shelter should have a formal relationship with a veterinarian who has direct knowledge of the organization, its population, and its facilities.
  • Preventive protocols are developed in consultation with this veterinarian and may be implemented by trained staff under indirect supervision. Treatment of common conditions by trained staff may also occur under indirect supervision per written protocol, or by telephonic or written instruction.
TermDefinition
Direct supervisionA licensed veterinarian is readily available on the premises and has assumed responsibility for the care given.
Indirect supervisionThe veterinarian need not be on the premises, has given written or oral instructions, is readily available by phone or immediate communication, and has assumed responsibility.
Direct knowledgeThe veterinarian regularly visits and examines the shelter population in person.
Written protocolA document detailing standard care for a condition or procedure (policy, disease recognition, dose and route, diagnostics, environmental risk, adverse-effect response, storage and handling, documentation).
Where this comes fromThese definitions track the 2019 AVMA Model Practice Act, cited by the ASV Veterinary Supervision position statement (2021). Most preventive and common-condition care in shelters runs under indirect supervision.

Telemedicine and the VCPR ASV PS

  • Telemedicine is the use of remote communication technology to provide medical examination and treatment. It broadens access, removes geographic barriers, reduces travel stress, and supports biosecurity.
  • Veterinarians use professional discretion to decide whether telemedicine is appropriate for a given diagnosis or judgment.
  • The ASV supports legislation allowing a veterinarian-client-patient relationship (VCPR) to be created through telephone, digital, or electronic communication, and allowing prescribing through such a VCPR.
  • Keep medical records for each encounter, ensure follow-up, and respect the jurisdictional limits of veterinary licenses.

Evidence-based protocols and the prescribing standard ASV

  • Evidence-based protocols are essential and must be developed in consultation with the shelter's veterinarian. Ensuring compliance, at population and individual level, is part of veterinary oversight.
  • Protocols include six areas: diagnosis, treatment, animal housing, sanitation, decision-making, and communication. When a concern falls outside standard protocols or does not respond as expected, the veterinarian must be consulted.
  • Prescribing standard: medications only by prescription or written protocol, requiring a reasonable presumptive diagnosis, the ability to administer as directed, and a plan to monitor so success or failure can be judged.
  • Antibiotic stewardship: giving medications when not needed, such as antibiotics to prevent a viral infection, causes harmful side effects and promotes antibiotic resistance.
  • Drug use and dispensing must follow federal and state regulations, which may limit off-label and compounded drugs.

Records and the prescription label ASVnotes

  • Medical records include identifying information; signalment (age, sex, species, reproductive status); and a dated list of exam findings, vaccinations, diagnostic results, procedures, and treatments (with drug dose and route). A copy must be provided when the animal leaves the shelter's care.
  • Prescription drug label, seven components: name of the prescribing veterinarian; clinic or shelter name, phone, and address; patient ID and species; date dispensed and expiration date; drug name, form, and amount; directions for use; and cautionary statements.

Program quality and surveillance ASVcorrected

  • Track disease rates and outcomes to measure population health. Deficiency indicators: a decline in animal health and welfare after intake; sick or injured animals held without prompt treatment; wide-scale disease outbreaks; animals dying or euthanized from shelter-acquired disease or injury; and chronically high disease rates.
  • Population health surveillance: track morbidity and mortality trends and develop targeted strategies; individual and population health monitoring cannot exist without each other.
CorrectionYour notes list "side scale disease outbreaks." The ASV term is wide-scale disease outbreaks. It is a typo, but a key term, so it is corrected here.

Monitoring & Examination

From intake onward, every animal is assessed on a defined cadence. Knowing the exact intervals (intake, daily, monthly, six-monthly) and the FeLV/FIV testing logic is high-yield.

The monitoring cadence ASVnotes

  • Intake: every animal gets at least a cursory health assessment by trained personnel (confirm age, sex, description, identification, microchip); a comprehensive physical exam is ideally performed within 24 hours of intake. Animals with signs of infectious disease are isolated until determined low-risk.
  • Daily: trained personnel must visually observe every animal at least once every 24 hours, ideally before cleaning, noting food intake and enclosure condition (feces, urine, vomit).
  • Monthly: reweigh and repeat body condition score.
  • Every 6 months: a comprehensive exam for long-term residents, including those in foster.

Exam trapThe ASV does not recommend euthanasia of a clinically healthy cat based on a single positive point-of-care retroviral test. Confirmatory testing follows the current AAFP guidelines, and complete history (test date and type) goes with the cat at adoption or transfer.

Quarantine versus isolation ASV

  • Isolation separates animals with suspected or confirmed infectious disease until diagnosis or treatment determines them low-risk.
  • Quarantine of healthy animals at intake is not generally recommended; it is appropriate only for animals with a history of direct, high-risk infectious-disease exposure. Unnecessary holds increase length of stay and harm welfare.
Exam trapBlanket intake quarantine of healthy animals is a distractor. It lengthens stays without benefit. Reserve quarantine for documented high-risk exposure.

Pain Management

Pain control is mandatory, multimodal, and pre-emptive where possible. Failure to treat pain is explicitly unacceptable, and euthanasia is a legitimate part of treatment when suffering cannot be relieved.

Recognizing and treating pain ASV

  • Pain must be recognized and treated to alleviate suffering. Failure to provide treatment for pain is unacceptable. Treatment of pain can include providing euthanasia.
  • Assess pain through behavior and knowledge of causes; if a condition is painful in humans, assume it is painful in animals. Several validated pain scales exist.
  • Unrelieved pain is a significant welfare concern and can cause weight loss, muscle breakdown, increased blood pressure, prolonged recovery, and mental and emotional distress.

Pre-emptive, multimodal, and reassessed ASVnotes

  • Pain control must be of an appropriate strength and duration to preempt or relieve pain. When pain can be anticipated, as with surgery, provide control before the painful event.
  • The use of controlled drugs must be supervised by a veterinarian as required by statute.
  • Non-pharmacological methods that supplement drugs: the presence of littermates, a quiet environment, massage, physical therapy, heat, and deep bedding. These increase comfort and reduce anxiety.
  • Reassess animals frequently to judge the efficacy of pain relief. When relief is inadequate, emergency medical care must be provided.
NoteNon-pharmacological methods supplement, not replace, analgesia. They are adjuncts to a veterinary pain-control protocol.

Emergency & Health-Concern Response

Every shelter needs a written plan for timely emergency care, a structured triage method, command of the core emergency drugs, a clear rule for when euthanasia relieves suffering, and the principle that legal status never blocks treatment. Infectious-disease and outbreak response round out the framework.

The emergency medical plan ASVnotes

  • Every shelter must have an established, written emergency medical plan to provide appropriate, timely veterinary care for any animal that is injured, in distress, or showing signs of significant illness.
  • The plan must indicate how staff recognize and report conditions needing emergency care, and whether services are on-site or at an outside clinic. Animals in foster or off-site are covered by the same guidance, with clear after-hours instructions.
  • Three execution paths guarantee prompt care: on-site stabilization and immediate treatment; immediate transfer to a partnered 24-hour emergency or specialty facility; or prompt, humane euthanasia by qualified personnel (as local law permits) when suffering cannot be alleviated.
  • Staff-training mandate: a shelter cannot rely on veterinary presence alone. Non-veterinary care staff must be trained to actively recognize critical conditions so they can trigger the emergency protocol without delay.

Triage: the ABC primary survey standard ref

Triage is the rapid assessment of animals entering or residing in the facility to determine physiological criticality and prioritize treatment. Work the primary survey in order: Airway, Breathing, Circulation.

Minimum database and operational controls standard refASV

  • The "big five" minimum database for a critical case: PCV and total protein (hidden hemorrhage, anemia, dehydration); blood glucose (pediatric and neonatal triage); BUN and creatinine (renal perfusion baseline); urine specific gravity or dipstick (separates pre-renal dehydration from primary renal failure before aggressive fluids); and a thorough physical exam.
  • Expired-drug rule: avoid using expired pharmaceuticals. They compromise efficacy and patient safety and undermine the shelter legally by raising questions about the baseline quality of care.
  • Equipment acquisition: shelters may legally accept donated capital equipment (fluid pumps, anesthesia machines, forced-air warmers) and unexpired drugs from private practices or human hospitals; verify calibration and log items in inventory.
  • Written medical-record mandate: each animal has a distinct, unalterable record with its unique shelter ID and signalment, objective exam findings, a working diagnosis or problem list, and prescribed treatments (exact dose, route, frequency, and administrator initials).

Legal status does not bar treatment ASV PS

  • The legal status of an animal must never interfere with necessary preventive care or treatment of painful injuries, significant illness, debilitating conditions, or urgent behavioral needs.
  • When a shelter is legally required to hold such an animal (stray, evidence, boarder), it must enact a plan to provide adequate treatment or seek alternative placement.
  • If suffering cannot be alleviated, the shelter should seek permission to euthanize. Agreements with relevant parties can clarify emergency-care expectations in advance.

Infectious disease and outbreak response ASV

  • Animals with suspected infectious disease must be isolated until a veterinarian determines them low-risk. Allowing animals with severe infectious disease to remain in the general population is unacceptable.
  • An outbreak is more than the usual number of affected animals, or an increase in case severity. Response: risk assessment to identify exposed animals, physical separation of sick, exposed, at-risk, and unexposed groups, and possibly halting animal movement or intake.
  • Monitor at-risk animals for disease at least once a day; limit animal handling and foot traffic; avoid returning recovered or exposed animals to the general population while transmission risk remains.
  • Follow reportable-disease laws. Depopulation is a last resort for extraordinary circumstances, not an initial outbreak response (see the Euthanasia hub).
Exam trapFootbaths often become contaminated and aid transmission rather than control it. Do not treat a footbath as a primary outbreak control.
