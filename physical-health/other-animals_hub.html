Ferrets source chapter

Obligate carnivores, predator species, and the only common shelter mammal in which canine distemper is 100% fatal. The ferret infectious-disease list is long; the exam-critical anchors are CDV (uniformly fatal), vaccine anaphylaxis, adrenocortical disease tied to early spay/neuter, and the requirement to house them away from prey species.

Biology & behaviour

  • Weight ~800–1200 g; lifespan 5–11 yr. GI transit time 3–4 h in adults (very fast). Sexual maturity ~6–12 mo; weaning ~6–8 wk; gestation 41–43 days.
  • Seasonal weight swing up to 40% — lose weight in summer, regain in winter. Do not interpret as disease without context.
  • Musky odour is largely sebaceous, not anal-gland, in origin. Mark territory with urine, feces and anal-gland secretions even when group-housed.
  • Sleep in bursts; like to burrow under blankets and through tunnels/hammocks; strong instinct to dig. Eyes adjust poorly to bright light — give time to wake before lifting or risk being bitten.
  • Extremely curious and nearly fearless; will chew almost any material → frequent GI foreign bodies/obstruction (foam, rubber). Because of this curiosity they are not significantly stressed by novel environments.
  • Play can turn aggressive when threatened (lunging, side-attacks, "dancing," clucking); defensive threats include hissing, screaming, jaw-snapping.

Housing, diet & handling

  • Predators — must NOT be housed near birds, rodents or rabbits. Secure, well-ventilated, ferret-proof wire cage; will burrow into and eat foam/mattress stuffing → obstruction. Usually litter-train to a corner. Can be group-housed (monitor for aggression).
  • Obligate carnivores: diet high in meat-based protein and fat, low in carbohydrate and fibre. Fat 15–20%, protein 30–35%. Cat food is not protein-rich enough; high-quality kitten food is acceptable. Dry food available at all times.
  • Handling: gently restrain on the table one-handed; scruff and lift so all four feet leave the table to take temperature/auscultate. They often yawn when lifted — use the moment for an oral exam.

Preventive medicine & vet exam

  • Vaccinate against canine distemper (CDV) and rabies. CDV: 3 doses q3–4 wk from 8 wk; adults 2 doses then annual boosters. Rabies vaccine licensed for ferrets >12 wk; one booster if vaccine history unknown.
  • Dog CDV vaccine can induce distemper — in Australia 1/3 of the dog dose is used; in the US a ferret-licensed CDV vaccine exists.
  • Heartworm preventive in endemic areas. Endoparasites rare; ectoparasites occur (Advantage / Frontline / Revolution by labelled age and dose).
  • Empirically treat all incoming ferrets for ear mites (Otodectes cynotis) before mixing — mites are very common in ferrets.
  • Vet exam: check for fractured canines, peripheral lymph nodes (lymphoma is common with age), cardiac disease (a pronounced sinus arrhythmia is normal), GI foreign body, and vulvar size in spayed jills (enlargement = adrenal disease). Generalized alopecia (vs normal seasonal coat change) is a major indicator of adrenal disease.
Vaccine anaphylaxis in ferretsFerrets have a high prevalence of anaphylactic reaction to CDV and rabies vaccines, typically within 25 minutes: vomiting, dyspnoea, nausea, drooling, tachycardia, erythema, hypotension, collapse. First-line: diphenhydramine 2–4 mg/kg IM/IV; severe cases need epinephrine, dexamethasone, oxygen. Monitor every vaccinated ferret for ~25 min.

Key bacterial & GI diseases

  • Bacterial gastroenteritis (Campylobacter, E. coli, Salmonella) — zoonotic. From raw/undercooked meat, stress, poor hygiene, crowding. Campy incubation <24 h; E. coli/Salmonella up to days and can cause DIC and death within 24 h. Do not delay antibiotics even though campy can self-limit. Chloramphenicol covers all three; amoxicillin-clavulanate + a fluoroquinolone or aminoglycoside also first-line. Immunosuppressed people and children should NOT handle infected ferrets.
  • Helicobacter mustelae — essentially all US ferrets are exposed; spiral Gram-negative rod, causes gastritis/ulcers (gastric adenocarcinoma & lymphoma association). Mostly asymptomatic until stressed. Dx: silver-stained gastric biopsy (definitive); response to therapy is NOT diagnostic. Triple therapy (e.g. clarithromycin + metronidazole + bismuth subsalicylate) 3–4 wk. Isolation isn't realistic (near-universal infection) — isolate only ferrets with GI signs.
  • Proliferative bowel disease (Lawsonia intracellularis) — fecal-oral, young ferrets 10 wk–6 mo; thickened intestinal loops, mucoid/bloody diarrhoea, partial rectal prolapse. Only 1–3% of exposed develop signs (stress + nutrition triggers). Tx chloramphenicol or metronidazole 10–14 d. Same agent causes wet tail in hamsters & disease in swine/rabbits.
  • Listeriosis (L. monocytogenes) — ubiquitous Gram-positive rod; ingestion/inhalation; CNS signs (ataxia, paresis, paralysis) 1–90 d. Treat with CNS-penetrating antibiotic (penicillin/chloramphenicol) or euthanise. Raw diets recalled for Listeria are a source.
  • Botulism (C. botulinum types A/B/C; type C usually fatal) — from contaminated canned/meat food; descending flaccid paralysis (dysphagia, ataxia, ascending paresis, third-eyelid prolapse, respiratory paralysis) 12–96 h. Keep on the ddx for neuro signs.
  • Mycobacteriosis (M. bovis, avium, etc.) — ZOONOTIC. Ferrets are sentinels for M. bovis in New Zealand. Weight loss, lymphadenopathy, draining lesions. Acid-fast stain/PCR/culture. Not practical to treat in a shelter.
  • Mycoplasmosis — host-specific; chronic non-productive cough, haemoptysis; high morbidity, low mortality. Infection may be life-long; segregate and inform adopters of recurrence risk.

Parasitic & fungal diseases

  • Coccidiosis (Eimeria, Isospora) — usually subclinical but outbreaks cause severe enteric disease/death; foul bloody diarrhoea, rectal prolapse, tenesmus. Sporulated oocysts resist most disinfectants → use steam, 10% ammonia, or boiling water; bleach/QAC/heat for cages.
  • Cryptosporidiosis (C. parvum) — ZOONOTIC. Partial acid-fast; fecal-oral, highly contagious between stressed animals; self-limiting 2–3 wk. Paromomycin 125–165 mg/kg PO BID × 5 d (not curative). Oocysts resist bleach — destroy with 5% ammonia or 3% hydrogen peroxide ×20 min, or temp <0 °C / >65 °C.
  • Ear mites (Otodectes cynotis) — very common; treat all incoming ferrets empirically. 1% ivermectin diluted 1:10 in propylene glycol, 400 µg/kg split between ears, repeat in 14 d; clean/disinfect environment.
  • Giardiasis — ZOONOTIC. Diarrhoea; fenbendazole 50 mg/kg SID ×5 d or metronidazole 25 mg/kg BID ×5 d; gloves/masks; bleach/QAC.
  • Sarcoptic mange — ZOONOTIC. Rarely infests ferrets (usually from dogs/fomites); generalized alopecia + intense pruritus or foot-confined disease; skin-scrape false-negatives common.
  • Toxoplasmosis — from raw meat / cat feces; unlikely in shelters that don't feed raw.
  • Dermatophytosis (Microsporum canis, Trichophyton) — ZOONOTIC. Kits/young/immunosuppressed; usually non-pruritic. Lime sulphur dips + systemic itraconazole/griseofulvin.
  • Systemic fungal disease — Blastomyces dermatitidis (SE US, Mississippi/Ohio valleys), Coccidioides immitis (SW US), Cryptococcus. Respiratory + systemic signs. Not contagious between ferrets and unlikely to arise in a shelter unless exposed before entry.

Non-infectious disease & spay/neuter

  • ~1/3 of ferrets >3 yr develop one of the classic three neoplasias, and often more than one if they live long enough:
  • Lymphoma (lymphocytic) — lethargy, weight loss, cervical/popliteal lymphadenopathy; poor prognosis.
  • Insulinoma (beta-cell tumour) — chronic increasing lethargy/weakness, hind-limb weakness, ptyalism/pawing at the mouth, hypoglycaemic collapse/seizures. Glucose <60 mg/dL. Surgery + prednisone.
  • Adrenocortical disease — almost exclusively US ferrets, due to early-age spay/neuter. Bilateral symmetrical alopecia from the tail base progressing rostrally, pruritus in 1/3, increased aggression; jills get vulvar swelling, hobs get prostatic hypertrophy/dysuria. Dx US or adrenal hormone panel; surgical or medical management.
  • Spay/neuter: US shelters neuter as early as 6 wk (with first CDV vaccine). Ferrets are induced ovulators — an unbred jill develops persistent oestrus → hyperestrogenism → non-regenerative anaemia and bone-marrow suppression.
  • Not a suitable pet for young children; ownership regulations vary by locality/state.
Adrenal disease ↔ early spay/neuterThe classic spayed-jill presentation — symmetrical tail-base alopecia advancing forward plus vulvar swelling — is adrenocortical disease, driven by gonadectomy at a very young age (the US norm). Distinguish from normal seasonal coat change, which is not accompanied by vulvar swelling.

Rabbits source chapter

Hindgut-fermenting, obligate-nasal-breathing prey animals with continuously growing teeth. The exam anchors: oral penicillin is contraindicated (fatal dysbiosis), fipronil is contraindicated, RHDV is a reportable non-enveloped calicivirus, uterine adenocarcinoma drives routine spaying, and E. cuniculi is the dwarf-breed head-tilt. (Housing/welfare detail — ASV 2026 — lives in the Rabbit Housing tab of the Nutrition hub.)

Anatomy & biology

  • Size 1–7 kg. Dental formula I 2/1 C 0/0 P 3/2 M 3/3 = 28; the small second upper incisor is the peg tooth; teeth grow continuously — malocclusion and dental disease are common, so a thorough oral exam is essential.
  • Hindgut fermenters: most nutrition from cecal fermentation; fibre passes as hard fecal pellets, non-fibre as cecotrophs eaten directly from the anus in the early morning — essential to wellbeing.
  • GIT = 10–20% of body weight; cecum holds ~40% of ingesta (most prominent abdominal organ). Liver has 6 lobes — liver-lobe torsion occurs. Thymus persists into adulthood (thymoma/thymic lymphoma reported).
  • Obligate nasal breathers with a small thoracic cavity → URT disease causes severe signs; elevate the head intra-op to keep abdominal contents off the diaphragm under GA.
  • Fractional urinary calcium excretion 45–60% (vs <2% in most species) → prone to hypercalciuria, urolithiasis and "urine sludge." Calcium absorption is vitamin-D-independent.
  • Crepuscular prey species: flight response dominates behaviour; spend 30–70% of time foraging; territorial, scent-mark with urine/feces/chin-rubbing; social (groups of 2–3) but pairs can fight; whiskers very sensitive — dislike having the nose touched.

Diet, handling & preventive health

  • Hay-based diet — timothy/grass hay available at all times; alfalfa only for pregnant, lactating or growing rabbits (high Ca²⁺). Pellets ~¼ cup per 5 lb. High water intake 120–150 mL/kg/day — offer a sipper bottle and/or bowl and monitor.
  • Handling: support the hindquarters always — unsupported kicking causes vertebral fracture/luxation. Blood: cephalic/saphenous/femoral/jugular; avoid ear-vein injection (thrombosis & pinna necrosis).
  • No routine vaccination in the US (myxomatosis & RVHD vaccines used in Europe). Intake exam to ID external parasites and treat before entering the general population. Antibiotics for rabbits: doxycycline, TMS, fluoroquinolones.
  • Uterine adenocarcinoma in 50–80% of intact does >3–4 yr → ovariectomy/OVH recommended in all non-breeding does at 6–9 mo. Males: low testicular-neoplasia incidence; neuter mainly for behaviour (urine marking).
Oral penicillin is contraindicated in rabbitsEnteral penicillin disrupts the cecal microbiome and causes fatal Clostridium overgrowth (antibiotic-associated enterotoxaemia). For rabbit syphilis use injectable penicillin G — enteral penicillin must never be given. The same dysbiosis risk applies to other narrow-spectrum Gram-positive oral antibiotics.

Bacterial respiratory & enteric disease

  • Pasteurella multocida — most common isolate, >50% of rabbits; non-spore-forming bipolar Gram-negative rod. "Snuffles," pneumonia, otitis externa/media/interna, abscesses, conjunctivitis (via nasolacrimal duct), torticollis, endocarditis, pyometra. Disease emerges with stress/crowding/pregnancy/poor nutrition. Symptomatic rabbits may stay with bonded, already-exposed mates but should be ≥6 ft from other rabbits or in a separate room. Survey of respiratory rabbits: 50% Pasteurella, 50% also Bordetella, 28% Pseudomonas, 17.4% Staphylococcus → culture, don't assume Pasteurella.
  • Bordetella bronchiseptica — often subclinical in rabbits but rabbits are a reservoir that infects guinea pigs → never co-house rabbits with guinea pigs. Also house rabbits away from rats (which carry M. pulmonis, Pasteurella, Pseudomonas, BB).
  • Enteropathogenic E. coli (EPEC) — large outbreaks in weaning commercial rabbits; effaces cecal/colonic microvilli; watery diarrhoea; mortality >50%; paintbrush hemorrhages on the cecal wall at necropsy. Supportive care + TMS/fluoroquinolone.
  • Tyzzer's disease (Clostridium piliforme) — motile spore-forming obligate intracellular bacterium; weanlings crash from lethargy/anorexia/watery diarrhoea to acute death; ante-mortem diagnosis is not possible (won't grow in culture); necropsy = patchy liver/proximal-colon necrosis + myocardial lesions. Spores killed by 0.3% sodium hypochlorite or 80 °C ×30 min.
  • Proliferative enteritis (Lawsonia intracellularis) — weanlings 2–4 mo, diarrhoea + wasting; chloramphenicol/macrolides; environmental disinfection.

Rabbit syphilis & viral diseases

  • Treponema paraluiscuniculi (rabbit syphilis) — highly contagious venereal spirochaete of rabbits; NOT contagious to non-rabbit species, NOT zoonotic. Crusting/ulceration/scabs of face & anogenital region. Dx darkfield of skin scrapings. Tx benzathine/procaine penicillin G (e.g. 42,000–60,000 U/kg SC q7d ×3 wk, or 40,000 U/kg IM q24h ×5–7 d) in ALL exposed rabbits — enteral penicillin must NOT be given.
  • Myxomatosis (Leporipoxvirus) — enzootic in wild rabbits on the west coast of N. America (Oregon, California); arthropod-vector/direct contact. Almost always fatal in domestic rabbits: lethargy, fever, swollen eyelids, ocular/nasal discharge, skin lesions/hemorrhage. No vaccine in the US; immediate isolation + full PPE.
  • Rabbit haemorrhagic disease (RHDV1 & 2, calicivirus) — REPORTABLE. See box and algorithm below.
  • Rotavirus — weanlings 2–4 mo; green-yellow watery diarrhoea; high morbidity, generally low mortality; isolate, reduce crowding, increase dietary fibre.
  • Shope fibroma & rabbit (cottontail) papillomavirus — arthropod-transmitted; wart-like skin tumours; fibromas regress ~by 6 mo; papillomas can progress to SCC and metastasize to lymph nodes. Surgical removal + arthropod control.
  • Rabies — rare; nonspecific signs then paresis/paralysis/head tremor. No licensed rabbit rabies vaccine in the US → protect outdoor-housed rabbits from wildlife.
  • Herpes simplex virus (HSV) — reverse-zoonotic: humans with cold sores transmit to rabbits → encephalitis, severe neuro signs, death. Handle rabbits minimally during a human HSV outbreak.
RHDV — the high-yield calicivirusNon-enveloped calicivirus, reportable, affects only European/domestic rabbits; disease in rabbits >2 mo (neonates resistant). Replicates in liver → hepatic necrosis + DIC. Morbidity/mortality high (mortality approaching 100%; range 40–100%). Sudden death with blood from orifices — but absence of bleeding does NOT rule it out. No US-licensed vaccine. Disinfect with non-enveloped-virus agents: 0.5% sodium hypochlorite or 1% formalin. Tx not recommended (high mortality + transmission risk); isolate, full PPE.

The RHDV intake-quarantine algorithm Fig. 6

Routine incoming quarantine is rarely indicated for domestic shelter animals, but RHDV2 is a severe risk to the general rabbit population, so the decision is risk-assessment based:

  • Trigger question: is the shelter within 500 miles / 800 km of an area with RHD reported in the past 2 years?
  • If YES → individual risk assessment. A rabbit is "at risk" if ANY of: outdoor / outdoor-wild/feral-rabbit exposure in the last 10 days; the general shelter rabbit population is unvaccinated; no proof of current RHDV2 vaccination. At-risk rabbits → 10-day quarantine of incoming.
  • If NO (or "no" to all individual risks) → shelter assessment. Does the shelter house >1 rabbit / a bonded pair-trio? Can quarantine be done without increasing length of stay? If quarantine would lengthen stay or there's a single rabbit, risks of quarantine outweigh benefits → may house in general population.
  • Either path then → enact risk-mitigation + closely monitor evolving regional risk.
  • Vaccine onset of immunity: Medgene (RHDV2) fully protective 14 d after the 2nd booster (35 d after initial); Filavac (RHDV1&2) and Eravac (RHDV2) onset of immunity at 7 d.

Mitigation bundle: keep population within humane-care capacity; housing that meets material/space standards; consistent intake history; a case definition + response plan (immediate isolation, report to authorities); train field/shelter/volunteer staff; vaccinate incoming rabbits in affected regions; quarantine per assessment; biosecurity with PPE and low-to-high order of care; protect feed/browse/enrichment from contamination; sanitation targeting durable non-enveloped viruses; proactive LOS management to limit at-risk rabbits on site.

Parasites — ectoparasites & the fipronil trap

  • Fleas (Ctenocephalides) — pruritus, self-biting, alopecia. Fipronil must NOT be used on rabbits; imidacloprid, lufenuron and selamectin are generally safe (selamectin is metabolized faster than in cats → different regimen).
  • Ticks — in outdoor/wild-exposed rabbits; can transmit myxomatosis, papillomatosis, tularemia, Lyme, Rocky Mountain spotted fever. Remove mechanically; ivermectin 0.2–0.4 mg/kg for remainder. Dog/cat tick products and flea/tick collars must NOT be used on rabbits.
  • Lice (Haemodipsus ventricosus) — rare but heavy burdens → anaemia; can transmit tularemia; ivermectin q2 wk ×2–3.
  • Psoroptes cuniculi (ear mite) — head-shaking, painful crusty aural discharge, head tilt; 3-wk life cycle, survives ~3 wk off host; not transmissible to other species. Ivermectin 0.2–0.44 mg/kg SC q10–14d ×3, or selamectin/imidacloprid/moxidectin. Fipronil and insecticide baths/dips must NOT be used.
  • Cheyletiella parasitovorax (fur mite) — ZOONOTIC ("walking dandruff") — contagious to people, dogs, cats; dry scaly pruritic dermatitis; tape prep; SC ivermectin (repeat 10–14 d) or selamectin.

Internal parasites & E. cuniculi

  • Coccidia — the most common GI parasite of rabbits. 12 Eimeria spp.; E. stiedae is hepatic (bile-duct) — hepatic coccidiosis can be fatal in young rabbits by obstructing liver function (↑AST/ALT/bile acids/total bilirubin; liver studded with encapsulated abscesses at necropsy). Intestinal Eimeria → diarrhoea, intussusception, weight loss. Severe disease in rabbits <6 mo. Tx sulfadimethoxine, TMS, ponazuril, or amprolium in water; focus on young/pregnant/immunocompromised.
  • Cryptosporidiosis (C. parvum) — ZOONOTIC. Self-limiting diarrhoea (4–5 d) in young rabbits; supportive care; PPE + handwashing.
  • Helminths: pinworms (Passalurus ambiguus) common but usually non-pathogenic — ID of eggs should not automatically prompt treatment; benzimidazole if needed. Cerebrospinal nematodiasis (Baylisascaris) — ZOONOTIC, fatal/severe neuro disease (neural larva migrans). Rabbits are the intermediate host for Taenia spp. (cysts in SC/skeletal muscle) — praziquantel 5–10 mg/kg, repeat in 10 d.
  • Encephalitozoon cuniculi — intracellular microsporidian (a fungus); seroprevalence 7.5–84.7%. Spores shed in urine for up to 3 mo; horizontal + transplacental transmission; targets brain, kidney, lung, lens, myocardium → head tilt, nystagmus, vestibular signs, paresis, uveitis, cataract, renal failure. In dwarf breeds, head tilt is most often E. cuniculi; in standard breeds it's more likely Pasteurella or otitis. Serology does NOT correlate with clinical severity and seroconversion is not protective; definitive Dx = histopath. No treatment reliably clears it; albendazole/fenbendazole/oxibendazole (7.5–20 mg/kg) ×30 d + NSAIDs may slow it — but these drugs are potentially toxic. Most disinfectants kill it. Zoonotic only to immunocompromised people.

Non-infectious disease

  • GI stasis — anorexia, weight loss, diarrhoea; always check the teeth first; may be precipitated by improper antibiotic use (dysbiosis).
  • Dental / URI — excessive lacrimation + periocular crusting; rabbits have a single ventral nasolacrimal gland per eye (not a pair), so obstruction → epiphora; tear-duct flush with topical proparacaine.
  • Urinary sludge — vitamin-D-independent calcium absorption + high dietary Ca (e.g. alfalfa) → sludge/sand in the bladder. Distinguish red urine: rabbit urine is often red/orange from porphyrins (intermittent, lasts 3–4 d) — use a Wood's lamp to differentiate porphyrinuria (fluoresces) from true hematuria.
  • Tularemia (Francisella tularensis, "rabbit fever") — ZOONOTIC, reportable in some states. Mostly wild/outdoor rabbits; aerosol/direct contact/ectoparasites; diarrhoea, neuro signs, death.
  • Spay/neuter: castrate mainly for behaviour; spaying is important — 80% of does >4 yr develop uterine adenocarcinoma.
  • Adoption: nervous/shy rabbits should not be handled by children or unsupervised minors.

Guinea Pigs source chapter

Monogastric herbivores that cannot synthesize vitamin C, develop ovarian cysts with age, and are uniquely vulnerable to Bordetella carried asymptomatically by rabbits, dogs and cats. They tolerate stress and illness poorly — a sick guinea pig has a guarded-to-grave prognosis.

Biology & husbandry

  • Weight 700–1200 g. Dental formula 2(I 1/1 C 0/0 PM 1/1 M 3/3); elodont (continuously growing) teeth — cheek-tooth overgrowth from low-fibre diet, congenital malocclusion or trauma. The vet exam MUST include a thorough oral exam.
  • Monogastric herbivore with a voluminous, very thin-walled cecum; uses a mucous-trap method of colonic separation and practices coprophagy multiple times daily.
  • Sow pubic symphysis is fibrocartilaginous and relaxes before parturition, but fuses permanently at ~7–8 mo if she has never had a litter → dystocia risk if bred for the first time after that age.
  • Crepuscular; startle/stampede/circle when frightened (trampling risk for young); develop rigid preferences with age — disrupt them and you get anorexia/GI upset. Social — house in pairs/small groups or harems (1 male : up to 10 females).
  • Housing: solid floors (wire → pododermatitis/fractures); mesh walls for ventilation; bedding shredded paper / newspaper pellets / hardwood — pine & cedar contraindicated (aromatic compounds). Heat/humidity/draft sensitive but cold-tolerant: 65–79 °F, humidity <50%, avoid >80 °F. Provide a hide box. Don't jump → closed-top cage with ≥10-inch walls suffices.

Diet — vitamin C

  • Guinea pigs cannot synthesize vitamin C (lack L-gulonolactone oxidase) and must get it from the diet → scurvy without supplementation. Supply via species-formulated guinea-pig pellets and fresh vegetables (kale, peppers, oranges). High water requirement ~100 mL/kg/day.
  • Grass-hay-based diet; give fruit sparingly (sugar → cecal dysbiosis). Never feed muesli/seed-and-fruit mixes to elodont-toothed species — they selectively pick out high-carb pieces and leave the fibre, reducing the lateral chewing needed to wear teeth → dental disease.
Vitamin C is non-negotiable in guinea pigsUnlike rabbits and most mammals, guinea pigs lack the enzyme to make ascorbic acid. Plain rabbit pellets do not supply enough vitamin C; feed guinea-pig-specific, vitamin-C-formulated pellets plus fresh vegetables. Deficiency → scurvy (poor wound healing, bleeding, lethargy, painful gait).

Respiratory disease

  • Bordetella bronchiseptica — a major guinea-pig respiratory pathogen. Rabbits, dogs, cats and non-human primates are asymptomatic carriers that infect guinea pigs (sharing airspace can suffice). ~1-wk incubation; fibrinopurulent pneumonia/pleuritis, nasal-ocular discharge, dyspnoea, otitis, possible abortion. Tx TMS/doxycycline/fluoroquinolone + fluids + vitamin C. Prevent exposure to other species. Injectable BB bacterin (with a booster) has had moderate success in outbreaks but routine vaccination of all shelter guinea pigs is NOT recommended.
  • Adenoviral pneumonia — necrotizing bronchopneumonia, 5–10 d incubation, low morbidity but high mortality (may die acutely); mostly lab populations.
  • Streptococcus pneumoniae / S. pyogenes / S. moniliformis / Haemophilus / Klebsiella — poor husbandry is the major risk factor; bronchopneumonia, fibrinopurulent pleuritis, pericarditis; treat by culture.

GI & systemic infectious disease

  • Tyzzer's disease (Clostridium piliforme) — fecal-oral; young/stressed/immunocompromised; intestinal inflammation + focal hepatic necrosis; can't culture on routine media; treatment unrewarding → prevention by husbandry; euthanasia may be reasonable.
  • Salmonella typhimurium / enteritidis — ZOONOTIC. Less common but high outbreak mortality; unkempt coat, weakness, diarrhoea, conjunctivitis, abortion; fecal-oral from contaminated feed/water. Don't treat with antibiotics (creates asymptomatic carriers + zoonotic risk) — supportive care for mild cases; euthanise severe cases or intermittent shedders.
  • Antibiotic-associated enterotoxaemia — improper antibiotics (doxy/TMS/fluoroquinolone are appropriate; monitor for diarrhoea) → Clostridium difficile overgrowth.
  • GI parasitism — Eimeria caviae, Balantidium caviae, Paraspidodera; juveniles more affected. Cryptosporidium wrairi — ZOONOTIC, failure to thrive in young/immunosuppressed; no effective treatment; biosecurity as for ferret/rabbit crypto (oocysts destroyed by 5% ammonia or 3% H₂O₂ ×20 min, temp <0 / >65 °C).
  • Cervical lymphadenitis ("lumps") — Streptococcus zooepidemicus (normal oropharyngeal/nasal flora) ± S. moniliformis (zoonotic); oral abrasions from dental malocclusion/rough feed let bacteria invade cervical nodes → abscesses that may rupture; advanced cases → pneumonia/metritis/septicaemia. Systemic antibiotics by C&S ± surgical node removal.
  • LCMV (Lymphocytic choriomeningitis virus) — ZOONOTIC arenavirus. Common in mice/hamsters/chinchillas, less so guinea pigs; meningitis + hind-limb paralysis; transmission via urine/saliva/feces, transplacental, biting insects; euthanasia often warranted (severity + zoonotic risk).

Dermatologic disease & the zoonotic mite

  • Trixacarus caviae (sarcoptic mite) — ZOONOTIC. Severe pruritus with white-to-yellow crusts and excoriations; scratching so intense it can be confused with seizure activity. Can lead to secondary fungal/bacterial infection; survives in the environment → reinfection without decontamination. Dx skin scrape; ivermectin 0.2–0.4 mg/kg SQ q10–14d ×3 doses + NSAID/antihistamine/analgesia. Temporarily infects humans → environmental decontamination required.
  • Chirodiscoides caviae (fur mite) — milder dermatitis; heavy burdens → alopecia/crusts.
  • Lice (Gliricola porcelli, Gyropus ovalis), C. felis fleas, Demodex caviae (rare).
  • Dermatophytosis — usually Trichophyton mentagrophytes (also M. canis, M. gypseum); potentially zoonotic. Circular alopecia/erythema/scaly crusts on nose/forehead/pinnae/around eyes/feet. T. mentagrophytes does NOT fluoresce under Wood's lamp → fungal culture required. Terbinafine > itraconazole; correct overcrowding; clean with bleach 1:10.

Non-infectious disease

  • Ovarian cysts — 76% of guinea pigs by 5 yr. Space-occupying cysts → decreased fertility, anorexia, abdominal distension, lethargy, bilateral symmetrical truncal alopecia.
  • Bacterial conjunctivitis (Chlamydia caviae) — ZOONOTIC potential. Inclusion conjunctivitis; keratoconjunctivitis, serous-to-purulent ocular discharge, chemosis, follicular hypertrophy, uveitis; usually self-limiting 3–4 wk; treat with topical tetracycline ophthalmic to limit spread.
  • Anorexia/weight loss/diarrhoea — full dental check (often needs GA).

Small Rodents source chapter

Rats, mice, chinchillas, sugar gliders, hamsters and gerbils. Incessant chewers with continuously growing incisors. The exam anchors: wet tail (Lawsonia) in weanling hamsters, lifelong M. pulmonis respiratory disease in rats, and the zoonotic trio — rat-bite fever (S. moniliformis), LCMV and hantavirus.

Biology, housing & handling

  • Incessant chewers (incisors grow continually); mostly nocturnal except gerbils (day-active); sense of smell is the most acute sense — staff must wash hands / change gloves after handling predator species or rodents will panic and bite.
  • Bite likelihood: rats & gerbils least likely to bite when startled; hamsters have a low startle threshold.
  • Sociality varies: rats live in mixed groups (introduce on neutral ground); male mice aggressive together (house as single male or with females); dwarf hamsters more social than golden/Chinese; gerbils territorial in captivity → house individually unless a compatible pair in a large enclosure. House only same-gender groups.
  • Escape-proof, well-ventilated enclosures (can chew through plastic); good climbers — provide tubes/ramps/cave structures; removable bottoms, high sides, secure locks. Substrate: shredded/recycled paper preferred; cedar (and pine) anecdotally linked to respiratory/skin disease — avoid; bacteria/respiratory irritation lower on paper bedding.
  • Most rodents omnivorous → species-specific pelleted diet + fresh foods; avoid seed/fruit mixes (selective feeding). Hamsters urinate in one corner; clean weekly. Scruffing works for hamsters but not rats; tail restraint not recommended for pet rodents.
  • Wellness exam: check for lumps (mammary/scent-gland masses are common and best treated small); no routine vaccination/testing. Zoonotic risks: hantavirus, LCMV, and S. moniliformis (rat-bite fever).

Respiratory disease (rats & mice)

  • Minute respiratory mycoplasmosis — Mycoplasma pulmonis = the most common major respiratory pathogen of rats & mice. Colonizes respiratory epithelium, nasal passages, middle ear → increased respiratory noise, porphyrin staining, sneezing, chattering, laboured breathing, head tilt; co-infections cause immunosuppression. Intracellular → cannot be fully cleared; recrudescence is the rule. Chronic bronchopneumonia & bronchiectasis → pulmonary fibrosis & right-heart disease with age. Enrofloxacin 10 mg/kg + doxycycline 5 mg/kg controls but does not eliminate; sildenafil may mitigate pulmonary fibrosis. Worsened by ↑environmental ammonia (>19–25 ppm), cedar/pine substrate, vitamin A/E deficiency, tobacco smoke. Serology hard (young rats serong up to 4 mo post-exposure).
  • Sendai virus & sialodacryoadenitis virus (SDAV) — acute respiratory infection; sneezing, ocular porphyrin staining, conjunctivitis, keratitis; mild/self-limiting (2 wk–2 mo) in adults but neonates/weanlings die; often co-pathogens with M. pulmonis. SDAV → cervical salivary-gland inflammation/necrosis; tissues heal 7–10 d, signs resolve ~30 d.
  • Bacterial pneumonia (Streptococcus pneumoniae) — most common bacterial pneumonia but seldom the sole agent (often with M. pulmonis / Sendai / CAR bacillus); more severe in juveniles (may die with no antemortem signs); also C. kutscheri in debilitated/immunosuppressed. Treat with beta-lactamase-resistant penicillins by C&S.
Rat-bite fever — Streptobacillus moniliformisPrimary cause of rat-bite fever in humans. Wild & pet rats are asymptomatic carriers (nasopharynx, middle ear, respiratory tract; present in urine/blood). Transmission via bites, scratches, handling rat excreta, or contaminated food/water. Human signs (fever, headache, vomiting, myalgia) ~7 days post-exposure. Prevention: careful handling, hygiene, husbandry, PPE.

GI disease — wet tail & others

  • Wet tail — Lawsonia intracellularis proliferative ileitis, the classic pathogen of weanling hamsters (4–8 wk) but any age under stress (breeding, overcrowding, poor nutrition, concurrent disease). Intracellular bacterium targeting dividing enterocytes → proliferative ileitis (spreads to jejunum/cecum/colon); called "enzootic intestinal adenocarcinoma." Acute (7–10 d): haemorrhagic diarrhoea, ill thrift, lethargy; subacute: hunched posture, palpable abdominal mass; symptomatic hamsters may die within 48 h. Mortality ~90%. Aggressive Tx (oral/SC/IO fluids, electrolytes, bismuth subsalicylate, nutrition) or humane euthanasia; empiric antibiotics — metronidazole, tetracycline, chloramphenicol, enrofloxacin, TMS. Survivors → intestinal obstruction/intussusception/rectal prolapse.
  • Salmonella (S. typhimurium) — ZOONOTIC. Rare in mice/rats; more severe in hamsters; take precautions with rodent feces.
  • Clostridium difficile — adult diarrhoea several days after antibiotics (especially penicillins and lincomycin); PCR diagnosis.
  • Clostridium piliforme (Tyzzer's) — hamsters & gerbils; acute death; driven by stress/crowding/poor husbandry; does NOT usually occur in healthy immunocompetent animals.
  • Taenia taeniaeformis (tapeworm) — ZOONOTIC. Cats/dogs are definitive hosts, rodents (and rabbits) are intermediate hosts; hepatic cysts, gastric pH changes; a risk factor for hepatic sarcoma in rats; praziquantel 30 mg/kg PO q14d ×3 (caution with encysted larvae → host immune reaction).

Skin disease & ectoparasites

  • Dermatophytosis (T. mentagrophytes) — ZOONOTIC. Uncommon in pet mice/rats; patchy alopecia/crusting on face/head/neck/tail; usually minimal pruritus; does NOT fluoresce under Wood's lamp. Environmental decontamination + biosecurity.
  • Fur mites — Radfordia ensifera (rats), Myobia musculi / M. musculinus / Radfordia affinis (mice); usually subclinical, heavy burdens → self-trauma/ulcerative dermatitis; Dx microscopy of mites/eggs on hair shafts; ivermectin or selamectin + environmental decontamination (pyrethroids or fipronil).
  • Ornithonyssus bacoti (tropical rat mite) — rats/mice/gerbils/hamsters; heavy infestation → anaemia, debilitation, death; will bite humans when the intended host is unavailable.
  • Staphylococcal dermatitis — nude/strain mice at risk; local abscesses, conjunctivitis, ulceration, self-trauma on face/head; necrotizing dermatitis (streptococcal) on shoulder/lumbar/hip; treat by C&S + debride/drain.

Reptiles source chapter

Shelters should provide temporary housing only, after reviewing local law (federal/state/county/municipal restrictions vary by species). Reptiles are poikilotherms that thermoregulate behaviourally — get the thermal gradient, UVB and disinfectant choices right and most short-term care is straightforward.

Intake, ID & basic husbandry

  • Review local laws before accepting any reptile/amphibian — ownership may be restricted. If adequate husbandry can't be provided, find alternative placement (especially for legal-investigation animals).
  • Individualize ID and records — never group multiple reptiles under one ID. Use temporary marking: non-toxic paint or fingernail polish.
  • Not social — do not house with carnivorous mammals (stress) or together (territorial aggression, cannibalism). Develop written husbandry/handling protocols.
  • Enclosure size ≥ 1–1.5× the animal's total body length; for snakes the enclosure perimeter must exceed the snake's total length. Arboreal → vertical climbing space; terrestrial → horizontal space; aquatic → swimming + basking. Most common reptiles fit 10–20-gallon aquaria; small species in sealed ventilated containers; secure screen tops with weights/clips.
  • Substrate: clean recycled newspaper is best (also pebbles, sand, aspen chips, pelleted corn cob, rabbit pellets, cypress mulch); cat litter must never be used. Cage design: secure wire-mesh top, hiding place, heavy ceramic water bowl, focal secondary heat source.

Temperature, heating, humidity & light

  • Ambient 80–85 °F (27–29 °C) with a secondary focal heat source ~10 °F warmer. The animal must be able to choose its body temperature from a gradient: too cold → chronic hypothermia → immunosuppression, GI stasis, lethargy; too hot → hyperthermia, thermal burns, acute death.
  • Focal heating: radiant heat from below (warms ventral surface, mimics a sun-warmed rock) + radiant heat from above (light bulbs, ceramic heat lamps). The resting surface should not feel hot to the human hand after ~1 min; heat sources never placed directly inside the enclosure.
  • Humidity 30–80% with a gradient if possible.
  • Lighting / UVB: sunlight if possible; no more than 10–14 h light/day. UVB matters most for herbivorous & omnivorous reptiles (skin synthesis of active vitamin D). UVB wavelength 290–320 nm; bulb 12–18 inches from the animal; must NOT be filtered through glass or acrylic; replace bulb every 12 months (output falls before visible light does).

Sanitation

  • Remove all animals before cleaning; remove gross contamination with mild detergent + water; then disinfect with dilute bleach 1:32 or quaternary ammonium compounds.
  • Rinse enclosure surfaces thoroughly with clean water after disinfection — reptile/amphibian skin is very sensitive.
  • Sanitize before housing a new group; discard items that cannot be disinfected (wood, cardboard).
Phenolics & glutaraldehyde are toxic to reptiles and amphibiansThese compounds — present in many common shelter disinfectants — must never be used in reptile/amphibian enclosures. Use dilute bleach (1:32) or QAC instead, and always rinse thoroughly with clean water because their skin is highly absorptive.

Horses source chapter

Trickle-feeding herd grazers whose physical health and behaviour are tightly coupled to a continuous high-fibre intake and conspecific company. Exam-relevant for the shelter setting: squamous gastric ulcers, colic, the quarantine pathogens (EHV, Salmonella, strangles), and a cluster of behaviour problems driven by underlying discomfort or management.

Diet, water & gastric health

  • Require continuous through-put of high-fibre, calorie-sparse forage — they alternate eating and resting around the clock, rarely >30–60 min of rest before resuming foraging.
  • Equine squamous gastric ulcer disease — prevalence varies by breed/discipline: 17–39% in untrained/unworked horses, 66–100% in those in training/competition. Colic prevalence >4% annually.
  • Provide free access to water (domestically managed). Freshly voided fecal balls should be slightly moist, not dry/crumbly — reduced water intake is a common cause of GI discomfort/dehydration colic. Horses drink less in cold weather or with sudden drops to below-freezing temps.
  • Stress behaviour from infrequent, calorie-dense, highly palatable grain meals: stomping, weaving, head-shaking, pawing, teeth-grinding, stress vocalization.

Social needs & housing

  • Like to be with other horses; more comfortable in outdoor enclosures with — or within sight of — other horses. Under natural conditions eating and resting are conspicuously synchronized across herd members.
  • Indoor enclosure ≥ 100 sq ft; give at least 1–several hours of daily turn-out to pasture/paddock — not confined 24/7 to a stall.

Quarantine

  • Quarantine 2–3 weeks.
  • Equine herpes myeloencephalopathy (EHV) and Salmonella — large proportions of latently infected horses shed under the stress of transport/management change.
  • Strangles (Streptococcus equi) — common in auction and holding facilities that rescue horses pass through.
  • Isolation from other horses is stressful → provide visual contact with another equid, or a substitute (mirror, poster, companion-horse video, or another species for company).
  • Limit human handling during quarantine — horses acclimate more efficiently with less human interaction, and minimal handling reduces both biosecurity risk and human-animal stress.

Behaviour problems in the shelter

  • Fear/alarm: may aggress toward the handler rather than the scary stimulus; forced exposure to a feared species without gradual introduction → distress (locomotor stereotypies, frantic self-injurious escape, inappetence, restlessness, cowering depression).
  • Aggression: most common cause is underlying physical discomfort or illness (often mislabelled misbehaviour). Horses learn aggression from punitive handling and inadvertent reinforcement of fear responses. Intact adult males have a lower aggression threshold → stallions need specialized housing + skilled non-confrontational handling; castrate non-breeding stallions (stallion-like behaviour takes weeks+ to fade; geldings may still react to mares).
  • Food urgency/aggression: poor nutrition or starvation → food-urgent/aggressive horses that guard feeding areas, aggress at herd-mates, kick barriers; worse with infrequent palatable grain meals. Give continuous all-forage diet + salt/trace-mineral licks. Horses mark feed/water with feces.
  • Wood chewing / object licking — sign of salt/mineral deficiency.
  • Stereotypies: locomotor (pacing, circling, weaving, pawing) — natural responses to physical/psychological discomfort; should not be punished. Cribbing (oral stereotypy — grasping a surface with the upper incisors, arching the neck, gulping air with a grunt) occurs around palatable grain meals; 2–10% of the population.
  • Sleep deprivation: horses light-sleep standing but need recumbent rest for REM sleep; physical discomfort or insecurity → won't lie down; after 2–3 wk without recumbent rest they collapse into standing sleep → somnolence, dull attitude, hyperreactivity, repeated abandoned attempts to lie down.
  • Overhandled foal syndrome — orphan foals over-bonded to humans: follow people like a dog, suckle clothing, vocalize to humans, initiate horse-play (rearing/bucking/mounting) with people → unsafe handling and limited trainability. Limit human interaction; bucket-feed (not bottle); disassociate humans from mealtime (remote bucket filling); provide equine companionship.
  • Self-mutilation: self-biting, kicking/slamming body parts against objects, pawing, frenetic weaving/pacing.
  • Relinquishment drivers: handling difficulties, aggression to humans/herd-mates, stereotypies/cribbing — many trace to undiagnosed physical discomfort often not relayed to the receiving organisation. (Of 187 slaughter-auction rescue prospects: 66 euthanised for medical problems, 26 for behaviour problems not responsive to training.)

Avian Avian Care in the Shelter — Ch. 11

Birds are low-frequency on the exam, so this tab is deliberately tight. The high-yield anchors are the avian zoonoses (psittacosis above all), management/sanitation in the shelter, and a handful of biology/welfare facts. Most companion birds are psittacines; legally, birds were excluded from the 1966 Animal Welfare Act and there are no federal laws for companion psittacines — defer to state law.

Psittacosis is the avian exam anchorChlamydophila psittaci is an obligate intracellular bacterium stable in dried droppings for months, so the single most effective shelter control is daily feces removal. Treatment is doxycycline for a full 45 days. Tie it to public-health risk: people are infected by inhaling dried fecal dust.

Microbiology, biology & welfare notes

  • Droppings flora rule: in psittacines, normal flora is predominantly gram-positive; gram-negative organisms in droppings are regarded as potential pathogens. Contagious bacterial disease is rare in parrots but when it occurs it's usually Salmonella, Mycobacterium, or Chlamydophila psittaci. Most useful lab tests: PCV (normal 45–55), gram stains, fecal wet mounts (yeast, protozoa, megabacteria). Blood volume ≈ 10% of body weight.
  • Normal heart rate 150–350 bpm; normal (cloacal) temperature 103–107 °F. Crop sits on the right of the thoracic inlet — should hold food, not fluid or gas. Small birds pass ~15–20 droppings/day.
  • Hypovitaminosis A is the most common cause of metaplastic changes of the oropharyngeal mucosa. Feather dust (cockatoos, cockatiels, African greys) is a respiratory irritant → keep the environment clean.
  • Psittacine beak and feather disease (PBFD) damages developing feathers → a pinched feather appearance; significant in a solitary bird (not over-preening by a cage-mate). Beak malocclusion may indicate developmental abnormality, trauma, or chronic infection.
Birds lack a diaphragm — two handling traps(1) Chest excursion is essential to avian respiration → never wrap the cloth tightly. (2) Air sacs connect directly to the lungs, so intracoelomic (intraperitoneal) fluids can drown a bird — give fluids orally/SC/IO, not intracoelomically. Also: many birds regurgitate defensively when restrained → aspiration-pneumonia risk if the beak is forced closed.

Key viral & fungal disease notes

  • Aspergillosis — the most common fungal disease of psittacines. Ubiquitous soil fungus; severe respiratory disease via spore inhalation; opportunistic and non-contagious. High incidence reflects suboptimal management (poor nutrition, ventilation, hygiene, stress). Prevention: clean daily and avoid corncob and walnut-shell bedding. Immunocompromised raptors and seabirds are highly susceptible → prophylactic itraconazole.
  • PBFD (circovirus) — extremely stable, resistant to most disinfectants; devastating in cockatoos/lovebirds; abnormal pinched feathers, beak lesions, sudden death; sheds from feathers/skin → isolate.
  • Avian polyomavirus ("budgerigar fledgling disease") — non-enveloped, stable; budgies <15 d of age usually die, >15 d survive but become reservoirs; survivors shed 60–90 d.
  • Pacheco's disease (avian herpesvirus) — enveloped, unstable, sensitive to most disinfectants; survivors become latent carriers that shed under stress.
  • Newcastle disease (paramyxovirus) — velogenic "Exotic ND" strain is a serious poultry risk; reportable and managed by government agencies.

Shelter management, blood feathers & euthanasia notes

  • Sanitation: use an unscented, non-aerosol disinfectant (dilute bleach); avoid household odours, chemicals, and aerosols (avian respiratory tracts are very sensitive).
  • Blood feathers: birds have a poor intrinsic clotting cascade; a damaged large blood feather can cause a bird to bleed to death. Stop bleeding with styptic powder or pressure; do not pull the blood feather (follicle damage → abnormal regrowth).
  • Restraint: control the head near the angle of the mandible; even a momentary release lets a bird strike. Cold metal tables add stress — cover first.
  • Euthanasia (AVMA-accepted): inhalant anaesthetic overdose, CO, barbiturate overdose; IV/IM/intracoelomic pentobarbital acceptable; a small dose into the pectoral muscle is effective within minutes; IV may not act as in mammals and is reserved for anaesthetized birds.
  • Legal: the federal Migratory Bird Treaty Act prohibits possessing any part (feather, nest, egg) of native migratory birds without a USFWS permit. Exceptions: pigeons, European starlings, English (house) sparrows, and game species. Birds were excluded from the original 1966 Animal Welfare Act; no federal law covers companion psittacines → check state law (and the voluntary Model Aviculture Program).

Wildlife Wildlife Care Basics (HSVMA)

For the shelter veterinarian, native wildlife is mostly about three things, and the exam reflects it: the legal limits on what a hospital may do, the rabies-vector-species (RVS) precautions, and the zoonoses — with Baylisascaris (raccoon roundworm) as the standout. The clinic's job is emergency stabilization "before the rehabilitator arrives," then transfer.

Legal framework — what a hospital may do notes

  • A veterinarian may take in wildlife and give emergency care, but it is illegal to provide long-term care without a state wildlife rehabilitator's permit. In most states vets/techs may possess wildlife only up to 48 hours, then must transfer to a permitted rehabilitator.
  • State permit usually covers small mammals + non-migratory birds; a federal USFWS permit is needed for migratory birds and endangered species (Migratory Bird Treaty Act). Separate permits are needed for rabies vector species, deer, and some large mammals/raptors; marine mammals/sea turtles need National Marine Fisheries Service permits.
  • It is illegal in most states to relocate rabies vector species for release — done to limit disease spread; consult local animal control / fish & game.
  • Stabilize, then transfer. Wildlife are easily killed by stress — dark, warm, quiet, private housing; prey species (small mammals, non-raptor birds) "can literally die of fright." Do a visual exam first; wildlife feign good health, so a bird/mammal that looks weak is usually far more compromised than it appears.

Rabies & rabies vector species rabies

  • Rabies vector species (RVS): raccoons, skunks, foxes, bats — plus woodchucks, the one rodent group with higher rabies susceptibility (eastern US). Handle all wild mammals as potential rabies carriers — age has no bearing on whether an animal carries the virus (an infant raccoon or wild kitten can carry it).
  • No veterinary staff member may handle wildlife — especially potential RVS — without rabies pre-exposure prophylaxis. This is a liability mandate. Always handle RVS with gloves "no matter how cute."
  • Transmission is by saliva into a bite wound, cuts/abrasions, or mucous membranes. Rabies is NOT spread by blood, urine, or feces, is not airborne, cannot penetrate intact skin, and the virus is not viable once saliva dries. Only ~2–3 human rabies deaths/yr occur in the US.
  • Bats: <1% of bats carry rabies, but a bat that bit someone or was in a room with a sleeping person should be tested. People mostly get bat rabies by picking up a downed bat — use protection every time.
  • Opossums are remarkably rabies-resistant (low body temperature doesn't support the virus); their hissing/drooling/swaying is a bluff routine, not rabies. Squirrels, chipmunks, other rodents, and rabbits rarely have rabies.
  • Woodchucks & squirrels mimicking rabies: circling/falling/"drunk" behaviour is often the roundworm (Baylisascaris) brain parasite, which looks exactly like rabies and is fatal.
Rabies myth-busting (high-yield)You cannot get rabies from sitting on grass a rabid animal drooled on (dried saliva isn't viable; intact skin blocks it), nor from blood/urine/feces. Saliva entering a bite, fresh scratch, or mucous membrane is the route. Counsel the public toward sensible precautions — vaccinate pets, don't approach/feed wildlife — not panic.

Practical clinic points notes

  • Cat-caught wildlife = antibiotics ASAP. Cat saliva carries highly virulent bacteria (incl. Pasteurella) that cause rapid septicaemia and death in small prey; assume invisible puncture wounds even with no visible injury. Clavamox (amoxicillin-clavulanate) is the most effective for cat bites.
  • Wildlife is not always orphaned — many juveniles are normally left alone for long periods; finding a neonate alone doesn't mean abandonment. Don't give the public advice on raising wildlife; have them bring the animal in or to a rehabilitator.
  • Medication precautions in wildlife: ivermectin is fatal to turtles/tortoises/terrapins; penicillins avoided in rabbits, porcupines, beavers, woodchucks; fenbendazole not for doves or porcupines; fipronil/Frontline not for rabbits; steroids/dexamethasone not for rabbits, hares, birds; clindamycin/cephalexin avoided in lagomorphs/rodents/ruminants.
  • Euthanasia — federal rules for migratory birds require euthanizing any bird that cannot feed itself, perch upright, or ambulate where care won't reverse it; any completely blind bird; and any bird needing amputation of a leg, foot, or a wing at or above the elbow (humero-ulnar joint) — unless special permits are granted.
