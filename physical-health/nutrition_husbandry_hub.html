Quality of Life & Welfare

Quality of life (QOL) is fundamentally about feeling: the balance of an animal's pleasant and unpleasant experiences. This tab covers the affective-balance model from your notes, the behavioral measures used to assess it, species-specific stress signs, and a structured clinical scoring tool.

The affective-balance model of QOL notes

  • QOL definition: the affective and cognitive assessment an animal makes of its life overall, on a continuum of good to bad, derived primarily from the balance of pleasant and unpleasant feelings. QOL can be thought of as enjoyment of life.
  • QOL is a matter of feeling: objective factors, however precisely measured, cannot by themselves represent an individual's QOL.
  • Emotional pain may cause greater suffering than physical pain, and emotional factors weigh more heavily than physical pain in animals' behavioral choices.

Behavioral measures and species stress signs notes

  • Behavioral criteria: normal species-typical behavior (social interaction, grooming, foraging, exploring, play), abnormal behavior (pacing, rocking, bizarre postures, self-mutilation), response to enrichment, avoidance and aversion, and preference tests.
  • Cats: hiding, apathy, low activity, and feigned sleep are stress indicators; hiding is negatively correlated with cortisol and may be an important coping behavior. Cats usually adjust to a shelter within 2 to 5 weeks; some never adjust.
  • Dogs: fear and anxiety appear almost immediately on admission; signs include displacement behaviors, looking back at the handler, elimination, lip licking, body shaking, and yawning. Chronic stress raises cortisol and can blunt the HPA axis and suppress immune function. High cortisol indicates chronic stress and does not always track with behavioral change.
  • Barking is the most noticed indicator of poor welfare in kennels, alongside repetitive pacing and wall rebounding.

Good QOL, poor QOL, and the euthanasia threshold notes

  • Good QOL features include: alert and species-typically busy; a range of context-appropriate behaviors; play and enjoyment; minimal abnormal behaviors; species-typical sleep; confidence and free movement without inappropriate fear; able to rest relaxed without constant vigilance; eats normally with stable body weight; and good general health.
  • Euthanasia decision: weigh all pleasant against all unpleasant feelings. Ask whether any pleasant feelings remain, whether anything fun remains (for species capable of fun), and whether there is a reasonable probability the negative state can be made positive again. If not, the QOL has fallen to a level where euthanasia is the humane choice.
Do not wait for the last good dayWaiting until every positive experience is gone means the only experiences left are unpleasant. The threshold is reached while some unpleasant-dominant balance is established, not at total absence of pleasure.

A structured scoring tool: the HHHHHMM scale textbook

  • The Villalobos HHHHHMM scale (the "H5M2" scale) scores seven variables 0 to 10: Hurt, Hunger, Hydration, Hygiene, Happiness, Mobility, and More good days than bad.
  • A total above 35 (out of 70) is generally considered an acceptable quality of life; a total below 35 suggests QOL has tipped to an unacceptable state.
Two tools, one principleThe HHHHHMM scale is a clinical scoring instrument, separate from the affective-balance model in your notes. Both express the same idea: QOL is the net balance of welfare states. Use the scale as a structured aid, not a replacement for clinical judgment.

Nutrition Principles

Shelter diets must be complete, balanced, and life-stage appropriate, with feeding driven by energy requirements rather than guesswork. The dog-versus-cat distinction (omnivore versus obligate carnivore) underpins everything.

Complete and balanced, and the AAFCO statement notes

  • Feed a complete, nutritionally balanced, life-stage-appropriate diet of as high a quality as possible, with fresh water available at all times. Dietary consistency reduces stress, inappetence, and gastrointestinal irritation.
  • The AAFCO statement of nutritional adequacy on the label indicates how the diet meets requirements and should not be expired. AAFCO sets standards, definitions, and nutritional requirements for pet foods.
  • A diet labeled complete and balanced for all life stages is suitable for puppies, adults, and pregnant or nursing animals.
  • Food recalls remove a product from the market and may be initiated by the manufacturer or by FDA request or order. Do not underestimate the health risk of feeding recalled food.

Exam trapA taurine-deficient diet in cats causes dilated cardiomyopathy and central retinal degeneration. The obligate-carnivore requirements (taurine, arginine, vitamin A, arachidonic acid) are the classic distractor target against dog nutrition.

How much to feed: RER and MER textbooknotes

  • RER (resting energy requirement) is the baseline energy at rest; MER (maintenance energy requirement) applies a life-stage and activity factor to RER. Overweight and underweight animals should have RER and MER calculated; formal MER calculation is accurate but seldom practical at shelter scale.
  • Typical calorie densities: roughly 350 to 500 kcal per cup of dry food and 120 to 190 kcal per 5.5 oz can of wet food.
CorrectionYour dog note allows treats up to "10 to 25%" of daily calories. The standard is the 10% rule: treats should not exceed 10% of daily caloric intake (your cat note correctly states 10%). Use 10% as the ceiling.

Water requirements & food safety ASVtextbook

Daily water intake

  • Cats: approximately 50 mL/kg/day. Cats eating dry food exclusively need roughly twice the water of cats eating wet food, since wet food provides 70–80% water. Cats fed only dry food are at increased risk of urinary tract disease from concentrated urine.
  • Dogs: approximately 50–80 mL/kg/day, varying with size, activity, temperature, and diet type. Fresh water must be available at all times for both species.
  • Dehydration assessment (≥ 5%): skin tent at the scruff persists > 1–2 seconds; tacky or dry mucous membranes; sunken eyes. Severe dehydration (≥ 10%) produces depression, weak pulse, and prolonged capillary refill time.

Water hygiene in the shelter

  • Scrub water bowls daily; biofilm and algae indicate contaminated standing water. Community water troughs or shared bowls are fomite transmission risks — individual water bowls are preferred for biosecurity.
  • Use the same sanitation schedule for water bowls as for food bowls: wash, disinfect, and rinse between occupants and daily for continuous-stay animals.

Raw diet contraindications in the shelter

  • Raw meat diets carry Salmonella and Campylobacter shedding risk — a hazard for immunocompromised animals, young animals, and staff. Multiple Salmonella outbreaks in shelters have been linked to raw feeding.
  • The ASV recommends against raw diets in shelter settings due to public health risk, difficulty ensuring nutritional balance, and regulatory concerns in multi-animal facilities.
  • Raw diets are seldom nutritionally complete, and preparing them correctly is impractical at shelter scale.

Food storage safety

  • Dry food: store in sealed, pest-proof containers; label with the date opened; use within the manufacturer's recommended period after opening.
  • Wet or canned food: refrigerate opened cans; discard uneaten portions after 2 hours at room temperature or per the feral-cat husbandry protocol (4–6 hours maximum).
  • Any food involved in an active recall must be removed immediately and not fed.
Exam trap · dry food and water intakeA cat eating exclusively dry food needs roughly double the water of a cat on wet food. Underestimating this requirement promotes concentrated urine, urinary tract disease, and kidney disease. "Fresh water available at all times" is not sufficient if the cat is not drinking voluntarily — wet food supplementation is a practical solution.

Feeding Management

Beyond what to feed, how and when to feed shapes welfare and disease risk: meal versus free-choice, group-feeding safety, anorexia and its feline consequences, and the danger of refeeding the chronically starved.

Feeding schedules and methods ASVnotes

TopicGuidance
Adult dogsRegular portion feeding at least once, ideally twice daily. Large, deep-chested dogs fed twice or more often to reduce gastric dilatation-volvulus (GDV) risk.
Puppies under 6 months and high-demand dogs (pregnant, nursing, underweight, debilitated)Fed more frequently, or offered dry food free-choice based on age and condition.
Adult catsAt least two meals per day; many prefer small frequent meals. Meal feeding is ideal when intake must be controlled or monitored.
Group-housed catsFree-choice dry food is practical (canned food spoils at room temperature). Note that animals not eating can go unnoticed for days, and some overeat.
Cohoused feedingFeed separately, or monitor closely so all individuals get adequate food and none are stressed or injured by guarding or bullying.
  • Examine food regularly to confirm it has been consumed and is free of debris and not spoiled. Discard uneaten food at least every 24 hours, and discard contaminated or pest-infested food immediately.
  • Shy cats should be fed in privacy; many cats prefer shallow dishes or plates. Paper plates suit canned food (biosecure, cheap, disposable).
  • Proper food storage (including refrigeration of perishables) preserves health and limits rodents and pests.

Anorexia and its feline consequences notes

  • Anorexia arises from illness or stress. Kittens under 4 months who do not eat for over 24 hours need prompt exam (risk of hypoglycemia, weakness, death).
  • Adult cats refusing food for 3 to 5 days need examination, given the risk of hepatic lipidosis.
  • Food aversion develops when ill cats learn to associate a food's sight and smell with nausea; when a cat will not eat, offer food periodically rather than leaving it in the cage constantly.

Chronic starvation and refeeding syndrome textbooknotes

  • Chronic negative energy balance shifts metabolism to using fat and muscle and causes significant electrolyte alterations.
  • Reintroducing food too aggressively risks refeeding syndrome: weakness, seizures, coma, irritability, aggression, and death. Reintroduce nutrition gradually with veterinary input and electrolyte monitoring.
Exam trapThe danger in a severely starved animal is not the starvation alone but rapid refeeding. Electrolyte shifts (notably phosphate, potassium, and magnesium) drive the syndrome. Feed slowly and monitor.

Body Condition Score (BCS) — 9-point scale textbooknotes

The 9-point BCS system (Nestlé Purina scale) is the standard for nutritional assessment in shelter dogs and cats. Assessment is primarily by palpation, not appearance — a thick coat hides ribs. All animals should be scored at intake and at regular intervals during the stay.

BCSCategoryClinical appearance and palpation criteria
1EmaciatedRibs, lumbar vertebrae, and bony prominences visible from a distance. No discernible body fat. Obvious muscle wasting. Severe fat and muscle loss.
2Very thinRibs easily visible in short-haired animals. Lumbar vertebrae visible; pelvic bones prominent. Minimal fat covering; no muscle mass. Obvious waist.
3ThinRibs easily palpable with minimal fat covering; tops of lumbar vertebrae visible. Obvious waist and abdominal tuck. Some muscle definition.
4Underweight-idealRibs palpable with minimal fat covering. Waist easily observed from above. Abdominal tuck evident. Transitional zone approaching ideal.
5IdealRibs palpable without excess fat covering. Waist observed from above. Abdominal tuck evident. Target BCS for most shelter animals. Well-proportioned; normal muscle mass.
6OverweightRibs palpable with slight excess fat. Waist discernible from above but not prominent. Abdominal tuck apparent. Slight fat deposits over lumbar area.
7HeavyRibs palpable with difficulty due to moderate fat covering. Fat deposits noticeable over lumbar area and tail base. Waist absent or barely visible. Abdominal tuck may be absent.
8ObeseRibs not palpable under heavy fat cover. Fat deposits over lumbar area and tail base; abdominal distension may be present. No waist; no abdominal tuck. Obvious abdominal fat pad.
9Severely obeseRibs not palpable under very dense fat. Massive fat deposits over thorax, spine, and base of tail. Neck thickening. Obvious distension of abdomen. Fat deposits present in unusual locations (axillae, groin).
  • Summary grouping: BCS 1–3 = underweight (nutritional support required); BCS 4–5 = ideal; BCS 6–9 = overweight to obese (caloric restriction and monitoring).
  • Shelter surgical eligibility: a BCS of 3 or below requires veterinary clearance before general anesthesia (increased anesthetic risk due to reduced body reserves, hypothermia risk, and altered drug metabolism). Animals at BCS 1–2 should be nutritionally stabilized before elective surgery.
  • Monitoring frequency: weigh and score at intake, then weekly for animals staying more than 7 days. Flag to the medical team if BCS drops by 1 point or more within the stay.
  • Palpation technique: run both thumbs firmly along the rib cage, feeling individual ribs. In cats, also palpate the fat pad over the caudal abdomen (cats deposit fat there early before ribs become prominent). A BCS assigned by visual inspection alone without palpation is unreliable.
Exam trap · BCS requires palpationBCS is commonly misjudged in long- or thick-coated animals where coat obscures body contour. Palpation of the ribs is mandatory — a visual-only score will overestimate condition in heavily coated animals and underestimate it in thin short-haired animals. A BCS 5 animal should have ribs that are palpable but not visible and are covered by a thin layer of fat.

Obesity & body weight intervention textbooknotes

When to intervene

  • BCS 6–7/9: Dietary restriction with calorie counting; increase exercise; recheck weight weekly. Reduce daily caloric intake by 20–25% from current level while maintaining adequate protein.
  • BCS 8–9/9: Structured weight-loss program with veterinary oversight. Calculate target weight, determine target caloric intake (typically RER at target weight or 80% RER at current weight), and recheck every 2 weeks.

Safe weight-loss rate

  • Target 1–2% of body weight per week. Faster loss is unsafe in both species but is especially dangerous in cats due to hepatic lipidosis risk.
  • For a 6 kg obese cat, safe weekly loss is 60–120 g; for a 30 kg obese dog, 300–600 g per week.

Hepatic lipidosis in obese cats — the weight-loss trap

  • Obese cats are paradoxically at greatest risk of hepatic lipidosis when food is suddenly restricted or withheld. When caloric intake drops sharply, peripheral fat is mobilized faster than the liver can process it, causing fat accumulation in hepatocytes.
  • Risk is highest in cats with BCS 7–9 when intake drops by more than 50% acutely. Never fast an obese cat aggressively.
  • Clinical signs: jaundice, vomiting, lethargy, anorexia, hepatomegaly. Diagnosis: elevated liver enzymes (ALT, ALP), bilirubin, ultrasound findings.
  • In shelters: obese cats that stop eating for any reason (stress, illness, transport) must be monitored closely and encouraged to eat within 48–72 hours. If not eating by 72 hours, veterinary exam and syringe/assisted feeding are indicated.
Exam trap · rapid weight loss in obese catsIf a shelter cat is BCS 8/9 and has eaten very little for 5 days, the wrong answer is "continue caloric restriction to promote weight loss." The correct answer is veterinary examination and nutritional support, because this cat is at high risk of hepatic lipidosis. The goal of weight management in obese cats is gradual, controlled reduction — not starvation.

Cat housing essentials: hiding and vertical space ASVnotes

Meeting cats' behavioural needs starts with the housing unit itself. All cats in the shelter — not only ferals — require species-appropriate spatial features that allow them to cope with the novel environment and exercise choice and control.

Hiding

  • Minimum one hide box per cat is required in every housing unit. Cats cannot adequately cope in a bare cage without a hiding option. Hide-box access reduces cortisol, supports stress recovery, and decreases the tendency to hide behind the litter box (which increases soiling and disease risk).
  • Ideal hide box: enclosed on five sides with an opening on one side, large enough for the cat to enter and turn around, with a solid bottom so the cat sits inside rather than on top. The Nightingale-style hide box (solid plastic, five-sided) is widely used in shelters and is easy to disinfect.
  • A cat that persistently hides and does not respond to enrichment is showing a welfare red flag requiring evaluation — not a quirk to work around or resolve by removing the hide box.

Vertical space and perching

  • Cats naturally use elevated positions for observation, rest, and safety. A cage without any elevated surface deprives the cat of a core behavioural need. At minimum, a shelf, elevated platform, or hammock should be provided within the unit.
  • The ASV and Cat-Friendly Shelter guidelines recommend both a hide box and an elevated resting surface in every primary cat housing unit.
  • In ward design, stacking cages at multiple heights does not substitute for in-cage vertical options — a cat on a bottom shelf does not experience vertical space in a welfare-meaningful way.
  • Multi-room housing: where space allows, cat exercise pens or rooms with ceiling-height climbing structures, window perches, and shelf networks represent a substantial welfare upgrade and are associated with lower Cat Stress Scores and faster adoptions.
Exam trap · hide box and hidingHiding is not a welfare problem in cats — it is a welfare solution. Providing a hide box is a pro-welfare intervention that reduces stress. Removing the hide box because "the cat hides" eliminates the animal's primary coping strategy and worsens welfare. Studies show cats with access to a hide box have lower cortisol, lower illness rates, and faster adoption than cats without access to hiding.

Feral and fractious cat husbandry notesASV

Feral and fractious cats are unsocialized or highly fear-reactive cats that resist or cannot tolerate direct handling. Standard husbandry protocols must be adapted to minimize physical handling while meeting all basic needs. Mishandling causes harm to both the cat (escalated stress, defensive injury) and staff (bite/scratch exposure).

Housing requirements

  • Provide a cage or unit with a squeeze panel (divider) that allows the cat to be safely confined to one half while the other half is cleaned and serviced without any direct handling.
  • Include a hide box in every feral cat unit — hiding is a primary coping behavior and is associated with reduced cortisol and improved welfare. The Nightingale-style hide box (fits in the cage, has a bottom, is easy to clean) is preferred for shelter use.
  • Provide access to litter, water, and food daily, via the squeeze panel or through cage-specific feeding ports. Do not reach into the cage for any reason other than an emergency.

Trap-assisted husbandry

  • Cats presented in a humane trap can remain in the trap for brief veterinary procedures when handling is not safe. Food, water, and litter can be provided via a divider panel at the end of the trap.
  • For TNR processing, cats are typically held in the trap overnight with water and a small amount of food, examined in the trap, and transferred to a squeeze cage or directly to surgery with minimal handling. Pre-anesthetic sedation via pole syringe or a squeeze panel is standard.
  • Clean the trap with the cat confined to the back section, using a squeeze plate to divide the trap before opening the door-end.

Feeding and water access

  • Check food consumption daily (note how much was eaten, not just whether the dish is empty — overfilling masks inappetence). Daily observation of food intake is one of the primary welfare monitoring tools for non-handleable cats.
  • Offer wet food in paper/disposable trays to allow cage-side serving without placing hands near the cat. Remove uneaten wet food within 4 hours (4–6 hours at room temperature before spoilage risk).
  • Fresh water is available at all times; check and refill from the safe side of the squeeze panel.

Medical assessment without handling

  • Daily visual observation through the cage front is sufficient for monitoring body condition, respiratory rate, discharge, and gait abnormalities in non-handleable cats.
  • BCS can be estimated visually in thin-coated feral cats, but palpation under sedation (scheduled during TNR processing or trap-based exams) is required for an accurate score.
  • If a feral cat shows signs of respiratory distress, marked lethargy, severe injury, or anorexia for more than 48 hours, sedation for examination is required — these are veterinary emergencies in which the handling risk is justified.
Exam trap · feral cats and length of stayFeral cats should NOT be held in shelters long-term simply because they are unadoptable. Prolonged shelter stay is harmful (chronic stress, disease risk, resource drain). The appropriate disposition is TNRM (trap-neuter-return-monitor) with return to the colony location, or transfer to a colony caretaker/barn cat program. Only cats that are genuinely injured or ill and cannot survive independently are held for extended medical management.

Neonatal & Pediatric Care

Neonates are fragile: temperature, feeding volume, and feeding frequency must be exact, and a cold neonate must never be fed. Fading kitten syndrome is the high-yield emergency.

Housing, temperature, and feeding cadence notestextbook

  • House nursing mothers and litters separately, away from isolation and quarantine, with extra bedding. Vaccinate the dam (DA2PP for dogs) and deworm; check the dam's milk and weigh pups or kittens daily for adequate intake.
  • Neonates are tube- or bottle-fed every 2 to 3 hours for the first few days, every 4 to 5 hours until 3 weeks, then 4 times daily until 6 weeks. Stomach capacity is about 40 to 50 mL/kg per feeding; do not exceed it (over-distension causes reflux, emesis, and fatal aspiration).
  • Puppy weight gain is roughly 1 to 1.25 g/day per pound of expected adult weight in the first month. Orphan neonates: weigh daily for the first week and stimulate urination and defecation.
  • Weaning begins at 3 to 4 weeks with a warm semisolid slurry in a shallow pan; for hand-reared kittens, complete weaning from formula by 5 to 6 weeks.

Fading kitten syndrome textbooknotes

  • Acute rapid decline and death of young kittens in the first weeks; often one or two fade first, then others over days. Necropsy is the best tool to determine a definitive diagnosis, though the cause may remain undetermined.
  • Supportive treatment: rewarm first, then tube feeding, subcutaneous fluids, active warming, treat the littermates aggressively, rule out feline panleukopenia, and broad-spectrum antibiotics by injection as directed.
  • Never feed a hypothermic neonate: a cold gut cannot move milk, which ferments and causes endotoxemia. Restore normothermia, then feed.

Reproduction & Life-Stage

Feline reproduction, pregnancy diagnosis, kitten age estimation, and life-stage care (geriatric, grooming) round out husbandry. Age estimation by dentition is heavily tested.

Feline reproduction and pregnancy diagnosis notes

  • Cats are seasonal breeders, becoming active as day length increases; queens cycle roughly every 2 weeks during the season until bred, spayed, or season's end. Receptivity lasts about 4 to 7 days.
  • Gestation is about 65 days, with 3 to 5 kittens; most kittens are born mid-spring and late summer. Queens reach puberty at 5 to 9 months (range 3.5 to 18); toms at about 8 to 13 months.
  • Pregnancy diagnosis: abdominal palpation from day 14 to 17 (small uterine swellings); ultrasound as early as day 10; serum relaxin elevated by day 25; radiographs limited until fetal skulls calcify around day 43. Obvious abdominal distension means birth is usually under 2 weeks away.

Kitten age estimation notes

  • Pound-a-month rule: kittens gain roughly 1 lb per month, so a 3 lb kitten is about 3 months old. Eyes open at 7 to 10 days; walking and play around 3 to 4 weeks.
  • Dentition: deciduous teeth erupt around 3 to 4 weeks; deciduous incisors erupt in order I1, I2, I3. Adult canines erupt around 5.5 to 6 months. In males, permanent canines with small testes suggest about 6 to 9 months; mature testes suggest 12 to 14 months.
  • Orphan kittens: a foster queen with similarly sized kittens reduces competition; neonates are born about 100 g and should gain 10 to 15 g/day. A daily gain under 7 g is inadequate and signals a need for supplemental feeding or a health check. Screen the queen for FeLV and FIV. Kittens under 6 to 8 weeks should not be housed in the shelter due to infection risk.

Geriatric care and grooming notesASV

  • Geriatric dogs commonly have orthopedic disease (provide soft bedding and extra warmth), may need soft food, and can have cognitive decline, cataracts, or hearing loss. Weigh regularly and provide checkups with appropriate lab work.
  • Grooming: basic grooming (regular brushing, bathing as needed) is part of care for all shelter animals and helps minimize fecal-oral transmission (for example giardia and parvovirus). ASV frames grooming and bathing as essential when needed for health or comfort.

Shelter Toxicology

Cats carry a metabolic vulnerability that makes several common shelter and household products dangerous. Knowing the mechanism explains the pattern of feline toxicoses.

The feline metabolic basis correctedtextbook

  • Cats have a genetic deficiency of hepatic glucuronosyltransferase (UGT1A6 is a pseudogene, and they lack other UGT isoforms), which impairs glucuronide conjugation and slows clearance of many phenolic compounds and drugs.
  • This deficiency underlies feline sensitivity to phenols, aspirin (salicylates), and acetaminophen, and contributes to permethrin sensitivity.
CorrectionThe draft answer described a "complete absence" of glucuronosyltransferase. That overstates it: cats have a deficiency / reduced activity (UGT1A6 is a pseudogene) and still express some UGT isoforms. The deficiency is real and is an accepted contributor to permethrin sensitivity, but it is a deficiency, not a total absence.
Acetaminophen is multifactorialFeline acetaminophen toxicity (methemoglobinemia, Heinz-body anemia) is driven by both deficient glucuronidation (UGT1A6) and a lack of N-acetyltransferase 2 (NAT2), with the p-aminophenol pathway producing the methemoglobinemia. It is not glucuronidation deficiency alone.
