Overview & Program Setup

Where spay-neuter sits in the exam, the ASV definition and four pillars, and a full walk-through of how to plan, fund, staff, and run an HQHVSN program. ASV content verified against Griffin et al. 2016 (JAVMA 249:165).

Blueprint placement

  • Companion Animal Homelessness = 12% of the exam; Spay-Neuter = 16% of that domain.
  • Sibling subdomains: Shelter Diversion, Adoption/Placement, Epidemiology of Homelessness, Transfer, Disaster, Reunification, Non-Surgical Sterilization.

HQHVSN definition & four pillars ASV

Goal: efficient surgical initiatives that meet or exceed veterinary medical standards of care, providing accessible, targeted sterilization of large numbers of cats and dogs to reduce overpopulation and euthanasia.

  • High Quality: safe protocols; individual-animal care never sacrificed for volume.
  • High Volume: efficient flow; next patient prepped on a second table as the current surgery is closed (no surgeon downtime). Needs adequate staffing and layout.
  • Accessible: easy to reach/use; barriers = cost, geography, transport, language, cultural norms, sense of safety.
  • Targeted: animals unlikely to otherwise be neutered (low-income owned pets, community animals).
Exam trapTargeting by income = below-median-income areas + pricing, NOT income screening. Intact status is the leading relinquishment risk, so targeting prevents litters AND surrender. Non-surgical sterilization is explicitly out of scope of the 2016 guideline.

Setting up a program, step by step ASV

1. Mission, goals, needs assessment

  • Ask: what program do you want, what change do you hope to effect and why, what is the level of need, what resources do you have.
  • Articulate goals first, then plan services that reasonably contribute to them. Understand the resources (financial and human) available to meet your target community's need.

2. Funding (the hardest resource) ASV

  • Sources: grants, donations, public funding, fees for service.
  • Grants generally need nonprofit or government status and an application; often purpose-specific (startup, or subsidies for community cats or pit-bull-type dogs).
  • Public funds: animal-control budgets, pet-friendly license plates, dog licensing.
  • Fees for service: to stay accessible, fees must sit below local general-practice prices. The shortfall is covered by efficiency, volunteer labour, or other funding.
  • For-profit programs may partner with a nonprofit that can accept grants/donations and supply volunteers.

3. Human resources & model selection

  • Employees, independent contractors, volunteers; be realistic about volunteer reliance. Large monthly MASH community-cat clinics often run all-volunteer.

4. Protocols, SOPs & checklists

  • Clear protocols and documentation reduce communication lapses, errors and unnecessary variability, and cut cognitive load during emergencies.
  • Design the protocol, then implement; a good medical-record form doubles as a checklist. Deviations invite error and should be avoided, while staying flexible for individual needs.

5. Physical layout & flow

  • Minimize staff travel between tasks; keep needed items nearby; keep sedated animals visible and monitored; target improvement at the riskiest processes/locations.

6. Staff roles, culture & leadership

  • Collaborative (not strictly hierarchical) cultures; greater teamwork correlates with fewer complications. Defined duties plus team interaction; each role understood and performed with minimal wasted time.
  • Leaders who value and empower staff raise performance, satisfaction and well-being, and lower turnover. Leadership can be taught.

7. Data tracking & improvement

  • Safety: capture peri-anaesthetic and post-op morbidity/mortality; track when/where/why an error occurred; software can sort complications by surgeon, species, or surgery type and correlate outcome changes with protocol changes.
  • Effectiveness: local shelter intake and euthanasia rates, nuisance complaints, colony size, kitten production, disease incidence.

8. Personnel health, ergonomics & mental health

  • Reduce distractions (loud music/conversation, foot traffic, barking, slamming cages, phones). Manage chemical/biological safety, waste gas, sharps, noise, zoonoses, physical injury, compassion fatigue.
  • Safe handling (lift tables, stretchers, blankets) prevents bites, lifting strains, slips/falls.
  • Ergonomics: sit or alternate sit/stand; firm elastic mats, cushioned shoes; micropauses 15–30 s several times per hour with stretching reduce fatigue and raise accuracy.
  • Mental health: shelter/HQHVSN veterinarians carry elevated risk of depression, burnout, compassion fatigue, suicidal ideation. Provide resources, support groups, crisis lines, and scheduling flexibility; train staff to recognize early signs.

Clinic models in depth HQHVSN literature

Stationary clinic

  • Dedicated SN facility. To be sustainable it must fill its schedule: a benchmark of about 35 surgeries per vet per day, 5 days/week, 48 weeks/year.
  • Typically needs a catchment of at least 250,000 people within a 60-mile radius, and an opening reserve of about $35,000.
  • Optional transport program (vehicle, driver, fuel, insurance, registration, maintenance) extends reach to clients with limited transport; the driver is the first line of patient monitoring and may decline an animal that looks unwell.
  • Regulatory: city/county zoning, the veterinary practice act, ADA alignment, ventilation/air exchange, waste and drain rules, and the oxygen delivery choice (central, portable E-tanks cheapest up front but costly over time, or concentrator).

Mobile clinic (self-contained vehicle)

  • Travels to communities, surgery on board, same-day discharge; often at least 25 dogs/cats per 10-hour day.
  • Needs fresh/grey water tanks, generator, HVAC, fridge/freezer, latching cabinets, at least two anaesthesia machines, ideally two tables; parked in a locked garage when idle.
  • Regulatory: state/local vet law, inspection/registration/insurance, possibly a commercial licence; consult the Veterinary Mobility Act and local DEA. Carbon monoxide detector if generator-powered.
  • Anaesthetic tactics: premedicate groups in surgical order to reduce barking, do longer-recovery animals early, use reversible drugs, minimise masking to limit waste gas.

MASH clinic

  • Staff transport equipment to a temporary venue (shelter, church basement, fire station, gym). Fastest, lowest-cost way to start HQHVSN; good for rural low-density and dense urban areas alike.
  • Lower investment than stationary/mobile (vehicle, surgical/anaesthetic equipment, autoclave, initial consumables, roughly $20,000 to $35,000).
  • Surgical capacity: about 5 hours of surgery is a full MASH day, from as few as 15 to 20 dogs up to 50 to 60 cats for one vet.
  • Collaborative MASH: host group supplies venue, 2 to 5 staff/volunteers, scheduling, admission/discharge and non-medical supplies; the MASH org supplies vets, techs, surgical supplies, and trains hosts. Independent MASH does it all itself.

Hybrid, community-cat, in-clinic and voucher models

  • Hybrid: a building plus a mobile/MASH component (e.g. a cargo trailer).
  • Community/feral cat clinic: tractability, health and rabies status unknown, so all are treated as feral and unvaccinated; IM-only induction, never handle without sedation, capture nets and squeeze cages, recover in the trap. A large study put community-cat euthanasia at about 0.4%. A cited feral GA recipe is Telazol reconstituted with 4 mL ketamine + 1 mL xylazine, dosed by volume (about 0.125 mL small kitten, 0.25 mL average, 0.3 mL tom); reported death rate about 0.4%. notes/source-specific
  • In-clinic clinic / voucher: a private practice partners with a welfare group; the welfare partner handles advertising, scheduling, income screening and reminders, the hospital does pre-op instructions, exams, surgery and aftercare. In-clinic blocks use otherwise idle time; best for communities under ~25,000 that cannot support a full-time program. Pitfalls: communication breakdown, judging low-income clients, poor pricing transparency or upselling.

Staffing

  • Ratio about 1 vet to 2 to 3 trained support staff; shifts of 4x10 h or 3x12 h work better than 5x8 h.

Patient selection ASV

  • Depends on staffing, anaesthetic capability, locale, training, economics.
  • A veterinarian makes the final acceptance decision based on history, PE, and the schedule; the surgeon sets minimum/maximum age and weight.
  • PE may be before or after premedication at the vet's discretion; verify sex/reproductive status, check for tattoo, scan for microchip.

Special-case decisions ASV

  • Estrus, pregnant, lactating, pyometra, mild URI/parasites, subclinical heartworm: generally proceed. The welfare cost of prolonged shelter stay and disease exposure outweighs the surgical management of an engorged or compromised tract. Grant an exemption only when surgery poses significant patient risk.
  • Pregnant en bloc: fetal euthanasia not required when the gravid uterus is removed intact (fetuses stay unconscious). If the sac is opened, a near-term fetus may rouse, so humane euthanasia is then warranted. Terminating pregnancy at any stage is considered humane in shelters.
  • Cryptorchid: full PE, inspect penis, palpate the entire inguinal region. Remove both testes; locate the retained testis first. If it cannot be found, do not remove the descended one (refer). Diagnostics: ultrasound, hCG/GnRH stimulation (testosterone >3 ng/mL post-stim = testicular tissue), LH and AMH testing. repro ref
  • Mind state/local ordinances on timing relative to legal holding periods.

Anaesthesia, Analgesia & Fluids

Balanced anaesthesia for high throughput, with specific agents and doses. ASV principles are badged ASV; all mg/kg figures are representative standard-reference ranges (badged Std ref).

Dose disclaimer. The ASV guideline gives no doses. The mg/kg ranges below are representative values compiled from standard references (Sara White, HQHVSN and Other Shelter Surgeries; ACVAA pain-management guidelines; Plumb's). Ranges and combination "recipes" vary by source, edition, formulation, and region. Cross-check against your own formulary before clinical or exam use.

ASV anaesthesia principles ASV

  • Four protocol criteria: analgesia/anxiolysis; immobility/muscle relaxation; safe, reversible CNS depression to unconsciousness; affordable balanced injectable + inhalant anaesthesia.
  • Use a drug chart to prevent calculation errors; at weight extremes dose by body surface area or metabolic scaling. Favour reversible agents; estimate weight accurately.
  • Multimodal analgesia; avoid NSAIDs in clinically or subclinically dehydrated patients (nephrotoxicosis).
  • Total IM anaesthesia: a single injection of alpha-2 + opioid + dissociative reduces pain and stress.
  • Anticholinergics available in all clinics but not used for alpha-2-induced bradycardia (use reversal/partial reversal).

Fluid therapy

  • Who needs IV fluids (ASV): high-risk patients – pregnant, pyometra, substantial blood loss, prolonged surgery; warmed fluids for pediatric, small, frail, ill. ASV
  • Routine healthy elective patients often do not need IV fluids; SC fluids may be given for comfort. Std ref
  • Anaesthetic IV rate (AAHA 2013): start ~5 mL/kg/h in dogs, ~3 mL/kg/h in cats, titrated down; the older standard was 10 mL/kg/h. Std ref
  • Pediatric/neonatal hypoglycemia: transmucosal sugar (ASV) or 2.5% dextrose support during recovery; recover with littermates, do not pile. ASV Std ref

Monitoring ASV Std ref

  • Hands-on observation is most reliable; monitor several variables (no single one suffices).
  • Pulse oximetry highly encouraged; shown to reduce anaesthetic death in cats (CEPSAF). SpO2 should be 98–100% on oxygen; under 95% = hypoxemia. Std ref
  • Pulse quality/rate/rhythm; respiratory rate/pattern; capnograph ETCO2 35–45 mmHg (flatline = disconnect/arrest; rising = hypoventilation; abrupt fall = falling cardiac output). Std ref
  • MAP minimum ~60 mmHg to autoregulate renal/cerebral perfusion. Std ref
  • Jaw tone unreliable in pediatric puppies (no mandibular tone); ventral eye rotation = surgical plane; palpebral may be absent with dissociatives; MM/CRT subjective.

  • Canine risk: low body weight (<5 kg), mask induction, age >12 y. Feline risk: subclinical HCM (prevalence up to ~15%), intubation trauma, age >12 y, extreme weights (<2 kg or >6 kg). The pack cites a ~15x mortality rise in cats under 2 kg.

Nociception & multimodal targets

  • Pathway: transduction, transmission, modulation, perception. Primary hyperalgesia is local; secondary hyperalgesia spreads to uninjured tissue via central sensitisation ("wind-up"), driven by NMDA receptor activation by glutamate.
  • Multimodal sites: opioids (brain/cord/periphery, mu/kappa/delta), NSAIDs (COX inhibition, periphery/cord), local anaesthetics (Na+ channel blockade, axons), ketamine (NMDA antagonist, dorsal horn).

MAC modifiers

  • MAC decreased by: hypothermia, pregnancy, severe hypotension, hypoxia (PaO2 <38 mmHg), hypercapnia (PaCO2 >95 mmHg), anaemia, acute blood loss, old age, premedications (alpha-2 agonists up to ~80%).
  • MAC increased by: hyperthermia, hypernatraemia, CNS stimulants.

Capnograph patterns

  • Elevated baseline (does not return to 0): rebreathing CO2 (exhausted soda lime or stuck expiratory valve).
  • Cardiogenic oscillations: small ripples at end-expiration, normal in relaxed deep-chested dogs.
  • Curare cleft: a dip in the plateau, the patient is breathing against the circuit (light plane or waning relaxant).

Thermoregulation (AIH) & fluid distribution

  • Three phases: (1) rapid core-to-periphery redistribution from vasodilation (conduction/convection ~80% of early loss), (2) slower linear loss (evaporation, radiation), (3) plateau after ~3 h when production matches loss.
  • Hypothermia: left-shifts the oxyhaemoglobin curve, slows drug clearance, causes platelet dysfunction and more blood loss, raises infection risk; shivering raises oxygen demand 200 to 300%.
  • Isotonic crystalloids: only ~25% stays intravascular at 30 to 60 min (75% shifts interstitial), so over-hydration drives pulmonary oedema.

RECOVER CPR

  • Compressions 100 to 120/min, 1/3 to 1/2 chest width, full recoil. Lateral cardiac-pump for <10 kg, thoracic-pump (widest point, lateral) for >10 kg.
  • Ventilate 10 breaths/min (1 every 6 s), inspiratory time 1 to 1.5 s; hyperventilation raises intrathoracic pressure and crashes preload.
  • Epinephrine low-dose 0.01 mg/kg IV/IO; if intratracheal, increase 10x to 0.1 mg/kg diluted. Atropine 0.04 mg/kg for high-vagal arrest/PEA/asystole. Avoid fluid boluses in CPR unless hypovolaemia is the cause (they raise right atrial pressure and lower coronary perfusion pressure).

Special populations

  • Acepromazine: reduces inhalant ~40%, antiemetic; alpha-1 vasodilation/hypotension; avoid if hypovolaemic; caution in Boxers and MDR1 herding breeds.
  • Alpha-2 agonists (selectivity dexmedetomidine > medetomidine > xylazine): biphasic BP; avoid with structural cardiac disease (feline HCM, canine MMVD) or raised intracranial/intraocular pressure.
  • Pediatric (6 to 12 wk): cardiac output is rate-dependent (fixed stroke volume), so bradycardia causes immediate hypotension; high metabolic/oxygen demand needs RR 2 to 3x adult; tiny airways (resistance proportional to 1/r^4); hypoglycaemia risk. Fasting: <4 wk none, 4 to 6 wk 1 to 2 h, 6 to 12 wk 2 to 4 h.

Surgical Technique & Recovery

Asepsis, suture selection, technique, identification of altered animals, and the recovery-to-discharge pathway.

Asepsis & surgeon prep ASV

  • Operating area: anaesthesia, surgery and immediate recovery performed safely; limit traffic to essential personnel; scheduled sanitation.
  • Surgeon: attire, cap, mask, hand/arm scrub; gown at discretion. Sterile gloves for all abdominal surgery and adult dog castration; exam gloves acceptable for routine cat and puppy castration.
  • Patient prep: empty bladder if possible; gentle skin prep (avoid stiff bristles/scraping – micro-abrasions promote infection); elevate the chest of pregnant animals; avoid limb hyperextension; drape all abdominal surgery.

Recovery, complications & discharge ASV

  • Recovery: continuous direct observation; secure level surface; clean/dry/warm; minimize noise (reduces emergence delirium); head/neck in slight extension aligned with thorax.
  • Complications (haemorrhage, CV/respiratory depression, pain, hypo/hyperthermia, distress, vomiting, regurgitation, aspiration) mostly occur in the first 3 hours; monitor HR, pulse, RR, airway, MM colour, pain/anxiety, temperature, arousal, ambulation.
  • Reversal for prolonged recovery or emergencies; partial alpha-2 reversal shortens recovery without losing analgesia; avoid rapid IV reversal except emergencies.
  • Return to housing: express the bladder under anaesthesia (comfort); cover and elevate traps; dogs walked if overnight.
  • Discharge: sternal, alert, responsive; normal mentation/RR, adequate analgesia, clean dry well-apposed incision; trapped cats returned to capture site once no longer anaesthetized.
  • Discharge instructions: procedure summary, normal vs abnormal recovery, pain signs, incision care, food/water timing, exercise restriction, medications, when/how to report complications, urgent re-evaluation signs, emergency care, ongoing-care recommendation.

Surgical approaches & special procedures HQHVSN literature

Flank spay

  • Lateral recumbency, ideally left lateral to keep clear of the spleen. In cats, incision about 2/3 to 3/4 of the way from the last rib toward the wing of the ilium, ~2 cm ventral to the transverse processes, 1.5 to 2 cm long.
  • Through external oblique, internal oblique, then peritoneum; if the uterus is elusive, sweep the hook dorsally to the transverse processes. Dogs need a 3-layer closure.

Pyometra

  • Dehydration, azotaemia, acid-base derangement; larger incision; avoid the spay hook (friable uterus); meticulous broad-ligament haemostasis; protect the abdomen with towels; flush the stump, do not oversew; change gloves/instruments before closure.

Caesarean and en bloc + surgical detail

  • Avoid xylazine (the one agent linked to neonatal and maternal mortality); avoid alpha-2 agonists generally; preoxygenate 3 min; rapid IV induction (propofol or alfaxalone), cuffed ET tube (aspiration risk); glycopyrrolate does not cross the placenta whereas atropine does; ephedrine 0.04 to 0.1 mg/kg IV is the preferred pressor (preserves uterine flow); single NSAID dose only if normovolaemic/normotensive.
  • En bloc (OHE then hand the uterus to a resuscitation team) gives the shortest dam GA time, least contamination, and the highest neonatal survival. Cut suspensory and broad ligaments first, leave blood supply intact, then 45 to 60 s maximum from clamping to delivery; one resuscitator per neonate.
  • Oxytocin if poor involution/haemorrhage: dogs 5 to 20 IU IM (or 0.5 to 1 IU into the wall, or 1 to 5 IU IV), cats 2 to 4 IU IM, while anaesthetised.

Neonatal resuscitation

  • Dry and warm (ambient 27 to 32°C); rub for tactile stimulation; do not swing/shake (intracranial haemorrhage). Clear airways; mask O2; positive-pressure ventilation if no breathing in 30 s; intubate with a 14 to 18 G catheter if needed; compressions if still bradycardic.
  • Epinephrine 0.01 to 0.03 mg/kg (umbilical vein, ET, or IO); atropine/glyco NOT recommended (bradycardia is hypoxic); naloxone 0.1 mg/kg only if the dam had opioids; doxapram not recommended in the hypoxic newborn; dextrose for hypoglycaemia (normal ~2.2 to 3.3 mmol/L).

Ovary-sparing spay & uterine prolapse

  • ASV recognises both OHE and OVE. Ovary-sparing spay (hysterectomy keeping ovaries) leaves hormones intact, prevents pregnancy but not pyometra unless all uterine tissue to the cervix is removed; estrus behaviour persists.
  • Uterine prolapse: more common in cats, during/after delivery; check for abdominal contents in the prolapse; reduce and OHE if viable, or ovariectomy plus external amputation if not.

Complications & the surgical checklist HQHVSN literature

Haemorrhage

  • Frank haemorrhage is usually technical: torn ovarian pedicle (more often the right, more cranial/harder to expose) or ligature failure. Prevention: adequate pedicle length, ligature placed proximally (~5 mm distal stump), correct suture gauge, sound technique, gentle minimal post-ligation inspection.
  • Oozing: in-heat/recent-heat (endogenous oestrogen), anticoagulant rodenticide, von Willebrand (Dobermann). Management: large incision, scrubbed assistant, Balfour retractors, suction; consider autotransfusion (medium/large dog with >100 cc free blood; deliver through a filter, ~5 mL/s).

Post-op complications

  • Early haemoabdomen (within ~12 h): slow recovery, prolonged recumbency, refractory hypothermia, tachycardia without pain, pale MM, low BP. Abdominocentesis caudal/right of midline; a negative tap does not rule it out.
  • Dehiscence (median ~4 days, often evisceration at night): self-trauma from uncomfortable/over-tight skin closure or faulty knots; always send home with an E-collar.
  • Secondary haemorrhage 4 to 16 days post-spay (uterine-vessel erosion under the ligature, often infection): any serious vaginal bleed warrants immediate exploration and re-ligation (Miller's knot).
  • Sinus tracts/stump granulomas: non-absorbable or excessive ligature, poor asepsis, excess stump; definitive treatment is surgical.
  • Orchiectomy: scrotal swelling in >20% at 24 to 48 h (open castration higher risk); abdominal haemorrhage if a cord retracts; iatrogenic urethral/prostatic trauma when removing an abdominal cryptorchid testis.

Ovarian remnant syndrome

  • ~0.3% feline, ~0.04% canine; surgeon error or ectopic/accessory ovarian tissue. Signs of estrus return; diagnose by vaginal cytology (cornified cells), hormone assays (progesterone >5 ng/mL after GnRH/hCG-induced luteinisation; LH rises and AMH falls after ovariectomy), ultrasound near the kidneys. Surgery best 2 to 3 weeks after luteinisation or during behavioural estrus; submit tissue for histopathology.

Surgical checklist (prevention)

  • READ-DO or DO-CONFIRM format, brief (1 to 2 min), relevant and consistent. Pre-op: client/patient name, signed consent, sex verified, fasting, other procedures, ASA, special risks. Between-patient & clinic biosecurity ASV

  • Clean and disinfect all direct-contact equipment between patients: tables, ET tubes, masks, laryngoscope blades, pulse-ox clips, esophageal stethoscopes, thermometers.
  • Maintain anaesthetic equipment on a schedule matched to surgical volume; hand hygiene or glove change between patients and litters.
  • Instruments: clean before sterilization; sterility indicators inside and outside the pack; outer wrap barrier at least equivalent to dry 270-thread-count pima cotton; double non-woven wrap gives a tortuous-path barrier.
  • Suspected-contagious but still-eligible animals are operated on LAST, after all apparently healthy animals.
  • Steam sterilization: wrapped goods 121°C (250°F), ~15 min, 15 psi; unwrapped/flash 132°C, 3–4 min. Biological indicator = Geobacillus stearothermophilus (test weekly; confirm against ASV Standards of Care). Heat-sensitive items: hydrogen peroxide gas plasma. Std ref

Instruments & suture HQHVSN literature

  • Core instruments: #10/#15 blade, Metzenbaum (tissue) and Mayo (heavy tissue/suture) scissors, Adson-Brown forceps (most common in HQHVSN), Snook or Covault spay hook, Olsen-Hegar (with scissors) or Mayo-Hegar needle holder, haemostats (mosquito for pedicle ties, Kelly/Crile small-medium vessels, Rochester-Carmalt for large canine ovarian pedicles).
  • Suture: synthetic monofilament absorbable is the HQHVSN default. Monocryl loses ~75% strength by day 14, gone by ~121 days. PDS II keeps ~75% at 14 days, ~50% at 28 days, absorbed ~180 days (but has memory). Vicryl (braided) ~75% at 14 d, ~25% at 28 d, absorbed 56 to 70 days. Stainless steel: highest strength, no reaction, suitable for high-volume pedicles/body wall (not skin). Reverse-cutting needles preferred. Sizes 4-0 to 1 (0 or 1 for large-dog linea alba).
  • Packs: adult canine spay (Olsen-Hegar, Adson-Brown, scissors, spay hook, haemostats including Carmalts); feline/pediatric spay (mosquito/Kelly/Crile instead of Carmalts); adult canine neuter (two Kelly haemostats, Mayo scissors, needle holder, thumb forceps).

Instrument processing & sterilisation HQHVSN literature

  • Clean before sterilising: wipe organic matter, flush lumens, immerse in warm (80 to 110°F) detergent (not >20 min), scrub with an instrument brush (no abrasives), rinse with tap then distilled water, dry open on a lint-free towel.
  • Packing: woven cotton muslin (minimum 140 thread count), non-woven SMS, or paper-plastic peel pouches; launder reusable textiles every use; double-wrap to extend shelf life. Indicators inside and outside; biological indicators are the most accurate, run weekly.
  • Methods: gravity-displacement steam is the HQHVSN standard (instruments ~250 to 275°F, 10 to 30 min, 15 to 30 psi); also ethylene oxide, gas plasma, and liquid chemical (field setting) needing 6 to 12 h immersion, sterile-water rinse, single-use solution. A pressure cooker can sterilise if used like an autoclave.
  • Storage: dry and cool before removal (15 to 45 min), closed cabinets, not stacked, <75°F and <70% humidity; shelf life 4 weeks (double-wrapped woven) up to 1 year (peel pouches). Secure paper-plastic pouches with masking tape, not standard tape (melts ~150°F and breaks the seal).

Surgeon & patient prep HQHVSN literature

  • Hand prep: alcohol-based rubs are most effective, then chlorhexidine, then iodophors; brushless preferred (brushes damage skin). Initial scrub at least 5 min contact, subsequent scrubs at least 2 min; re-scrub after asepsis breaks or procedures >60 min.
  • Patient prep: clip just before induction (clipping >4 h before surgery roughly triples SSI risk); #10 blade then #40 along hair growth; avoid over-clipping; scrub in one direction with adequate contact time; air-dry before draping.

Infection control & cohorting ASV HQHVSN literature

  • Transmission routes: direct contact, fomites, aerosols, oral, vector-borne. Control by sanitation, hand hygiene, traffic flow, isolating sick animals, and separating by source, age and vaccination status.
  • Cohort by source/age/health/vaccination status; pediatric patients are housed separately (immature immunity, MDA, likely unvaccinated).
  • Operate healthy animals first, suspected-contagious last; mucous-membrane-contact items (laryngoscope, pulse-ox) need a disinfectant effective against non-enveloped viruses between patients.
  • Clean the most vulnerable patients first (pediatric, then unknown-vaccination adults); sanitise then disinfect; vaccinate ~1 week before admission where possible.

Vaccination & Immunology

Shelter immunology essentials kept alongside the surgical material.

  • Vaccinate core at intake; MLV core (CDV/CPV/FPV) gives rapid protection and can overcome low MDA.
  • Naive adult: FVRCP at intake + one booster 2–4 weeks later.
  • Avoid MLV panleukopenia in pregnant queens (fetal cerebellar hypoplasia).
  • Feline sites (distal limb, avoid interscapular to prevent unresectable FISS): FVRCP right fore, rabies right hind, FeLV left hind. AAFP 2020 emphasizes "as distal as possible," including the tail.
  • 1-2-3 FISS rule: biopsy a post-vaccinal mass if it persists 3 months, grows over 2 cm, or enlarges 1 month post-injection.
  • Rabies bite (no vaccine history): 10-day observation regardless of status; do not vaccinate during the window.
  • Anaphylaxis: epinephrine first-line.

Population Impact

Why spay-neuter is a homelessness intervention: access to care, targeting, community cats, and the metrics that show it works.

Access to care & community cats ASV

  • Accessibility barriers: cost, transport, scheduling, geography, language, sense of safety. HQHVSN fees must sit below local general-practice prices.
  • HQHVSN clinics are a gateway to veterinary care and can coexist with private practice.
  • Community cat programs (TNVR / return-to-field) at minimum provide affordable HQ spay-neuter, FVRCP, rabies where endemic, ID (ear tip + tattoo), and humane euthanasia for irredeemable suffering; strategic resource use (e.g. not routinely FeLV/FIV testing).

Effectiveness metrics & funding ASV

  • Local shelter intake and euthanasia rates, nuisance complaints, colony size, kitten production, disease incidence.
  • Funding: grants (nonprofit/government), donations, public funds (animal-control budgets, plates, licensing), fees for service.

Community cats: epidemiology & population models community-cat literature

Epidemiology

  • Pregnancy is highly seasonal (rising day length): rates can exceed 50% of females presented in spring (peaks March/April), lowest October to December; ~0.98 to 2 pregnancies/year, 4 to 5 kittens each. Feline pyometra ~0.4% at spay; community-cat cryptorchidism ~0.8 to 1.8% (bilateral more often abdominal).
  • Infectious load: FeLV/FIV ~4% (similar to outdoor pet cats); FIV ~4x higher in males (bite transmission); retroviral false positives reach ~50% in low-prevalence healthy populations, so do not test-and-cull. High parasite burdens (fleas, ear mites, ascarids, Toxoplasma seropositivity), and Bartonella henselae common.
  • Rabies: rabid cats outnumber rabid dogs in the US; treat all community cats as unvaccinated.

Population dynamics & the four vital rates

  • Birth, death, immigration, emigration. Closed populations (no in/out) are easy to model but rare; open populations need immigration control. Models range from Ricker logistic to demographic, simulation, deterministic vs stochastic; sensitivity/elasticity analysis identifies the most influential rate.
  • Free-roaming cat dynamics are more sensitive to survival than fecundity (adult survival most of all), which is why removal often outperforms sterilisation of equal intensity, and why a single untreated litter can undo intensive effort.

Stress & Low-Stress Handling

Fear and stress are welfare, safety and anaesthetic-risk issues. Reducing them improves handling and lowers peri-induction arrhythmia risk.

Triggers, signs & impact

  • Triggers: transport, novel environment, slippery floors, separation, strange smells/noises, unfamiliar animals/people, unpredictable events, handling/restraint/injection.
  • Acute fear activates the sympathetic nervous system (raises HR/RR/BP); fear-induced tachycardia raises peri-induction arrhythmia risk. Signs span the 4 Fs (fight, flight, fret/fidget, freeze): tense body/face, wide eyes, ears back, hissing or silence, refusing treats, dilated pupils, excessive shedding. Fear can imprint and is contagious between animals.

Senses & environment

  • Hearing: reduce barking, separate dogs and cats, minimise sudden noise. Smell: avoid alcohol odours; lavender can soothe dogs. Sight: minimise looming/reaching, cover carriers, shut doors. Touch: slow steady contact, avoid feet and ventral abdomen.

Practical tactics

  • Train staff in low-stress handling; allow acclimatisation; calm reception (scheduling to cut waits); consistent housing (same spot before and after); keep littermates together; warmth, secure footing, treats; pheromones/aromatherapy; towel wraps; never drag with a slip lead; pole syringe/blow pipe only for truly fractious large dogs.
  • Community cats: keep in the trap, cover, move slowly, hands-off, quiet.

Repro Pathology & Disorders of Sexual Development

Intersex and developmental anomalies encountered at spay-neuter. In all DSD cases, gonadectomy is recommended.

Embryology (three steps)

  • Chromosomal sex at fertilisation, then gonadal differentiation, then phenotypic development. Sertoli cells secrete anti-Mullerian hormone (regresses the female ducts); Leydig cells secrete testosterone and INSL3. Testicular descent has three stages (abdominal translocation, transinguinal migration, inguinoscrotal) and needs active testes plus the gubernaculum.

DSD classification

  • Sex-chromosome DSD: XXY (Klinefelter, the most common in dogs/cats), XO; underdeveloped genitalia, abnormal cycling/infertility.
  • Pseudohermaphrodite: gonads disagree with phenotype. Female pseudohermaphrodite = ovaries + XX but masculinised genitalia (enlarged clitoris, sometimes prepuce/prostate). Male pseudohermaphrodite = testes + XY but feminised genitalia.
  • True hermaphrodite: at least one ovary and one testis (or an ovotestis). Gonads sit between the caudal kidney pole, inguinal area and perineum.

Named conditions

  • Persistent Mullerian duct syndrome: hereditary (Miniature Schnauzer in the US, Basset Hound in Europe); male external genitalia with male and female internal ducts, often cryptorchid; hyperoestrogenism from retained-testis neoplasia, gynaecomastia, alopecia, pyometra.
  • Male calico cats: castrate if testes external; abdominal surgery only if cryptorchid or clinical problems.
  • Hypospadias (abnormal ventral urethral opening), penile frenulum, micropenis, phimosis; monorchidism/polyorchidism (rare).

Female anomalies

  • Ovarian agenesis, ovarian/follicular cysts (persistent estrus), uterus unicornis (hunt for the ipsilateral ovary to prevent remnant syndrome), segmental aplasia, hydro/muco/pyometra, ectopic fetuses, clitoral hypertrophy/os clitoris (check urethral placement before any clitoral surgery).
  • Feline mammary fibroadenomatous hyperplasia: benign progesterone-driven proliferation in young cycling queens or progestin-treated cats; firm glands, sometimes brown milk; flank spay recommended.
