Overview, Legislation, and Disaster Types

Disaster and emergency response for shelter veterinarians sits at the intersection of federal legislation, community planning, and animal welfare. Understanding the statutory framework and types of disasters is foundational before any operational content.

Good Samaritan immunity notes ASV

Shelter veterinarians acting in an emergency response context have the same Good Samaritan immunity as any other veterinarian. Familiarity with FEMA courses is recommended for all shelter veterinarians who may be involved in emergency response. This immunity does not remove the obligation to practice competently and document care.

Key federal legislation notes

LegislationYearKey requirements
PETS Act (Pets Evacuation and Transportation Standards Act) 2006 Regional planning authorities and communities must take companion animals into account in their emergency response planning. This is a statutory mandate, not a recommendation.
Robert T. Stafford Disaster Relief and Emergency Assistance Act Amended post-Katrina (1) State and local plans must address rescue, care, shelter, and essential needs of individuals AND their pets and service animals. (2) FEMA administrator may provide funding to states for household pet and service animal capabilities. (3) FEMA is authorised to use federal resources for household pets and service animals within certain federal disaster declarations.
Exam trap · PETS Act is a mandate, not guidance The PETS Act of 2006 mandates that regional planning authorities include companion animals in emergency planning. It is not advisory. An exam option stating this is optional or discretionary is incorrect.

Advance planning is required to safeguard animal welfare and to protect human health and safety across both categories.

11 animal categories requiring emergency planning notes ASV

  1. Family pets: keeping pets safe has an extremely positive effect on the mental health of owners and communities.
  2. Service animals: must ALWAYS remain with their owners per the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA). No exceptions.
  3. Commercial farm animals: affect community health and economics.
  4. Backyard/non-commercial livestock: horses, small poultry flocks, hobby farm animals. Local communities may need to accommodate these to prevent evacuation failure.
  5. Working animals: law enforcement/SAR horses and dogs. Very strong handler bond; best managed in team units with their handlers.
  6. Wildlife animals
  7. Wildlife facilities: zoos, aquaria, entertainment entities, wildlife ranching facilities.
  8. Animal facilities: vet hospitals, pet stores, rescues, sanctuaries, shelters, foster care groups, biomedical research facilities, breeding operations, kennels, equestrian centres.
  9. Travelling events: rodeos, roadside menageries, petting zoos, circuses.
  10. Animals used illegally: hoarding cases, fighting operations. These may surface during a disaster.
  11. Free-roaming, stray, feral, and undersocialised animals: a feral or undersocialised animal caught and confined in a box trap can be extremely fearful.
Exam trap · service animals and ADA Service animals must always be permitted to stay with their owners in accordance with the Americans with Disabilities Act. Emergency sheltering cannot separate a person from their service animal. This is the only category with an absolute legal mandate for co-housing.

Community animal resources for disaster response notes

An effective community emergency response draws on a wide network. Key entities include: animal control and sheltering agencies, law enforcement, veterinarians and local hospitals, public health and environmental health officials, livestock producers, cooperative extension agents, fairgrounds and parks departments, SAR entities (mounted SAR, sheriff posses), animal exhibitors, wildlife rehabilitation facilities, animal feed and supply retailers, animal rescue organisations, Local Emergency Planning Committees (LEPC), community voluntary organisations (Red Cross, Salvation Army), boarding/breeding kennels, equestrian organisations, citizen corps, animal competition entities, universities, biomedical research facilities, and locally based state agency representatives (agriculture, state animal health officials, brand inspectors, wildlife agencies).

Shelter planning: buy-in and structure notes

A six-step shelter planning process for disaster preparedness:

  1. Buy-in from senior management, board of directors, and/or political leadership to commit adequate time and resources.
  2. Risk assessment (hazards, vulnerabilities, consequences).
  3. Review of current plans, operating procedures, insurance coverage, equipment, and systems.
  4. Input from key staff, community experts, and professional advisors.
  5. Development of the written plan, addressing shelter responsibilities, emergency animal care, evacuation, power/water disruption, data backup, communications, recovery, training, and plan update schedules.
  6. Periodic review, exercise (drills), and update of the plan.

Planning, ICS Structure, and the Four Phases

The four phases of disaster management provide the operational framework. Understanding the ICS/NIMS/NRF hierarchy and its specific components is among the most exam-tested content in this subdomain.

The four phases of disaster management notes ASV

Phase 1: Mitigation

  • Ongoing, preemptive activities. Must first identify the events most likely to affect the shelter and community.
  • Risk assessment: hazards, vulnerabilities, and consequences.
  • Once risks are identified, develop and implement mitigation strategies to reduce future impact.

Phase 2: Preparedness

  • Written plan: must be accessible to all personnel, used in drills, and regularly reviewed and updated.
  • Plans must address essential services for ALL animals currently in care, including those in foster homes.
  • Plans must address supply chain disruptions, utility outages, and evacuation strategies.
  • Zoonotic disease control: plans must include surveillance, isolation, and treatment of infected animals.
  • Personnel structure: identify critical roles and how the shelter will fill them.
  • All personnel should complete NRF, NIMS, and ICS modules before a disaster strikes.

Phase 3: Response

  • ICS initiated as soon as a disaster is anticipated or has occurred (not after it is declared).
  • Animals admitted must receive at least a cursory assessment at intake: signs of infectious disease, conditions requiring emergency medical care, and exposure to hazards.
  • Animals should receive vaccinations including rabies and parasite control.
  • Animals must be decontaminated where applicable. PPE is recommended until decontamination is complete.
  • Multiple channels for owner reunification: social media, flyers, electronic billboards, neighbourhood ambassadors.
  • System for managing physical and monetary donations must be in place before disaster strikes.

Phase 4: Recovery

  • Lasts until the shelter and affected communities return to normal, which can be days to years.
  • When residents are struggling to rebuild, fostering and local adoption are unlikely to be priorities. Alternative strategies: adoption events outside the impacted area, increased transfer, shelter-neuter-return.
  • Sustained housing instability may increase requests for temporary animal sheltering.
  • Debrief and evaluate planning, response, and recovery processes. Update the plan.

The ICS / NIMS / NRF hierarchy notes

What each system is

SystemWhat it isKey point
NRF (National Response Framework)A national plan setting forth roles, responsibilities, and relationships among local, state, federal, tribal, territorial, and non-government entities in all phases of emergency management.Defines the coordinated hierarchy for assistance requests. If local resources are overwhelmed, requests escalate through local ICS, to state, then federal.
NIMS (National Incident Management System)The overall response system for preparedness and response. Provides the framework within which ICS operates.Must complete NIMS training before disaster strikes. All shelter personnel should be familiar.
ICS (Incident Command System)A standardised, flexible, and scalable system for directing on-scene emergency response.Incident Commander (IC) leads. Command staff: Safety, Liaison, Public Information Officers. Four General Staff sections report to IC.
EMAC (Emergency Management Assistance Compact)Mutual aid system allowing the 50 states to share personnel and equipment across state lines.If a state cannot respond, it can seek resources laterally (from other states via EMAC) before escalating to federal level.
Exam trap · ICS General Staff: four sections The four key functions under ICS General Staff (reporting directly to the Incident Commander) are: Operations (tactical response), Planning, Logistics, and Finance/Administration. The command staff (Safety, Liaison, Public Information Officers) is different from the General Staff. An exam option listing mitigation/preparedness/response/recovery as ICS sections is incorrect; those are the four disaster phases, not ICS sections.

Unified Command vs. Unity of Command scenario doc

Two distinct and frequently confused ICS concepts, both tested on the ABVP exam:

ConceptDefinitionWhen usedKey principle
Unified Command Multiple agencies or jurisdictions with distinct responsibilities manage an incident together by co-authoring a single Incident Action Plan (IAP). Multi-jurisdiction or multi-agency incidents (e.g., HAZMAT spill + flood + animal rescue involving Fire, Police, and Animal Control simultaneously). No agency loses its legal authority. Resources are pooled efficiently. A single IAP with shared objectives is agreed upon.
Unity of Command Every individual responder reports to, and receives assignments from, only ONE designated supervisor. Field personnel at every level of the response. Prevents conflicting directives and maintains a clear chain of command. A vet tech on the animal evacuation team does NOT take orders from a passing police officer.
Exam trap · conflicting orders in the field If a veterinary professional working under the Shelter Group Supervisor is given a conflicting order by a responder from another agency, the correct response is to maintain your assigned task and report only to your designated supervisor. Unified Command at the top does not override Unity of Command for field personnel. Accepting orders from outside your chain breaks the Unity of Command principle.

CART/SART and mutual aid notes

  • CART (Community Animal Response Team): organised and operated under the local emergency management agency. Varies considerably in design and mandate. Provides emergency management training and can deploy personnel through ICS. Addresses volunteer liability and injury issues.
  • SART (State Animal Response Team): state-level counterpart. May be a component of state government or a nonprofit formally affiliated with the state. Accepts volunteers, donated services, materials, and funds.
  • MAA/MOU (Mutual Aid Agreements/Memoranda of Understanding): formal agreements for legal reasons, reimbursement, and expediting resource requests. Shelters should establish these with local government before disasters occur.

Six Emergency Response Missions

The ASV framework identifies six distinct emergency response missions. Each has specific protocols, personnel requirements, and legal considerations. Ownership and intake protocols apply across all missions.

Ownership issues during disasters notes ASV

Ownership statusRequired actions
Owned animal (owner known) Obtain: (1) permission to care for and treat the animal; (2) a release of liability; (3) an indemnification agreement; (4) all available information (medical history, contact info); (5) an agreement as to a definite term, at the end of which, unless renewed, the animal will be forfeited to the shelter. The owner should be required to cover costs of care to the extent they are able.
Ownership unknown or unsettled The animal is handled as a stray until claimed. Holding times for disaster animals may be influenced by lost property statutes in addition to stray animal laws. Consult legal counsel before adopting out or fostering animals recovered in a disaster.
Exam trap · legal counsel for holding times Disaster animal holding times are governed by both stray animal laws AND lost property statutes. Shelters must consult legal counsel before adopting out or placing disaster animals. An exam option suggesting immediate adoption of unclaimed disaster animals is incorrect.

The six emergency response missions notes ASV

#MissionKey protocols
1 Activation and rapid needs assessment Conducted via the lead agency per ICS. Initial assessment determines which plan components and resources to activate. Shelters must provide periodic reports through ICS on pet numbers, capacities, challenges, and resource needs.
2 Evacuation and transportation Owners have primary responsibility for evacuation of their animals. Citizens using public transport will need assistance. Whenever possible, animals and owners should be transported together. Transport environments must protect from temperature extremes and be adequately ventilated. Livestock transport teams must be pre-identified and equipment evaluated for mechanical safety.
3 Sheltering Three sheltering models: (a) co-located (animal shelter adjacent to human shelter, separate management), (b) cohabitation (people and animals in one facility, unified management), (c) pet-friendly locations. Intake must include animal ID, photographs (with owner if possible), records, brief health and behavioural evaluation. Owner responsibilities must be communicated in writing. Zoonotic and infectious disease risk must be addressed.
4 Veterinary medical response Provision of veterinary care at emergency sheltering sites. Includes general examination, preventive care, and monitoring. Preventive protocols (vaccination, parasite control) must be established at each site. Out-of-state veterinary personnel require appropriate licensure. Animals transferred out of state for adoption or sheltering require workup to prevent transport of endemic pathogens: vaccinations, treatments, testing, Certificates of Veterinary Inspection (CVIs), and permits determined in collaboration with state animal health officials.
5 Animal Search and Rescue (ASAR) Search, rescue, or trapping. Must be coordinated with human SAR operations. May continue after human SAR has ended. Requires substantial training to operate safely alongside human SAR. Typically led by local animal control; shelters may provide trained personnel.
6 Animal decontamination Ranges from removing floodwater contaminants to radioactive particulates. Typically conducted via bathing and rinsing. PPE required (see below). Gold standard: soap and water bathing followed by clean water rinse, which reduces or eliminates most contaminants. Coordination with incident HAZMAT personnel and the ICS Safety Officer is imperative.

Decontamination PPE and OSHA requirements notes

Minimum PPE for basic animal decontamination

  • Barrier protection
  • Gloves
  • Apron or water-resistant coveralls
  • Goggles
  • Boots
  • Cap
  • Barrier mask or face shield
Exam trap · OSHA respiratory protection: three prerequisites If respiratory protection is required for decontamination, OSHA mandates a strict three-step prerequisite protocol that must be completed before the incident:
  1. Medical screening
  2. Fit testing of the respirator
  3. Formal training on respirator use
All three must be completed PRIOR to the incident. Completing them during a response is not acceptable.

Intake protocols at disaster admission notes ASV

  • Immediately upon intake: a licensed veterinarian must examine all animals entering a shelter's custody as part of disaster relief. Each intake must be thoroughly evaluated and documented.
  • Photographs must be taken. Some form of physical identification (ID tag, band) must be securely placed on the animal for ready identification and to minimise administrative errors.
  • All vaccinations and preventive medications necessary for the shelter environment must be administered or directed by a veterinarian licensed to practice in that state.
  • For the entire duration in the shelter's custody, all aspects of care and handling must be documented.
  • Animals from outside the region should be evaluated by a licensed veterinarian upon intake and remain under veterinary care for a period equivalent to customary quarantine precautions. They may arrive with diseases to which the local area has no existing immunity.

Documentation and forms system notes

A system of forms is crucial to document all services, care, and observations made of animals while in the shelter's custody. Detailed information including staff time dedicated to response may be requested for reimbursement grants from local, state, federal, or private organisations. This documentation is both a welfare requirement and a financial tool.

Post-Disaster: Behavioural Pathology, Reunification, and Recovery

Animals exposed to uncontrollable aversive disaster events can develop trauma responses analogous to PTSD. Reunification rates are dramatically affected by the availability of pet-inclusive emergency sheltering. Both areas are statistic-dense and high-yield.

Post-disaster trauma in animals notes research

Behavioural profile of disaster trauma

Animals subjected to unpredictable and uncontrollable aversive events can exhibit a characteristic profile:

  • Generalised fear and arousal
  • Heightened vigilance
  • Fear of specific stimuli
  • Passive avoidance and resignation

Military working dogs (MWDs) failing deployment

A study of 14 military working dogs who failed to perform well during deployments reported:

  • Signs of hypervigilance and hyperresponsivity to normally encountered stimuli
  • Attempts to escape or avoid previously positive or neutral environments
  • Failure to perform previously trained responses
  • Changes in social interactions with their handlers
High-yield research · Fukushima earthquake 2011 A study compared 17 dogs abandoned after the 2011 Japanese earthquake (Fukushima area) with a control group of 8 homeless dogs from Kanagawa, an area unaffected by the disaster. Findings:
  • Fukushima dogs showed significantly lower levels of aggression toward unfamiliar people, poorer trainability, and less attachment to their caretakers compared to control dogs.
  • Urine cortisol levels were 5 to 10 times higher in Fukushima dogs than in controls.
  • The cortisol elevation persisted even after 10 weeks in the rescue centre. Physiological stress markers did not normalise quickly.
This study demonstrates that disaster trauma causes lasting physiological and behavioural changes in dogs that are not rapidly reversible upon placement in a safe environment.

Hurricane Katrina: reunification rates and the shelter effect notes

High-yield statistics: 5% vs 40.2% vs 70.1% All three reunification figures come from Hurricane Katrina data and are all testable. The baseline (no temporary pet shelter) is 5%. With temporary co-located sheltering, rates rose to 40.2% and up to 70.1%. One source in your notes also cites a 15 to 20% rate from another shelter. The key message is that temporary pet-inclusive sheltering dramatically multiplies reunification rates.

Critical incident stress and psychological first aid notes

Critical incident stress refers to stress and anxiety brought on by the psychological impacts of a disaster. It can affect both survivors and responders. Sources include deaths, injuries, loss of animals, loss of property, community destruction, environmental damage, and the general impacts of fear and uncertainty.

Human safety is the first priority of any disaster response plan. Addressing the physical and mental stress of personnel, community members, and responders must be built into the plan.

Animal stress and anxiety also increase the likelihood of dog bites during disasters. This is a public safety and personnel safety consideration.

Communication during disasters notes

  • Consider backup communication systems: cellular text messaging, radios, older hard-wired telephones, amateur radio operators, written messages, and messengers.
  • Emergency instructions (evacuation orders) should include specific information for animal owners.
  • Animals are an emotionally charged topic. Inaccurate information spreads rapidly. Dedicate a person or team to monitor internet and social media communications during the response.
