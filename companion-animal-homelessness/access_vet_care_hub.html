Epidemiology of Pet Ownership and At-Risk Populations

Data from the national AVCC/CARE study (University of Tennessee, 2018) establishes the demographic landscape of companion animal ownership in relation to income, age, gender, region, and housing type. These epidemiological patterns directly predict access to veterinary care.

Study context

  • Conducted by the University of Tennessee's Centre for Applied Research and Evaluation (CARE) in partnership with the Access to Veterinary Care Coalition (AVCC).
  • Three income strata defined by the federal poverty level (FPL): below 138% FPL, 138% to 250% FPL, and above 250% FPL.
  • 2017 guidelines: FPL for a family of 4 was $24,600. SNAP eligibility is at or below 138% FPL.
  • Average household owned 2.2 pets; approximately 1/4 of households owned only 1 pet.
  • 80.2% of respondents strongly agreed their pets are considered members of the family.

Income-stratified pet ownership patterns AVCC 2018 notes

Demographic patterns in pet ownership notes AVCC 2018

Household type

  • 46.5% of households owned dogs only; 24% owned cats only; 29.6% had a blended family (dogs and cats).
  • Condo and apartment dwellers: more likely to have fewer pets than other residence types.
  • Higher-income households: more likely to own dogs only.
  • Males: more likely to have only dogs. Younger respondents: more likely to have blended families.

Age trends

  • Older respondents: more likely to own only one pet; more likely to own cats; more likely to have acquired from friend/family or taken in a stray cat.
  • Younger respondents: more likely to have a blended family; more likely to have received their pet as a gift; less likely to have spayed/neutered their pets (if done, more likely at a free or reduced cost clinic).
  • Younger pet owners more likely to hold pet insurance. Western US had the highest insurance ownership rate.

Acquisition and spay/neuter

  • Approximately 1/4 of dogs and cats were reportedly adopted from shelters.
  • More dogs came from breeders; cats were more often strays or shelter-adopted.
  • Dogs were more likely than cats to remain intact (not spayed or neutered).

Human-animal bond in context notes AVCC 2018

Two theoretical frameworks underpin the human-animal bond (HAB) and its relevance to access to care:

  • Attachment theory: originally developed studying mother-infant relationships; now extended to human-animal bonds. Recognises four attachment styles. The strength and style of attachment shapes how an owner responds to barriers in providing care.
  • Social support theory: social support acts to buffer the health impacts of stress, either through the perception that support is available or through tangible/intangible supportive actions. Companion animals function as social support for their owners.
Exam trap · two different family-membership statistics Your notes contain two distinct survey figures that are both testable and easily confused:
  • 80.2% = strongly agreed "my pets are members of the family" in the AVCC 2018 national study.
  • 95% = considered their animals family members in a separate 2015 survey also referenced in the notes.
An exam question citing the AVCC/CARE study expects 80.2%. An exam question citing the 2015 survey expects 95%. Read the source carefully.

Additionally: 59% of dog owners and 56% of cat owners viewed their animal as a child or family member (2015 data). Social support has been shown to be a protective factor for overall physical and mental health.

Vulnerable populations and relinquishment notes

Defined vulnerable populations

  • Elderly individuals
  • Homeless individuals
  • Victims of abuse
  • Individuals with mental illness
  • Racial and ethnic minorities

HAB benefits in vulnerable populations (three categories)

  • Physiological benefits
  • Psychological and emotional benefits
  • Psychosocial benefits

Relinquishment drivers related to life circumstance

  • Moving as the result of a life situation is one of the most frequent reasons for relinquishment.
  • Associated circumstances: loss of or change in employment, housing issues, health issues, financial difficulties, natural disasters.

Homelessness definition

A person who lacks a fixed, regular, and adequate nighttime residence. Includes individuals living on streets, in cars or abandoned buildings, or in temporary shelter (staying with family/friends, in a homeless shelter, or in a hotel/motel).

Factors associated with homelessness

  • Living below the poverty level; insufficient resources for low-income families
  • Substance abuse; mental illness; family violence
  • In the US: 17.6% of families with children under 18 years fall below the poverty level; 19.5% of those with children under 6 years
  • Families with children: approximately 33% of the homeless population
  • Unaccompanied youth (under 25 years, without parents/guardian): 7% of the total homeless population

Homeless individuals and pets

  • Difficulty affording veterinary care.
  • Refuse services (shelters, transitional housing) when accommodation policies do not allow animals.
  • Some homeless individuals report putting the animal's needs above their own.
  • Studies show attachments to animals in homeless individuals are stronger than in the general population.

Barriers to Veterinary Care: Three Care Types

The AVCC/CARE study quantifies barriers across three distinct types of veterinary care. The financial barrier is dominant across all types but varies in percentage. Secondary barriers and demographic risk factors also differ by care type.

Exam trap · emergency care has a lower barrier rate (not because it is easier to access) Emergency care barriers are documented at the lowest rate not because care is more accessible, but because emergency care is needed less frequently. Only approximately 1/3 of pet owners indicated they needed emergency care in the past 2 years. Do not interpret the lower barrier rate as evidence of better accessibility.

Financial barrier rates across care types AVCC 2018

Preventative care barriers in detail notes AVCC 2018

  • 1/4 of all respondents had been unable to obtain preventative care for at least one pet in the recent past.
  • Barrier prevalence decreases as household income increases.
  • Younger respondents disproportionately experienced preventative care barriers.
  • Financial barrier was the dominant reason regardless of income category.

Most desired preventative care services

  • 65% of those facing a barrier wanted vaccination for their pet.
  • 50.7% wanted heartworm, flea, or tick preventative treatment.
  • Younger respondents were significantly more likely to want behavioural advice and guidance on how to care for their pets.

Sick care: the middle-income convergence notes AVCC 2018

A critical and exam-tested finding: pet owners in the middle-income group (138% to 250% of the poverty level) were as likely to cite financial inability as a barrier to sick care as those in the lowest income category. This economic convergence is not seen for preventative care, where barriers decrease clearly with income.

  • Almost half of those who faced a preventative care barrier also experienced a sick care barrier.
  • Younger respondents were more likely to cite not knowing where to seek care for sick animals.
Exam trap · sick care middle-income convergence For sick care, income does not clearly protect against the financial barrier in the way it does for preventative care. Middle-income and lowest-income households report equivalent levels of financial inability. An exam option stating "higher income protects against sick care financial barriers" is incorrect.

Proposed resolutions notes

  • Means testing: implement income verification to determine eligibility for discounted services.
  • Geographic means testing: offer services in communities known to be low-income, avoiding the need for personal financial documentation at the front desk.
  • Multi-modal collaboration: collaboration between for-profit practices, nonprofit vet organisations, and other nonprofits may provide better solutions than any single model.

Key clinical principle · interdisciplinary approach required A collaborative and interdisciplinary approach is needed to address care access for vulnerable populations. Social workers trained in human-animal bond dynamics can play a critical role in understanding how the systems within a vulnerable individual's world (housing, financial, social) interact to create barriers to animal care. The VCE Principles 2024 framework reinforces this through its One Health and One Welfare framing: improving animal welfare in these populations also improves human welfare.

The "Champion" concept notes

Successful programs require a "Champion": a person or group who:

  • Recognises the need for a program or service.
  • Has the passion and connections to convince others of its importance.
  • Has the stamina to stay committed for the long term.

Programs start with a dialogue in the community, often beginning with a phone call: "What can you do? What are your boundaries?" It requires like-minded practitioners who are seeing the same problem and are willing to initiate collaborative action.

Building community trust notes VCE 2024

  • Trust is built through relationships and sensitivity to the culture of the community being served.
  • Successful programs must meet owners where they are and foster relationships that eventually lead to trust.
  • Involve community members directly: they can go back to neighbours and families, reaching people who actually trust them.
  • Most veterinarians and veterinary students have not experienced poverty. Sensitivity training to build cultural competencies is recommended.
VCE Principles 2024 alignment The trust-building approach described in the CARE focus groups directly mirrors VCE Principles 1 and 2: know your community (its culture, history, social networks, and power structures) and build trust with formal and informal leadership. The concept of "meeting owners where they are" reflects Principle 2's asset-based approach, working with community strengths rather than imposing external solutions.

Language access notes

Successful programs must offer services in the pet owner's native language:

  • Having a veterinarian or veterinary technician who is fluent in Spanish is optimal for US-based programmes.
  • At a minimum: all written information must be offered in the second most common language spoken in that service area.
Exam trap · minimum language standard The minimum requirement for language access is written material in the second most common language of the service area. English-only documentation fails this standard. An exam option stating "English is sufficient" is incorrect.

Economic sustainability principles notes

The focus group explicitly warned against programmes that create "fiefdoms": siloed operations that refuse to collaborate with other organisations. The recommended approach is cooperative, shared-resource networking that avoids duplication and maximises community impact.
