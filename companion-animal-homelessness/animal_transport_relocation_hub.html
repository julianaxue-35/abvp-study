Relocation Framework, Goals and Regulations

Relocation programs transfer animals from a source organisation (typically a community with excess pet population) to a destination organisation (typically one with unmet adopter demand). Every design decision is judged against the welfare of all affected animals and against length of stay (LOS).

Core principle

  • Intentionally designed relocation programs weigh the health, behaviour and safety risks and benefits for all affected animals and minimise negative impacts through careful selection and planning.
  • Decision making must prioritise a decrease in LOS. Holding animals for relocation when live outcomes are available locally pushes shelters beyond their capacity for care and damages community relationships.
  • When destination shelters accept more animals than they have capacity to care for, welfare is compromised for both relocated and resident animals, and LOS increases.
  • It is UNACCEPTABLE to transport an animal when the transport itself is likely to be harmful to its immediate or long-term health or welfare.

Relocation program models: risk and cost gradients textbook Fig 20.2

Risk and operating cost both rise along three independent axes: how far the animal travels (geographic), how many organisations are involved (population), and how the animal moves (transport mode). A program can sit at a different point on each axis.

Responsibilities of the program ASV 2022 notes

Regulatory and legal

  • All participants must follow federal transport regulations plus local and state regulations for both source and destination.
  • Departments of Agriculture and Departments of Health commonly require health certificates (CVIs), specific vaccinations, and age or health restrictions for imported animals. For commercial air, the airline must be consulted.

Emergency planning (made before transport)

  • Emergency contact information; safe locations to stop; protocols for vehicle problems; a plan for animal and human medical emergencies. Travelling animals must have contact information for both source and destination.

Agreements, records and metrics

  • Written agreements between all parties, reviewed regularly, addressing at minimum medical and behavioural selection criteria plus transportation and destination requirements.
  • A contact person must be identified at each transfer point, and a travel record kept from source to destination to allow tracing of source and contacts along the route.
  • Track standard metrics: animal demographics, behavioural and medical conditions, and outcomes.
Exam trap · the no-return resource paradox Unless there are extenuating circumstances, animals should NOT be returned to the source, even when unexpected medical or behavioural concerns appear at the destination. Transport is a major physiological stressor and a heavy resource investment. Programmatic failures are corrected by revisiting selection criteria and goals, not by re-shipping the animal. If a destination repeatedly receives ineligible animals, all parties revisit selection criteria, they do not raise capacity or return animals.
Gap filled · named regional zoonoses (ASV 2022, 11.2) Your notes list "zoonotic diseases, HW disease" generically. The ASV chapter specifically names plague, rabies and leptospirosis as regionally distributed zoonoses requiring special consideration, alongside aggressive behaviours. Clarification with scientific backing: heartworm (Dirofilaria immitis) is a relevant vector-borne and public-health consideration in relocation, but it is not a classic zoonosis (human infection is rare and aberrant, and is not the reason ASV flags it). Keep heartworm separate from the named zoonoses on the exam.
Gap filled · the manifest must survive an accident (ASV 2022, 11.3) Beyond the travel record in your notes, ASV requires that a copy of the transport manifest (identifying every animal on board) is kept accessible and separate from the vehicle itself, so it survives if an accident destroys the copy travelling with the animals. A cloud-based digital manifest shared in real time with source, transporter and destination satisfies this.

Responsibilities at the Source

The source organisation carries the bulk of preventive and screening duties: population-wide vaccination, parasite control, heartworm testing, records, identification, and the fit-for-transport examination.

Vaccination, parasites and heartworm ASV 2022 notes

  • All eligible animals in the source population must be vaccinated at or before intake. Vaccinating only the animals selected for relocation is insufficient because it leaves the majority of the population unprotected.
  • It is not recommended to hold animals back from transport just to allow a vaccine response or a booster.
  • Treatment for fleas, ticks and internal parasites is strongly recommended.
  • Ideally, all dogs 6 months of age and older are tested for heartworm disease prior to relocation.
Exam trap · vaccinate the population, not just the travellers A tempting wrong answer is "vaccinate only the animals selected for transport." The standard is population-wide vaccination at or before intake. Selective vaccination of only the transport pool is explicitly inadequate.

Records, certificates and identification ASV 2022

  • Health and behaviour records must be shared with the destination. When required, a valid CVI and proof of rabies vaccination must accompany each animal (requirements vary by state).
  • Visual identification is required: collars or tags routinely, with alternatives such as marking the inner ear or painting a claw on a neonate. Microchipping before transport is ideal because it provides permanent identification.
  • Each primary transport enclosure must be marked with the animal's unique identifier.

The fit-for-transport examination ASV 2022 notes

  • Animals must be examined by trained staff within 24 hours prior to travel and deemed fit for transport.
  • Purpose: look for evidence of infectious disease and evaluate the animal's ability to tolerate the physical and emotional experiences of travel (prolonged confinement, handling by different people, direct exposure to other animals).
  • A veterinarian must confirm fitness for transport for any animal with medical concerns or recovering from surgery.
Who clears whom Trained staff can screen and clear apparently healthy animals within the 24-hour window. A licensed veterinarian is specifically required to clear animals with medical concerns or post-surgical recovery. Exam options naming the driver, an agriculture inspector, or a foster coordinator are wrong.

Local and regional vs international sources ASV 2022 notes

  • Before accepting transport from an international source, organisations should weigh the needs of partners closer to home and the impact of the international partnership on their own mission.
  • Selection criteria must account for suitability for adoption, disease management, long-term quality of life, and the effects of extensive travel.
  • Shelters accepting international transports should have a written policy statement explaining the decision.
Gap filled · importing animals under 8 weeks (ASV 2022, 11.4.2) Importing animals less than 8 weeks old may be prohibited in some states. This is a regulatory constraint to check at the destination jurisdiction before planning pediatric transports.

Vehicles, Climate and Enclosure Standards

Engineering controls protect animals from heat, exhaust and injury. The high-yield numbers cluster here: temperature and humidity bounds, ventilation geometry, and the reasons factory cargo climate units are rejected.

Primary enclosure standards ASV 2022 notes

  • Large enough for the animal to stand and sit erect, turn around normally while standing, and lie in a natural position without lying on another animal.
  • Unfamiliar animals must not be transported together in the same primary enclosure.
  • No sharp edges; flooring must prevent injury, discomfort and leakage of fluids into other enclosures. Absorbent bedding must be provided unless it poses a risk to the individual animal.
  • Vent openings on at least 3 sides, with at least 1 inch (2 cm) of unobstructed space between vent openings and adjacent structures.
  • If an enclosure is permanently fixed so only a single door ventilates, that door must face an unobstructed aisle.
  • Enclosures must be secured against movement, doors secured against accidental opening, and operators must be able to remove animals swiftly in an emergency.

Vehicle climate parameters ASV 2022 notes

These are the most heavily tested numbers in the subdomain. Memorise both the absolute safety bounds and the narrower comfort target.

Vehicle engineering rules

  • Control over heating and cooling in the animal compartment is essential and must be monitored; alarms help when drivers and animals are in separate compartments.
  • Air must be fresh and free of exhaust fumes; a battery-operated carbon monoxide detector is placed in the animal compartment to protect all occupants from an undetected leak.
  • Interior temperatures in direct sunlight can rapidly exceed safe levels even when it is comfortable outside.
  • Small vehicles carrying few animals per trip are preferred.
Exam trap · why factory cargo climate units are rejected Factory-installed cargo climate units are designed for static, non-heat-producing loads (the classic example is flowers). They lack the airflow, heat distribution and power to clear the metabolic body heat of live animals. The recommendation is a roof-mounted climate unit, plus insulation of sides, roof and floor, and an insulated floor mat (covered with bedliner or diamond-plate flooring) to block road heat reaching the lowest tier of enclosures.

Kennel selection notes

  • Sound, appropriately sized, and matched to the length of transport; must not collapse; minimise injury or escape; easy to clean and disinfect; durable for repeated use.
  • Use a disinfectable barrier (e.g. roll-down outdoor solar shades) to reduce visual stimulation and act as a physical barrier to respiratory droplets.
  • Choose kennels that can be removed without taking the animal out of the safety of the kennel; built-in cargo control bars help achieve this.

Exam trap · water vs food intervals Water and food are not on the same clock. Water at least every 4 hours at observation stops (adults and juveniles). Food at least every 24 hours for adults, while juveniles eat small amounts every 4 hours. An option giving "food and water every 4 hours for adults without exception" is wrong.

Overnight and unattended care

  • When feasible, an overnight facility that houses cats and dogs off the vehicle is preferred (foster homes, shelters, hotels, or transport hubs).
  • Animals should never be left unattended unless monitoring is sufficient and attendants can immediately respond to needs.
  • Cats may benefit from remaining in their carriers if large enough, and must have litter box access if housed overnight.
  • Dogs must be walked or exercised on trips requiring an overnight stay.

Air transport notes live-animal air standards

  • Most expensive method and the greatest degree of risk; accommodates only individuals or small groups. Commercial airlines often have breed, species or conformational restrictions for liability and safety.
  • When well managed, flight can shorten travel time, which may reduce stress, illness and LOS at the destination.
  • Plan the whole chain: travel to the airport, loading, flight, travel to the destination, and unloading.
  • Hold temperature should stay comfortable; not below -7°C or above 27°C on the ground.
  • Loading time should not exceed 45 minutes; do not load if interior temperature exceeds 29°C.
  • Animals should not sit on a loaded plane more than 30 minutes without take-off if ground temperatures exceed 23.9°C.
  • Consider weight balance within the plane.

Responsibilities at the destination ASV 2022

  • Sufficient trained personnel ready to receive and evaluate animals on arrival.
  • Each animal receives a brief health assessment at intake to identify infectious disease and problems needing emergency or follow-up care.
  • Adequate housing prepared without displacing the existing population.
  • Isolation or quarantine need is informed by regulatory requirements, animal health status, and infectious disease risk. Quarantine is appropriate only for high-risk animals with direct exposure; unnecessary holds increase LOS and harm animal health and organisational goals.
Gap filled · aggregation of sources (ASV 2022, 11.4.5) If animals from different sources cannot travel on separate vehicles, they should ideally be housed in separate compartments, with protocols in place to minimise exposure and cross-contamination between populations.

Behavioural Stress, Sedation and Post-Relocation Outcomes

Transport is a measurable physiological and behavioural stressor. This tab covers the stress-biomarker studies, behavioural patient selection, the evidence for anxiolytics, special-population handling, and disease and outcome data after relocation.

Transport stress physiology study data notes

  • Beagles after 22 hours of long-distance ground transport took 2 weeks to return serum cortisol and total bilirubin to normal.
  • Beagles travelling 9.5 hours had elevated cortisol and corticosterone during ground transport that returned to baseline overnight.
  • Beagles flown showed increased plasma cortisol after ground transport, increased salivary cortisol after both ground and air, and higher neutrophil with lower lymphocyte counts after both modes. Heart rate was significantly higher during loading than during take-off, flight, landing or turnaround; the largest heart-rate increases were during loading and unloading.
  • In flight, dogs were lying down about 50% of the time and inactive about 75% of the time; the most common behaviours were sitting and lip licking.
  • 44% of owned dogs that travelled were reported to show anxiety, fear and motion sickness. Nausea-consistent behaviours (vomiting, salivation, hiding, cowering) were ameliorated by a dog-appeasing pheromone collar in one report.
Exam trap · heart rate peaks during loading, not flight The intuitive wrong answer is that heart rate peaks at take-off or during turbulence. The data show the largest heart-rate increases occur during loading and unloading. The flight itself was relatively stable, with animals mostly inactive.

Behavioural patient selection ASV 2022 notes

  • Emphasis on preparing animals: low-stress handling, acclimation to the transport enclosure, and selecting behaviourally appropriate candidates.
  • At minimum, candidates should be easily handled by a variety of people, able to cope with prolonged confinement, and not show overt aggression toward any species that may share their individual transport.
  • Animals with known behavioural health concerns should not be relocated unless their safety and that of personnel can be ensured during the journey, and their best opportunity for continued management and improved welfare lies at the destination.

Sedatives and anxiolytics ASV 2022 study data

  • Psychopharmaceuticals must only be used under veterinary direction, and clear communication between partners is essential when behaviour medications are used.
  • Safe programs do not use sedatives to compensate for poor transport practices. It is unacceptable to transport animals sedated or anaesthetised to the point they cannot swallow, walk or thermoregulate (risk of choking, aspiration pneumonia, hypothermia, cardiac and respiratory arrest without continuous monitoring).
  • Evidence: dogs given clomipramine twice daily starting 3 days before transport had a significantly smaller rise in plasma cortisol, a lower neutrophil-to-lymphocyte ratio, lower average heart rate during transport, and drooled less often than placebo.

Outcomes after relocation study data notes

Dogs

  • Common conditions include spay/neuter (SN) complications, CIRDC, gastrointestinal and dermatologic disease.
  • 99% of relocated dogs were adopted after a mean LOS of 9 days; at 30 days post-adoption, 10% had been returned.
  • 2% of dogs developed SN complications within 2 weeks of relocation; neither the timing of SN nor the method of transport affected complication or disease rates.
  • The authors recommend performing SN at the destination to balance surgical capacity, LOS and community needs.

Cats

  • Cats transported a mean of 17 hours with a mean destination LOS of 18 days; 95% adopted, under 6% returned within 30 days.
  • Prevalence at destination: FURI 25.8%, FPV 1.6%, dermatophytosis 0.9%. Younger age, longer transport time and longer LOS were associated with higher FURI prevalence.
  • Disease incidences were under 5% both at the destination and at 30-day follow-up, except CIRDC at 17% at follow-up.
Exam trap · spay/neuter timing and complications The recommendation is to perform SN at the destination, and transport does not raise the baseline 2% complication rate. An option claiming travel increases SN complications, or that SN must be done at the source 14 days before loading, is wrong.

Data collection and reporting ASV 2022 notes

  • At minimum, both source and destination track the number of animals relocated, the incidence of unanticipated medical and behavioural concerns, and outcomes.
  • Tracking total LOS (source plus destination) captures the animal's overall time in the system and complements outcome metrics.
  • Collect non-urgent health and outcome data within 14 days of relocation and perform a quarterly analysis of aggregate data to catch the majority of individual, partner and programmatic concerns in time to respond.
  • These findings then inform selection and preparation policies, improving short-term success and long-term viability.
