Overview: Adoption and Placement

The field of shelter adoption has shifted decisively away from high-barrier, income-based screening toward a conversational, partnership-based model. Understanding this philosophy and the research underpinning it is central to the ABVP exam.

The conversational adoption philosophy notes

Over the past two decades, animal sheltering has shifted toward removing barriers to adoption. The contemporary model, known as conversational adoptions, is built on two foundational assumptions:

  • Every adopter is an individual with personal strengths and weaknesses.
  • Adopters inherently mean well and are capable of gathering knowledge for appropriate animal care.

Role of the adoption counsellor

  • Create a partnership with the adopter, not a gatekeeping relationship.
  • Focus on aligning the adopter's expectations with the characteristics of the specific animal.
Exam trap · returns are not failures A return should be viewed as a learning opportunity about that specific animal, making the next placement match easier. A returned animal can be adopted to the same person as a better match, or that person can become a foster carer or shelter advocate. Framing returns as screening failures or blacklisting returners is inconsistent with conversational adoption philosophy.

Adoption fees and income requirements notes shelter research

Historically, high adoption fees and income requirements were justified by the flawed assumption that high family income was necessary to guarantee a good quality of life for the adopted animal. Research refutes this:

  • No differences in owner-pet attachment were found in adult cat adopters when adoption fees were reduced.
  • No change in return rates when adoption fees were reduced.
  • A low advertised price was an important predictor of a visitor choosing to come to a specific shelter (visitor survey data).
Exam trap · income thresholds are not evidence-based Imposing income requirements or premium fees as a welfare safeguard is not supported by research. Lowering fees does not degrade attachment or increase returns. These barriers simply reduce access without improving animal welfare outcomes.

Visitor dynamics and geographic catchment notes

  • Most adopters search within a 30-mile radius. Some are willing to travel up to 90 miles, but this is the exception.
  • Shelters should understand local community demographics to tailor programs and ensure no sectors are neglected.
  • Track visitor geography by collecting zip codes at the door.

The 2/3 rule: visitors who do not adopt

  • As many as 2/3 of visitors have no intention of taking an animal home that day.
  • These visitors still matter: a positive experience can convert them into future adopters, foster carers, or volunteers.
  • Deploy trained volunteers to proactively engage with "just looking" visitors.

Spontaneous adoption

Studies show no difference in long-term retention between spontaneous (unplanned) adoptions and planned adoptions. Spontaneous adopters are not higher-risk; do not discourage or slow them down.

Exam trap · spontaneous vs. planned adoptions Spontaneous adoption retention rates are statistically equivalent to planned adoption retention rates. Restricting or discouraging spontaneous adoptions is not evidence-based.

Decision Making at the Shelter

Adoption decisions are driven by a sequential process: initial browsing is morphology-driven, the meet-and-greet phase is behaviour-driven. Understanding both phases separately is high-yield for the exam.

Canine adoption decision sequence notes shelter research

Canine morphology preferences (browsing phase)

  • Small body size
  • Puppies
  • Certain breed types
  • Long coat length
  • Lighter coat colour (tentative evidence)
Exam trap · in-kennel training does not drive canine adoption Studies confirm that in-kennel training has no effect on adopter browsing behaviour. Only the morphology of the dog while in the kennel predicted visitor stopping behaviour. Training resources should be invested in meet-and-greet skills (lying down, calm proximity), not in-kennel behaviours.

Meet-and-greet: the experimental evidence

  • An experimental study found that when dogs were encouraged to stay in proximity and lie down next to the adopter, and only the dog's favourite toy was present in the meet-and-greet room, adoptions nearly doubled.
  • Training goal: teach dogs to lie down next to a bench or chair (where adopters will subsequently sit). Teach relaxation, not play performance.
  • Excessive play and attention before the meet-and-greet may work against adoption by over-arousing the dog.

Feline adoption: environmental and labelling factors notes shelter research

Housing environment effects

  • Frequency of viewing the same cat (not duration) predicted ultimate adoption.
  • Top-tier cage placement + toy = more adopter interest.
  • Active cats with lower stress and human-directed social behaviour had shorter length of stay (LOS).
  • Singly housed cats in traditional horizontal cages had the fewest adoptions and most stress behaviour.
  • Re-orienting a cage from horizontal to vertical and adding a shelf and towel (hiding area): directly reduced stress and improved adoption outcomes.

Adopter preferences

  • Kittens preferred over adults.
  • Exotic breed status preferred.
  • Unique coat patterns preferred.
  • Lighter coat colours preferred.
  • Black cats: longer time to adoption; judged as less friendly, less playful, and more aggressive than light-coloured cats (without behavioural evidence). This is a documented cognitive bias.
Exam trap · the owner-surrender vs. stray label bias Cats labelled as "owner-surrendered" are adopted significantly faster than cats labelled as "stray," even when both groups have identical latency to approach people and the only difference is the label. This is a cognitive labelling bias documented in peer-reviewed research. It is not explained by actual behavioural differences. This is one of the most exam-tested concepts in this subdomain.

Choice availability: limiting vs. variety

  • Some research recommends limiting choices; other studies show adopters prefer variety and may avoid shelters they perceive as having a limited selection.
  • Allowing the public to view all animals, even those not yet ready for adoption, is likely to increase public trust and establish the public as a partner in the shelter's mission.

Evidence-Based Marketing

Every element of an animal's public-facing profile, from photo composition to word choice, has been studied for its effect on length of stay (LOS) and adoption interest. The evidence is specific and high-yield.

Online profile best practices notes shelter research

  • Photo: outdoor setting, animal standing, making direct eye contact. For cats, include a toy in the photo.
  • Video: participants who viewed videos of cats rated them as significantly more adoptable than those who viewed photos alone. Video is preferred for feline profiles.
  • Breed labels: removing breed labels from dog profiles did not create adverse consequences. One NYC shelter study found LOS was reduced for all dogs after breed label removal, with no change in return rates.
  • No warnings in the public profile: matching concerns, restrictions, and behavioural caveats should be raised during the individual counselling phase, never in the initial marketing profile.
  • Do not blame the previous owner in the animal's profile text.
Exam trap · matching restrictions belong in counselling, not marketing The online animal profile is a marketing tool. Behavioural concerns or potential matching restrictions placed in the initial profile reduce adoption interest and increase LOS. These must be reserved for individual counselling conversations with serious prospective adopters.

Language style

Adoption advertisements that use concrete and analytic language, rather than narrative or story-based language, result in higher interest from potential adopters. Avoid emotional vignettes; focus on clear, action-oriented descriptors.

The mere exposure effect

Repeatedly posting the same animal's photo on social media, even without interaction, makes viewers perceive that face as more attractive over time (mere exposure effect). Frequent and consistent social media posting is evidence-based, not just good practice.

Training to improve adoption metrics notes

Dogs

  • Focus training on meet-and-greet skills, not in-kennel performance.
  • Teach the dog to lie down next to a bench or chair (where an adopter will sit).
  • Teach calm relaxation in the presence of a person, rather than high-energy play.
  • Avoid over-stimulating the dog with excessive play and attention prior to the meet-and-greet.

Cats

  • Clicker training with food treats to approach the front of the kennel.
  • Increases time spent at the front of the cage.
  • Increases exploratory behaviour; decreases inactivity.
  • Cats who approached an unfamiliar experimenter had a shorter time to adoption.
  • Adopters consistently reported preferring cats who were friendly toward them.

Matching programs: benefits and trade-offs notes

Matching programs conduct counsellor-led conversations before potential adopters browse any animals, showing only dogs assessed as a good match for that individual.

  • Benefit: Better positive placement outcomes and higher adopter-animal compatibility.
  • Trade-off: Results in a longer average length of stay (aLOS) for the animals.
Exam trap · matching programs and aLOS Matching programs produce better outcomes but increase, not decrease, average LOS. An exam option stating matching programs "compress" LOS is incorrect.

Post-Adoption: Returns, Counselling, and Support

Behavioural problems are the leading cause of non-retention. The timing of returns is predictable, and targeted counselling at the time of adoption measurably reduces them. This section is statistic-dense and high-yield.

Return rates and timing notes shelter research

Overall return statistics

  • Return rate: 7% to 24% across published studies.
  • 20% of all adopters no longer have their pet within 1 year of adoption.
  • Of those lost from homes, only 49% are returned to the shelter. The remainder use alternative rehoming methods.
  • Key reason for not returning to the shelter: fear of being judged as a failure by the organisation. This underscores the importance of shelters positioning themselves as a non-judgmental resource.

Behavioural reasons for return

  • Problematic behaviour is the leading cause of non-retention of adopted animals.
  • 64% of animals were no longer in the same home one year post-adoption due to behaviour problems.
  • 54% to 74% of returned dogs were returned for behavioural reasons.
  • 38% of returned cats were returned due to behavioural reasons.
Key board statistic · first-week returns 41% of returned dogs came back within the first week of adoption. Separate studies report 54% and 39% of returned animals within the first 2 weeks. Approximately 50% to 70% of adopters globally report a behavioural concern within the first week. These statistics establish that the highest-risk period for placement failure is immediately post-adoption, justifying intensive early support.

Aggression and return risk notes shelter research

Aggression toward people is the adopter's primary concern. The provision of management advice at the time of adoption has a dramatic, quantified effect on return risk:

  • Caregivers who received no behavioural advice regarding a dog's human-directed aggression were 11 times more likely to return the dog.
  • Caregivers who received basic management advice regarding the same behaviour were 5.5 times more likely to return (approximately half the risk of no advice).
High-yield statistic: 11x to 5.5x The management advice exactly halves the return risk: from 11 times more likely (no advice) to 5.5 times more likely (with advice). An exam option stating advice "eliminates" return risk is incorrect. The correct figure for "with management advice" is 5.5 times, not 1 times.

Allow adopters to play an active role in learning training skills by interacting with the animal during counselling, rather than receiving passive verbal instruction only.

At-adoption counselling: the 5-minute housetraining study notes

A study showed that adopters who received a targeted 5-minute counselling session on housetraining at the time of adoption reported significantly greater success one month post-adoption than adopters who received no such information. Short, focused counselling is effective.

For separation-related problems (SRP): 30% of adopted dogs showed signs of SRP. Providing an SRP prevention handout reduced prevalence. The two strategies with highest adopter compliance from the handout were:

  • Do not interact with the dog immediately before leaving the house.
  • Provide a food-filled enrichment toy when the dog is left alone.
Key principle · less is more in counselling Compliance drops when counselling covers too many topics. The core principle is to focus on only a few high-priority issues and keep information short and simple. This maximises retention and adopter follow-through.

Post-adoption support programs notes

The primary goal of post-adoption support is to be available to provide assistance to adopters who may be experiencing problems with their new companion. Shelters must do more than simply saying "call us if you need help." Proactively reaching out, even by email, meaningfully reduces silent relinquishment to non-shelter pathways.

Topics to cover in proactive follow-up

  • Animal adjustment to the new home
  • Animal health concerns
  • Animal behavioural concerns
  • Need for assistance with post-adoption problems
  • Adopter satisfaction with the animal match
