Philosophy, Decision-Making, and Regulatory Framework

Relocation carries real risks to health, behaviour, and safety. ASV guidelines require that every transfer program be intentionally designed, evidence-based, and driven by animal welfare outcomes, not organisational convenience.

Core ASV relocation principles notes ASV

  • Decision-making must prioritise a decrease in length of stay (LOS). Holding animals for relocation when live outcomes are available locally can push shelters beyond their Capacity for Care (C4C) and compromise their community relationship.
  • When destination shelters accept more animals than they can care for, welfare for both relocated AND destination animals may be compromised, and LOS increased for all.
  • It is UNACCEPTABLE to transport animals when the transport itself is likely to be harmful to their immediate or long-term health or welfare.
  • Relocation programs must consider risks and benefits for ALL affected animals and minimise negative impacts through careful selection and planning.
Exam trap · the "no-return" rule Unless extenuating circumstances exist, animals should NOT be returned to the source shelter, even if unexpected medical or behavioural concerns arise at the destination. Transport is a significant physiological stressor and a major resource investment. If transported animals are repeatedly found ineligible, the correct response is to revisit the selection criteria and program goals, not to return the animals.

Regulatory requirements notes ASV

  • All participants must follow federal regulations for animal transportation, plus local and state regulations for both source and destination locations.
  • Departments of Agriculture and Health commonly require: health certificates (Certificates of Veterinary Inspection, CVIs), specific vaccinations, and restrictions for age and health conditions.
  • Airline requirements must also be considered for air transport.
  • Written agreements between all parties must be developed and reviewed regularly. At minimum, agreements must address medical and behavioural selection criteria, transportation requirements, and destination requirements.
  • A contact person must be identified at each transfer point.
  • A complete record of each animal's travel from source to destination must be maintained, allowing tracing of source and contacts along the route.
  • Public health and safety must be considered: zoonotic diseases, heartworm (HW) disease, and other conditions.

Emergency planning prior to transport notes

Emergency plans must be established BEFORE transport begins. Required elements:

  • Emergency contact information for all parties
  • A safe location to stop if necessary
  • Protocol to address vehicle problems
  • Plan for animal medical emergencies during transit
  • Plan for human medical emergencies during transit
  • Contact information for both the source and destination shelters

Data collection and reporting notes ASV

  • At a minimum, both source and destination partners should track: number of animals relocated, incidence of unanticipated medical and behavioural concerns, and animal outcomes.
  • Tracking total LOS (source + destination combined) provides important information about overall shelter time.
  • Collect non-urgent animal health and outcome data within 14 days of relocation.
  • Perform a quarterly analysis of aggregate data to capture programmatic trends and allow timely response.
Exam trap · 14-day data collection, quarterly analysis These are two separate and distinct timelines. Non-urgent data must be collected within 14 days of relocation (not 24 hours, not 30 days). Aggregate data is analysed quarterly (not monthly, not annually). An exam question asking about either interval expects the precise value.

International transport considerations notes

Prior to accepting transport from an international source, organisations should carefully consider:

  • The needs of regional and local partners closer to home, and the impact of the international partnership on their own mission.
  • Animal selection criteria must address: suitability for adoption, disease management, long-term quality of life, and the effects of extensive travel on the animals.
  • Shelters accepting international transports should have a written policy statement explaining the reasons behind this decision.

Source and Destination Responsibilities

Both sending and receiving organisations carry distinct and non-negotiable responsibilities. Source responsibilities begin before the animal is selected; destination responsibilities begin at the moment of arrival.

Source responsibilities notes ASV

Vaccination (critical exam point)

  • ALL eligible animals within the source population must be vaccinated at or before intake. It is insufficient to vaccinate only animals selected for relocation, because this leaves the majority of animals unprotected.
  • It is not recommended to hold animals back from transport just to allow vaccine response or to receive a booster.
Exam trap · vaccinate the whole population, not just transport candidates Vaccinating only the animals chosen for transport is explicitly stated as INSUFFICIENT. Herd immunity within the source shelter requires population-wide vaccination at or before intake. An exam option proposing "vaccinate only selected animals" is incorrect.

Pre-transport health requirements

  • Treatment for fleas, ticks, and internal parasites is strongly recommended.
  • All dogs 6 months of age and older must be tested for heartworm disease prior to relocation.
  • Health and behaviour records must be shared with the destination.
  • When required: a valid Certificate of Veterinary Inspection (CVI) and proof of rabies vaccination must accompany each animal.

Pre-travel assessment

  • Animals must be examined by trained staff within 24 hours prior to travel and deemed fit for transport.
  • Assessment evaluates: signs of infectious disease, the animal's ability to tolerate prolonged confinement, handling by different people, and direct exposure to other animals.
  • A licensed veterinarian must confirm that animals with medical concerns or recovering from surgery are fit for transport. Trained staff alone is not sufficient for medically complex animals.
  • Animals must have visual identification: collar, tag, marking on the inner ear, painting on the dew claw (neonates), or microchip.

Animal selection criteria notes

At minimum, animals considered for relocation should be:

  • Easily handled by a variety of people.
  • Able to cope with prolonged periods of confinement.
  • Not exhibiting overt aggression toward any species of animal that may also be participating in the transport.

Animals with known behavioural health concerns should NOT be considered for relocation unless: their safety AND the safety of human personnel can be ensured during the journey, AND their best opportunity for continued management and improved health and welfare lies at the destination.

Destination responsibilities notes ASV

  • Sufficient trained personnel must be ready to receive and evaluate animals upon arrival.
  • Each animal must receive a brief health assessment at intake to identify signs of infectious disease and problems requiring emergency or follow-up medical care.
  • The destination facility must have adequate housing prepared for arriving animals without displacing the existing population.
  • Quarantine is usually only appropriate for high-risk animals with direct infectious disease exposure. Routine quarantine for all transferred animals is not mandated unless required by regulatory requirements.
Key recommendation · S/N at the destination, not the source Research shows that performing spay/neuter (S/N) at the destination shelter (rather than at the source before transport) balances resources such as surgical capacity, LOS, and community needs. Data demonstrates that travel does not impact the postoperative complication rate, which is 2% within 2 weeks of relocation regardless of when S/N is performed. Performing S/N at the source adds length of stay there without reducing complications.

Sedation and behaviour medications notes

  • Should be considered when an animal is likely to have emotional welfare concerns during transport.
  • Psychopharmaceuticals must only be used under veterinary direction.
  • Safe and humane relocation programs do not use sedatives or behaviour medications to compensate for poor transportation practices.
  • Clear communication between partners is essential when behaviour medications are used.
Exam trap · sedation to point of incapacity is unacceptable It is UNACCEPTABLE for a relocation program to transport animals that are sedated or anaesthetised to the point that they are unable to swallow, walk, or thermoregulate. These animals face severe risk of: choking, aspiration pneumonia, hypothermia, and cardiac or respiratory arrest without continuous monitoring by trained medical personnel.

Transport Standards: Vehicles, Enclosures, and Species Protocols

The ASV guidelines specify precise engineering standards for vehicles, enclosures, temperature, and humidity. Species-specific and population-specific requirements add additional layers. Many of the exact numbers here are directly exam-tested.

Vehicle standards and climate control notes ASV

  • Vehicle operators must be licensed and trained in the specific vehicle they will operate.
  • Factory-installed cargo climate control units must be avoided. They are designed for static loads (such as flowers) and cannot handle the metabolic heat load of live animals. Their airflow distribution and power are insufficient.
  • Roof-mounted climate control units are recommended for animal transport vehicles.
  • Vehicles require proper insulation on sides, roof, and floor. An insulated floor mat covered with bedliner or diamond plate flooring greatly reduces road heat for animals on the lowest tier.
  • Small vehicles transporting a few animals at a time are much preferred over large commercial vehicles.
  • Interior vehicle temperatures in direct sunlight can rapidly exceed safe levels even when outside temperatures are comfortable.

Safety monitoring equipment

  • Battery-operated CO detector must be placed in the animal cargo area to detect exhaust leaks.
  • Temperature and humidity sensors must be placed at animal level where kennels are located.
  • A dual-control remote monitoring unit with programmed alarms at predetermined set points is required.
  • Scented air freshener sprays and essential oils are NOT recommended in transport programs.

Exam trap · ground vs air temperature limits are different Ground transport: 7.2°C minimum, 29.5°C maximum. Air transport (cargo hold): -7°C minimum, 27°C maximum on ground, and loading banned if interior exceeds 29°C. These are distinct sets of values. Do not apply ground transport limits to air transport questions.

Enclosure standards notes ASV

Sizing requirement

Large enough for the animal to: stand and sit erect, turn around normally while standing, and lie in a natural position without lying on another animal.

Structural requirements

  • No sharp edges; flooring must prevent injury, discomfort, and fluid leakage into other enclosures.
  • Absorbent bedding is required unless it poses a health risk to an individual animal.
  • Ventilation openings on at least 3 sides, maintaining at least 2cm of unobstructed space between vent openings and adjacent structures.
  • Enclosures must be secured to prevent movement within the vehicle, and doors secured to prevent accidental opening.
  • Use a disinfectable visual barrier (such as roll-down outdoor solar shades) to reduce visual stimulation and act as a physical barrier to respiratory droplets.
Exam trap · unfamiliar animals in the same enclosure Unfamiliar animals MUST NOT be transported together in the same primary enclosure. There are no exceptions based on size, age, temperament assessment, or duration of travel. This is a hard rule.

Behavioural and physiological transport research notes research

Beagle ground transport study (22 hours)

  • After 22-hour ground transport, beagles required 2 weeks to return serum cortisol and total bilirubin to normal levels.
  • This demonstrates profound and prolonged physiological stress from long-distance ground transport.

Beagle ground transport study (9.5 hours)

  • Elevated cortisol and corticosterone during transport; returned to baseline overnight.

Beagle air transport study

  • Increased plasma cortisol after ground transport; increased salivary cortisol after both ground and air transport.
  • Higher neutrophil counts and lower lymphocyte counts after both ground and air transport (stress leukogram).
  • Heart rate was significantly highest during loading and unloading phases, not during flight.
  • During flight: 50% of the time dogs were lying down; 75% of total time they were inactive.
  • Most commonly demonstrated behaviour: sitting and lip licking.
Exam trap · HR spikes during loading, not during flight The largest increases in heart rate during air transport occur during loading and unloading, not during cruise altitude flight, turnaround manoeuvres, or takeoff. An exam option stating HR is highest during cruise is incorrect. The mechanical act of loading is the most physiologically stressful component.

Owned dog survey

  • 44% of owners of dogs that travelled reported their animals showed signs of anxiety, fear, and motion sickness during travel.

Clomipramine pre-treatment study

  • Dogs treated with clomipramine BID starting 3 days prior to transport showed significantly:
  • Lower increase in plasma cortisol compared to placebo.
  • Lower neutrophil to lymphocyte (N:L) ratio.
  • Lower average heart rate during transport.
  • Less frequent drooling.
Exam trap · clomipramine protocol: BID x 3 days BEFORE transport The key details are: clomipramine is given twice daily (BID), starting 3 days before transport (not on the day of transport, not as a single dose). An exam option describing acepromazine at loading or single-dose administration is a distractor.

Key clinical finding · FURI risk factors in cats In the feline outcome study, three factors were associated with increased FURI prevalence: younger age, longer transport duration, and longer length of stay at the destination. These factors inform selection criteria and transport planning for feline populations.
Key finding · CIRDC is the highest post-transport disease concern in dogs While most disease incidences were below 5% in dogs, canine infectious respiratory disease complex (CIRDC) was 17% at 30-day follow-up. This is significantly higher than other diseases and should inform post-transport monitoring protocols and destination shelter infection control practices.
