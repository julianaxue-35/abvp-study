Overview, Ideal Criteria, and HPG Axis

Non-surgical sterilization exploits the hypothalamic-pituitary-gonadal (HPG) axis at three distinct levels. Understanding the axis architecture and the population modeling thresholds is foundational before learning individual product profiles.

Population modeling: the 40 to 50% rule notes

Non-surgical contraception was shown to be effective at controlling feral cat populations only when two conditions are met simultaneously:

  • Immigration and emigration must be extremely limited (essentially a closed population).
  • Treatment rates must exceed 40 to 50% of cats per 6-month interval.
Exam trap · both conditions must be met The 40 to 50% threshold alone is insufficient if immigration and emigration are unrestricted. In an open population, treated animals are continuously replaced by incoming intact animals, negating any population-level effect. Both conditions are required and testable.

The HPG axis: three-level architecture notes

Three levels of HPG axis interference notes

  • Level 1: Hypothalamus / anterior pituitary. GnRH agonists (deslorelin, gonazon) and progestins/androgens interfere with GnRH signalling and gonadotropin release.
  • Level 2: Gonads. Chemical contraceptives (zinc gluconate, calcium chloride) destroy testicular tissue directly. GnRH vaccine targets GnRH after it leaves the hypothalamus but before it crosses the blood-brain barrier, shutting down both ovulation and spermatogenesis.
  • Level 3: Downstream / oocyte level. Porcine zona pellucida (PZP) vaccine targets the glycoprotein matrix surrounding the ovulated egg, preventing sperm recognition. Works downstream, sparing the HPG axis, with fewer hormonal and behavioural side effects.

The HPG axis function is highly conserved across mammalian species, which gives GnRH-targeting approaches particularly broad cross-species efficacy. Gonadal targets include the zona pellucida, proteins expressed on the ovulated egg to facilitate sperm recognition, and the thecal support cells of the ovaries and testes.

All currently approved products are labelled for use in dogs only. Product timelines, formulations, and geographic availability are all exam-tested. Know which product is approved where and when it became available or unavailable.

Identification marking for non-surgical sterilants notes

Because chemical and hormonal sterilants leave the testicles in situ and animals retain their normal phenotypic anatomy, a form of identification is required to prevent redundant surgeries and to allow field identification of treated animals.

Acceptable identification methods

  • Microchip
  • Tattoo over the caudolateral ventral abdomen
  • Tattoo on the inner pinna of the ear
  • Placement of a small metal ear band

Identification requirements for field / feral animals

The ideal marker for wild or feral contracepted animals should be:

  • Non- or minimally invasive
  • Easy to apply in a field setting
  • Visible from a distance (to inform capture decisions)
  • Lasting approximately as long as the contraceptive is expected to last, OR containing encoded information about the contraception type and when it was applied
Exam trap · ear-tipping is CONTRAINDICATED for non-surgical contraception Ear-tipping is reserved for surgically sterilised animals. It is a permanent, irreversible mark. If applied to an animal with a temporary non-surgical contraceptive (such as a 6-month or 12-month Suprelorin implant), the implant will eventually wane and the animal will return to full fertility. Field teams seeing the ear-tip will incorrectly assume the animal is permanently sterile, completely undermining population management data and control.

Contraceptive Modality Mechanisms

Five distinct modalities exist for non-surgical contraception. Each targets a different level of the reproductive system with different mechanisms, species applicabilities, and failure modes.

1. Chemical contraceptives notes

Chemical contraceptives destroy target tissue (typically the testes) through direct injection, causing permanent sclerosis, rendering the organ non-functional.

Zinc gluconate (Neutersol / Esterilsol)

  • Intratesticular injection causes testicular sclerosis.
  • Results in permanent azoospermia or severe oligospermia and a partial reduction in circulating testosterone.
  • Testicles remain in situ; identification marking is required.
  • Subcutaneous misdirection of the drug causes severe necrotising injection-site reactions and scrotal ulcers, necessitating complete scrotal ablation.
  • Chemical restraint is strongly advisable to prevent misdirection and adverse events.
  • Indicated when surgeons, surgery suites, or skilled staff are unavailable, or where physical castration is culturally unacceptable.

Feline use

Veterinarians are significantly more reluctant to use zinc gluconate in cats for two reasons: (1) feline surgical castration is much faster than performing a chemical castration, and (2) cats experience a significantly higher rate of severe local inflammatory reactions, scrotal necrosis, and self-mutilation following intratesticular zinc gluconate injection compared to dogs.

Calcium chloride

  • Can also be used to sterilise male dogs via intratesticular injection.
  • Must be formulated in a lipid vehicle (such as alcohol or lidocaine) to achieve deep testicular necrosis without causing extreme pain and acute scrotal sloughing.
  • Limited peer-reviewed evidence compared to zinc gluconate.

Saponin (experimental)

  • Harmless in the bloodstream; toxic once taken into a cell.
  • Once taken up by gonadal support cells, triggers apoptosis.
  • Reduces sperm number and motility in male rats; reduces estrous cycling in female rats.
  • Not in routine clinical use.

2. Immunocontraceptives notes

Immunocontraceptives use the immune system to generate antibodies against specific reproductive proteins at one or more levels of the HPG axis.

Porcine zona pellucida (PZP) vaccine

AttributeDetail
TargetZona pellucida glycoprotein matrix surrounding the ovulated egg; prevents sperm recognition.
SexOnly effective in females (by definition).
AdvantageWorks downstream, avoiding upstream HPG axis disruption. Fewer concomitant changes in hormone levels, behaviour, and health consequences.
LimitationCross-species efficacy is poor: a protein developed against proteins from one species may work poorly in another.
Feline failureCompletely ineffective in queens. Results in abnormal estrous cycles and severe injection-site reactions. Not recommended in cats.
CostExpensive.

GnRH vaccine

  • Targets GnRH after it leaves the hypothalamus but before it crosses the blood-brain barrier.
  • Shuts down both ovulation and spermatogenesis.
  • Effective in large free-roaming animals: horses, white-tailed deer, elephants. Requires an annual booster injection. Local chronic sterile abscess may develop at injection site.
  • Canine failure: deemed neither safe nor effective in dogs. Caused severe injection-site reactions without reducing sperm population density or correcting sperm morphology.

3. Hormonal contraceptives notes

GnRH superagonists: deslorelin (Suprelorin) and gonazon

  • Provide continuous, non-pulsatile GnRH receptor stimulation, which initially activates then down-regulates and desensitises pituitary GnRH receptors.
  • Result: suppressed release of LH and FSH, reduced gonadal hormone production.
  • Cross-species efficacy is broad due to evolutionary conservation of the HPG axis.
  • As the implant's effectiveness wanes, reproductive hormone levels rise again; females experience an estrous cycle shortly after implantation and at the end of effectiveness.

Historical progestins and androgens (1970s)

  • Megestrol acetate (progestin) and mibolerone (androgen): introduced in the 1970s.
  • Required frequent oral administration (high dosing burden).
  • Severe adverse effects: weight gain, pyometra, induction of mammary hyperplasia and carcinomas, diabetes mellitus, adrenal suppression, and behaviour changes.
  • Largely abandoned due to this adverse effect profile.

Feralstat

  • A low dose of megestrol acetate in powdered form, designed to be mixed into canned cat food weekly and fed to feral cats for pregnancy prevention.
  • Ethical concern: no mechanism to ensure the medication is administered only to target animals. Non-target species and wildlife may consume medicated food.
  • Not generally recommended by shelter veterinary authorities.

4. Physical devices (IUD) notes

The dogspiral is the primary example. In humans, IUDs change the uterine environment to prevent implantation of fertilised eggs and are widely used. In dogs:

  • No peer-reviewed safety or efficacy studies have been conducted in dogs.
  • Significant concerns about insertion given the anatomy of the canine cervix.
  • Device safety once inserted is also a concern.

5. Gene delivery (experimental) notes

Adeno-associated virus (AAV) vectors delivering anti-GnRH antibodies

  • Antibody levels increased after administration but returned to normal levels within just one month. Insufficient for practical field use.

Mullerian-inhibiting substance (MIS)

  • A ligand produced by the ovaries that inhibits primordial follicle activation.
  • Lifelong super-physiological expression of MIS would theoretically lead to permanent contraception in females.
  • Tested in cats: only lasted a few months. Not currently a viable field tool.

Board-Critical Gaps and Exam Traps

Four high-yield items identified in the gap analysis documents that are thin or missing from the primary notes and are likely to appear on the ABVP exam.

Gap 1: the deslorelin "flare window" timing gap analysis board gap

Gap · deslorelin flare timing Your notes correctly state that deslorelin initially stimulates before it suppresses. The high-yield board fact is the specific timeline:
  • The initial stimulation phase (the "flare effect") causes a transient increase in LH, FSH, and gonadal steroids lasting 1 to 3 weeks before receptor downregulation occurs.
  • During this 1 to 3 week flare window: female dogs can experience a fertile estrus; male dogs may show increased libido or aggression.
  • This must be communicated to owners/handlers at the time of implantation.
Exam trap · deslorelin does NOT immediately suppress An exam option stating that deslorelin produces "immediate, profound downregulation of pituitary receptors within 2 hours" is incorrect. Receptor downregulation and suppression take 1 to 3 weeks. During this window, fertility is transiently enhanced, not suppressed. Female dogs should not be exposed to intact males during the flare window.

Gap 2: feline reluctance for zinc gluconate is not only about speed gap analysis board gap

Gap · feline zinc gluconate: two reasons for reluctance Your notes state that veterinarians are reluctant to use chemical castration in cats because surgical castration is faster. This is correct but incomplete. The board-complete answer requires BOTH reasons:
  1. Feline surgical castration is indeed very fast and straightforward.
  2. Cats experience a significantly higher rate of severe local inflammatory reactions, scrotal necrosis, and self-mutilation following intratesticular zinc gluconate injection compared to dogs.
An exam option that cites only speed, or only local reactions, is partially correct. The correct answer includes both.

Gap 3: calcium chloride vehicle requirement gap analysis board gap

Gap · calcium chloride must be suspended in a lipid vehicle Your notes mention that studies on calcium chloride as an intratesticular sterilant are limited, without specifying formulation requirements. The board-complete fact is:
  • Calcium chloride must be suspended in a lipid vehicle such as alcohol or lidocaine.
  • This is required to achieve deep testicular necrosis without causing extreme acute pain and scrotal sloughing at the injection site.
  • A pure aqueous solution of calcium chloride would cause severe pain and surface sloughing, not controlled deep necrosis.

Gap 4: ear-tipping contraindication: the operational reason gap analysis board gap

Gap · WHY ear-tipping fails for non-surgical contraception Your notes state that ear-tipping is not appropriate for non-surgical contraception, which is correct. The operational rationale that must be understood for the board exam is:
  • An ear-tip is a permanent, irreversible mark that communicates to field teams that an animal is permanently sterile.
  • Non-surgical products (such as a 6-month or 12-month Suprelorin implant) are temporary.
  • When the implant wanes, the animal returns to full fertility. But the ear-tip persists.
  • Field monitors seeing the ear-tip will assume the animal is still sterile, and will not re-treat or re-capture it.
  • This completely undermines population management models: the animal reproduces freely while flagged as sterile.
The correct marker must match the duration of contraception, or encode information about contraception type and expiry date.
